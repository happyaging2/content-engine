What to Know

  • Ashwagandha has solid human trial evidence for reducing cortisol and perceived stress, two real contributors to perimenopause symptoms.
  • Evidence for ashwagandha as a direct hormonal intervention in perimenopause is much weaker than supplement marketing suggests.
  • Women with thyroid conditions should use caution: ashwagandha may raise thyroid hormone levels and can interact with thyroid medications.
  • The women most likely to benefit are those whose perimenopause symptoms are driven primarily by cortisol overload and chronic stress rather than estrogen deficit.
  • Ashwagandha is not a replacement for hormone therapy when hormone therapy is medically appropriate.
  • Quality and standardization vary enormously between products; look for KSM-66 or Sensoril extracts with documented withanolide content.

If you have searched for perimenopause relief, you have almost certainly encountered ashwagandha. It is marketed as a hormone balancer, a cortisol crusher, a libido restorer, and an all-purpose midlife rescue supplement. Some of those claims have real evidence behind them. Others are extrapolated far beyond what the research supports. Here is an honest review of what the trials actually show and what they do not.

What Is Ashwagandha?

Ashwagandha (Withania somnifera) is a root herb from the Solanaceae family, native to India, North Africa, and the Mediterranean. It has been used in Ayurvedic medicine for over 3,000 years as a rasayana, a class of tonic herbs used to promote vigor, vitality, and longevity. The name translates loosely to "smell of horse," a reference to both its distinct aroma and its traditional association with strength and stamina.

Its primary active compounds are withanolides, a class of steroidal lactones that are concentrated in the root and, to a lesser extent, the leaves. Standardized extracts specify withanolide content, typically 2.5 to 5 percent, to ensure consistent potency across batches. The two most clinically studied extract forms are KSM-66 (full-spectrum root extract) and Sensoril (root and leaf extract). Most of the human clinical trial data involves these standardized extracts at doses of 300 to 600mg per day.

Ashwagandha works through several mechanisms that are relevant to perimenopause. It modulates the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, reducing the cortisol response to stress. It interacts with GABA-A receptors, producing a calming effect that supports sleep onset. It appears to influence thyroid-stimulating hormone and thyroid hormone levels. And it has some evidence for supporting sexual function through central nervous system pathways. The question is not whether ashwagandha does anything. It clearly does. The question is what specifically it does for women in perimenopause, and at what level of evidence.

Why It Matters for Women Over 40

Perimenopause is not a single hormonal event. It is a multi-year transition in which estrogen and progesterone fluctuate erratically before declining, cortisol regulation becomes less precise, thyroid function is more likely to shift, and the adrenal glands are asked to compensate for declining ovarian hormone production. This hormonal complexity is why women in perimenopause often feel like their bodies have become unpredictable in ways that do not map neatly to any single diagnosis.

Cortisol plays a particularly important and underappreciated role. Chronic stress elevates cortisol, and elevated cortisol actively suppresses progesterone production by competing for the same precursor molecule (pregnenolone) in a process sometimes called "pregnenolone steal." This means that a woman under sustained high stress may experience worsened perimenopause symptoms not just because of age-related hormonal decline but because stress is actively depleting the building blocks her body would otherwise use for progesterone. Any intervention that meaningfully reduces cortisol therefore has plausible biological relevance to perimenopause symptom burden.

This is where ashwagandha has its strongest foothold: as a cortisol-modulating adaptogen that addresses one specific upstream driver of perimenopausal symptoms.

What the Research Says

The evidence for ashwagandha spans a spectrum from strong and replicable to preliminary and extrapolated. Here is what the human trials actually show:

Cortisol and stress reduction: This is the best-established benefit. Multiple double-blind, placebo-controlled trials demonstrate that ashwagandha root extract reduces serum cortisol and perceived stress scores in stressed adults. A study published in Medicine by Chandrasekhar et al. found that 300mg of KSM-66 twice daily reduced serum cortisol by 27.9 percent over 60 days compared with placebo (PMID: 23439798). A separate trial in the Journal of the American Nutraceutical Association found significant reductions in anxiety scores and cortisol. This is not weak preliminary data. The cortisol benefit from standardized ashwagandha is one of the better-supported claims in the adaptogen literature.

Fatigue and energy: Multiple trials show improvements in energy levels and reduced fatigue with ashwagandha supplementation. A randomized controlled trial in the Journal of Ethnopharmacology found that ashwagandha improved fatigue scores and quality of life in adults with self-reported fatigue (Mahdi et al., PMID: 21170205). Given that perimenopausal fatigue often has a cortisol and sleep-disruption component, this benefit is indirectly relevant.

Female sexual function: A randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial by Dongre et al. published in BioMed Research International found that 300mg of ashwagandha root extract twice daily significantly improved arousal, lubrication, orgasm, and sexual satisfaction scores in healthy women compared with placebo (PMID: 26834003). This is a genuine human RCT in women, not an animal study or in vitro finding. The mechanism likely involves reduced anxiety and improved general vitality rather than direct hormonal action.

Thyroid support: A pilot study found that ashwagandha improved TSH, T3, and T4 levels in adults with subclinical hypothyroidism (Sharma et al., PMID: 29840404). This is a small study, but it is consistent with ashwagandha's known HPA-thyroid axis interactions. Perimenopause is associated with higher rates of thyroid dysfunction, so this is a relevant finding.

Menopausal symptoms: A randomized, double-blind trial published in the Journal of Ethnopharmacology specifically assessed ashwagandha in climacteric women (perimenopausal and early postmenopausal) and found significant reductions in menopause-related quality of life scores compared with placebo, including improvements in hot flash frequency, mood, and sleep (Gopal et al., PMID: 32540634). This is the most directly relevant trial, and it does show benefit. However, the study was relatively small (n=91) and did not measure hormone levels, so it is unclear whether the improvements reflect direct hormonal effects or cortisol and anxiety reduction.

What the Evidence Doesn't Support

The marketing around ashwagandha and perimenopause often implies that it "balances hormones" or acts as a natural alternative to hormone therapy. The evidence does not support this framing, and women making decisions about their health deserve a clearer picture.

Ashwagandha does not raise estrogen in humans. There are animal studies suggesting weak estrogenic activity from specific withanolide compounds, but these have not been reproduced in human clinical trials measuring serum estradiol. Women who are considering or avoiding HRT because of concerns about estrogen should know that ashwagandha does not provide estrogen or function as an estrogen substitute. If low estrogen is driving your symptoms (severe vasomotor symptoms, vaginal dryness, rapid bone density loss), ashwagandha is not addressing the root cause.

Ashwagandha does not reverse progesterone decline. The cortisol and pregnenolone relationship is real, but ashwagandha's effect on progesterone levels specifically has not been demonstrated in human trials. Reducing cortisol may free up some pregnenolone for progesterone synthesis, but this is a speculative mechanistic pathway, not a measured clinical outcome in perimenopausal women.

The claim that ashwagandha "balances hormones" is a marketing phrase, not a clinical description. Adaptogens modulate stress response systems and support the body's homeostatic mechanisms. They do not target specific hormone levels in the way that pharmaceutical hormone therapy does, and they cannot compensate for the substantial ovarian hormone decline that characterizes late perimenopause and menopause. Calling this "hormone balance" sets expectations that the evidence does not support.

Some women use ashwagandha expecting the same effect as hormone therapy for moderate-to-severe hot flashes. Clinical trials do not support ashwagandha as an effective treatment for vasomotor symptoms at that severity. If you have frequent, disruptive hot flashes, the evidence-based options include hormone therapy, certain antidepressants (SSRIs/SNRIs), and fezolinetant (a newer non-hormonal option). Ashwagandha is not in this category.

Who This Is NOT For

Women with thyroid disease or on thyroid medication. Ashwagandha's effects on thyroid hormone levels are real enough that women taking levothyroxine or other thyroid medications should not start ashwagandha without discussing it with their prescribing physician. Raising T3 and T4 while on thyroid medication could push thyroid levels outside the therapeutic range. This is not a theoretical concern; it is a pharmacodynamic interaction that warrants monitoring.

Women who are pregnant or actively trying to conceive. Ashwagandha has traditional use as a uterine stimulant and has shown abortifacient effects in animal studies at high doses. It should not be used during pregnancy. Women actively trying to conceive should discuss it with their OB-GYN before use.

Women with autoimmune conditions. Ashwagandha is an immune modulator, and in theory it could exacerbate autoimmune conditions by stimulating immune activity. People with lupus, rheumatoid arthritis, Hashimoto's thyroiditis, or multiple sclerosis should use caution and consult with their rheumatologist or immunologist before starting.

Women expecting a substitute for hormone therapy. If your perimenopause symptoms are severe (frequent disruptive hot flashes, significant vaginal atrophy, sleep disruption that is impacting your function), ashwagandha is not the right primary intervention. Hormone therapy, prescribed and monitored by a physician, has far stronger evidence for managing moderate-to-severe perimenopausal symptoms. Use ashwagandha as a supportive tool within a broader protocol, not as a reason to delay a medical conversation.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best dose of ashwagandha for perimenopause?

The clinical trials that showed benefits in women used 300mg of a standardized extract twice daily (total 600mg/day). This is a reasonable starting point. Some women do well with a single 600mg dose at night, which leverages ashwagandha's mild GABA-A activity for sleep support.

How long before I see results from ashwagandha?

Most clinical trials showing cortisol and stress benefits ran for 8-12 weeks. Some women notice shifts in anxiety and sleep quality within 2-4 weeks, but the full benefit for stress resilience and fatigue typically takes 6-8 weeks of consistent daily use.

Can I take ashwagandha with NAD+ supplements?

Yes. Ashwagandha and NAD+ precursors (such as NMN or NR) work through different mechanisms and there are no known interactions. Ashwagandha modulates the stress response and HPA axis; NAD+ precursors support mitochondrial and cellular energy systems. They are complementary.

Does ashwagandha affect sleep?

Yes, positively for most women. Ashwagandha has GABA-A receptor activity that supports sleep onset and reduces nighttime anxiety. Several trials have shown improvements in sleep quality and sleep onset latency with ashwagandha supplementation. Taking it at night captures this benefit.

What form of ashwagandha is best?

Look for standardized extracts specifying withanolide content. KSM-66 is the most studied full-spectrum root extract. Sensoril uses both root and leaf and may have a slightly different withanolide profile. Both have human clinical trial support. Avoid products that list only "ashwagandha powder" with no standardization details, as potency cannot be assured.
