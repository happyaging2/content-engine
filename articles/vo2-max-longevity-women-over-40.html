VO2 max, your body's maximum oxygen-processing capacity during intense exercise, is one of the strongest predictors of longevity, and the good news for women over 40 is that it can be meaningfully improved with consistent training.

If you have ever wondered why some women in their 50s and 60s seem to have boundless energy while others struggle to climb stairs without effort, VO2 max is one of the most important biological variables separating them. As of 2026, longevity researchers increasingly regard cardiorespiratory fitness as a top-tier biomarker, one that outperforms many traditional risk factors in predicting how long, and how well, you will live. This article breaks down what VO2 max is, why it declines after 40, and what the evidence says about improving it.

What to Know

  • VO2 max is the gold standard measure of cardiorespiratory fitness and a powerful predictor of all-cause mortality risk.
  • Research suggests VO2 max declines approximately 10% per decade after age 30 in sedentary women, but training can slow or partially reverse this decline.
  • A combination of Zone 2 cardio and high-intensity interval training (HIIT) produces the greatest VO2 max improvements.
  • NAD+ supports mitochondrial oxygen utilization, which underpins aerobic capacity and may complement a VO2 max training program.
  • Women with cardiac conditions, joint problems, or those new to exercise should start with walking and consult their physician before high-intensity training.

What Is VO2 Max?

VO2 max stands for "maximal oxygen uptake" and is defined as the maximum volume of oxygen your body can consume and utilize per minute during intense aerobic exercise, expressed in milliliters of oxygen per kilogram of body weight per minute (mL/kg/min). It is the gold standard measurement of cardiorespiratory fitness because it captures the integrated efficiency of your heart, lungs, blood vessels, and muscle mitochondria working together to deliver and use oxygen during exertion. A higher VO2 max means your body can sustain more intense exercise for longer, recover faster, and operate with greater physiological reserve, all of which translate directly to quality and length of life. It is typically measured in a laboratory using a graded treadmill or cycling test with a breathing mask, though wearable devices now provide reasonable estimates during outdoor exercise.

Why VO2 Max Matters After 40

After age 30, VO2 max begins to decline. Research suggests that in sedentary women, this decline runs approximately 10% per decade, meaning that by age 50 a woman who has not trained consistently may have a VO2 max roughly 20% lower than at her peak. This is not merely an athletic concern. Research suggests that low cardiorespiratory fitness carries a mortality risk comparable in magnitude to smoking, making it one of the most modifiable longevity risk factors available. The perimenopause years add another layer: declining estrogen affects heart efficiency, vascular tone, and muscle mitochondrial function, all of which contribute to VO2 max. Research suggests women in their 40s who maintain or improve VO2 max during this transition have better cardiovascular outcomes over the following decades. This is one of the most actionable windows in a woman's life to invest in cardiorespiratory fitness.

How VO2 Max Predicts Longevity

Research suggests that people with higher VO2 max scores have significantly lower all-cause mortality risk. The relationship is dose-dependent: each incremental improvement in fitness category (e.g., moving from "below average" to "average" or from "average" to "above average") is associated with a meaningful reduction in mortality risk. Importantly, this relationship holds even for women over 40 who have spent years sedentary. Starting a VO2 max-focused training program in your 40s produces measurable improvements in fitness within 8-12 weeks, and those improvements translate to real-world health benefits.

VO2 Max Differences Between Women and Men

Research suggests women typically have 15-25% lower VO2 max values than men of the same age, primarily due to differences in body composition, hemoglobin concentration, and heart size. This does not mean women have inferior fitness capacity. It means that normative VO2 max ranges must be interpreted using female-specific reference values. Research suggests that average VO2 max for women aged 40-49 falls in the range of approximately 27-38 mL/kg/min, with trained athletes scoring significantly higher and sedentary women lower. Use these benchmarks as context, not judgment.

The Cooper Test: A Simple Field Assessment

The 12-minute Cooper Test offers a simple way to estimate your VO2 max without laboratory equipment. The test works like this: find a flat running surface (a track is ideal), run or walk as fast as you can for exactly 12 minutes, and record the distance covered in kilometers. Your estimated VO2 max is approximately: (distance in meters minus 504.9) divided by 44.73. Many fitness apps and GPS watches will calculate this automatically. This gives you a starting baseline and a way to track progress over a 12-week training program.

12-Week VO2 Max Protocol for Women Over 40

According to Happy Aging's review of cardiorespiratory fitness research, the following progressive protocol is appropriate for most healthy women over 40 with at least a basic fitness base. Consult your physician before starting if you have any cardiac history or joint limitations.

  1. Weeks 1-2: Establish your aerobic base. Walk or light jog 30-40 minutes, 4-5 days per week, at a pace where you can hold a full conversation (Zone 2). This builds mitochondrial density and conditions your cardiovascular system for more intense work ahead.
  2. Weeks 3-4: Add short intervals. Twice per week, add 4-6 intervals of 1 minute at a hard but sustainable effort (roughly 80-85% max heart rate), followed by 2 minutes of easy walking or jogging. Total interval session: 25-30 minutes.
  3. Weeks 5-6: Increase interval intensity. Progress to 4x4-minute intervals at 85-90% max heart rate, with 3-4 minutes of active recovery between each. This is one of the most evidence-supported HIIT formats for VO2 max improvement.
  4. Weeks 7-8: Build total weekly volume. Increase Zone 2 sessions to 40-50 minutes and maintain 2 HIIT sessions per week. Add one strength training session if not already doing so.
  5. Weeks 9-10: Peak training load. Push HIIT intervals to 5x4 minutes at 90% max heart rate. Zone 2 sessions at 50 minutes. Monitor recovery. If resting heart rate is elevated or energy is low, take a lighter week.
  6. Weeks 11-12: Deload and retest. Reduce volume by 30% while maintaining intensity. At the end of week 12, repeat the Cooper Test or use your wearable device's VO2 max estimate to track your progress.
  7. Ongoing: Maintain with 2 Zone 2 + 1-2 HIIT sessions per week. Research suggests that the fitness gains from a focused VO2 max program are preserved with consistent maintenance training at lower volumes than the building phase.

How NAD+ Supports VO2 Max and Aerobic Capacity

NAD+ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide) is a coenzyme found in every cell of the body and is essential to the mitochondrial process of converting oxygen and nutrients into ATP (cellular energy). When your muscles are working hard during aerobic exercise, mitochondria are the engine rooms converting oxygen into the energy that keeps you moving. Research suggests that NAD+ levels decline with age, and this decline is associated with reduced mitochondrial efficiency, which shows up as earlier fatigue, slower recovery, and reduced aerobic capacity. Supporting NAD+ levels through supplementation with precursors such as NMN or NR may help maintain the mitochondrial machinery that aerobic performance depends on. While direct human trials specifically measuring VO2 max in women supplementing NAD+ precursors are limited as of 2026, the mechanistic connection between NAD+, mitochondrial efficiency, and aerobic capacity is well supported in the cellular biology literature.

What the Evidence Doesn't Support

The evidence does not support any single supplement or shortcut significantly raising VO2 max without consistent training. VO2 max improvement is primarily an adaptation driven by progressive aerobic and interval training. No pill replaces this. The evidence also does not support high-intensity training as universally appropriate for all women over 40. Women with undiagnosed cardiac conditions, advanced osteoporosis, or significant joint damage can injure themselves or worse with unsupervised HIIT. The evidence also does not support treating a low VO2 max score as a fixed limitation. Research consistently shows that even previously sedentary women in their 50s and 60s can achieve meaningful VO2 max improvements with appropriate training, sometimes comparable in magnitude to what younger women achieve.

Who Should NOT Start High-Intensity Training Without Medical Clearance

Women with diagnosed cardiac conditions (arrhythmia, structural heart disease, recent cardiac event), significant joint problems (advanced knee or hip arthritis), or severe osteoporosis should not begin high-intensity interval training without explicit physician clearance. Women who are completely new to exercise and have been sedentary for more than two years should start with walking programs before progressing to any intensity above Zone 2. If you experience chest pain, significant shortness of breath beyond normal exertion, dizziness, or palpitations during exercise, stop immediately and seek medical evaluation.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a good VO2 max for a woman in her 40s?

Research suggests that average VO2 max for women aged 40-49 falls in the range of approximately 27-38 mL/kg/min. A score above 38 is generally considered above average for this age group, while below 27 indicates an area for meaningful improvement. Elite endurance athletes in their 40s can score above 50 mL/kg/min.

Can I improve my VO2 max after 40 without a gym?

Yes. Outdoor running, brisk walking with hills, cycling, swimming, and jump rope are all effective VO2 max training tools that require no gym. The key is progressive overload: gradually increasing intensity or duration over weeks and months to keep pushing your cardiovascular system to adapt.

How quickly will I see VO2 max improvement?

Research suggests that consistent training over 8-12 weeks produces measurable improvements in VO2 max, even in previously sedentary individuals. Early gains tend to come quickly, with diminishing returns over time as fitness level rises. Most women see meaningful changes within a 12-week focused program.

Does menopause permanently lower VO2 max?

Estrogen decline during perimenopause and menopause does affect cardiovascular efficiency and muscle mitochondrial function, contributing to VO2 max decline. However, research suggests this decline is not irreversible. Postmenopausal women who train consistently can achieve and maintain strong VO2 max scores, often comparable to those of younger sedentary women.

Is walking enough to improve VO2 max?

For completely deconditioned individuals, brisk walking will initially improve VO2 max. However, as fitness improves, the intensity of walking is generally not sufficient to drive further VO2 max gains. Progressing to jogging, incline walking, cycling at higher intensities, or interval training is needed to continue improving aerobic capacity.

How does sleep affect VO2 max?

Quality sleep is essential for the cardiovascular and mitochondrial adaptations that drive VO2 max improvement. Research suggests that chronic sleep deprivation blunts training adaptations and increases recovery time. Prioritizing 7-9 hours of quality sleep alongside your training program is not optional. It is part of the protocol.
