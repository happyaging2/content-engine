BDNF, brain-derived neurotrophic factor, is often called "fertilizer for the brain." It is a protein that supports the survival of existing neurons, promotes the growth of new ones, and strengthens synaptic connections, the physical basis of learning and memory. BDNF declines measurably after 40, and this decline is linked to the cognitive changes many women notice in midlife: the slower recall, the difficulty sustaining focus, the occasional word-finding struggle that did not happen in your 30s. The encouraging news is that BDNF is highly responsive to lifestyle interventions, and the strategies that boost it are among the most health-promoting things you can do for your whole body, not just your brain.

What to Know

  • BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) is the most important neuroplasticity protein in the adult brain and declines with age, stress, poor sleep, and sedentary behavior
  • Exercise is the single most powerful BDNF booster known to science, with aerobic exercise increasing BDNF by 200 to 300 percent in some studies
  • Estrogen directly stimulates BDNF production, which is why cognitive changes are common in perimenopause when estrogen fluctuates and declines
  • Specific foods, supplements, and habits have measurable BDNF-boosting effects, including omega-3 fatty acids, intermittent fasting, and cold exposure
  • Chronic stress is one of the fastest ways to lower BDNF, because cortisol actively suppresses BDNF gene expression in the hippocampus

What BDNF Does in the Brain After 40

BDNF is a member of the neurotrophin family of growth factors. It binds primarily to the TrkB receptor (tropomyosin receptor kinase B) and activates downstream signaling cascades including the MAPK and PI3K pathways, which promote neuronal survival, synaptic plasticity, and the formation of new synaptic connections. In practical terms, BDNF is what allows the brain to change in response to learning and experience, a process called neuroplasticity.

The hippocampus, the brain region most critical for new memory formation and spatial navigation, is especially dependent on BDNF. Hippocampal volume correlates strongly with circulating BDNF levels in adults, and people with higher BDNF have measurably larger hippocampi. This is not a trivial anatomical observation: hippocampal shrinkage is associated with memory decline, difficulty learning new information, and increased risk of dementia.

BDNF also plays a key role in mood regulation. Lower BDNF levels are consistently found in people with depression and anxiety, and BDNF increase is proposed as a key mechanism behind the antidepressant effects of both exercise and pharmaceutical antidepressants (most of which are known to upregulate BDNF through various pathways). For women over 40 experiencing mood changes alongside cognitive symptoms, addressing BDNF is relevant to both concerns simultaneously.

After 40, BDNF production declines through several converging mechanisms: reduced estrogen signaling (estrogen directly upregulates BDNF gene expression through estrogen response elements), increased stress and cortisol load, reduced sleep quality, declining exercise capacity, and dietary shifts that reduce key BDNF-supporting nutrients.

Exercise: The Most Powerful BDNF Strategy

The relationship between exercise and BDNF is among the best-documented findings in neuroscience. Aerobic exercise increases circulating BDNF levels acutely by 200 to 300 percent during and immediately after a session, and with regular exercise, resting BDNF levels rise significantly above pre-exercise baselines.

The landmark study by Erickson and colleagues, published in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences in 2011, demonstrated that older adults who walked briskly for 40 minutes three times per week for one year showed a 2 percent increase in hippocampal volume, alongside measurable increases in BDNF and improvements on memory tests. The control group who did stretching (without aerobic exercise) showed the typical 1 to 2 percent age-related hippocampal shrinkage over the same period. This study was the first to demonstrate that aerobic exercise could actually grow the hippocampus in older adults rather than merely slowing its decline.

For women over 40, three to five sessions of aerobic exercise per week at a moderate intensity (brisk walking, cycling, swimming, jogging) of 20 to 40 minutes each are consistently shown to produce measurable BDNF improvements. High-intensity interval training (HIIT) produces some of the strongest acute BDNF spikes, though the sustainable benefits come from consistency rather than intensity. Resistance training alone produces more modest BDNF increases than aerobic exercise, but combined aerobic and resistance training protocols produce the greatest sustained benefit.

Diet and Nutrition for BDNF After 40

What you eat has a measurable influence on BDNF levels, primarily through metabolic and inflammatory pathways that modulate BDNF gene expression.

Omega-3 fatty acids, particularly DHA (docosahexaenoic acid), have the strongest evidence among dietary nutrients for BDNF support. DHA is incorporated into neuronal membranes and directly supports BDNF signaling efficiency. In both animal models and human observational studies, DHA intake correlates positively with BDNF levels and hippocampal function. Cold-water fatty fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel) are the richest dietary sources; supplemental omega-3s providing at least 1,000mg of DHA daily are the most studied doses for cognitive support.

Blueberries and other polyphenol-rich berries have been shown to increase BDNF in animal studies and improve memory performance in human trials, with proposed mechanisms involving flavonoid activation of the CREB transcription factor, which is upstream of BDNF gene expression. Regular consumption of a variety of colorful plant foods provides a broad range of polyphenols that support BDNF signaling.

Curcumin (from turmeric) activates BDNF gene expression and has shown cognitive benefits in some human trials, though bioavailability is a challenge with standard curcumin preparations. High-bioavailability curcumin formulations (liposomal, phospholipid complexes) are more likely to produce meaningful BDNF-related brain effects than standard turmeric powder.

Intermittent fasting and time-restricted eating have been shown to increase BDNF production in animal models, likely through a ketone body mechanism: the ketone beta-hydroxybutyrate activates the transcription factor CREB, which directly upregulates BDNF gene expression. Even 14 to 16 hour fasting windows (easily achievable with an early dinner and a later breakfast) have produced BDNF-relevant metabolic shifts in human studies.

Sleep, Stress, and BDNF: The Hidden Drivers

Sleep is a critical period for BDNF function. During slow-wave deep sleep, BDNF is produced and released in the hippocampus at higher rates than during wakefulness, supporting the overnight consolidation of memories from short-term to long-term storage. Sleep deprivation, even a single night of poor sleep, measurably reduces circulating BDNF levels and impairs the hippocampal consolidation processes that depend on it.

Chronic stress is one of the most potent suppressors of BDNF. Cortisol inhibits BDNF gene expression in the hippocampus through glucocorticoid receptor-mediated transcriptional suppression. This is the neurobiological mechanism behind the observation that chronic stress shrinks the hippocampus and impairs memory: it does so in part by shutting down BDNF production in the region where BDNF is most critically needed. Managing stress through regular relaxation practices, social connection, and lifestyle strategies that reduce cortisol load is therefore not just emotionally supportive but neurologically necessary for maintaining BDNF levels after 40.

Learning new skills is another BDNF activator that is often overlooked. Novel cognitive challenges, whether learning a new language, a musical instrument, a craft, or even a new software tool, create the neural demands that trigger BDNF upregulation as the brain works to form new circuits. Maintaining cognitive novelty is a sustainable long-term strategy for BDNF support.

Supplements That Support BDNF Production

Several supplements have evidence for supporting BDNF-related brain function in humans.

Lion's mane mushroom (Hericium erinaceus) stimulates nerve growth factor (NGF) production, a related neurotrophin, and has shown cognitive improvements in randomized trials in adults with mild cognitive impairment. While NGF is distinct from BDNF, the neuroplasticity pathways overlap and lion's mane's cognitive benefits likely include BDNF-adjacent mechanisms.

Phosphatidylserine, a phospholipid concentrated in neuronal membranes, has shown improvements in cognitive performance and memory in trials with older adults, with mechanisms that include supporting BDNF receptor (TrkB) signaling at the synapse. Bacopa monnieri has been shown in multiple trials to improve memory consolidation and reduce cortisol, the BDNF suppressor, creating a favorable environment for BDNF function.

The amino acid acetyl-L-carnitine (ALCAR) crosses the blood-brain barrier and has demonstrated neuroprotective effects in several studies, including support for BDNF signaling and mitochondrial function in neurons. These effects make ALCAR a particularly relevant brain-support nutrient for women over 40 whose BDNF decline is compounded by declining mitochondrial energy in neurons.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the signs that BDNF is low?

There is no direct test for BDNF available in standard clinical settings. Low BDNF is associated with: difficulty forming or retaining new memories, slower mental processing, persistent low mood or depression that does not have an obvious life-circumstances cause, reduced motivation, and a general sense of mental fogginess. These symptoms overlap with many conditions, but if they cluster together with risk factors for BDNF decline (sedentary lifestyle, chronic stress, poor sleep, low omega-3 diet), addressing BDNF-boosting strategies makes practical sense without a formal test.

How quickly does exercise boost BDNF?

Acutely, BDNF rises within 10 to 15 minutes of moderate aerobic exercise and peaks 15 to 30 minutes after exercise ends. Resting BDNF levels begin to rise with consistent training within 2 to 4 weeks. The hippocampal volume increase documented by Erickson et al. required 12 months of three-times-weekly aerobic exercise. For most practical purposes, you can expect improvements in mental clarity and focus within 2 to 4 weeks of starting regular aerobic exercise, with longer-term memory and neuroplasticity benefits building over 3 to 12 months.

Does estrogen affect BDNF?

Yes, strongly. Estrogen directly upregulates BDNF gene expression in the hippocampus and other brain regions through estrogen response elements in the BDNF promoter region. This is a key reason why cognitive symptoms including brain fog and memory difficulty often emerge during perimenopause when estrogen becomes erratic: declining and fluctuating estrogen removes a major driver of BDNF production. Strategies that stabilize other BDNF inputs (exercise, sleep, omega-3s) become particularly important during this transition to compensate for reduced estrogenic BDNF stimulation.

Can you test your BDNF level?

BDNF can be measured in research settings from blood serum or plasma, but serum BDNF does not perfectly reflect brain BDNF because the majority of circulating BDNF comes from platelets rather than neurons. Some functional medicine practitioners offer BDNF testing, but interpretation is limited by the lack of standardized reference ranges and the uncertainty about what peripheral BDNF measurements mean for brain function specifically. Tracking cognitive performance outcomes (memory tests, processing speed) alongside lifestyle interventions is often more practically useful than tracking BDNF numbers.

Does cold water therapy boost BDNF?

Emerging evidence suggests yes. Cold water immersion and cold showers activate the norepinephrine system, and norepinephrine is one of the upstream signals that stimulates BDNF gene expression. Studies in humans have shown that cold water immersion raises norepinephrine by up to 300 percent, and this is proposed as a mechanism for the mood-lifting and cognitive-boosting effects many people report with regular cold exposure. Formal BDNF measurement in humans after cold therapy is limited but consistent with the norepinephrine mechanism.
