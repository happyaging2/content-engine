GLP-1 medications do cause muscle loss, and women over 40 face higher risk because they already have less muscle mass and slower recovery than younger adults.

If you are on semaglutide, tirzepatide, or another GLP-1 receptor agonist, protecting your lean muscle is one of the most important things you can do. As of 2026, research continues to show that rapid weight loss from any cause, including GLP-1 therapy, leads to loss of both fat and muscle. The good news is that you can minimize muscle loss with the right nutrition, exercise, and supplement strategy.

What to Know
  • GLP-1 medications reduce appetite, which can lead to insufficient protein intake and muscle breakdown.
  • Women over 40 are already losing muscle mass naturally (sarcopenia begins in your 30s).
  • Resistance training 2-3 times per week is the single most effective muscle-preserving strategy.
  • Protein targets for GLP-1 users are higher than standard recommendations: aim for 1.2-1.6 grams per kilogram of body weight daily.
  • Strategic supplementation with leucine-rich protein and creatine may support lean mass retention.

What Is GLP-1-Related Muscle Loss?

GLP-1 stands for glucagon-like peptide-1, a hormone that regulates appetite and blood sugar. GLP-1 receptor agonists like semaglutide (Ozempic, Wegovy) and tirzepatide (Mounjaro, Zepbound) mimic this hormone to reduce hunger and slow gastric emptying. They are highly effective at producing significant weight loss.

GLP-1-related muscle loss refers to the reduction in skeletal muscle mass that can occur during GLP-1 therapy. During rapid weight loss, the body does not exclusively burn fat. A portion of the weight lost comes from lean tissue, including muscle. Research suggests that without deliberate intervention, roughly 25-40% of total weight lost during GLP-1 therapy may come from lean mass rather than fat.

This matters because muscle is metabolically active tissue. It supports strength, mobility, metabolic rate, insulin sensitivity, and long-term functional independence. Losing significant muscle mass during GLP-1 therapy can leave you lighter on the scale but physically weaker and metabolically less resilient.

Why It Matters for Women Over 40

Women over 40 are at a compounded disadvantage when it comes to muscle preservation during GLP-1 therapy.

First, sarcopenia (age-related muscle loss) naturally begins in the late 30s and accelerates through perimenopause and menopause. Estrogen has muscle-protective properties, so as estrogen declines, muscle protein synthesis slows and muscle breakdown rates increase.

Second, women typically carry less absolute muscle mass than men to begin with, making any loss more consequential. A 5-pound muscle loss represents a higher percentage of total muscle for a smaller-framed woman than for a larger-framed man.

Third, reduced appetite from GLP-1 therapy means many women are eating significantly fewer calories, often making it difficult to hit protein targets. When total calorie intake drops, the body is more likely to break down muscle tissue for energy unless protein intake is specifically prioritized.

Recent research through 2026 suggests that women on GLP-1 therapy who do not actively protect lean mass may see declines in grip strength, functional mobility, and resting metabolic rate that persist even after stopping the medication. Happy Aging's position: GLP-1 therapy is a powerful tool, but it must be paired with a muscle-preservation protocol.

What the Research Says

According to Happy Aging's review of current evidence on GLP-1 medications and body composition, several findings are well-supported:

GLP-1 therapy and lean mass loss: Clinical trials of semaglutide and tirzepatide consistently report that 25-40% of total weight lost includes lean mass. The absolute amount varies by baseline body composition, diet quality, and physical activity level. This pattern is consistent with other calorie-restriction interventions, not unique to GLP-1s.

Resistance training as the primary intervention: Research across multiple exercise science studies shows that progressive resistance training (lifting weights or using resistance bands) is the most effective strategy for preserving or building muscle during calorie restriction. The benefit applies specifically to older adults and women in perimenopause and postmenopause.

Protein intake and muscle protein synthesis: Research suggests that older adults require more dietary protein to achieve the same muscle protein synthesis response as younger adults, a phenomenon called anabolic resistance. Current evidence-based recommendations for older adults in caloric deficit range from 1.2-1.6 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight daily, significantly above the general RDA of 0.8 g/kg.

Leucine as a trigger: The amino acid leucine plays a specific role in signaling muscle protein synthesis. Research suggests that hitting a minimum leucine threshold at each meal (roughly 2.5-3 grams) is important for maximizing the muscle-building signal. This is why leucine-rich protein sources are particularly useful for GLP-1 users eating smaller meals.

Creatine monohydrate: Multiple systematic reviews support creatine supplementation as a low-risk, evidence-based strategy for preserving lean mass in older adults, particularly when combined with resistance training. Research suggests it may be especially beneficial for women, who tend to have lower baseline creatine stores.

What the Evidence Doesn't Support

Despite bold claims in the supplement and wellness space, the following are not well-supported by current evidence:

High-dose collagen as a muscle builder: Collagen protein is low in leucine and does not meaningfully stimulate muscle protein synthesis. It has benefits for connective tissue, but it should not replace leucine-rich protein sources for GLP-1 users trying to preserve muscle.

Fat burners or "metabolic boosters": No supplement has been shown to meaningfully accelerate fat-specific loss or prevent muscle loss in the context of GLP-1 therapy. Thermogenic supplements may interact with cardiovascular effects of GLP-1 medications.

Skipping resistance training: Some sources claim that high protein alone is sufficient for muscle preservation. The evidence does not support this. Resistance training provides the mechanical stimulus that tells muscles to adapt and grow. Protein provides the raw material. Both are required.

Extreme calorie restriction: Going very low calorie while on a GLP-1 medication accelerates muscle loss. Many women on GLP-1s naturally fall below 1,200 calories per day because of appetite suppression. This level of restriction significantly increases the risk of lean mass loss and nutritional deficiencies.

A Practical Muscle-Preservation Protocol for GLP-1 Users

Here is a step-by-step protocol based on current research for women over 40 on GLP-1 therapy:

  1. Calculate your protein target. Multiply your body weight in kilograms by 1.4 as a starting point. A 150-pound (68 kg) woman should aim for approximately 95 grams of protein daily. Spread this across 3-4 meals.
  2. Prioritize protein at every meal. Even if appetite is suppressed, lead each meal or snack with protein before eating other foods. This ensures you hit your protein threshold even when total eating volume is low.
  3. Start resistance training 2-3 times per week. Focus on compound movements that use multiple muscle groups: squats, hip hinges (deadlifts or Romanian deadlifts), rows, and presses. Even 20-30 minutes per session is effective. Resistance bands are a valid option if free weights feel intimidating.
  4. Add creatine monohydrate (3-5 grams daily). Take it consistently, any time of day. Creatine supports both muscle performance during workouts and muscle preservation during calorie restriction. It is one of the best-studied supplements for older women.
  5. Use a leucine-rich protein supplement if whole food intake is low. Whey isolate or a pea-rice blend with added leucine can help you hit leucine thresholds when appetite is suppressed and meal sizes are small.
  6. Track your calories to avoid going too low. Most women on GLP-1 therapy do well targeting 1,400-1,600 calories per day. Below 1,200 calories significantly increases lean mass loss risk. A registered dietitian can help you set an individualized target.
  7. Include at least 7,000-8,000 steps of daily movement. Non-exercise activity (walking, standing, daily errands) supports muscle maintenance and insulin sensitivity. Do not rely on formal exercise sessions alone.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much muscle do most women lose on GLP-1 medications?
Research suggests that without intentional intervention, 25-40% of total weight lost during GLP-1 therapy may come from lean tissue. The absolute amount depends on baseline body composition, protein intake, and physical activity. Women who exercise and eat sufficient protein consistently lose less lean mass.

Can I build muscle while on a GLP-1 medication?
Building muscle while in a caloric deficit is difficult, but preserving muscle is achievable. Some women, particularly those new to resistance training, may see small muscle gains even during weight loss (a phenomenon called body recomposition). The primary goal, however, should be preservation rather than growth during active GLP-1 therapy.

Is creatine safe for women over 40?
Yes. Creatine monohydrate is one of the most extensively studied dietary supplements. Research consistently shows it is safe for healthy adults, including older women. It does not cause kidney damage in healthy individuals. Common effects include a slight increase in water weight within muscle tissue (1-2 pounds), which is not fat gain.

What if I cannot tolerate protein shakes due to GLP-1 nausea?
Start with smaller, more frequent portions of whole food protein: eggs, Greek yogurt, chicken, fish, cottage cheese. If nausea is severe, speak to your prescribing physician. Adjusting timing or dosage of your GLP-1 medication can sometimes reduce gastrointestinal side effects enough to improve food tolerance.

Should I stop my GLP-1 medication if I am losing too much muscle?
Do not stop a prescription medication without consulting your doctor. Instead, raise the muscle loss concern with your prescriber. They may adjust your dose, recommend a registered dietitian, or refer you to an exercise physiologist who works with GLP-1 patients.
