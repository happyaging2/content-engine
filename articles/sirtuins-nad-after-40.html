Sirtuins are a family of proteins that sit at the intersection of aging, metabolism, and gene regulation — and they cannot do their job without NAD+. Understanding how sirtuins work and why they slow down after 40 explains a lot about why aging feels different after midlife: why recovery takes longer, why inflammation becomes more persistent, why energy is less reliable. This guide covers the key sirtuins, what each one does, and how NAD+ decline directly affects their activity.

What Sirtuins Are and Why They Matter

Sirtuins (SIRT1 through SIRT7) are a conserved family of enzymes first discovered in yeast and subsequently found in every organism from bacteria to humans. Their defining feature is that they are NAD+-dependent deacylases: enzymes that remove chemical tags (acetyl groups) from proteins, changing those proteins' function. By deacylating specific target proteins, sirtuins turn gene expression programs on or off, activate or silence metabolic pathways, and regulate cellular stress responses.

The NAD+-dependence is critical. Unlike most enzymes that use ATP as an energy source, sirtuins use NAD+ directly as a chemical co-substrate — they consume it in the process of doing their job. This means sirtuin activity is gated by the availability of NAD+. When NAD+ is abundant (as in youth, during fasting, or after exercise), sirtuins are active. When NAD+ is low (as in aging, chronic inflammation, or sedentary states), sirtuin activity falls proportionally.

This NAD+-sirtuin connection is one of the most compelling mechanistic explanations for why NAD+ decline matters to aging. It is not just that cells have less energy substrate; it is that the regulatory proteins responsible for maintaining cellular health become less active as NAD+ falls.

SIRT1: Metabolism, Inflammation, and Circadian Rhythm

SIRT1 is the most extensively studied sirtuin and the one most directly relevant to the metabolic and inflammatory changes of aging. Located primarily in the cell nucleus, SIRT1 deacetylates a broad range of transcription factors and co-activators, producing effects across multiple systems simultaneously.

SIRT1 activates PGC-1 alpha, the master regulator of mitochondrial biogenesis — the process of creating new mitochondria. When SIRT1 activity falls with declining NAD+, mitochondrial biogenesis slows, and the proportion of dysfunctional mitochondria in cells increases over time. This is a direct mechanistic link between NAD+ decline and the energy loss of aging.

SIRT1 also deacetylates and inactivates NF-kB, the primary transcription factor that drives inflammatory gene expression. Active SIRT1 therefore has a potent anti-inflammatory effect. When NAD+ falls and SIRT1 activity diminishes, NF-kB becomes more active and chronic inflammatory signaling increases — which is consistent with the well-documented rise in systemic inflammation (inflammaging) that occurs with age.

SIRT1 is also a core component of the molecular circadian clock, working with CLOCK and BMAL1 to maintain daily rhythms of gene expression. This connects NAD+ and sirtuin decline to the circadian disruption that becomes increasingly common after 40, affecting sleep, metabolism, and hormonal rhythms.

SIRT3: Mitochondrial Efficiency and Oxidative Stress

SIRT3 is located inside mitochondria — which is fitting, since its primary job is to regulate mitochondrial function. SIRT3 deacetylates and activates key mitochondrial enzymes, including components of the electron transport chain (which produces ATP), antioxidant enzymes (particularly MnSOD, the primary mitochondrial antioxidant), and enzymes involved in fatty acid oxidation.

When SIRT3 activity falls with declining NAD+, mitochondrial electron transport becomes less efficient, generating more reactive oxygen species (free radicals) per unit of ATP produced. This increases oxidative stress inside the mitochondria, accelerating the accumulation of mitochondrial DNA damage and degrading mitochondrial function further. It is a self-amplifying cycle: lower NAD+ reduces SIRT3 activity, which impairs mitochondrial efficiency, which produces more oxidative damage, which depletes more NAD+ (through PARP activation).

Research on SIRT3 knockout animals has demonstrated that loss of SIRT3 activity produces a phenotype that resembles accelerated aging: reduced exercise capacity, increased oxidative stress, metabolic dysfunction, and increased susceptibility to age-related diseases. These findings are consistent with the mechanistic importance of SIRT3 in maintaining the mitochondrial health that underlies cellular vitality.

SIRT6: DNA Repair and Telomere Stability

SIRT6 plays a specific role in maintaining genomic stability — the integrity of your DNA. It does this through two mechanisms. First, SIRT6 deacetylates histone proteins at sites of DNA double-strand breaks, helping to recruit DNA repair machinery to those sites. When SIRT6 is less active (due to low NAD+), DNA repair is less efficient, and damage accumulates faster.

Second, SIRT6 is recruited specifically to telomeres — the protective caps at the ends of chromosomes — where it maintains chromatin structure and suppresses telomere dysfunction. Research published in Nature (Kanfi et al., 2012, DOI: 10.1038/nature10815) showed that SIRT6 overexpression extended lifespan in male mice, and that SIRT6 deficiency accelerated aging phenotypes. In humans, reduced SIRT6 expression has been associated with age-related increases in genomic instability.

The connection between SIRT6, NAD+, and DNA repair is one reason why the combination of declining NAD+ and accelerating DNA damage in aging is particularly problematic: not only does more DNA damage consume more NAD+ through PARP activation, but the lower NAD+ reduces SIRT6 activity, which means DNA repair itself becomes less efficient. The damage accumulates faster while the repair capacity falls.

How to Support Sirtuin Activity After 40

Because sirtuins are NAD+-dependent, any strategy that raises NAD+ levels will restore sirtuin function. The most directly applicable strategies for women over 40 include the following.

NMN (nicotinamide mononucleotide) supplementation raises tissue NAD+ by providing a direct precursor that bypasses the NAMPT bottleneck in the salvage pathway. Human clinical trials have demonstrated that oral NMN raises blood NAD+ metabolite levels measurably. Higher NAD+ means more substrate available for SIRT1, SIRT3, and SIRT6 to perform their regulatory functions.

Caloric restriction and intermittent fasting raise NAD+ through multiple pathways: they activate AMPK (which upregulates NAMPT expression), reduce the PARP load by decreasing oxidative stress, and shift cellular metabolism in ways that favor NAD+ conservation. According to Happy Aging's review of the sirtuin activation literature, caloric restriction is the most consistently studied sirtuin activator, and its longevity effects in animal models are partially dependent on intact sirtuin signaling.

Exercise, particularly aerobic exercise, upregulates NAMPT expression in muscle tissue and raises intramuscular NAD+ levels. This is one of the mechanisms through which exercise consistently produces anti-aging effects at the cellular level, independent of body composition changes.

Resveratrol, a plant polyphenol found in red wine and grapes, is a direct SIRT1 activator that works allosterically — it changes SIRT1's shape in a way that makes it more active even at a given level of NAD+. Resveratrol does not raise NAD+ directly; rather, it makes SIRT1 more sensitive to the NAD+ that is available. Combining resveratrol with NMN (which raises the NAD+ substrate) is the rationale for the popular NMN plus resveratrol combination studied by researchers including Dr. David Sinclair.

What the Evidence Doesn't Support

The idea that resveratrol alone, without adequate NAD+, is a sufficient sirtuin activator for meaningful anti-aging effects in humans is not well supported. While resveratrol does activate SIRT1 allosterically, sirtuin activity is fundamentally limited by NAD+ availability. In a NAD+-depleted cellular environment (which is the condition of aging), making sirtuins more sensitive to NAD+ produces limited benefit if there is very little NAD+ to be sensitive to. The most mechanistically sound approach is to raise NAD+ through supplementation (providing the substrate) alongside resveratrol (sensitizing the enzyme to the available substrate). Resveratrol without NAD+ support is half the equation.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are sirtuins and why do they matter for aging?

Sirtuins are a family of seven proteins (SIRT1 through SIRT7) that regulate gene expression, DNA repair, inflammation, and metabolic function. They matter for aging because their activity directly influences most of the biological processes that determine how well cells maintain themselves over time. They require NAD+ as a co-substrate, which means their activity is gated by the NAD+ levels that decline progressively with age.

Does NMN directly activate sirtuins?

NMN does not directly activate sirtuins. What it does is raise NAD+ levels, and since sirtuins require NAD+ to function, higher NAD+ means more sirtuin activity — the sirtuins were already present and ready to work, but were limited by NAD+ availability. Think of NMN as restoring the fuel supply that sirtuins need, rather than switching them on directly.

Is resveratrol worth taking alongside NMN?

Yes, for women who want to maximize sirtuin activation. Resveratrol is a direct SIRT1 activator that makes the enzyme more sensitive to NAD+, while NMN raises NAD+ levels. The combination produces more SIRT1 activity than either alone, because you are simultaneously increasing the substrate and sensitizing the enzyme. The dose used in most longevity protocols is 200 to 500 mg resveratrol taken with food (resveratrol is fat-soluble and is better absorbed with a fatty meal or supplement).

Can I test my sirtuin activity?

Sirtuin activity is not routinely measurable through consumer lab tests. What can be measured indirectly are NAD+ metabolite levels in blood, which give an indication of whether NAD+ is in a range sufficient for sirtuin function. Some functional medicine labs offer NAD+ metabolite panels. Biological age tests (which measure epigenetic methylation patterns influenced by sirtuin activity) are also becoming more available, though they are still relatively expensive. Most women assess sirtuin support indirectly through changes in energy, inflammation, sleep quality, and exercise recovery.
