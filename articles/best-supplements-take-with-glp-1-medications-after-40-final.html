Women over 40 on GLP-1 medications commonly develop nutrient gaps that standard multivitamins do not fully address, and targeted supplementation can support energy, muscle preservation, and overall health during therapy.

GLP-1 medications like semaglutide and tirzepatide are transforming how many women approach weight and metabolic health. But reduced appetite and smaller meal volumes mean that key vitamins, minerals, and macronutrients often fall below optimal levels without deliberate planning. As of 2026, clinicians and registered dietitians increasingly recognize that nutrition support is a critical, often overlooked component of GLP-1 therapy.

What to Know
  • GLP-1 medications reduce appetite significantly, which can create nutrient deficiencies even in women eating healthy diets.
  • The most common gaps in GLP-1 users include protein, vitamin B12, magnesium, zinc, and iron.
  • Supplementation should complement, not replace, a nutrient-dense diet.
  • Not all supplements are appropriate during GLP-1 therapy; some may interact or cause GI distress.

What Are GLP-1 Medications and Why Do They Create Nutrient Gaps?

GLP-1 (glucagon-like peptide-1) receptor agonists are a class of medications that mimic the GLP-1 hormone to reduce appetite, slow gastric emptying, and regulate blood sugar. Common examples include semaglutide (Ozempic, Wegovy) and tirzepatide (Mounjaro, Zepbound). They produce substantial, sustained weight loss, typically 15-25% of body weight in clinical trials over 12-18 months.

The nutrient gap problem is straightforward. When you eat 30-50% fewer calories than you previously consumed, you take in proportionally fewer vitamins and minerals. The body's micronutrient needs do not decrease at the same rate as caloric intake. The result is a widening gap between what your body requires and what your reduced eating provides.

This gap is compounded in women over 40 by several factors: hormonal shifts from perimenopause that increase demand for certain nutrients, age-related reductions in nutrient absorption efficiency, and the physiological stress of rapid body composition changes.

Why It Matters for Women Over 40

Women in their 40s and 50s are already navigating significant hormonal transitions. Estrogen decline affects bone density, cardiovascular health, energy production, and mood regulation. Many of the nutrients depleted by GLP-1 therapy (B12, magnesium, zinc) play direct roles in supporting these systems.

Recent research through 2026 suggests that nutrient depletion during GLP-1 therapy can manifest as fatigue, brain fog, hair thinning, muscle cramps, and mood changes. These symptoms are often attributed to the medication itself, when in some cases they are signs of nutritional insufficiency that can be addressed.

According to Happy Aging's review of nutritional research in GLP-1 users, the most actionable gaps are protein, vitamin B12, magnesium, and zinc. Addressing these four areas accounts for a significant portion of the most common symptoms women on GLP-1 therapy report.

Happy Aging's position: if you are on a GLP-1 medication and not actively managing your nutrition, you are leaving a meaningful portion of your health outcomes to chance. Supplementation is not optional support for GLP-1 therapy. It is a clinical priority.

What the Research Says

Protein insufficiency is the most common and consequential gap. Research suggests that women on GLP-1 therapy frequently consume far below the recommended 1.2-1.6 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight that supports lean mass retention in caloric restriction. Protein deficiency during GLP-1 therapy is associated with accelerated muscle loss, hair thinning, and slower recovery.

Vitamin B12: GLP-1 medications slow gastric emptying and may reduce stomach acid output over time, both of which impair B12 absorption. B12 deficiency develops slowly but can cause fatigue, nerve tingling, cognitive changes, and anemia. Research on other gastric-motility-affecting conditions (including metformin use, which often co-occurs with GLP-1 therapy) shows significant B12 depletion over 12-24 months.

Magnesium: Magnesium is involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions in the body, including energy production, muscle contraction, and sleep regulation. Reduced food intake reliably reduces magnesium intake. Research suggests that magnesium deficiency is already widespread in American women over 40, and GLP-1 therapy can deepen this gap. Common symptoms include muscle cramps, poor sleep, anxiety, and constipation.

Zinc: Zinc supports immune function, wound healing, taste perception, and hair growth. Reduced dietary variety and lower protein intake (zinc is concentrated in animal proteins) are associated with zinc insufficiency. Women on GLP-1 therapy who reduce meat consumption or eat very small portions may be at particular risk.

Omega-3 fatty acids: Omega-3s are anti-inflammatory and support cardiovascular, brain, and metabolic health. Women eating smaller meals with less fish and healthy fat intake often fall below optimal omega-3 levels. Research consistently supports omega-3 supplementation for cardiovascular protection in women, an important consideration given that GLP-1 medications are increasingly prescribed for cardiovascular risk reduction as well.

What the Evidence Doesn't Support

Some popular claims about supplementation for GLP-1 users are not well-supported by current evidence:

Digestive enzymes as a required supplement: Some practitioners recommend digestive enzymes for all GLP-1 users. While these may help some individuals with specific digestive complaints, there is no strong evidence that they are universally necessary or that they prevent nutrient malabsorption in GLP-1 therapy.

High-dose vitamin D alone for bone protection: Bone loss is a genuine concern during rapid weight loss. However, vitamin D supplementation alone is not sufficient for bone health. Calcium intake, weight-bearing exercise, and protein are all required for bone preservation. Do not rely on a single supplement to address complex structural health.

Detox or cleanse products: No cleanse product has been shown to enhance nutrient absorption during GLP-1 therapy. Many contain laxatives or compounds that may actually worsen nutrient loss and GI symptoms already common with GLP-1 medications.

A Practical Supplement Protocol for GLP-1 Users

  1. Start with a high-quality multivitamin. Choose a food-based or methylated multivitamin (with methylcobalamin B12 and methylfolate). This covers broad micronutrient gaps without targeting specifics. Take with food to minimize GI effects.
  2. Add sublingual or methylated B12 separately. Even if your multivitamin contains B12, the absorption issue from GLP-1-related gastric changes means extra supplementation is often warranted. Sublingual (under the tongue) delivery bypasses the need for strong stomach acid.
  3. Add magnesium glycinate 300-400 mg in the evening. This form is well-absorbed, gentle on the digestive system, and supports sleep and muscle function simultaneously. Evening dosing aligns with sleep benefits.
  4. Supplement with protein powder on low-appetite days. When meals are small, protein shakes or smoothies can make hitting daily protein targets much easier. Target at least 20-25 grams of protein per serving.
  5. Add omega-3 fish oil or algae-based DHA (1-2 g daily). Take with your largest meal to improve absorption and reduce the likelihood of "fish burps."
  6. Consider a NAD+ precursor supplement. NAD+ levels decline with age and metabolic stress. Emerging research through 2026 suggests NMN supplementation may support cellular energy and mitochondrial function, both of which are relevant during the metabolic stress of calorie restriction.
  7. Review your supplement protocol with your prescribing physician or a registered dietitian at each quarterly appointment. Nutrient needs shift as your weight changes and therapy progresses.

Who This Guide Is NOT For

This guide applies to adults actively on GLP-1 therapy under medical supervision. If you are not on a GLP-1 medication, these specific nutrient gaps do not apply to you. The elevated protein targets, B12 supplementation strategy, and GLP-1-specific rationale described here are not a general wellness protocol. Starting supplements without a clinical reason adds cost and unnecessary complexity without clear benefit.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I take supplements at the same time as my GLP-1 injection or pill?
Injectable GLP-1 medications (semaglutide, tirzepatide) are administered subcutaneously and do not interact with oral supplements in the gastrointestinal tract. You can take supplements at any time relative to your injection. If you take an oral GLP-1 medication (like oral semaglutide), take it on an empty stomach as directed and wait at least 30 minutes before taking supplements or eating.

Will supplements help with GLP-1 side effects like nausea?
Some nutrients may help with specific symptoms. Magnesium may reduce constipation. Ginger (as a supplement or food) has some evidence for nausea reduction. Protein supplements may be more tolerable than solid meals during early therapy. However, supplements do not replace dose adjustment or physician guidance for managing significant GLP-1 side effects.

How do I know if I am actually deficient in B12 or magnesium?
Blood tests can measure B12 levels. Magnesium is harder to assess because serum magnesium does not reflect intracellular levels accurately. If you are experiencing fatigue, tingling, or cognitive changes, ask your doctor to check B12, complete blood count, and a metabolic panel. Symptoms plus low-normal blood values often justify supplementation even before frank deficiency develops.

Are there supplements I should avoid while on GLP-1 therapy?
Avoid supplements that significantly affect gastric motility, such as high-dose fiber supplements taken all at once (spread fiber intake throughout the day instead). Be cautious with thermogenic or stimulant supplements, which may interact with cardiovascular effects. Always inform your prescribing physician about your supplement routine.

Should I take a calcium supplement for bone health?
This is a nuanced question. Calcium from food sources is preferred. Supplemental calcium has a mixed evidence profile, with some studies suggesting potential cardiovascular concerns with high-dose calcium supplements. If your dietary calcium is consistently low, discuss the risk-benefit balance with your doctor. Vitamin D, weight-bearing exercise, and adequate protein matter as much as calcium for bone health.
