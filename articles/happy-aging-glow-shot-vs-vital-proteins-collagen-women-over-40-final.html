What Is Collagen, and Why Does It Change After 40?

Collagen is a structural protein that gives skin its firmness, elasticity, and moisture-retention capacity. Your body makes collagen from amino acids (primarily glycine, proline, and hydroxyproline), and the process requires vitamin C as a critical cofactor. Without adequate vitamin C, the collagen synthesis enzyme cannot function properly.

After your mid-20s, collagen production starts a gradual decline. For women, the rate of decline accelerates sharply around the menopausal transition. Research suggests that women may lose as much as 30% of skin collagen in the first five years after menopause, with ongoing losses of roughly 2% per year afterward. This is not cosmetic vanity. Skin changes at this life stage are a measurable, documented biological process.

Collagen supplements work by delivering hydrolyzed collagen peptides (short amino acid chains) that are absorbed through the gut. These peptides are thought to both provide raw materials for collagen synthesis and signal fibroblasts (the cells that make collagen in skin) to increase production. The evidence that this signaling effect is real is what makes collagen supplementation more than just eating protein.

Why This Matters for Women Over 40

The hormonal transition of perimenopause affects skin in multiple ways. Estrogen promotes collagen synthesis, supports skin hydration through hyaluronic acid regulation, and maintains skin barrier function. As estrogen declines, all three of these pathways weaken simultaneously. This is why skin changes in your 40s feel different from normal aging: multiple mechanisms are shifting at once.

For women navigating this window, the goal is not to turn back the clock. It is to give your body the most complete set of raw materials and cofactors for maintaining skin structure. That is where the formulation differences between these two products become relevant. Providing collagen peptides alongside vitamin C (the synthesis cofactor) and hyaluronic acid (the primary molecule responsible for skin moisture retention) is a more complete approach than collagen alone.

The question is whether a liquid marine collagen shot with added cofactors outperforms a straightforward bovine collagen powder for women specifically. Let's look at the evidence.

What the Research Says

The clinical evidence for oral collagen supplementation and skin health is more solid than many people expect. A 2014 double-blind, placebo-controlled study by Proksch et al. found that women who took specific bioactive collagen peptides for eight weeks showed significant improvements in skin elasticity and had measurably improved skin moisture compared to placebo (PMID: 24401291). That was a human clinical trial with objective skin measurements.

A 2017 study by Hexsel et al. found that oral collagen peptide supplementation over 24 weeks improved nail growth and reduced symptoms of brittle nails in women (PMID: 26840887). Nail strength and skin structure share similar collagen-dependent mechanisms, making this relevant to the broader conversation about collagen and women's connective tissue health.

According to Happy Aging's review of the current literature on marine collagen for skin, marine collagen sources tend to yield a high proportion of Type I collagen peptides, which are the form most concentrated in facial skin. Bovine sources provide a mix of Type I and Type III, which is useful for joint and tendon health but may be less targeted for facial skin specifically. Whether this source difference produces a measurable skin outcome difference in head-to-head human trials has not been firmly established.

The role of vitamin C in collagen synthesis is well-established biochemistry, not a marketing claim. Prolyl hydroxylase, the enzyme that creates the cross-links in collagen fibers, requires vitamin C as an obligate cofactor. A formula that includes vitamin C alongside collagen peptides is directly addressing the synthesis pathway, not just providing raw protein.

What the Evidence Does Not Support

The idea that collagen supplements can fully reverse facial aging or eliminate wrinkles is not supported by the published research. The clinical trials show improvements in objective measures like skin elasticity and hydration, but these are relative improvements. No study has shown that oral collagen supplementation produces the same outcomes as dermatological procedures or prescription topical retinoids.

The absorption advantage of liquid formats over powders is often stated in marketing but has limited peer-reviewed comparative data in humans. Hydrolyzed collagen peptides in both formats are absorbed as dipeptides and tripeptides through the gut. If both products use well-hydrolyzed collagen of similar molecular weight, the delivery format may matter less than the cofactor profile. Vitamin C and hyaluronic acid inclusion are more clearly evidence-backed differentiators than liquid vs. powder format.

It is also worth noting that neither marine nor bovine collagen provides complete skin repair on its own. Sleep, UV protection, hydration, and overall dietary protein intake are all foundational. No supplement replaces sunscreen or a protein-adequate diet for skin health.

We are transparent that a head-to-head comparison trial between Glow Shot and Vital Proteins has not been published. The ingredient-level evidence favors a cofactor-complete approach, and we stand behind the formulation rationale. However, individual results will vary.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is marine collagen better than bovine collagen for skin?

Marine collagen is predominantly Type I, which is the collagen type most concentrated in the dermis (the structural layer of facial skin). Bovine collagen includes both Type I and Type III, making it broader for overall connective tissue but slightly less targeted for skin specifically. For women over 40 whose primary concern is facial skin, the marine source offers a more focused approach. For joint health as well as skin, bovine may be more comprehensive.

How long does it take to see results from collagen supplements?

The published clinical trials showing skin elasticity improvements used supplementation periods of 8 to 24 weeks. Most dermatologists suggest a minimum of 12 weeks before evaluating skin changes. Collagen synthesis is a slow biological process, not an overnight transformation. Consistency over months is more important than any single dose.

Does Vital Proteins have vitamin C?

The standard Vital Proteins Collagen Peptides powder does not include vitamin C. Some Vital Proteins product variants (like their Beauty Collagen line) do add vitamin C and hyaluronic acid. If vitamin C is important to you in a Vital Proteins product, check the specific product label rather than assuming it is included in all versions.

Can I take collagen supplements while taking other medications?

Collagen peptides are generally considered safe with most medications. However, if you are on blood thinners or have kidney conditions (high protein intake can affect some kidney conditions), speak with your physician first. Vitamin C at high doses can interact with some chemotherapy agents. Always review your supplement regimen with a healthcare provider.

What is hyaluronic acid doing in a collagen supplement?

Hyaluronic acid is the primary molecule responsible for water retention in skin tissue. It can hold up to 1,000 times its weight in water. As estrogen declines in perimenopause, hyaluronic acid production in skin decreases, which is why skin often feels drier and less plump in your 40s and 50s. Including oral hyaluronic acid alongside collagen peptides addresses both structural and hydration aspects of skin health simultaneously.
