What Urolithin A Is and Where It Comes From

Urolithin A is a compound your body produces, but you cannot eat it directly. It is made by specific bacteria in your gut when they metabolize ellagitannins: a class of polyphenols found in pomegranates, walnuts, raspberries, strawberries, and some oak-aged wines. The conversion goes like this: you eat pomegranate or walnuts, ellagitannins reach your large intestine, certain bacterial species (including Gordonibacter pamelaeae and Ellagibacter isourolithinifaciens) convert them to ellagic acid and then to urolithin A.

The challenge is that this conversion requires a specific set of gut bacteria that not everyone has. Studies have estimated that somewhere between 30 and 40 percent of people have essentially no urolithin A production capacity from food, even when eating pomegranate regularly. Another large group are low producers. Only a subset of people are high producers who efficiently convert dietary ellagitannins to meaningful levels of urolithin A.

And here is the aging problem: gut microbiome diversity typically declines with age. The specific bacteria responsible for urolithin A production tend to decrease as we get older, which means that even people who were once efficient producers may become poor producers by their 40s and 50s. This is why urolithin A is increasingly being offered as a direct supplement rather than only promoted through pomegranate consumption. You cannot guarantee production from food alone.

What Mitophagy Is and Why It Matters After 40

To understand why urolithin A matters, you need to understand mitophagy. Mitophagy is a specific type of autophagy (the general cellular cleanup process) targeted specifically at mitochondria, your cells’ energy-producing organelles.

Mitochondria are constantly exposed to oxidative stress as a side effect of producing energy (ATP). Over time, individual mitochondria accumulate damage: their membranes degrade, their DNA mutates, their electron transport chains become inefficient and “leak” electrons that generate excess free radicals. A healthy cell detects these damaged mitochondria and removes them through mitophagy, tagging them for degradation and replacing them with newly synthesized, efficient mitochondria.

When mitophagy is impaired, damaged mitochondria accumulate. This has cascading consequences. Cells produce less ATP per mitochondrion, overall energy output drops, and the damaged mitochondria generate more oxidative stress that damages surrounding cellular components. This is not a theoretical problem. It shows up functionally as reduced stamina, slower muscle recovery, fatigue that does not respond to sleep, and the general loss of vitality that many women notice beginning in their early 40s.

After 40, mitophagy slows for the same reasons that general autophagy declines: mTOR becomes overactive, AMPK loses responsiveness, and NAD+ levels fall, reducing the sirtuin activity that regulates these processes. The practical result is a buildup of dysfunctional mitochondria that contributes directly to the energy and muscle changes of aging. Urolithin A directly addresses this by triggering the mitophagy pathway through a specific mechanism.

What the Research Shows About Urolithin A

Urolithin A is unusually well-studied for a longevity compound. Much of the clinical work has been conducted by Amazentis, a Swiss biotech company, using their purified urolithin A formula (Mitopure). This research, published in peer-reviewed journals, gives us a clearer picture of what urolithin A actually does in human subjects.

A phase 2 clinical trial (Timeline trial) published in Nature Metabolism (Andreux et al., 2019, PMID: 32555195) evaluated the effects of urolithin A supplementation on mitochondrial health in older adults. The trial found that 500 mg and 1000 mg of urolithin A daily for 4 weeks increased mitochondrial gene expression markers (PGC-1 alpha, a key regulator of mitochondrial biogenesis) and improved mitochondrial health biomarkers in muscle tissue compared to placebo. These are meaningful findings because they demonstrate actual cellular changes, not just surrogate markers.

A follow-up study (Singh et al., 2022) published in JAMA Network Open examined the effects of urolithin A on muscle endurance in older adults over 4 months. Participants who received 1000 mg of urolithin A daily showed significant improvements in hand grip strength and in the 6-minute walk test compared to placebo, while the placebo group showed no improvement. The urolithin A group also showed improvements in plasma biomarkers of mitochondrial health.

According to Happy Aging’s review of 8 clinical and mechanistic studies on urolithin A, the compound stands out among longevity supplements because it has both clear mechanistic evidence (triggers mitophagy through the PINK1/Parkin pathway) and growing clinical evidence in human subjects. Most longevity compounds have one or the other. Urolithin A is one of the few with both.

The Connection Between Urolithin A and Muscle Health After 40

The clinical findings on muscle strength and endurance are particularly relevant for women over 40. Beginning in the early 40s and accelerating through the 50s, women lose muscle mass at a rate of roughly 1 to 2 percent per year in the absence of resistance training. This is sarcopenia, and it is driven partly by hormonal changes but also by impaired mitochondrial function in muscle cells.

Muscle cells are among the most mitochondria-dense cells in the body, because movement requires enormous amounts of ATP. When muscle mitochondria are dysfunctional, the cell produces less force per contraction, recovers more slowly, and signals for protein breakdown rather than repair. This contributes to the decline in muscle quality (not just quantity) that makes exercise feel harder and recovery take longer as women age.

Urolithin A’s ability to trigger mitophagy in muscle cells is why the clinical trials showed improvements in grip strength and walking endurance. By clearing dysfunctional mitochondria and supporting the replacement of new, efficient ones, urolithin A helps restore the cellular energy environment that muscles need to function well. This is also why urolithin A is increasingly being studied as a potential intervention for sarcopenia.

Molecular Hydrogen and Cellular Recovery

While urolithin A works specifically through the mitophagy pathway, another approach to cellular recovery after 40 addresses oxidative stress more broadly: molecular hydrogen (H2). Molecular hydrogen is the world’s smallest antioxidant molecule, small enough to penetrate cell membranes, enter mitochondria, and selectively neutralize the most damaging reactive oxygen species (hydroxyl radicals) without interfering with the reactive oxygen species that your cells use for beneficial signaling.

This selectivity matters. Conventional antioxidants like vitamin C or E scavenge oxidative stress broadly, which can interfere with hormetic signaling (the beneficial cellular stress response from exercise). Molecular hydrogen targets only the most damaging free radicals, leaving beneficial redox signaling intact while reducing the oxidative damage that accelerates mitochondrial dysfunction and telomere erosion.

For women over 40, combining strategies that clear dysfunctional mitochondria (urolithin A, through mitophagy) with strategies that reduce the oxidative damage that causes mitochondrial dysfunction in the first place (molecular hydrogen) represents a more comprehensive approach to cellular recovery and longevity.

How to Support Urolithin A Production and Mitophagy After 40

There are two strategies here: supporting natural urolithin A production and considering direct supplementation for women whose gut microbiome may not convert efficiently.

For dietary support: eat pomegranate (particularly pomegranate juice or arils, which are the highest ellagitannin sources), walnuts, raspberries, and strawberries regularly. These foods provide the ellagitannin substrates that gut bacteria convert to urolithin A. However, given that production efficiency declines with age and microbiome diversity, dietary intake alone may not produce therapeutic levels in many women over 40.

For gut microbiome support: a diverse gut microbiome is the foundation of urolithin A production. High-fiber diets (vegetables, legumes, whole grains) support the bacterial diversity needed. Fermented foods like yogurt, kefir, and kimchi also help maintain beneficial bacterial populations. This is good advice regardless of urolithin A production, but it is particularly relevant here.

Direct urolithin A supplementation: purified urolithin A supplements are now available that deliver the compound directly without depending on gut conversion. Doses used in clinical trials range from 500 mg to 1000 mg daily. Look for standardized extracts rather than generic pomegranate extracts, which deliver ellagitannins (the precursor) rather than urolithin A itself.

Exercise is also a direct trigger of mitophagy, independent of urolithin A. Both aerobic exercise and resistance training activate the PINK1/Parkin pathway in muscle cells, the same pathway that urolithin A activates pharmacologically. Combining regular exercise with urolithin A supplementation is more effective than either alone, because they work through overlapping but complementary mechanisms.

What the Evidence Doesn’t Support

Not everyone converts dietary ellagitannins to urolithin A, and pomegranate juice alone may not deliver effective urolithin A levels for non-converters. Studies estimate that up to 40 percent of people lack the specific gut bacteria (including Gordonibacter and Ellagibacter species) required for efficient conversion. For this group, consuming more pomegranate will not meaningfully raise urolithin A levels. Gut microbiome composition, not food intake volume, is the determining factor. Women in this non-converter group need direct urolithin A supplementation rather than increased fruit consumption to access the mitophagy benefits demonstrated in clinical trials.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can you get enough urolithin A from eating pomegranates?

It depends on your gut microbiome. Some people efficiently convert the ellagitannins in pomegranate to urolithin A. Others, especially after 40 when microbiome diversity typically declines, produce very little. Eating pomegranate regularly is beneficial for general health and will contribute to urolithin A production if you have the right gut bacteria. But for reliable, dose-specific urolithin A levels comparable to those in clinical trials, direct supplementation is more predictable than relying on food alone.

How long does urolithin A take to work?

The cellular changes from urolithin A take time to accumulate, since mitophagy is a gradual process of replacing dysfunctional mitochondria over multiple cycles. The clinical trials showing improvements in muscle function and endurance ran for 4 months. Expect to take urolithin A for at least 2 to 3 months before evaluating whether you notice functional differences in energy and exercise recovery. The mitochondrial gene expression changes seen in the Andreux et al. study were detectable at 4 weeks, but the functional improvements in muscle performance required longer.

Is urolithin A safe?

Based on available clinical evidence, urolithin A appears to be well-tolerated. The Amazentis trials found no adverse safety signals at doses of 500 to 1000 mg daily over 4 months. Urolithin A has GRAS (Generally Recognized As Safe) status in the US for food use. As with any supplement, if you have underlying health conditions or take medications, check with your healthcare provider before starting.

What is the difference between mitophagy and autophagy?

Autophagy is the general cellular process of clearing and recycling damaged cellular components. Mitophagy is a specific type of autophagy that targets mitochondria exclusively. The same general machinery is involved (autophagosomes, lysosomes), but mitophagy is triggered specifically when a mitochondrion is detected as dysfunctional, using the PINK1/Parkin pathway as a quality-control sensor. Urolithin A directly activates mitophagy without requiring the general autophagy-triggering conditions (like fasting) that activate broader cellular cleanup.

Can urolithin A help with fatigue?

Possibly, for fatigue that has a mitochondrial component. If your fatigue is partly driven by accumulated dysfunctional mitochondria producing less ATP than you need, triggering mitophagy and replacing those mitochondria with new ones could improve cellular energy output. The clinical trial improvements in muscle endurance and walking performance in older adults suggest this may translate to real-world energy capacity. However, fatigue has many causes. Urolithin A is most relevant when the fatigue pattern aligns with reduced cellular energy and exercise intolerance, rather than fatigue from poor sleep, thyroid issues, or other conditions.
