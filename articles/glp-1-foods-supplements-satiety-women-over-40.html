What to Know

  • GLP-1 is a natural gut hormone produced in your small intestine after eating, not something only injections can provide
  • High-fiber foods, quality protein, and specific supplements may modestly support your body's natural GLP-1 secretion
  • Gut bacteria that ferment fiber produce short-chain fatty acids (butyrate, propionate) that directly stimulate GLP-1 release
  • These dietary strategies are NOT a substitute for GLP-1 medications if your doctor has prescribed them
  • After 40, GLP-1 sensitivity may decline due to hormonal shifts, making dietary support more important

What Is GLP-1?

GLP-1 stands for glucagon-like peptide-1. It is an incretin hormone produced by L-cells in the small intestine and colon in response to eating. GLP-1 signals to the brain that you are full, stimulates insulin release, slows gastric emptying, and reduces glucagon secretion.

Why GLP-1 Matters More After 40

After 40, estrogen decline affects insulin sensitivity, gut microbiome composition, and appetite regulation. Gut microbiome diversity tends to decline with age and hormonal change, reducing the production of short-chain fatty acids that stimulate GLP-1 release.

What the Research Says

Dietary Fiber and Short-Chain Fatty Acids

Propionate in particular is a potent stimulator of GLP-1 release from L-cells. Practical fiber sources include oats (beta-glucan), legumes (lentils, chickpeas, black beans), Jerusalem artichokes and chicory (inulin), and cruciferous vegetables.

Protein

Protein, particularly leucine-rich protein sources, promotes GLP-1 release from intestinal L-cells. Leucine-rich sources: eggs, chicken breast, fish, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, and whey protein.

Berberine

Berberine activates GLP-1 secretion through multiple pathways, including gut microbiome modification and direct L-cell stimulation.
