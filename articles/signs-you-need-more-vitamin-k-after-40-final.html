Signs You Need More Vitamin K After 40 (Bones, Heart, and More)

Vitamin K is one of the most underappreciated nutrients for women over 40. While most discussions about bone health focus on calcium and vitamin D, vitamin K plays an equally critical role in making sure that calcium actually ends up in your bones rather than in your arteries. Understanding the signs you need more vitamin K after 40, and what this nutrient actually does, can meaningfully change how you support both bone density and cardiovascular health through the years ahead.

What to Know

  • Vitamin K exists in two main forms: K1 (phylloquinone) from leafy greens, and K2 (menaquinone) from fermented foods and animal products
  • K2 is the form most relevant to bone and cardiovascular health after 40
  • Vitamin K activates proteins that direct calcium into bones and keep it out of arteries
  • Many women over 40 are low in K2 specifically because it comes from foods rarely eaten in adequate amounts
  • Deficiency signs are often subtle and easy to attribute to other causes
  • Vitamin K2 works synergistically with vitamin D3 and calcium for bone health

What Vitamin K Does in Your Body After 40

Vitamin K is a fat-soluble vitamin that serves as a cofactor for a class of proteins called vitamin K-dependent proteins (VKDPs). These proteins perform critical functions across multiple body systems, but they require vitamin K to become activated. Without adequate vitamin K, these proteins remain inactive, and their biological roles go unfulfilled.

The most important VKDPs for women over 40 include:

Osteocalcin: A protein produced by bone-forming cells (osteoblasts) that helps bind calcium into the bone matrix. Activated by K2, osteocalcin is essential for bone mineralization. When K2 is insufficient, osteocalcin remains undercarboxylated (inactive), and calcium incorporation into bone is impaired even when calcium intake is adequate.

Matrix Gla Protein (MGP): The most potent known inhibitor of arterial calcification. MGP circulates in blood vessels and, when activated by K2, prevents calcium from depositing in arterial walls. Inactive MGP (from K2 deficiency) leaves arteries vulnerable to calcification, a major contributor to cardiovascular disease risk after 40.

Protein S and Protein C: These are clotting regulation proteins activated by K1 that help prevent abnormal clot formation. K1 deficiency, which is less common, can affect clotting factor production.

Vitamin K1 vs Vitamin K2: What Is the Difference?

K1 and K2 have different food sources, absorption profiles, and biological roles:

Vitamin K1 (phylloquinone) is found primarily in leafy green vegetables: kale, spinach, broccoli, collard greens, and parsley. It is absorbed with dietary fat and is primarily used by the liver for clotting factor activation. It has a short half-life and is cleared from the bloodstream relatively quickly.

Vitamin K2 (menaquinone) comes in several subtypes (MK-4 through MK-13). MK-4 is found in animal products like egg yolks, butter, and organ meats. MK-7, the most bioavailable and longest-acting form, is found primarily in natto (fermented soybeans), which is rarely eaten in Western diets. K2 has a much longer half-life than K1 and is distributed more broadly to extrahepatic tissues including bones and blood vessels, making it the form most relevant to bone health and cardiovascular protection.

Most people, including those who eat plenty of leafy greens, are adequate in K1 but potentially low in K2, particularly the MK-7 form. This is the gap that matters most for women over 40.

Why Women Over 40 Are at Higher Risk of Low Vitamin K

Several factors increase the likelihood of suboptimal vitamin K status after 40:

Dietary patterns: Western diets provide reasonable K1 from vegetables but very little K2, since natto is not commonly consumed and organ meats (high in MK-4) have declined in Western eating patterns.

Fat-soluble vitamin absorption changes: As digestive enzyme production and bile acid output shift with age, absorption of fat-soluble vitamins including K can decline. This means even women eating adequate K foods may absorb less of it.

Antibiotic use: Gut bacteria synthesize some menaquinones. Frequent or recent antibiotic use disrupts this production, potentially lowering K2 availability further.

Medications: Cholesterol-lowering drugs (statins and bile acid sequestrants) and some blood thinners affect vitamin K metabolism. Warfarin, in particular, works specifically by blocking vitamin K activity, and patients on warfarin must be careful about K intake changes.

Declining bone density: The postmenopausal decline in estrogen accelerates bone resorption. In this context, having insufficient osteocalcin activation (from low K2) compounds the bone density challenge significantly.

Signs You May Need More Vitamin K

Vitamin K deficiency rarely produces dramatic, obvious symptoms in otherwise healthy adults. Instead, the signs are typically subtle and long-term:

Easy bruising. Vitamin K1 is involved in clotting factor activation. Women who bruise very easily from minor contact, or who notice bruises appearing with no clear cause, may have K1 status that warrants attention.

Heavy or prolonged menstrual bleeding. Before menopause or during perimenopause, abnormally heavy periods can be related to several causes including low vitamin K, which affects clotting factor production.

Faster than expected bone density loss. If a DEXA scan shows bone density declining more rapidly than age-predicted norms, and calcium and vitamin D intake are adequate, suboptimal K2 status may be a contributing factor.

Arterial stiffness or elevated arterial calcification markers. Functional tests that show arterial stiffness or imaging that reveals arterial calcification in the absence of obvious cardiovascular risk factors may reflect insufficient MGP activation from low K2.

Dental cavities or enamel weakness. Vitamin K2 is involved in the activation of osteocalcin in dental structures as well as bone. Some research links K2 deficiency to dental health issues including cavity susceptibility.

Difficulty with wound healing. Adequate vitamin K is needed for several aspects of tissue repair. Slow wound healing is a general sign of multiple nutritional gaps, but K deficiency can be a contributing factor.

The Vitamin K and Bone Density Connection

The relationship between vitamin K2 and bone density is one of the most well-supported nutritional findings for women over 40. A systematic review and meta-analysis published in the Archives of Internal Medicine examined 7 trials and found that vitamin K2 supplementation significantly reduced vertebral fracture risk and hip fracture risk in postmenopausal women.

The mechanism is direct: K2 activates osteocalcin, which physically binds hydroxyapatite (the calcium-phosphate mineral in bone) to the collagen matrix of bone. Without sufficient activated osteocalcin, the mineral component of bone is less effectively integrated, reducing mechanical strength even when bone density measures look acceptable.

Critically, vitamin K2 also works synergistically with vitamin D3. Vitamin D promotes calcium absorption from the gut and supports osteocalcin production. But osteocalcin cannot become activated without K2. The two vitamins are co-dependent for bone mineralization: D3 without adequate K2 can actually increase the risk of soft tissue calcium deposits because calcium is absorbed but not directed efficiently into bones. This is why K2 and D3 are increasingly recommended together for bone health.

Best Food Sources and Supplement Approaches

For vitamin K1: Eat leafy greens daily, particularly spinach, kale, Swiss chard, and parsley. Dress salads with olive oil, as K1 absorption requires fat. A large salad with olive oil dressing provides a full day's K1 intake. Most Western women who eat some vegetables are reasonably K1-replete.

For vitamin K2 (MK-7): Natto is the single richest source, providing 1000 mcg or more per 100 g serving. For women who do not eat natto, supplementation with MK-7 is the most practical approach. Effective doses in clinical trials have ranged from 90 to 180 mcg of MK-7 daily. MK-7 has a half-life of approximately 72 hours, meaning it accumulates in the body and maintains activity between doses more effectively than shorter-acting K forms.

Other K2 food sources: Aged hard cheeses (gouda, brie), egg yolks from pasture-raised hens, and grass-fed butter contain MK-4. These provide meaningful but smaller amounts than natto or supplements.

Supplementation note: Women taking blood thinners (anticoagulants) should consult their healthcare provider before supplementing with vitamin K in any form, as K directly affects clotting factor production and can interact with anticoagulant medications.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I take vitamin K1 or K2 for bone health?

Vitamin K2, particularly the MK-7 form, is the most relevant for bone health. While K1 supports clotting, K2 is what activates osteocalcin and MGP, the proteins directly responsible for directing calcium into bones and keeping it out of arteries.

Can I get enough vitamin K2 from diet alone?

If you regularly eat natto (a Japanese fermented soybean food), yes. Most Western women do not eat natto, and other dietary sources of K2 provide relatively small amounts. Supplementation with 90 to 180 mcg of MK-7 is a practical way to ensure adequate K2 status.

Does vitamin K interact with calcium supplements?

Not negatively. In fact, the relationship is synergistic. K2 activates the proteins that direct calcium into bones rather than arteries, making calcium supplementation more effective and potentially safer when K2 status is adequate.

How long does it take to see bone health benefits from vitamin K2?

Most bone-density trials run 12 to 36 months before measuring outcomes. Significant changes in bone density typically require consistent supplementation over 12 to 24 months, though improvements in osteocalcin activation can be measured within weeks of supplementation.
