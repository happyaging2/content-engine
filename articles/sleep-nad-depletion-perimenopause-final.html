If you're waking at 3 a.m. with a racing heart or lying in bed unable to fall asleep despite being exhausted, you're not imagining things and you're not alone. Sleep disruption is one of the most common and least discussed symptoms of perimenopause. Understanding why it happens at a biological level is the first step toward addressing it effectively.

What to Know Before You Read
  • Progesterone decline is the primary driver of perimenopausal sleep disruption. Progesterone promotes GABA activity, the brain's main calming neurotransmitter.
  • Estrogen supports serotonin synthesis, which is a precursor to melatonin. As estrogen falls, melatonin production may also be affected.
  • NAD+ is essential for circadian clock function through its role in the SIRT1-CLOCK interaction. NAD+ depletion disrupts the internal biological clock.
  • Poor sleep and NAD+ depletion create a vicious cycle: sleep deprivation activates PARP1 (a DNA repair enzyme), which consumes NAD+ and deepens the depletion.
  • Magnesium, apigenin, and L-theanine have evidence supporting GABA-mediated sleep quality. NAD+ support addresses the circadian component.
  • No supplement is a substitute for addressing hot flashes, sleep apnea, or other clinical sleep disruptors that may need medical evaluation.

What Is Actually Happening to Sleep in Perimenopause?

Perimenopause is not a single event. It's a transition that can span four to ten years before the final menstrual period. During this window, both progesterone and estrogen levels fluctuate erratically before ultimately declining. Each of these hormones has direct effects on the neurotransmitter systems that regulate sleep.

Progesterone is the most important hormone for sleep in this context. It works as a positive modulator of GABA-A receptors in the brain. GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid) is the primary inhibitory neurotransmitter: it quiets neural activity and promotes the calm state needed to fall and stay asleep. Many prescription sleep medications (benzodiazepines and related drugs) work by enhancing GABA signaling. Progesterone does this naturally.

When progesterone drops in perimenopause, you lose that natural GABAergic support. The brain becomes more excitable at night. The result is difficulty falling asleep, frequent waking, and that frustrating experience of lying awake with a mind that won't quiet down even though your body is clearly tired.

Estrogen's role in sleep is different but also significant. Estrogen supports the synthesis of serotonin, and serotonin is the direct precursor to melatonin. The pineal gland converts serotonin into melatonin in the evening hours in response to darkness. If serotonin availability is reduced because estrogen is no longer supporting its synthesis at prior levels, melatonin production may also be affected. This is why some perimenopausal women notice that their sleep timing shifts, often feeling less sleepy at their usual bedtime.

Why NAD+ Is Also Part of the Sleep Picture

This is the part most discussions of perimenopausal sleep miss. NAD+ is not just an energy molecule. It is also central to the function of the circadian clock itself.

The circadian clock in your cells runs on a feedback loop involving proteins called CLOCK and BMAL1. SIRT1, a NAD+-dependent sirtuin enzyme, regulates the activity of CLOCK protein through deacetylation. When NAD+ levels are adequate, SIRT1 can deacetylate CLOCK efficiently, which keeps the circadian feedback loop running on schedule. When NAD+ levels fall, this regulation weakens and the biological clock loses precision.

What this means practically: low NAD+ can cause the internal clock to drift. Sleep timing becomes less consistent. The depth of sleep stages may be less regular. The body's coordinated daily rhythms (cortisol peaks in the morning, melatonin rises in the evening, core body temperature drops at night) become less synchronized.

There is also a vicious cycle involved. Sleep deprivation activates PARP1, a DNA repair enzyme that becomes more active when cellular stress is high. PARP1 is a major consumer of NAD+. It uses NAD+ as a substrate to add ADP-ribose groups to damaged DNA. The more sleep-deprived and stressed your cells are, the more PARP1 is activated, and the more NAD+ gets consumed in DNA repair. This creates a feedback loop: poor sleep depletes NAD+, which disrupts circadian regulation, which impairs sleep quality further.

What the Research Says

The evidence for magnesium supplementation and sleep quality in older adults is perhaps the most solid of the supplement options relevant here. A 2012 double-blind, placebo-controlled clinical trial by Abbasi et al. published in the Journal of Research in Medical Sciences found that magnesium supplementation significantly improved subjective measures of insomnia in elderly participants, including sleep time, sleep efficiency, and early morning awakening frequency (PMID: 23853635). The mechanism is directly related to GABA: magnesium supports GABA receptor function and also reduces cortisol, the stress hormone that can keep the nervous system in an activated state at night.

Apigenin, a flavonoid found in chamomile, binds to benzodiazepine receptors in the brain and has mild GABA-A modulatory activity. Research suggests it may support relaxation and reduce sleep latency (the time it takes to fall asleep) through this mechanism, though large-scale human RCT data specifically for apigenin as an isolated compound is more limited than for magnesium.

L-theanine, an amino acid found in green tea, promotes alpha wave activity in the brain (associated with a relaxed but alert state) and increases GABA, dopamine, and serotonin activity. Research suggests L-theanine may reduce sleep latency and improve sleep quality without causing sedation or next-day grogginess. It is particularly useful for the racing-mind type of nighttime wakefulness common in perimenopausal women.

For the NAD+ circadian connection, a 2018 review by Yoshino et al. in Cell Metabolism described in detail how NAD+ intermediates, including NMN and NR, support circadian rhythm regulation through the SIRT1-NAD+-CLOCK axis (PMID: 29249689). While direct human RCTs measuring sleep outcomes specifically from NAD+ supplementation are still limited, the mechanistic connection between NAD+ and circadian function is well-established in the peer-reviewed literature.

According to Happy Aging's review of the current literature on sleep and NAD+ in perimenopausal women, addressing sleep disruption requires a dual approach: the GABA/calming side (magnesium, apigenin, L-theanine) and the circadian/clock side (NAD+ precursor support). Addressing only one side leaves the other unaddressed, which may explain why some women find standard sleep supplements only partially helpful.

What the Evidence Does Not Support

Supplements alone will not fix sleep disruption that has an underlying structural cause. Sleep apnea, which becomes more common in women after menopause as airway tone decreases, is a medical diagnosis that requires medical management. Restless leg syndrome, chronic pain, and anxiety disorders are all clinical conditions that can disrupt sleep and require clinical evaluation. If your sleep disruption is severe, unrelenting, or accompanied by symptoms like snoring, gasping, or daytime sleepiness, see your physician before turning to supplements.

Melatonin is frequently reached for first by women experiencing perimenopausal sleep disruption. The evidence for melatonin is actually mixed for this specific population. Melatonin is most helpful for circadian timing issues (jet lag, shift work) rather than the GABAergic sleep disruption driven by progesterone decline. High doses of melatonin can also suppress natural melatonin production over time. Research suggests starting with lower doses (0.5 to 1 mg) if you try melatonin, rather than the 5 to 10 mg doses common on store shelves.

The idea that taking a sleep supplement once in a while will reset sleep patterns is not supported by research. Consistency over weeks to months is how these compounds work. GABA-supporting supplements like magnesium work best as a nightly habit, not as an occasional intervention. The same is true for NAD+ support and circadian regulation: these are long-term cellular processes that require sustained supplementation to shift.

We believe that addressing both the GABA-mediated calming deficit (via magnesium, apigenin, and L-theanine) and the circadian regulation deficit (via NAD+ support) is the most complete strategy available with current evidence. We do not claim these supplements treat insomnia or any sleep disorder, and we recommend clinical evaluation for persistent or severe sleep disruption.

The Happy Aging Recommendation

Sleep disruption in perimenopause is not one problem with one solution. It's several overlapping problems. The most important thing you can do is identify which mechanisms are most prominent for you.

If your primary symptom is difficulty falling asleep or racing thoughts at bedtime, the GABA pathway is likely your primary target. Magnesium (especially magnesium glycinate or bisglycinate, which crosses the blood-brain barrier more readily) taken 30 to 60 minutes before bed is the first-line evidence-based option. Adding L-theanine and apigenin addresses this same pathway through complementary mechanisms.

If your sleep pattern has shifted, your sleep is light or unrefreshing even when you sleep adequate hours, or you feel chronically fatigued despite sleeping, the circadian component may be the primary driver. This is where NAD+ support becomes relevant. Supporting NAD+ levels addresses the SIRT1-CLOCK regulation that maintains circadian precision. Combining sleep-specific GABA support with NAD+ supplementation addresses both pathways simultaneously.

Foundation habits matter enormously here. Consistent sleep and wake times, even on weekends. A cool, dark bedroom. Limiting screens in the two hours before bed. Avoiding alcohol, which suppresses REM sleep despite initially helping you fall asleep. These are not optional extras. They are the non-negotiable foundation on which any supplement strategy is built.

Who This Is NOT For

  • Women with undiagnosed sleep apnea (snoring, gasping during sleep, unrefreshing sleep, daytime sleepiness): see a physician first
  • Women whose sleep disruption is primarily driven by severe hot flashes: a physician conversation about hormone therapy is appropriate before relying on supplements
  • Anyone currently taking benzodiazepines or other prescription sleep medications: discuss any supplement additions with your prescribing physician, as interactions are possible

  • Anyone expecting overnight results from a sleep supplement. These compounds support biological processes that take weeks to shift

Frequently Asked Questions

Why do I wake up at 3 a.m. every night in perimenopause?

The 3 a.m. wake time is a specific and very common perimenopausal pattern. It often reflects the combination of reduced progesterone (lower GABA support makes sleep lighter and easier to disrupt) and elevated nocturnal cortisol (the HPA axis can become dysregulated during the hormonal transition, causing cortisol to rise earlier in the night than it should). Magnesium, L-theanine, and a consistent bedtime routine help address both the GABA and cortisol components of this pattern.

Does NAD+ really affect sleep quality?

The connection between NAD+ and sleep is not direct in the way a sleep aid is direct. NAD+ supports the SIRT1-CLOCK system that regulates circadian timing at the cellular level (PMID: 29249689). When NAD+ is depleted, circadian regulation becomes less precise, which shows up as irregular sleep quality, shifted sleep timing, and poor sleep architecture. NAD+ supplementation addresses this circadian component. It is not a sedative and won't make you feel sleepy. It supports the biological clock that regulates when and how deeply you sleep.

Is magnesium glycinate better than other forms for sleep?

Magnesium glycinate (also called magnesium bisglycinate) is generally considered the preferred form for sleep support because the glycinate form crosses the blood-brain barrier more readily than other forms like magnesium oxide or magnesium citrate. It is also well-tolerated in the gut. Magnesium oxide is poorly absorbed. Magnesium citrate is well-absorbed but more likely to have a laxative effect at higher doses. For sleep specifically, glycinate is the form most commonly recommended and studied in the context of GABA-mediated relaxation.

How long does it take for a sleep supplement to work?

Magnesium can produce noticeable effects within a few days to two weeks for many people. L-theanine may work within a single dose for the racing-thoughts component. Apigenin typically shows effects over two to four weeks of consistent use. The circadian effects of NAD+ supplementation are more gradual and may take six to twelve weeks to manifest meaningfully. Consistency every night is more important than any single night's dose.

Should I talk to my doctor before taking sleep supplements in perimenopause?

Yes, particularly if you are on prescription medications. Magnesium can interact with some antibiotics and bisphosphonates (used for bone density). If you take blood pressure medication, discuss with your physician before adding L-theanine at higher doses. If your sleep disruption is severe or significantly affecting your functioning, a clinical evaluation is more appropriate than self-managing with supplements alone.
