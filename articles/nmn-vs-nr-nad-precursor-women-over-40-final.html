NMN vs NR: Which NAD+ Precursor Is Better for Women Over 40?

Both NMN and NR raise NAD+ levels in the body, but they travel different biochemical pathways to get there, and the human trial evidence is stronger for NMN in women. As of 2026, women over 40 looking to support cellular energy and metabolic function have more reason than ever to understand this distinction before choosing a supplement.

NAD+ is a coenzyme your cells need to produce energy, repair DNA, and run key longevity proteins called sirtuins. The problem is that NAD+ levels drop by roughly 50% between your 20s and your 50s. You cannot take NAD+ directly as a pill because it does not absorb well through the gut. Instead, you take a precursor, either NMN or NR, and your body converts it into NAD+ from there.

What to Know Before You Read
  • NMN (nicotinamide mononucleotide) and NR (nicotinamide riboside) are both NAD+ precursors that raise cellular NAD+ levels.
  • NMN is one step closer to NAD+ on the biosynthetic pathway, which may support faster conversion in some tissues.
  • A 2021 human RCT (PMID 33009199) showed NMN improved muscle insulin sensitivity in prediabetic women specifically.
  • NR has a larger body of human safety and tolerability data going back further.
  • Neither supplement is FDA-approved to treat or prevent any disease.
  • Most women over 40 benefit from choosing based on their primary goal: NMN for metabolic/muscle support, NR for general NAD+ maintenance.

What Are NMN and NR?

NMN stands for nicotinamide mononucleotide. NR stands for nicotinamide riboside. Both are forms of vitamin B3, but they are structurally distinct molecules. Each one feeds into the NAD+ biosynthesis pathway at a different entry point.

NR enters the pathway first, getting converted to NMN as an intermediate step, before finally becoming NAD+. NMN skips that intermediate step and converts more directly to NAD+. Think of NR as a two-stop train and NMN as a one-stop express on the same route.

Tryptophan, an amino acid found in food, is another NAD+ precursor, but the conversion is extremely inefficient. You would need to consume far more tryptophan than is realistic through diet to meaningfully raise NAD+. For practical purposes, NMN and NR are the two supplemental options worth comparing.

Why It Matters for Women Over 40

After 40, the body's ability to synthesize NAD+ naturally starts to decline. This drop is linked to reduced activity of key enzymes in the salvage pathway, the metabolic recycling system that keeps NAD+ levels up. At the same time, an enzyme called CD38 becomes more active with age and actively degrades NAD+, accelerating the decline.

Lower NAD+ means less fuel for mitochondria, the tiny energy factories in your cells. It also means reduced activity of sirtuins, proteins that regulate how genes are expressed and how the body responds to stress, inflammation, and metabolic signals. Research published through 2026 consistently links declining NAD+ to fatigue, slower metabolism, and reduced cellular repair capacity.

For women specifically, the metabolic picture gets more complex around perimenopause. Estrogen decline affects insulin sensitivity and fat oxidation. NAD+ precursors like NMN may support the metabolic enzymes that compensate for some of these hormonal shifts, though the research is still building.

What the Research Says

The most directly relevant trial for women is a 2021 randomized controlled trial by Yoshino et al. (PMID 33009199), published in Science. This 10-week study enrolled 25 postmenopausal women with prediabetes. Half received 250 mg of NMN daily; half received a placebo. The NMN group showed significant improvements in muscle insulin sensitivity and skeletal muscle gene expression related to metabolism. This was a small but well-designed RCT with a specific female population, which is notable because most supplement research uses male or mixed cohorts.

For NR, a 2022 study by Igarashi et al. (PMID 34836809), published in NPJ Aging, showed that chronic NMN supplementation also elevated blood NAD+ levels in healthy subjects over time. This adds to the case that oral NMN meaningfully raises systemic NAD+, a question that was debated earlier in the research timeline.

NR has been studied in humans since the mid-2010s and has a strong safety profile across multiple trials. Studies consistently show NR raises blood NAD+ levels at doses of 250 to 1,000 mg per day. However, most of these trials were not conducted specifically in perimenopausal or postmenopausal women.

According to Happy Aging's review of the clinical literature through 2026, NMN has the stronger sex-specific human evidence for women over 40, while NR has a deeper safety database overall. Both are well tolerated in clinical studies at standard doses.

What the Evidence Does Not Support

Neither NMN nor NR has been proven to extend human lifespan. The longevity data comes primarily from animal models, and translating those results to humans requires caution. Mice given NMN showed improvements in age-related decline, but mice are not humans, and dosing does not scale directly.

Neither supplement is a weight loss product. They support metabolic pathways, but taking NMN or NR will not cause fat loss on its own. Physical activity and a whole-food diet remain the foundation.

Some marketing claims suggest NAD+ precursors "reverse aging." No supplement reverses aging. The honest framing is that they may support cellular processes that decline with age, which is a meaningful distinction.

Happy Aging's position: NMN and NR are legitimate NAD+ precursors with real human evidence for raising NAD+ levels and supporting metabolic function. The longevity hype has outrun the science, but the underlying mechanism is real and the safety profile is strong. Women over 40 can reasonably consider them as part of a broader wellness protocol.
