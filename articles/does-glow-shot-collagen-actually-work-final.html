Why Skin Collagen Matters So Much After 40

To evaluate whether Glow Shot works, it helps to understand exactly what is happening to skin collagen after 40 and why supplementation can be meaningful. Collagen is the primary structural protein in skin, forming a matrix of fibers that gives skin its firmness, elasticity, and plumpness. Collagen also holds moisture within the dermal layer, contributing to the hydrated, bouncy quality that characterizes youthful skin.



The decline of collagen is continuous from the late 20s onward, but the rate dramatically accelerates at menopause. In the first five years after menopause, women lose approximately 30 percent of their skin collagen. This is why many women notice that skin changes that were gradual in their 30s and early 40s suddenly feel much more pronounced in the mid-40s to 50s. The hormonal environment is not just withdrawing estrogen, it is withdrawing the primary hormonal driver of fibroblast activity, which is the cellular machinery that makes new collagen.



The consequences appear as increased skin laxity, particularly around the jaw, neck, and eyes; deeper lines around the mouth and forehead; reduced skin hydration and a drier, less resilient texture; and the loss of the plumpness and volume that collagen structure previously maintained. These are not cosmetic concerns but physiological changes driven by measurable protein decline.



This is the context in which oral collagen supplementation makes scientific sense. If the body's collagen production infrastructure is still functional but undersupplied with precursor materials, providing the amino acids specific to collagen synthesis, hydroxyproline, proline, and glycine, in a highly bioavailable peptide form can stimulate fibroblasts to produce more collagen. The evidence suggests this is exactly what happens with well-formulated oral collagen products.

What the Clinical Evidence Shows

The evidence base for oral marine collagen supplementation is now substantial enough to support genuine confidence in the approach. A 2015 study by Asserin and colleagues published in the Journal of Cosmetic Dermatology found that oral collagen peptide supplementation for 8 weeks significantly increased skin moisture levels by 28 percent and produced measurable improvements in the dermal collagen network on skin biopsies. DOI: 10.1111/jocd.12174. This is direct structural evidence, not just subjective reporting.



A 2018 study by Kim and colleagues published in Nutrients examined oral low-molecular-weight collagen peptide supplementation for 12 weeks in women and found statistically significant improvements in skin hydration, elasticity, and the appearance of wrinkles compared to the placebo group. DOI: 10.3390/nu10070826. Importantly, effects continued to build through the 12-week endpoint, suggesting that longer supplementation produces progressive benefit.



A systematic review and meta-analysis published in 2019 in the Journal of Drugs in Dermatology evaluated 11 randomized controlled trials involving oral collagen supplementation, finding consistent improvements in skin elasticity, skin moisture, transepidermal water loss, and skin roughness across the studies. The evidence quality was rated as good, supporting the use of oral collagen peptides as a legitimate skin intervention.



Marine-specific evidence adds another layer of confidence. Fish-derived collagen has a lower molecular weight than bovine collagen, approximately 500 daltons compared to 300,000 daltons for intact collagen, which is why its absorption into the bloodstream has been demonstrated to be approximately 1.5 times higher than bovine sources in comparative studies. For women who are conscious of bioavailability, marine collagen is the better-evidenced choice.
