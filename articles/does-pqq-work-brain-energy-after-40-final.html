PQQ (pyrroloquinoline quinone) is a micronutrient that may support mitochondrial function and cognitive performance by stimulating the growth of new mitochondria, a process called mitochondrial biogenesis, which declines with age in women.

Brain fog, low energy, and slower recovery are among the most common complaints women bring up in their 40s. Many of these symptoms trace back to mitochondrial function. As of 2026, PQQ has moved from an obscure research compound to an ingredient appearing in an increasing number of brain and energy supplements. Here is what the science actually supports.

What to Know Before Reading
  • PQQ is a compound found in soil bacteria and small amounts in foods like kiwi, green peppers, and green tea. It is not made by the human body.
  • It is unique among brain supplements because it may stimulate mitochondrial biogenesis (the creation of new mitochondria), not just protect existing ones.
  • Human trials on PQQ are limited in number but show promising signals for cognitive function and energy-related markers.
  • PQQ is typically studied alongside CoQ10, as these two compounds support overlapping mitochondrial pathways.
  • Standard supplement doses (10-20 mg/day) appear to be safe in short-term studies.

What Is PQQ?

Pyrroloquinoline quinone (PQQ) is a redox-active compound, meaning it participates in electron transfer reactions inside cells. It was first identified as a cofactor for bacterial enzymes and later found to be present in human breast milk, suggesting it may play a role in early development. Whether it qualifies as a true vitamin in humans is still debated in nutritional science, but it functions as a micronutrient with measurable biological activity.

The most significant proposed mechanism of PQQ is its ability to activate PGC-1 alpha, a master regulator of mitochondrial biogenesis. Most antioxidants protect existing cellular structures from damage. PQQ is being studied for something more: it may prompt cells to generate entirely new mitochondria. In aging biology, where mitochondrial number and quality decline over decades, this distinction is meaningful.

PQQ also acts as a potent antioxidant. It is capable of carrying out thousands of reduction-oxidation cycles before being degraded, compared to most antioxidants that are consumed in a single reaction. This makes it unusually durable as a cellular protectant.

Why It Matters for Women Over 40

Mitochondrial function sits at the center of several concerns that matter most to women in their 40s and 50s. Every cell in the body runs on ATP (cellular energy currency) produced by mitochondria. When mitochondrial density declines, every energy-dependent function slows down, including neurotransmitter production, muscle repair, immune response, and hormonal signaling.

The brain is the most mitochondria-dense organ in the body relative to its weight. Neurons require enormous amounts of ATP to maintain their electrical signaling. Cognitive symptoms like brain fog, slowed processing, and reduced working memory in midlife women are at least partly attributable to changes in neuronal energy availability.

The connection to NAD+ is also relevant. PQQ supports electron transport chain function, the same mitochondrial pathway that NAD+ fuels. What the Research Says

According to Happy Aging's review of the human and preclinical research on PQQ, here is what the literature shows.

Cognitive function signals in humans. A study by Nakano et al. (PMID: 22896760) examined PQQ supplementation in older adults and found improvements in cognitive function scores, including tests of memory and attention, compared to placebo. The study population was middle-aged and elderly adults. The sample size was modest, and the findings require replication, but they represent meaningful preliminary human evidence.

PQQ metabolism in humans. Research associated with PMID: 24751447 has examined how the body processes PQQ, confirming it is absorbed from dietary sources and supplements. This establishes bioavailability as a foundation for any clinical effects.

Mitochondrial biogenesis signals. Preclinical studies have consistently found that PQQ activates PGC-1 alpha and promotes mitochondrial biogenesis in cell and animal models. Whether this mechanism operates at the same scale in adult human supplementation is not yet confirmed by large clinical trials, but the mechanistic evidence is robust enough to generate serious scientific interest.

Antioxidant activity. PQQ's redox cycling capacity has been documented in multiple studies. Its ability to repeatedly neutralize free radicals without being destroyed sets it apart from most dietary antioxidants and is relevant to protecting mitochondria from oxidative damage.

What the Evidence Doesn't Support

The human evidence base for PQQ is genuinely promising but small. Here is where honesty requires caution.

PQQ has not been proven to reverse cognitive decline or treat any neurological condition in humans. The existing human trials are small, short-term, and conducted in specific populations. No large randomized controlled trial has confirmed cognitive improvement from PQQ supplementation in perimenopausal or midlife women specifically.

The mechanism of mitochondrial biogenesis via PQQ supplementation has been well-documented in cell and animal studies. Its direct clinical translation to meaningful energy or cognitive improvement in humans at supplement doses (10-20 mg) has not been fully established.

PQQ is not a replacement for sleep, exercise, or addressing underlying causes of fatigue. These lifestyle factors drive mitochondrial health far more powerfully than any supplement. PQQ belongs in a supporting role, not as a first-line intervention for serious cognitive symptoms.

Happy Aging's position: PQQ is a mechanistically compelling mitochondrial support compound with preliminary human evidence for cognitive benefit. It is a reasonable addition to a brain and energy protocol for women over 40, particularly when combined with CoQ10 and NAD+ precursors.

Who Should NOT Take PQQ

PQQ is generally considered safe at standard doses in healthy adults, but it is not appropriate for everyone. If you are on medication for blood clotting or have a liver condition, consult your doctor before adding PQQ. As a redox-active compound, PQQ may theoretically interact with anticoagulant medications, though specific drug interaction studies are limited.

Pregnant and nursing women should avoid PQQ supplements due to insufficient safety data in these populations.

If you are currently managing a serious neurological condition or are on medication for a brain health diagnosis, discuss PQQ with your neurologist before starting. Supplements with CNS activity require careful consideration alongside prescription treatments.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does PQQ actually do in the body?
PQQ acts as a redox-active antioxidant and may activate PGC-1 alpha, a protein that stimulates the creation of new mitochondria. It supports the electron transport chain in mitochondria, which is the primary site of ATP (energy) production. Animal studies suggest it can increase mitochondrial density in tissues including muscle and brain.

How long does it take for PQQ to work?
The human study showing cognitive improvement by Nakano et al. measured outcomes over 12 weeks. Mitochondrial biogenesis is a slow process. Expecting noticeable effects before 8-12 weeks of consistent supplementation is not realistic based on the available evidence.

Should I take PQQ with CoQ10?
Yes, this is the most common combination studied and recommended by practitioners working with mitochondrial health. PQQ and CoQ10 support complementary aspects of mitochondrial function. PQQ promotes mitochondrial biogenesis while CoQ10 supports efficient electron transport within existing mitochondria.

Can PQQ help with menopause-related brain fog?
There are no clinical trials specifically studying PQQ in perimenopausal or menopausal women for brain fog. The mechanistic rationale is sound (mitochondrial energy decline contributes to cognitive symptoms), and the preliminary human evidence for cognitive support is encouraging, but a direct, confirmed answer is not yet available from the literature.

Is PQQ safe to take every day?
Short-term studies at 10-20 mg/day show a good safety profile in healthy adults. Long-term safety data in humans is limited, which is true of most dietary supplements. Annual review with your physician is recommended for any daily supplement regimen.
