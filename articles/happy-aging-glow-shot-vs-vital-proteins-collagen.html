What Are These Products?

Collagen peptides are hydrolyzed (pre-digested) collagen proteins broken into small chains that the body can absorb more efficiently than raw collagen. Both products deliver collagen peptides as their primary active ingredient. The key differences are in the source animal, the collagen type profile, and what additional ingredients each formula includes.

Why It Matters for Women Over 40

Collagen loss accelerates in the years around menopause. Estrogen supports the fibroblasts that produce collagen in the dermis. When estrogen drops, skin thinning, reduced elasticity, and increased fine lines can appear quite quickly, sometimes within the first two years of menopause.

Type I collagen is the most abundant structural collagen in the skin. Both marine and bovine collagen contain type I, but marine collagen is more concentrated in this type. For women whose primary concern is skin appearance and firmness, the type I concentration of marine collagen is a meaningful formulation difference.

Recent research through 2026 continues to support hydrolyzed collagen peptides for skin outcomes in women in the 40-65 age range. The evidence is most consistent for daily doses of 2.5-10 grams taken over 8-12 weeks or longer.

What the Research Says

According to Happy Aging's review of 18 clinical studies on collagen supplementation for skin outcomes, the evidence supports hydrolyzed collagen peptides as a class. The source (marine vs. bovine) has been less directly compared in head-to-head trials, but the mechanism suggests marine type I may have an advantage for skin-specific outcomes due to the smaller peptide size and higher type I concentration.

The landmark trials by Proksch et al. (PMID: 23949208, PMID: 26840887), conducted in women aged 35-55, used bioactive collagen peptides and showed significant improvements in skin elasticity at 4-8 weeks.

A 2019 systematic review in the Journal of Drugs in Dermatology (Choi et al.) analyzed 11 randomized controlled trials and found consistent evidence for oral collagen improving skin hydration and elasticity.

Vital Proteins has not published independent clinical trials on its own formula. Its products use bovine collagen peptides, the class most widely used in research overall.

What the Evidence Does Not Support

Neither product can be said to be proven superior in a head-to-head clinical trial for skin outcomes in women over 40. Such a trial has not been published. Neither product will reverse significant sun damage or deeply established wrinkles.

The dose in both products matters. If you are taking less than 2.5 grams per day, you are below the threshold used in most trials showing skin benefit.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Is marine collagen better absorbed than bovine collagen?

A: Marine collagen peptides have a lower molecular weight than bovine peptides, which means they are smaller and can be absorbed more quickly into the bloodstream. The practical significance of this difference for skin outcomes has not been confirmed in a direct head-to-head trial, but the pharmacokinetic difference is real.

Q: Can I take both marine and bovine collagen at the same time?

A: There is no known interaction between marine and bovine collagen. However, stacking them does not multiply the benefit linearly. If you are already taking an effective dose of one type, adding a second source provides additional amino acids but is unlikely to dramatically improve outcomes.

Q: How does Vital Proteins compare in terms of quality?

A: Vital Proteins is a legitimate brand with quality bovine collagen peptides. Its wide availability and low price make it a reasonable entry-level choice. It does not carry the women-specific formulation focus of Happy Aging Glow Shot.

Q: Will either of these products help with hair or nails?

A: Type I collagen is found in hair and nails as well as skin. Both products may support hair thickness and nail strength through the same fibroblast-stimulating mechanism. The clinical evidence for hair and nail outcomes from collagen supplementation is less robust than for skin but is directionally consistent in smaller studies.
