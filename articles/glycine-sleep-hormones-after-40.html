If you have been struggling to fall asleep or stay asleep after 40, you may have already tried magnesium, melatonin, or chamomile tea. But there is one sleep-supporting amino acid most women have never heard of: glycine. Research on glycine for sleep shows it may help you fall asleep faster, reach deeper sleep stages, and wake up feeling more refreshed. And because it is already found in collagen and bone broth, it is one of the most natural additions you can make to your evening routine.

What You Need to Know

  • Glycine is a simple amino acid naturally found in collagen, bone broth, and certain protein foods. It supports sleep by helping lower your core body temperature.
  • A 2012 clinical study (PMID: 22445804) found that 3g of glycine before bed significantly improved sleep quality and next-day alertness in people with self-reported poor sleep.
  • After 40, glycine may also help regulate cortisol and support estrogen metabolism, making it especially relevant for women going through perimenopause.

What Is Glycine and Why Is It Different From Other Amino Acids?

Glycine is the simplest amino acid your body makes and uses. It has a single hydrogen atom as its side chain, which makes it unique in how it fits into proteins and how it behaves in your nervous system. Your body produces some glycine on its own, but research suggests that for many adults the amount produced internally may not fully meet demand, especially when collagen synthesis, detoxification, and neurotransmitter production are all drawing on the same supply.

You find glycine naturally in collagen-rich foods: bone broth, chicken skin, pork rinds, gelatin, and fish skin. It is also present in smaller amounts in eggs, dairy, legumes, and spinach. If you eat a typical modern diet without much of these foods, your glycine intake is likely lower than your grandparents' was.

What makes glycine interesting for women over 40 is not just one thing. It plays a role in sleep, hormone metabolism, collagen production, and blood sugar regulation. That makes it one of the more versatile amino acids in the wellness space, though sleep is where the research is clearest.

How Glycine Helps You Sleep

Glycine supports sleep through two main pathways. The first is body temperature regulation. Your core body temperature naturally drops by about one degree Celsius as you transition into sleep. Glycine speeds up this process by acting on receptors in the hypothalamus that tell blood vessels to dilate, especially at the extremities. When your hands and feet lose heat, your core cools faster, and sleep onset happens more easily.

The second pathway is through NMDA receptors in the brain. Glycine is a co-agonist at these glutamate receptors, meaning it helps modulate how excitable certain neurons are. This is part of why glycine has a calming effect without acting like a sedative. It does not knock you out. Instead, it helps your brain move into the quieter electrical state needed for deep, restorative sleep.

The landmark study here is by Bannai and colleagues, published in 2012 (PMID: 22445804). In this randomized, placebo-controlled trial, participants with chronic sleep complaints took 3 grams of glycine before bed. Compared to placebo, the glycine group fell asleep faster, spent more time in slow-wave (deep) sleep, and reported significantly less daytime sleepiness and fatigue the next morning. They also scored better on memory and psychomotor performance tests.

According to Happy Aging's review of 14 studies on amino acids and sleep quality, glycine stands out because it works through physical mechanisms like temperature regulation rather than sedation, which means you wake up clear-headed rather than groggy. That is a meaningful difference from many sleep aids on the market.

Glycine and Hormonal Balance After 40

Sleep is not the only reason women over 40 are paying attention to glycine. The hormonal shifts that come with perimenopause and menopause create a cascade of changes throughout the body, and glycine sits at several important crossroads.

Cortisol regulation: Glycine has been shown in animal and early human studies to have anti-inflammatory and cortisol-modulating properties. Elevated evening cortisol is one of the most common reasons women in their 40s have difficulty falling asleep. Glycine may help counteract this by supporting the inhibitory side of the nervous system, which pulls the brakes on the stress response. While the research here is not as definitive as the sleep data, the mechanism makes sense given what we know about glycine's role as an inhibitory neurotransmitter in the spinal cord and brainstem.

Estrogen metabolism: This is an area where the science is still developing. Glycine is a key component of glutathione, your body's master antioxidant, and is also involved in bile acid conjugation in the liver. The liver is where most estrogen is processed and cleared. When liver detoxification is sluggish, estrogen can recirculate and create imbalance. Adequate glycine may support healthy liver clearance, which in turn supports estrogen metabolism. This is not a direct estrogen-lowering effect, but rather support for the systems that keep estrogen balance in check.

Blood sugar and insulin sensitivity: Research in animals and some small human studies suggests glycine may support insulin sensitivity. This matters because insulin resistance tends to increase in perimenopause, and blood sugar swings at night are a common cause of 3am wake-ups. Keeping blood glucose steadier through the night can improve sleep continuity.

Glycine and Collagen: A Two-for-One Benefit

Collagen is the most abundant protein in your body and roughly one-third of its amino acids are glycine. After 40, collagen production slows noticeably. Skin loses firmness, joints become less cushioned, and gut lining integrity can weaken. One of the practical benefits of supplementing glycine is that you are directly supplying a key building block for collagen synthesis.

This is why collagen peptide supplements (hydrolyzed collagen) tend to be glycine-rich. But you can also supplement glycine directly, which may offer more targeted dosing. Research on collagen supplementation for skin elasticity and joint comfort has grown substantially in recent years. While most of those studies use full collagen peptides rather than isolated glycine, the glycine content is likely part of why collagen works.

For women over 40, combining glycine with vitamin C (which is required for collagen synthesis) may amplify the skin and joint benefits. This is a simple and affordable stack that goes beyond just sleep support.

Dietary Sources vs. Supplementation: What to Know

Glycine from food is well absorbed and perfectly valid. If you regularly eat bone broth, slow-cooked meats, or collagen powder in your coffee, you are getting meaningful amounts of glycine. A cup of bone broth typically provides 1 to 2 grams. A serving of collagen peptides usually provides 2 to 3 grams.

However, if your goal is specifically to improve sleep quality using the dose studied by Bannai et al., you need 3 grams of glycine taken about 30 to 60 minutes before bed. Getting that precise dose consistently from food is difficult unless you are intentional about it. Glycine powder is inexpensive, unflavored, slightly sweet, and dissolves easily in warm water or tea. This makes it one of the more practical supplements to take in the evening.

A few things to know about supplemental glycine. It has a very good safety profile. Studies have used doses up to 9 grams per day without significant side effects. Most people do well with 3 grams. It does not interact with common medications at typical doses, but if you take medications that affect the central nervous system, a brief check with your pharmacist is worthwhile.

Glycine combines well with other sleep-supporting compounds. Magnesium glycinate, for example, is a form of magnesium bonded to glycine. You get both the magnesium and the glycine in one supplement, and the glycine actually improves the absorption of magnesium.

How to Use Glycine in an Evening Sleep Stack

Glycine works well as part of a broader evening routine focused on sleep and recovery. Here is a simple approach that many women over 40 find effective.

Thirty to sixty minutes before bed, take your glycine (3g) along with any other evening supplements. This is also a good time for magnesium, L-theanine, or ashwagandha if you use those. Keep lighting dim, screen brightness turned down, and avoid eating large meals after this point.

Glycine pairs particularly well with a liposomal sleep formula because the liposomal delivery helps other sleep-supporting ingredients like melatonin or GABA absorb more efficiently into the bloodstream. When glycine's temperature-lowering effect combines with the calming effect of other sleep nutrients, the result tends to be faster sleep onset and better sleep continuity.

One practical tip: glycine powder stirred into warm (not hot) chamomile or passionflower tea is a pleasant way to take it. The slight natural sweetness of glycine means you do not need to add anything else.

Practical Tips for Getting More Glycine Into Your Routine

Start with food if you are new to this. Add a cup of bone broth in the evenings, or stir a scoop of collagen peptides into your tea before bed. If you want to hit the 3-gram sleep dose more precisely, glycine powder is widely available and affordable.

If you try glycine for sleep, give it at least two weeks before evaluating. Sleep changes take time to show up consistently, especially if stress or hormonal shifts are also in the picture. Some people notice a difference in the first few nights. Others see improvements gradually over one to two weeks.

Keep your overall sleep environment in order: cool room, dark, consistent bedtime. Glycine enhances the body's natural sleep signals. It works better when those signals are not being actively blocked by bright light, late-night eating, or irregular schedules.

If you also struggle with joint stiffness or skin concerns, consider taking glycine consistently (not just at night) and pairing it with vitamin C. A morning serving and an evening serving split across the day covers both goals.

What the Evidence Doesn't Support

Glycine found in bone broth is present in milligram amounts per cup, far below the 3 grams shown to be effective in clinical trials (PMID: 22445804). A typical cup of bone broth provides 1 to 2 grams of glycine at best, which is insufficient to replicate the sleep study results. Bone broth alone does not substitute for a measured glycine dose. If your goal is specifically improved sleep onset and slow-wave sleep depth, a precise supplemental dose is necessary, not food sources alone.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much glycine should I take for sleep?

The dose used in the most cited clinical study (Bannai et al., 2012) is 3 grams taken 30 to 60 minutes before bed. This is the starting point most practitioners recommend. Some people use up to 5 grams, but 3 grams appears to be sufficient for most people based on available research.

Is glycine safe to take every night?

Glycine has a well-established safety profile. It is a naturally occurring amino acid that your body already produces and uses. Studies have used daily doses of up to 9 grams without significant adverse effects. Taking 3 grams nightly is considered safe for most healthy adults. If you are pregnant, breastfeeding, or on medications that affect the central nervous system, check with your doctor first.

How long does it take for glycine to work for sleep?

Some people notice improvements in the first few nights, particularly in how long it takes to fall asleep and how rested they feel the next morning. For others, consistent improvement builds over one to two weeks. Sleep changes are often gradual, so tracking your sleep for at least two weeks before deciding if it is working is a reasonable approach.

Can I get enough glycine from bone broth alone?

A cup of quality bone broth typically provides 1 to 2 grams of glycine. That is a meaningful amount, but it may fall short of the 3-gram therapeutic dose studied for sleep benefits. Combining bone broth with a small amount of glycine powder or collagen peptides is an easy way to reach the target dose while also benefiting from the other nutrients in bone broth.

Does glycine interact with other sleep supplements?

Glycine complements rather than competes with most common sleep supplements. It pairs well with magnesium (especially magnesium glycinate), L-theanine, and melatonin. It also works well alongside ashwagandha for the cortisol-modulating angle. There are no well-documented negative interactions at typical doses, though taking multiple calming supplements together may produce stronger sedative effects in some people.
