NAD+ is essential for the cellular DNA repair and energy production processes that keep skin cells functioning well, and its natural decline after age 40 is one of the underappreciated contributors to the visible signs of skin aging.

Most conversations about skin aging focus on what you put on your skin. But the most meaningful changes in how your skin ages are happening inside your cells, not on the surface. As of 2026, longevity researchers and dermatologically-minded physicians are paying increasing attention to NAD+ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide) as a foundational molecule for skin cell health, collagen synthesis support, and DNA damage repair. This article explains the cellular biology of NAD+ and skin aging, what the research does and does not support, and how women over 40 can build a skin health protocol grounded in current science.

What to Know

  • NAD+ is required by PARP enzymes that repair UV-induced DNA damage in skin cells, a process that becomes less efficient as NAD+ levels decline after 40.
  • Sirtuins, which depend on NAD+ to function, regulate skin cell turnover, inflammation, and melanin distribution, all of which affect visible skin aging.
  • Collagen synthesis depends on adequate cellular energy (ATP), which requires efficient NAD+-dependent mitochondrial function.
  • Research suggests oral NAD+ precursor supplementation may better support systemic NAD+ levels than topical NAD+ products, which have limited skin penetration.
  • Supplements support skin health at the cellular level but do not replace medical skincare for conditions like melasma, rosacea, or skin cancer history.

What Is NAD+ and Why Does It Matter for Skin?

NAD+ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide) is a coenzyme present in every living cell, essential for hundreds of biological reactions including cellular energy production, DNA repair, and the regulation of gene expression through proteins called sirtuins. In skin specifically, NAD+ serves several critical functions. First, it is the substrate required by PARP (poly ADP-ribose polymerase) enzymes, which detect and repair DNA strand breaks caused by UV radiation. Every time your skin is exposed to sunlight, UV radiation inflicts thousands of DNA damage events per cell. PARP enzymes rush to repair this damage, consuming NAD+ in the process. Second, NAD+ activates sirtuins, which regulate inflammatory pathways, skin cell turnover rates, and the distribution of melanin (the pigment responsible for age spots when unevenly distributed). Third, NAD+ is central to the mitochondrial processes that produce ATP, the cellular energy currency required for collagen synthesis, cell renewal, and wound healing. When NAD+ levels are adequate, these systems operate efficiently. When they decline, as they do after age 40, each of these skin health functions is compromised.

Why Skin Aging Accelerates After 40

After age 40, a convergence of biological changes accelerates visible skin aging. NAD+ levels in skin cells decline, reducing the efficiency of DNA repair and sirtuin activity. Collagen production decreases as the fibroblasts (cells that make collagen) slow down, and the collagen that exists becomes more cross-linked and less resilient. Estrogen decline during perimenopause further affects skin hydration, thickness, and collagen density, since estrogen receptors in the skin play a role in maintaining these properties. Cumulative UV damage from decades of sun exposure compounds on a foundation of diminished repair capacity, which is why the visible effects of past sun exposure often seem to "catch up" with women in their 40s. Together, these changes produce the classic signs of skin aging: fine lines, reduced elasticity, dullness, uneven tone, and enlarged pores.

NAD+ and UV DNA Repair in Skin Cells

One of the most biologically significant connections between NAD+ and skin aging is the DNA repair pathway. Research published in Cell Metabolism by Rajman, Chwalek, and Sinclair (2018) (PMID 29502867) reviewed the evidence for NAD+-boosting molecules and documented the role of NAD+ in supporting PARP-dependent DNA repair across multiple tissue types. In skin cells specifically, this pathway is particularly important because skin is the body's first line of defense against UV radiation, meaning skin cell DNA faces far greater daily damage than most other cell types. When NAD+ is abundant, PARP enzymes can repair UV-induced DNA breaks promptly, reducing the risk of mutations accumulating over time. When NAD+ is low, as it typically is in women in their 40s and beyond, repair capacity is reduced, and damaged cells may replicate with errors or undergo inflammatory pathways that accelerate skin aging.

Sirtuins, NAD+, and Age Spots

Sirtuins are a family of proteins that require NAD+ to function. In skin, SIRT1 and SIRT3 play roles in regulating inflammatory gene expression, controlling melanocyte (pigment cell) activity, and managing the skin cell renewal cycle. Research suggests that reduced sirtuin activity, driven by declining NAD+, may contribute to uneven melanin distribution, producing the age spots and hyperpigmentation that become more common after 40. Sirtuins also regulate the skin's inflammatory response. When sirtuin activity is low, pro-inflammatory pathways become more active, contributing to the chronic low-level inflammation (sometimes called "inflammaging") that accelerates both skin and systemic aging.

Collagen, ATP, and the NAD+ Connection

Collagen is the structural protein that gives skin its firmness and resilience. Producing collagen requires fibroblast cells to synthesize collagen precursor proteins through ATP-intensive biochemical processes. ATP is generated primarily through mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation, a process in which NAD+ plays a central role as an electron carrier. Research suggests that as mitochondrial function declines with age and NAD+ availability decreases, fibroblast energy production becomes less efficient, directly limiting the cell's capacity to produce collagen at adequate rates. This creates a compounding effect: less NAD+ means less mitochondrial efficiency, which means less ATP, which means less collagen synthesis, which means accelerated skin structural aging.

A Skin Health Protocol for Women Over 40

According to Happy Aging's review of cellular skin aging research, the following protocol addresses both the surface and cellular dimensions of skin health in women over 40.

  1. Daily broad-spectrum SPF 30+ sunscreen. This is the single most evidence-supported intervention for slowing visible skin aging. It reduces daily UV-induced DNA damage load, preserving NAD+ for repair processes other than UV damage response.
  2. Oral NAD+ precursor supplementation. Support systemic NAD+ levels to maintain PARP repair capacity, sirtuin activity, and mitochondrial energy for collagen synthesis. Look for physician-reviewed formulas containing NMN or NR with validated dosing.
  3. Antioxidant-rich diet. Vitamins C and E, found in colorful vegetables, berries, and nuts, are cofactors in collagen synthesis and also provide direct antioxidant protection against UV-generated free radicals that damage skin cells.
  4. Topical vitamin C serum (L-ascorbic acid, 10-20%). Vitamin C is a cofactor for collagen synthesis enzymes and provides measurable photoprotective benefits as an adjunct to sunscreen. Apply in the morning under SPF.
  5. Retinol or prescription retinoid at night. Retinoids are the most evidence-supported topical ingredient for stimulating collagen production, accelerating cell turnover, and reducing the appearance of fine lines. Start with low-strength retinol if new to retinoids.
  6. Prioritize quality sleep. Growth hormone, released primarily during deep sleep, drives skin cell repair and renewal. Chronic sleep deprivation is associated with measurable skin aging acceleration. Target 7-9 hours per night.
  7. Stay well hydrated. Skin hydration affects barrier function and the appearance of fine lines. Aim for adequate total fluid intake throughout the day, and use a hyaluronic acid moisturizer to support surface hydration alongside internal hydration.

What the Evidence Doesn't Support

The evidence does not support any oral supplement eliminating wrinkles or reversing significant UV-induced skin damage already present. Cellular NAD+ support maintains and supports skin health processes, but it works at the level of prevention and maintenance, not cosmetic reversal of decades of accumulated damage. The evidence also does not support most "NAD+ facial" or direct topical NAD+ treatments as meaningfully improving skin NAD+ levels in the viable epidermis, due to the molecule's poor transdermal penetration. The beauty industry markets topical NAD+ aggressively, but the research on skin penetration does not yet support the claims being made. Additionally, the evidence does not support taking NAD+ precursors without also addressing the lifestyle foundations (sunscreen, diet, sleep, stress) that govern how effectively your skin can use the NAD+ support you provide. Supplements amplify a good foundation; they cannot compensate for chronic UV damage without sunscreen or poor sleep.

Who Should See a Dermatologist Before Relying on Supplements

Supplements support skin health at the cellular level but are not treatments for skin conditions. Women with melasma (a common hormonal pigmentation condition in women over 40), rosacea, a history of skin cancer, or any rapidly changing skin lesion should see a board-certified dermatologist before adding any new supplement or topical product to their routine. Skin conditions with a medical component require physician-directed care. NAD+ supplementation and the lifestyle protocol above are appropriate complements to, not substitutes for, medical skincare treatment when a dermatological condition is present.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can NAD+ supplementation improve skin appearance?

Research suggests NAD+ precursor supplementation supports the cellular processes involved in skin DNA repair, sirtuin regulation, and collagen synthesis energy. Some women report improvements in skin clarity, luminosity, and overall tone after several months of consistent oral NAD+ supplementation alongside a comprehensive skin protocol. However, peer-reviewed clinical trials specifically measuring cosmetic skin outcomes in women over 40 taking oral NAD+ precursors are limited as of 2026. The mechanistic basis for skin benefit is well-supported; the direct cosmetic evidence in clinical trials is still emerging.

How long does it take to see skin benefits from NAD+ supplementation?

Cellular changes driven by NAD+ occur at a level that precedes visible skin changes. Most people who notice benefits from oral NAD+ supplementation report changes appearing after 8-16 weeks of consistent use, with the most visible improvements related to energy, mental clarity, and skin luminosity. Significant structural improvements (collagen density, line depth) operate on a longer timeline and are influenced by the full lifestyle protocol, not supplementation alone.

Is NMN or NR better for skin health?

Both NMN (nicotinamide mononucleotide) and NR (nicotinamide riboside) are NAD+ precursors that effectively raise cellular NAD+ levels through different biochemical pathways. Research comparing NMN and NR specifically for skin outcomes in women is limited. The most important factor is that the product you choose contains a clinically relevant dose and is manufactured to pharmaceutical-grade quality standards. Look for physician-reviewed formulas with transparent dosing.

Does collagen supplementation work alongside NAD+ for skin?

Research suggests hydrolyzed collagen peptide supplementation supports skin hydration and collagen density when taken consistently. NAD+ supplementation and collagen peptides work through different but complementary mechanisms: NAD+ supports the cellular energy and DNA repair infrastructure that skin cells need to function well, while collagen peptides may directly supply substrates for collagen synthesis. They are not redundant and can reasonably be used together as part of a comprehensive skin aging protocol.

Can I use retinol and NAD+ supplementation at the same time?

Yes. Retinoids (topical, applied at night) and oral NAD+ supplementation work through entirely different mechanisms and do not interact. Retinoids work at the retinoic acid receptor level to stimulate collagen production and accelerate cell turnover. NAD+ supplementation works at the mitochondrial and DNA repair level. They are complementary tools in a skin aging protocol, not competing approaches.

Does sun exposure deplete NAD+ in skin cells?

Research suggests yes. UV radiation damages skin cell DNA, which activates PARP enzymes that consume NAD+ to power the repair process. Heavy or chronic sun exposure without sunscreen protection creates a higher PARP-driven NAD+ consumption burden in skin cells, contributing to the NAD+ decline that is already occurring with age. This is one mechanistic reason why consistent daily sunscreen use is foundational to any cellular skin health protocol.
