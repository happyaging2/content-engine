Phosphatidylserine vs lion's mane for memory after 40 is one of the most specific and useful comparisons to make if you are focused on protecting your cognitive health rather than just experimenting with brain supplements. Both have genuine clinical evidence. Both target memory and cognitive aging. But they work through completely different biological mechanisms, which means they are not competing with each other as alternatives. Understanding what each does differently is what allows you to make an intelligent decision about which one fits your situation, or whether using both makes sense.

What to Know

  • Phosphatidylserine (PS) is a structural brain phospholipid with decades of clinical trials showing improvements in memory and cognitive function in aging adults.
  • Lion's mane stimulates nerve growth factor (NGF), which promotes neuronal repair and growth, offering a regenerative mechanism not shared by PS.
  • For immediate memory support, phosphatidylserine has stronger short-term evidence. For long-term neuroprotection and neurogenesis, lion's mane is the more unique option.
  • Both are safe and compatible for combined use, and their mechanisms are genuinely complementary.
  • Neither is a substitute for sleep, exercise, stress management, and adequate DHA, which have more foundational impact on memory than either supplement.

What Is Phosphatidylserine and How Does It Work?

Phosphatidylserine is a phospholipid, a type of fat molecule that forms the structural backbone of cell membranes throughout the body. It is found in highest concentrations in the brain, where it makes up a significant portion of the inner leaflet of neuronal cell membranes.

In neurons, PS has several important functional roles. It maintains membrane fluidity, which determines how efficiently neurotransmitter receptors move and respond to signals at synapses. It supports the calcium-dependent neurotransmitter release events that allow neurons to communicate. It interacts with key signaling proteins involved in neuronal survival and synaptic plasticity, including protein kinase C.

After 40, and particularly after menopause, PS levels in the brain decline. This is part of the broader pattern of membrane phospholipid loss that accompanies cellular aging. As PS levels fall, membrane dynamics become less fluid, neurotransmitter signaling becomes less efficient, and the synaptic plasticity that underlies learning and memory formation slows.

Supplementing with PS restores brain PS levels (PS crosses the blood-brain barrier effectively), which improves the membrane environment for the neurotransmitter signaling and synaptic plasticity that memory depends on. This is a maintenance and restoration mechanism, supporting what exists rather than building new connections.

What Is Lion's Mane and How Does It Work?

Hericium erinaceus (lion's mane) is a medicinal mushroom containing two classes of bioactive compounds: hericenones in the fruiting body and erinacines in the mycelium. Both compound classes stimulate the production of nerve growth factor (NGF) and brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) in neurons and supporting brain cells.

NGF is a neurotrophin: a protein that promotes the survival, maintenance, and growth of neurons. It plays a critical role in neuroplasticity, the brain's ability to form new connections and reorganize existing ones in response to experience. NGF production declines measurably with age, and low NGF activity is associated with faster cognitive aging and increased risk of neurodegeneration.

The erinacines in lion's mane mycelium are particularly remarkable because they are small enough to cross the blood-brain barrier and stimulate NGF production directly within neurons, not just in peripheral tissues. This means lion's mane can influence neuronal growth and repair processes from the inside of the blood-brain barrier, a capability that most supplements do not possess.

Where phosphatidylserine maintains and restores the existing membrane environment of neurons, lion's mane stimulates the neurotrophin pathways that promote neuronal health and the formation of new synaptic connections. These are genuinely different biological actions operating at different levels of brain physiology.

Clinical Evidence Comparison

Both compounds have controlled human clinical trials, but the depth and nature of the evidence differs.

Phosphatidylserine has the longer research record, with double-blind, placebo-controlled trials published starting in the 1980s and early 1990s. A landmark trial by Crook and colleagues published in Neurology in 1991 found that PS supplementation at 300 mg per day for twelve weeks significantly improved memory performance in adults with age-associated memory impairment compared to placebo, with the greatest benefits in participants with more significant baseline memory decline. Multiple subsequent trials have replicated improvements in verbal memory, recall, and cognitive flexibility. This body of evidence was sufficient for the FDA to allow a qualified health claim for PS and cognitive function in 2003.

Lion's mane has fewer published human trials but the existing ones are compelling. The 2009 trial by Mori and colleagues published in Phytotherapy Research found that lion's mane supplementation for sixteen weeks significantly improved cognitive function scale scores in older adults with mild cognitive impairment compared to placebo. A 2020 trial using a standardized lion's mane extract for twelve weeks found similar cognitive improvements in adults aged 50 to 80. What lion's mane lacks is the volume and duration of PS research, but its mechanistic uniqueness (NGF stimulation) means the trials that exist support a genuinely different and potentially more profound long-term impact.

The overall assessment: phosphatidylserine has stronger short-to-medium-term memory support evidence. Lion's mane has a more compelling long-term neuroprotective rationale through NGF and BDNF stimulation, though this is still being built in human trials.

Which Works Better for Women Over 40 Specifically?

The memory challenges that women commonly experience in their 40s and early 50s tend to cluster around specific patterns: word retrieval difficulty, slower processing speed, difficulty concentrating, and reduced ability to hold multiple pieces of information in mind simultaneously (working memory). These patterns are connected to both the membrane dynamics that phosphatidylserine supports and the neurotrophin signaling that lion's mane supports.

For the acute memory and concentration difficulties associated with perimenopause and early menopause, phosphatidylserine's evidence for improving verbal memory and cognitive processing speed is most directly relevant. Its mechanism (improving membrane efficiency for neurotransmitter signaling) maps directly onto the synaptic communication deficits that underlie processing speed and working memory challenges.

For women who are thinking about long-term brain health protection over decades, lion's mane's NGF-stimulating mechanism is compelling for reasons that go beyond the current clinical trial evidence. NGF is protective against neurodegeneration, and stimulating NGF production continuously over years may reduce long-term neuronal loss more than membrane maintenance alone. This is speculative at the scale of decades, but mechanistically well-grounded.

Women who want both short-term cognitive support and long-term neuroprotective strategy have a good case for using both together. They have no known adverse interactions, their mechanisms do not overlap, and the combination addresses brain health from two genuinely distinct angles simultaneously.

Practical Dosing and Use

For phosphatidylserine, the dose used in the clinical trials showing memory improvements is 100 mg three times per day (300 mg total per day). This dose can be taken with or without food, though taking it with a fat-containing meal may improve absorption since PS is a fat-soluble phospholipid. Results appear in clinical trials at eight to twelve weeks of consistent use.

For lion's mane, the effective dose in clinical trials ranges from 750 mg to 3,000 mg of dried mushroom powder per day, or lower amounts of a concentrated, standardized extract. If using a full-spectrum dual-extract (fruiting body and mycelium), look for a product standardized to at least 20 to 30 percent beta-glucans, and follow the product's dosing instructions based on its extraction ratio. Results in clinical trials appear at twelve to sixteen weeks.

Both can be taken in the morning with breakfast. PS can also be taken in the evening, as it may support cortisol regulation (some research suggests PS reduces cortisol response to stress, which could be beneficial for sleep quality in high-stress women over 40).

The key principle is consistency. Neither compound provides noticeable acute effects like caffeine. Their benefits accumulate through sustained biological processes: membrane restoration for PS, neurotrophin stimulation for lion's mane. The commitment is measured in months, not days.

What Supplements Cannot Replace

Both phosphatidylserine and lion's mane are worth taking seriously, but it is important to contextualize them against the lifestyle factors that have the largest measurable impact on brain aging after 40.

Sleep is the non-negotiable. During sleep, the glymphatic system (the brain's waste clearance system) removes metabolic debris including amyloid-beta and tau proteins. Women who consistently get fewer than seven hours of quality sleep accumulate more of these proteins, which are associated with neurodegenerative risk. No supplement compensates for chronic sleep deprivation.

Cardiovascular exercise is the most powerful known intervention for neurogenesis (the creation of new neurons in the hippocampus, the brain's memory center) and for increasing BDNF levels. Regular aerobic exercise produces BDNF elevations that rival what lion's mane produces through NGF, through a separate and equally powerful pathway.

Omega-3 DHA remains the foundational brain supplement because it is literally what brain cell membranes are made of. Without adequate DHA, neither PS supplementation nor lion's mane can work in an optimally functioning membrane environment.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I take phosphatidylserine and lion's mane together?

Yes. They have no known adverse interactions and work through completely different mechanisms. Phosphatidylserine supports membrane function and neurotransmitter signaling. Lion's mane stimulates NGF and BDNF production. Together they address brain health from two distinct biological angles and are commonly combined in cognitive health formulas.

Which one should I start with if I can only afford one?

If your primary concern is current memory and concentration after 40, phosphatidylserine has the stronger and longer evidence base for those specific outcomes. If you are more focused on long-term neuroprotection and have a family history of cognitive decline, lion's mane's NGF-stimulating mechanism makes it the more differentiated and potentially more important choice.

Does phosphatidylserine help with cortisol and stress?

Yes, this is a specific and well-documented benefit of PS. Research has found that PS supplementation at 300 to 400 mg per day significantly blunts the cortisol response to stress in healthy adults. For perimenopausal women who notice heightened stress sensitivity and anxiety, this cortisol-modulating effect may provide additional benefit beyond direct memory support.

How long does it take lion's mane to improve memory after 40?

Clinical trials show cognitive improvements at twelve to sixteen weeks of consistent daily use. Anecdotal reports of improved focus and mental clarity appear more quickly in some women (two to four weeks), which may reflect anti-inflammatory effects that improve cognitive function before full NGF-driven neuronal changes take hold.

Are there any women who should avoid these supplements?

Phosphatidylserine is generally well-tolerated. At doses above 300 mg per day, mild digestive upset has been reported in some people. Lion's mane is also well-tolerated; rare cases of allergic reaction have been reported in individuals with mushroom allergies. If you take blood thinners, consult your physician before adding any new supplements, including these.
