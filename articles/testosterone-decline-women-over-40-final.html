Testosterone decline in women over 40 is one of the most overlooked aspects of midlife hormonal health, and it is frequently dismissed, both by women themselves and by healthcare providers who associate testosterone primarily with men's health. The reality is that women produce testosterone throughout their lives, depend on it for energy, libido, muscle strength, bone density, mood, and cognitive function, and experience measurable and impactful declines in testosterone production that begin in their 30s and continue through the menopausal transition. Understanding what testosterone does for women and what its decline means is the first step toward addressing the symptoms that many women accept as inevitable aging.

What to Know
  • Women produce testosterone in the ovaries and adrenal glands. Levels peak in the mid-20s and decline by approximately 50 percent by the time a woman reaches menopause.
  • Testosterone in women supports libido, energy, muscle strength, bone density, mood stability, and cognitive function, making its decline impactful across multiple domains of wellbeing.
  • Unlike estrogen, which drops dramatically at menopause, testosterone declines more gradually throughout the 30s and 40s, making its effects easier to overlook as simply "getting older."
  • Symptoms of low testosterone in women including fatigue, reduced libido, muscle weakness, mood changes, and brain fog overlap significantly with low estrogen symptoms, which is why testosterone is frequently missed in the evaluation of perimenopausal complaints.
  • Natural lifestyle strategies including resistance training, stress reduction, adequate sleep, and certain nutritional interventions support testosterone production and sensitivity in women over 40.
  • Women with significant symptoms of testosterone deficiency can discuss testosterone replacement therapy with their healthcare provider, as it is approved for certain indications and has an established safety profile in women at physiologically appropriate doses.

Women Need Testosterone Too: Understanding Its Role

The cultural association of testosterone with masculinity has created a significant knowledge gap in women's health. Women's testosterone is present in concentrations roughly 10 to 15 times lower than in men, but it is produced throughout a woman's life and serves essential functions that go far beyond the popular association with male characteristics.



Testosterone in women is primarily responsible for maintaining libido and sexual responsiveness. Research consistently confirms that testosterone is the hormone most directly linked to sexual desire in women, more so than estrogen. Women with low testosterone frequently report a specific loss of spontaneous sexual desire and reduced pleasure from sexual activity that is distinct from the lubrication and comfort issues associated with estrogen decline.



Testosterone supports muscle protein synthesis and muscle strength. It acts on androgen receptors in muscle cells to promote the anabolic processes that maintain and build muscle tissue. Women with adequate testosterone maintain muscle mass more effectively than those with deficient levels, and this has downstream effects on metabolic rate, insulin sensitivity, and bone density through the mechanical loading that functional muscles provide.



Bone density is supported by testosterone independently of estrogen. Androgen receptors are present in osteoblasts (bone-building cells), and testosterone directly stimulates bone formation through these receptors. Women with confirmed testosterone deficiency have been found in research to have lower bone mineral density independent of estrogen levels, suggesting testosterone's bone-protective role is not fully replaced by estrogen even when estrogen levels are adequate.



Mood, energy, and motivation are significantly influenced by testosterone through its effects on dopamine signaling in the brain. Women with low testosterone frequently describe a loss of drive and ambition, a muted sense of pleasure in activities they previously found rewarding, and a general flatness of emotional tone that is qualitatively distinct from depression but often shares features with it. Brain fog and reduced cognitive edge are also commonly attributed to testosterone deficiency in the clinical literature on women's hormonal health.

How and When Women's Testosterone Declines

Women's testosterone production comes from two primary sources: the ovaries and the adrenal glands. Each contributes approximately half of baseline testosterone in premenopausal women, with the ovaries' contribution rising around ovulation and falling at other times of the cycle. The adrenal glands produce dehydroepiandrosterone (DHEA), which is a precursor that converts to testosterone in peripheral tissues.



Peak testosterone levels in women occur in the mid-20s. By the time a woman is 40 years old, her testosterone levels have already fallen to approximately half of their peak values, a decline that has been occurring gradually and continuously throughout her 30s. A landmark paper by Davison and colleagues published in the Journal of Clinical Endocrinology and Metabolism found that free testosterone in women declines by approximately 1.2 percent per year throughout reproductive life, with no dramatic drop at menopause but a continuing gradual decline that accelerates somewhat in the perimenopause years as ovarian function begins to falter.



At menopause, the ovaries dramatically reduce estrogen and progesterone production but continue to produce some testosterone through androstenedione conversion. Post-menopausal ovaries actually contribute approximately 40 percent of a woman's total testosterone through this conversion. However, the total amount produced is still substantially lower than pre-menopausal levels, and surgical menopause (removal of ovaries) produces an immediate and dramatic testosterone drop that causes more acute symptom onset than natural menopause.



The adrenal contribution through DHEA also declines with age, through a process called adrenopause. DHEA peaks in the mid-20s and falls by approximately 10 to 15 percent per decade thereafter. Since DHEA is the main building block for testosterone in post-menopausal women (when ovarian androgen production has declined significantly), adrenal DHEA decline compounds the ovarian testosterone reduction, creating a double deficit in testosterone precursor supply.

Symptoms of Low Testosterone in Women Over 40

Recognizing the specific symptoms associated with testosterone decline in women is important because they overlap with the symptoms of estrogen decline, making it easy to attribute everything to estrogen when testosterone is a significant independent contributor.



Reduced sexual desire is the most directly testosterone-specific symptom. While vaginal dryness and physical discomfort during sex are primarily estrogen-driven, the reduction in spontaneous desire, the loss of erotic thinking, and the reduction in sexual responsiveness that many women in their 40s describe are more specifically associated with testosterone decline than with estrogen deficiency.



Fatigue that is particularly pronounced in the early morning and that does not fully resolve with adequate sleep may reflect testosterone deficiency rather than estrogen-related fatigue, which more commonly presents as the afternoon energy crash pattern. This is because testosterone's role in cellular energy production and motivation is distinct from the circadian rhythm effects of estrogen.



Muscle weakness and the effort required to maintain muscle tone despite regular exercise is another testosterone-specific complaint. Women who were previously able to maintain muscle easily with moderate exercise often notice that the same effort produces less muscle maintenance in their 40s. This reflects both testosterone's anabolic role in muscle tissue and the synergy between testosterone and growth hormone that supports muscle protein synthesis.



Mood flattening, reduced motivation, and loss of the competitive or achievement drive that previously felt energizing are commonly reported in the clinical context of women's testosterone deficiency. These symptoms are qualitatively different from the irritability and anxiety of estrogen fluctuations and from the sadness of clinical depression, representing instead a muted, low-drive quality of mood that testosterone specifically supports.

Natural Strategies to Support Testosterone After 40

Several evidence-backed lifestyle and nutritional strategies support testosterone production and sensitivity in women over 40, working through different mechanisms to address the hormonal and metabolic context of testosterone decline.



Resistance training is the most potent natural testosterone-supporting intervention. Multiple studies confirm that resistance exercise acutely raises testosterone in women through a hormonal cascade involving growth hormone, IGF-1, and androgen production that is stimulated by the mechanical stress of resistance training. Regular resistance training also increases androgen receptor density in muscle tissue, meaning the testosterone that is present has greater functional impact even without raising absolute levels. Two to three sessions per week of compound resistance exercises (squats, deadlifts, pressing movements) provides the stimulus most linked to testosterone-relevant adaptations.



Sleep quality directly affects testosterone production. Growth hormone, which works synergistically with testosterone for muscle maintenance and tissue repair, is primarily released during deep slow-wave sleep. Testosterone production in women also follows a partial circadian pattern. Research confirms that sleep restriction reduces both growth hormone and testosterone in women, making sleep quality a hormone-management strategy rather than simply a comfort issue.



Stress management reduces cortisol, which has an inversely competitive relationship with testosterone. Chronically elevated cortisol suppresses the HPA axis signals that drive DHEA and testosterone production in the adrenal glands. This cortisol-testosterone antagonism is one reason why women who carry high chronic stress loads often have more pronounced testosterone deficiency symptoms. Reducing cortisol through mind-body practices, boundary-setting, adequate leisure time, and adaptogenic herbs directly supports testosterone production capacity.

Nutritional Support for Testosterone in Women Over 40

Specific nutrients are required for testosterone synthesis and receptor function. Ensuring adequate intake of these nutrients is a basic but meaningful foundation for testosterone optimization in women over 40.



Zinc is required for testosterone synthesis and for the function of androgen receptors. Zinc deficiency is associated with lower testosterone in both men and women, and zinc supplementation has been shown to raise testosterone in zinc-deficient individuals. Oysters, red meat, pumpkin seeds, and legumes are dietary sources. Women over 40 who are predominantly plant-based may be at greater risk for zinc insufficiency due to the lower bioavailability of zinc from plant foods compared to animal sources.



Magnesium is involved in testosterone metabolism and supports the production of sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG) at appropriate levels. High SHBG binds testosterone and makes it unavailable at tissue level, even when total testosterone appears normal on standard testing. Magnesium helps maintain the free testosterone fraction that is biologically active. A 2011 study by Cinar and colleagues found that magnesium supplementation significantly increased both free and total testosterone in sedentary and active subjects. DOI: 10.1016/j.biopha.2010.11.005.



DHEA supplementation is the most direct nutritional approach to testosterone support in post-menopausal women, because DHEA is the primary testosterone precursor in this life stage. DHEA converts to testosterone in peripheral tissues, and supplemental DHEA at doses of 25 to 50 mg daily has been shown to raise testosterone and DHEA levels in post-menopausal women with documented DHEA deficiency. Research by Davis and colleagues published in the Lancet Diabetes and Endocrinology specifically evaluated testosterone's role in women's health, confirming that testosterone therapy is appropriate for certain indications including female sexual dysfunction. DOI: 10.1016/S2213-8587(15)00284-3.



NMN (nicotinamide mononucleotide) and NAD+ precursor supplementation may support testosterone production indirectly by improving cellular energy metabolism and mitochondrial function in the adrenal and ovarian cells that produce testosterone. Research suggests that NAD+ supports the enzymatic reactions involved in steroid hormone synthesis, and that declining NAD+ in these tissues may contribute to reduced hormone production capacity with age.

When to Talk to Your Doctor About Testosterone

If natural strategies are not sufficient to address symptoms of testosterone deficiency, women have the option of discussing testosterone replacement therapy with their healthcare provider. Testosterone therapy is currently not FDA-approved specifically for women's indications in the US, but it is widely used off-label based on a body of clinical evidence and international guidelines from professional endocrinology and sexual medicine societies.



The most accepted indication for testosterone therapy in women is hypoactive sexual desire disorder (HSDD) with documented low testosterone levels. Research supports that physiological doses of testosterone, restoring levels to the normal pre-menopausal range rather than the supraphysiological levels associated with male replacement therapy, produce meaningful improvements in sexual desire, arousal, and satisfaction with an acceptable safety profile in postmenopausal women. An International Society for the Study of Women's Sexual Health position statement (2019) supports the use of testosterone therapy for HSDD in postmenopausal women.



Before seeking testosterone therapy, having blood tests that include total testosterone, free testosterone, and SHBG provides the information needed to establish whether your levels are below the normal range and whether SHBG levels are artificially reducing the bioavailable free testosterone fraction. These tests are not always ordered in standard annual blood panels and may need to be specifically requested.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the signs of low testosterone in women?

The most common signs include: reduced or absent sexual desire (particularly spontaneous desire); persistent fatigue despite adequate sleep; difficulty maintaining muscle tone despite exercise; mood flattening, low motivation, or emotional muting; brain fog or reduced cognitive sharpness; and thinning of pubic and axillary hair. These symptoms are often attributed to estrogen decline or general aging, but testosterone deficiency is a meaningful and addressable contributor.

Can I test my testosterone levels at home?

Yes. Several lab services now offer at-home hormone testing panels that include total testosterone, free testosterone, and SHBG. These provide a useful baseline but should ideally be interpreted with a healthcare provider who can contextualize them within the broader hormonal picture of your health history and symptoms.

Will testosterone supplementation cause masculinization in women?

Not at physiological doses designed for women. Testosterone therapy for women uses doses that restore levels to the natural pre-menopausal female range, which is 10 to 15 times lower than men's normal range. Acne and increased body hair are possible side effects of higher doses, but they are uncommon at the low doses appropriate for women. The masculinizing effects associated with testosterone are dose-dependent and do not occur at the amounts used in appropriately managed female hormone replacement.

Does low testosterone cause weight gain in women?

Indirectly, yes. Testosterone supports muscle mass, and muscle is the primary driver of resting metabolic rate. Women with low testosterone have less metabolically active muscle mass, which means lower caloric expenditure at rest and a greater tendency for body fat accumulation relative to muscle. This is not a direct fat-gain effect but a metabolic consequence of the muscle loss that testosterone deficiency accelerates.

Is DHEA a safe way to raise testosterone naturally?

DHEA supplements at doses of 25 to 50 mg daily have a reasonable safety record in postmenopausal women and are effective at raising DHEA and testosterone levels. However, DHEA also converts to estrogen in some tissues, so women with hormone-sensitive conditions should discuss its use with their healthcare provider. A blood test confirming low DHEA levels is useful context before beginning supplementation.
