DHEA (dehydroepiandrosterone) is the most abundant steroid hormone in the human body, and it declines more dramatically with age than almost any other hormone. At its peak in early adulthood, DHEA is produced in large quantities by the adrenal glands. By age 70, levels have fallen to roughly 10 to 20 percent of their youthful peak. This steep, consistent decline has made DHEA one of the most intensively studied compounds in longevity research, with hundreds of clinical trials examining whether restoring DHEA levels can counter aspects of aging. For women over 40, who face concurrent declines in estrogen and progesterone alongside DHEA, the question of what the research actually shows deserves a clear-eyed answer.

What to Know

  • DHEA is the precursor to both estrogen and testosterone and declines by 80 to 90 percent between ages 25 and 75 in most women
  • The adrenal glands produce DHEA regardless of ovarian function, meaning DHEA decline continues through and after menopause independently of estrogen loss
  • Clinical evidence for DHEA supports bone density preservation, libido improvement, and vaginal tissue health; evidence for mood and cognition is mixed
  • Low-dose DHEA (10 to 25mg) is generally safer for women than higher doses used in older research; very high doses can cause androgenic side effects
  • Vaginal DHEA (intravaginal prasterone) is FDA-approved for treating vaginal atrophy in postmenopausal women, representing the strongest regulatory endorsement for DHEA in women

What DHEA Does in the Body After 40

DHEA and its sulfate form (DHEA-S) are produced primarily in the adrenal cortex and converted downstream into sex hormones including estradiol, estrone, and testosterone. In peripheral tissues, this local conversion allows organs to produce their own sex hormones from DHEA without depending solely on ovarian or adrenal production of the end hormones directly.

This peripheral conversion becomes increasingly important after 40 and especially after menopause. When ovarian estrogen production drops sharply at menopause, DHEA-derived estrogen in peripheral tissues, including skin, bone, brain, and vaginal tissue, continues to contribute to local hormone signaling. The relative contribution of adrenal DHEA to total estrogen and androgen signaling increases significantly in postmenopausal women compared to premenopausal women, making adrenal DHEA production more functionally important as ovarian function declines.

Beyond being a hormone precursor, DHEA has direct receptor-level effects. It binds to GABA-A receptors (with mild anxiolytic effects), sigma-1 receptors (involved in neuronal function), and androgen receptors directly. These non-precursor effects mean that DHEA is not simply a "bank" of sex hormones but an active signaling molecule in its own right.

The Research on DHEA and Bone Health

The evidence for DHEA and bone health is among the strongest in human trials. Multiple randomized controlled trials have shown that DHEA supplementation at doses of 50mg per day increases bone mineral density in postmenopausal women, with the most robust effects seen in the spine and hip, the sites of greatest fracture risk.

A two-year randomized trial by Baulieu and colleagues (the DHEAge Study) involving 280 men and women aged 60 to 79 found that DHEA supplementation produced significant increases in bone mineral density in women, particularly at the femoral neck. Bone formation markers increased while bone resorption markers decreased, suggesting the effect was mediated through direct anabolic actions on bone rather than merely slowing resorption.

For women over 40 who are concerned about the trajectory toward osteoporosis, this evidence is meaningful. The effect size is smaller than that seen with pharmaceutical bisphosphonates, but DHEA's bone effects come with a broader hormonal and tissue benefit profile that bone-specific medications do not provide.

DHEA and Libido: What the Evidence Shows

DHEA's effect on libido in women is one of the most consistently supported findings in the clinical literature. Multiple trials have found that DHEA supplementation improves sexual desire, arousal, and satisfaction in postmenopausal women, with the effect appearing to be mediated primarily through conversion to testosterone and estrogen in genital tissues.

Intravaginal DHEA (prasterone, sold as Intrarosa) is FDA-approved specifically for treating dyspareunia (painful sex) and vaginal atrophy in postmenopausal women, representing the highest level of regulatory endorsement for any DHEA application. The approval was based on trials showing significant improvements in vaginal tissue health, lubrication, and sexual comfort with minimal systemic hormone exposure, since vaginal DHEA is largely converted locally rather than entering the bloodstream in significant quantities.

Oral DHEA at doses of 10 to 50mg has also shown libido improvements in trials, though effect sizes are more variable than with intravaginal delivery. Women who want systemic DHEA benefits (bone, mood, energy) alongside vaginal tissue effects may find oral supplementation the more practical route if they are working with a physician who can monitor levels.

DHEA and Mood, Cognition, and Energy: A Nuanced Picture

The evidence for DHEA's effects on mood and cognition is less consistent than for bone and libido, which reflects the complexity of these outcomes and the variability in study design.

Some trials have found that DHEA supplementation improves mood and reduces depression symptoms in people with documented adrenal insufficiency, where DHEA levels are severely depleted. In people with age-related DHEA decline (as opposed to pathological adrenal suppression), the mood effects are more modest. A systematic review of DHEA and depression found positive effects in studies using higher doses and in participants with clinically low baseline DHEA levels, but not consistently in normal aging populations.

For cognition, some observational studies have found associations between higher DHEA-S levels and better memory performance in older adults, and animal studies suggest neuroprotective effects through sigma-1 receptor activation. However, randomized trials in cognitively intact older adults have not consistently shown cognitive improvements from DHEA supplementation, suggesting the cognitive effects may be more relevant to people with clinically low levels rather than to typical age-related decline.

Energy and sense of well-being are frequently reported to improve in clinical trials at doses around 25 to 50mg, and this remains one of the most commonly cited reasons women in their 40s and 50s use DHEA. The subjective effect is real for many women, though it is difficult to isolate from placebo effects in self-reported measures.

Dosing, Safety, and What to Watch For

Standard DHEA doses used in research range from 10mg to 200mg per day, but the doses used in older studies (100 to 200mg) were substantially higher than what is now considered appropriate for women. At high doses, DHEA can cause androgenic side effects in women, including acne, oily skin, facial hair growth, and hair thinning, because excess DHEA can convert to testosterone above physiological ranges.

Current thinking among hormone specialists favors lower doses for women, typically 10 to 25mg per day, adjusted based on measured DHEA-S blood levels. This dose range has shown meaningful benefits for bone, libido, and well-being without androgenic side effects in most trials.

DHEA should not be used without baseline and follow-up testing of DHEA-S levels, because response is highly individual and depends on existing levels and the activity of conversion enzymes in peripheral tissues. Women with hormone-sensitive cancers (breast, ovarian, uterine) should not use DHEA without explicit guidance from their oncologist, because its conversion to estrogen and testosterone is a theoretical concern even without evidence of harm in healthy aging women.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should women over 40 supplement with DHEA?

It depends on measured DHEA-S levels and symptoms. DHEA supplementation makes the most sense for women with documented low DHEA-S levels and symptoms such as low libido, vaginal dryness, fatigue, or declining bone density. It is not appropriate as a blanket supplement for all women over 40. Testing DHEA-S levels before starting allows you to establish a baseline, choose an appropriate dose, and track whether levels normalize. Working with a functional medicine physician or gynecologist who specializes in hormonal health is the safest approach.

Is DHEA the same as estrogen or testosterone?

No. DHEA is a precursor hormone, meaning the body converts it into estrogen and testosterone rather than DHEA itself acting as either. The conversion occurs in peripheral tissues including skin, bone, brain, and vaginal tissue, where local enzymatic activity determines how much DHEA becomes estrogen versus testosterone. This peripheral conversion is why DHEA has different effects in different women, depending on which conversion enzymes they express more actively.

Does DHEA help with vaginal dryness after menopause?

Yes, particularly when delivered intravaginally. The FDA-approved prescription form of vaginal DHEA (prasterone/Intrarosa) is specifically indicated for vaginal atrophy and painful sex in postmenopausal women. It works by converting locally to estrogen and testosterone within vaginal tissue, improving lubrication, tissue integrity, and sexual comfort with minimal systemic hormone exposure. Oral DHEA also improves vaginal symptoms in many trials but with more systemic hormonal effects.

What are the side effects of DHEA in women?

At doses of 10 to 25mg, most women experience no significant side effects. At higher doses (50mg and above), the most common side effects are androgenic: acne, oily skin, and occasionally increased facial hair growth. These are dose-dependent and usually resolve with dose reduction. Heart palpitations, irritability, and sleep disturbance have been reported less commonly. Any woman experiencing these effects should reduce her dose and consult her physician. Regular DHEA-S monitoring prevents over-supplementation.

How long does it take for DHEA to work?

Bone density effects require at least six months to one year to measure reliably by DEXA scan. Libido and mood improvements are often reported within 4 to 8 weeks in clinical trials. Vaginal tissue improvements with intravaginal DHEA are typically noted within 4 to 12 weeks. Energy and sense of well-being are among the earliest-reported effects, sometimes noticed within 2 to 4 weeks, though this is subject to placebo effects and high individual variability.

Should women with breast cancer history avoid DHEA?

This requires careful individualized medical assessment. DHEA converts to estrogens in peripheral tissues, and estrogen exposure is a concern for women with estrogen receptor-positive breast cancer history. Most oncologists advise against DHEA supplementation in women with ER-positive breast cancer history without specialist guidance. However, the picture is more nuanced than a blanket prohibition: intravaginal DHEA (prasterone) has been studied in cancer survivors specifically because its local conversion minimizes systemic estrogen exposure, and some cancer survivorship guidelines have begun to address this more specifically. Women with hormone-sensitive cancer history who are experiencing vaginal atrophy or other DHEA-addressable symptoms should discuss their options with both their oncologist and a gynecologist familiar with post-cancer hormonal management rather than self-supplementing.
