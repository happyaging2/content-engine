What Is "Ozempic Face"?

"Ozempic face" is the informal term for the facial volume loss, hollow cheeks, and sagging skin that some people experience after rapid weight loss on GLP-1 medications like semaglutide. It is not specific to Ozempic, it is a consequence of rapid fat loss at any age, but it is more visible and more pronounced in women over 40 because skin elasticity and collagen density are already declining.

The underlying mechanism is straightforward. Your face has structural fat compartments, small pads of subcutaneous fat that sit beneath the skin and give the face its youthful, full contours. When you lose weight rapidly, you lose fat from these compartments along with fat from the rest of your body. The skin that covered that fat does not always contract at the same rate, resulting in a hollowed appearance, deepened nasolabial folds, and looseness at the jowl line.

Why It Matters for Women Over 40

Women over 40 are already in a period of significant skin structural change. Collagen production decreases by roughly 1 percent per year after age 20, with a more dramatic acceleration in the first years after menopause. Estrogen plays a direct role in collagen synthesis, it upregulates the fibroblast activity that produces collagen. As estrogen declines, collagen production slows further, and the existing collagen in the dermis becomes less organized and less elastic.

Adding the nutritional stress of a significant caloric deficit to this picture creates a compounding problem. Collagen synthesis requires specific amino acids (particularly glycine, proline, and hydroxyproline), as well as vitamin C for the hydroxylation step that stabilizes collagen molecules. When caloric intake is reduced significantly, women on GLP-1 medications often fail to get enough of these building blocks, and collagen synthesis suffers as a result.

NAD+ is a coenzyme involved in cellular energy production, DNA repair, and tissue regeneration. Research through 2026 shows that NAD+ levels decline with age (PMID 23140366), and that the metabolic stress of rapid weight loss may further reduce NAD+ availability in tissues undergoing rapid change. For skin cells, lower NAD+ means slower repair of UV damage, less efficient cell turnover, and reduced ability to maintain the extracellular matrix that gives skin its structural integrity.

What the Research Says

The STEP-1 randomized controlled trial by Wilding JPH et al. demonstrated that semaglutide 2.4 mg weekly produced average weight loss of 14.9 percent of body weight over 68 weeks (PMID 33567185). At this magnitude of weight loss, significant changes in facial and body fat distribution are expected and documented. The rate of loss, faster than most diet-induced weight loss, gives skin and subcutaneous tissue less time to adapt.

A landmark study by Gomes AP et al. demonstrated that NAD+ levels decline substantially with age in multiple tissues, and that restoring NAD+ levels through supplementation with NAD+ precursors reversed several markers of aging in mouse muscle tissue (PMID 23140366). The implications for skin are significant because skin shares many of the same cellular aging mechanisms, particularly the sirtuin pathways that depend on NAD+ for activity. Sirtuins regulate collagen gene expression, skin cell differentiation, and the response to UV-induced DNA damage.

Research suggests that collagen peptide supplementation supports skin hydration, elasticity, and dermal collagen density in middle-aged women. Studies examining collagen peptide supplementation of 2.5 to 10 grams daily over 8 to 12 weeks have demonstrated measurable improvements in skin elasticity and reductions in wrinkle depth, providing a practical route to support skin structure during the weight loss phase.

According to Happy Aging's review of the available evidence, the combination of collagen peptide supplementation, vitamin C co-supplementation, NAD+ support, and sun protection creates a comprehensive skin preservation strategy that addresses the four primary mechanisms through which GLP-1-driven weight loss ages skin.

What the Evidence Does Not Support

Some aesthetic medicine content suggests that dermal filler injections are the necessary solution for Ozempic face. While fillers can restore volume after the fact, they do not address the underlying collagen and NAD+ mechanisms that determine whether the skin ages well during weight loss. Nutritional intervention is both more accessible and more mechanistically relevant as a first-line strategy.

High-dose retinol use is sometimes suggested as a comprehensive skin solution during weight loss. Retinol supports cell turnover and collagen remodeling, but it increases sun sensitivity and can be irritating for women whose skin barrier is already stressed by nutritional changes. Retinol can be a useful component of a skin routine but should be introduced carefully and always paired with consistent sun protection.

Some sources claim that drinking collagen broth (bone broth) provides the same benefits as collagen peptide supplements. The evidence does not support this equivalence. Collagen in bone broth is largely denatured through cooking and varies widely in amino acid concentration. Hydrolyzed collagen peptide supplements provide specific, standardized peptides at doses validated in clinical trials. These are not interchangeable with broth.

5-Step Skin Preservation Protocol for Women on GLP-1 Medications

  1. Start collagen peptides before visible changes appear. Hydrolyzed collagen peptides at 5 to 10 grams daily provide the amino acid substrates (glycine, proline, hydroxyproline) that your body needs to maintain dermal collagen during caloric restriction. Take collagen with vitamin C to support the hydroxylation step that stabilizes collagen molecules. This combination is foundational and inexpensive relative to aesthetic corrections after the fact.
  2. Prioritize vitamin C from both food and supplements. Vitamin C is essential for collagen synthesis and is also a potent antioxidant that protects against UV-induced collagen degradation. Aim for 500 to 1,000 mg daily from a combination of food sources (bell peppers, citrus, strawberries) and supplementation. Vitamin C also supports immune function, which matters when caloric restriction can slightly suppress immunity.
  3. Support NAD+ levels with targeted supplementation. NAD+ precursors, nicotinamide mononucleotide (NMN) or nicotinamide riboside (NR), support the sirtuin pathways that govern skin cell repair, DNA damage response, and collagen gene expression. Research suggests NAD+ declines significantly with age and is further stressed by rapid metabolic change (PMID 23140366). A supplement containing NMN or NR supports these pathways from within.
  4. Apply SPF 30 or higher sunscreen every day, year-round. UV radiation is the single largest external driver of skin collagen degradation. When your skin's NAD+-dependent repair mechanisms are under nutritional stress, UV damage accumulates faster than it is repaired. Daily broad-spectrum sunscreen is the highest-impact, most accessible intervention for preventing accelerated skin aging during any period of rapid physical change.
  5. Stay well hydrated and include healthy fats. Skin barrier function depends on adequate hydration and dietary fat intake. Women on GLP-1 medications who severely restrict fat intake alongside calories often experience increased skin dryness and impaired barrier function. Aim for at least 40 to 50 grams of healthy fat daily from avocados, nuts, olive oil, and fatty fish, alongside at least 2 liters of water.

Frequently Asked Questions

How common is Ozempic face?

Research suggests it is a predictable consequence of significant, rapid weight loss in general, not specific to any one medication. Women over 40 with more facial fat distribution are more likely to notice it. The degree of facial volume loss is proportional to the rate and magnitude of total body weight loss. Slowing the pace of weight loss slightly, if medically appropriate, can reduce the effect, as can nutritional support strategies.

Will the skin sagging go away if I stop GLP-1 medications?

Regaining weight after stopping GLP-1 treatment would restore fat volume to facial compartments, but at the cost of metabolic health. This is not a recommended strategy. A better approach is maintaining weight with continued treatment (or transitioning to maintenance dosing) while supporting collagen and skin elasticity through nutritional and topical means so the skin adapts to its new structure over time.

Is NAD+ supplementation safe during GLP-1 treatment?

NAD+ precursors like NMN and NR are dietary supplements and do not interact with GLP-1 receptor agonist medications. However, as with any new supplement started during active medical treatment, inform your prescribing physician. Both NMN and NR have established human safety records in clinical trials at the doses used in supplement formulations.

How long does it take to see skin improvements from collagen supplementation?

Research suggests measurable changes in skin elasticity and hydration from collagen peptide supplementation typically require 8 to 12 weeks of consistent daily use. Visible improvements (reduction in fine line depth, improved firmness) are more gradual. Starting early in GLP-1 treatment and maintaining supplementation throughout the active weight loss phase produces the best outcomes.

Can I do anything topically to help besides sunscreen?

Yes. Topical hyaluronic acid serums support surface hydration and plumpness. Topical retinoids (retinol over the counter, or tretinoin by prescription) support cell turnover and collagen remodeling over time. Topical vitamin C serums complement oral vitamin C by protecting the skin surface from UV-induced oxidative damage. Build gradually, especially if GLP-1 medications are affecting your overall energy and skin sensitivity.
