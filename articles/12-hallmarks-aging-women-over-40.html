What Are the Hallmarks of Aging?

The hallmarks of aging are a scientific framework for categorizing the biological processes that cause cells, tissues, and organs to deteriorate over time. First published in Cell in 2013 by Lopez-Otin and colleagues (PMID 23746838), the original framework identified 9 hallmarks. A decade later, the same team published an updated version adding 3 more, bringing the total to 12 (PMID 36599349).

These hallmarks are not independent. They form an interconnected network where decline in one area accelerates decline in others. Understanding them gives women over 40 a precise vocabulary for what is happening at the cellular level during midlife, and a map for where to intervene. Why the Hallmarks Matter for Women Over 40

The hallmarks of aging do not activate at the same pace for everyone. Women over 40 experience a unique convergence: the hormonal shifts of perimenopause, which begin years before the final menstrual period, interact with and accelerate several of the 12 hallmarks simultaneously. Estrogen loss affects mitochondrial function, gut microbiome composition, inflammation levels, and epigenetic patterns, all of which are represented in the hallmarks below.

This means the years from 40 to 55 are a particularly high-leverage window for intervention. Changes made now do not just affect current energy and mood. They influence how quickly cellular aging processes compound over the next several decades. As Lopez-Otin et al. wrote in the 2023 paper, the hallmarks interact with each other in ways that make early, broad-spectrum intervention more effective than targeting a single process late in the aging cascade.

What the Research Says

The 2023 hallmarks paper (PMID 36599349) represented a scientific consensus across hundreds of research groups. The addition of dysbiosis, chronic inflammation, and disabled macroautophagy as formal hallmarks reflected the weight of evidence that had accumulated since 2013, particularly for the role of the gut microbiome and inflammatory signaling in systemic aging.

NAD+ decline is one of the most cross-cutting factors in the hallmarks framework. Gomes and colleagues demonstrated in 2013 (PMID 23140366) that NAD+ levels decline significantly with age in multiple tissues, and that restoring NAD+ can reverse aspects of mitochondrial dysfunction in animal models. This finding has since been extended to show NAD+'s role in DNA repair (hallmark 1), epigenetic regulation (hallmark 3), autophagy (hallmark 5), nutrient sensing (hallmark 6), mitochondrial function (hallmark 7), and inflammatory signaling (hallmark 10).

According to Happy Aging's review of the longevity literature, no single compound addresses all 12 hallmarks. However, NAD+ precursors (like NMN and NR), combined with lifestyle factors that target autophagy, inflammation, and gut microbiome health, come closer to a broad-spectrum approach than most single-ingredient strategies.

What the Evidence Does Not Support

The hallmarks framework is powerful, but it has attracted significant overpromising from the supplement industry. Products claiming to "reverse aging" or "target all hallmarks" often do so based on animal data that has not been replicated in humans at the claimed doses. NAD+ precursors show real promise in human trials, but the magnitude of effects in healthy adults is more modest than headlines suggest.

The hallmarks framework also does not imply that aging is optional or fully controllable. Environmental exposures, genetics, and decades of prior lifestyle choices all influence how quickly the hallmarks progress. Interventions can slow the rate of decline and preserve healthspan, but they cannot stop aging entirely, and they cannot undo decades of cellular damage overnight.

Happy Aging's position: the 12 hallmarks are a legitimate scientific framework that women over 40 can use to make more targeted decisions about lifestyle, supplementation, and healthcare. They do not require perfection across all 12 dimensions. Making meaningful progress on 4 or 5 of the most relevant hallmarks through sustainable habits produces real and compounding benefit over time.

The 12 Hallmarks of Aging: A Guide for Women Over 40

1. Genomic Instability

What it is: DNA damage accumulates over time, and the cell's ability to repair that damage declines. This increases the risk of cellular dysfunction and malignant transformation.

What it feels like: increased fatigue, slower recovery from illness, higher cancer risk with age.

Strategy: antioxidant-rich diet (berries, leafy greens), sun protection, avoiding tobacco and excessive alcohol. NAD+ supports DNA repair enzymes called PARPs.

2. Telomere Attrition

What it is: telomeres are the protective caps at the ends of chromosomes. They shorten with each cell division, and when they become critically short, cells stop dividing or die.

What it feels like: accelerated skin aging, reduced tissue regeneration, lower resilience to cellular stress.

Strategy: aerobic exercise consistently shows the strongest association with telomere length maintenance in human studies. Chronic stress accelerates telomere shortening; stress reduction is non-negotiable.

3. Epigenetic Alterations

What it is: epigenetics refers to how genes are expressed without changing the DNA sequence itself. With age, the epigenetic patterns that regulate which genes are turned on or off become increasingly disordered.

What it feels like: reduced cellular specialization, altered inflammatory responses, metabolic changes that do not respond to diet the way they used to.

Strategy: NAD+ is required for sirtuin enzymes, which are central epigenetic regulators (PMID 23140366). Caloric moderation and intermittent fasting also support epigenetic health through sirtuin activation.

4. Loss of Proteostasis

What it is: the body has elaborate systems for folding proteins correctly and clearing damaged ones. With age, these quality-control systems decline, allowing misfolded proteins to accumulate, which is a feature of many neurodegenerative conditions.

What it feels like: cognitive decline, slower cellular adaptation, increased susceptibility to protein-aggregation disorders.

Strategy: exercise (particularly resistance training) stimulates protein quality control pathways. Adequate dietary protein and sleep support cellular repair and clearance.

5. Disabled Macroautophagy

What it is: autophagy is the cell's internal recycling system, breaking down damaged organelles and proteins to recover their components. This process becomes less efficient with age.

What it feels like: accumulation of cellular "debris," less efficient energy production, increased inflammation from uncleared cellular waste.

Strategy: intermittent fasting is one of the most studied autophagy activators in humans. NAD+ supports AMPK signaling, which activates autophagy. Caloric moderation consistently promotes autophagy across animal models.

6. Deregulated Nutrient Sensing

What it is: the body uses several systems (mTOR, AMPK, insulin-IGF-1 signaling, sirtuins) to sense available energy and nutrients and adjust cellular activity accordingly. With age, these sensors become less accurate, leading to chronic mTOR overactivation and AMPK underactivation.

What it feels like: insulin resistance, difficulty losing weight, energy fluctuations, metabolic syndrome risk.

Strategy: reducing ultra-processed carbohydrate load, consistent exercise, and NAD+ precursors all support better nutrient sensing. Sirtuin activation through NAD+ helps recalibrate these pathways (PMID 23140366).

7. Mitochondrial Dysfunction

What it is: mitochondria are the energy-producing structures in every cell. With age, they become less efficient, generate more oxidative stress, and trigger inflammatory signals.

What it feels like: fatigue that does not resolve with sleep, reduced exercise tolerance, brain fog, cold intolerance.

Strategy: NAD+ is a direct cofactor in mitochondrial energy production. Declining NAD+ levels are a primary driver of mitochondrial dysfunction with age (PMID 23140366). Zone 2 cardio exercise stimulates mitochondrial biogenesis.

8. Cellular Senescence

What it is: senescent cells are cells that have stopped dividing but have not died. They accumulate with age and secrete inflammatory signals that damage neighboring healthy cells, sometimes called the "zombie cell" problem.

What it feels like: chronic low-grade inflammation, joint stiffness, skin changes, increased disease risk across multiple organ systems.

Strategy: senolytics are compounds being studied for their ability to clear senescent cells. In the meantime, caloric moderation, exercise, and anti-inflammatory diets reduce the burden of senescence-related inflammation.

9. Stem Cell Exhaustion

What it is: stem cells are the body's regenerative reserve, replacing damaged cells throughout life. Their function and number decline with age, reducing the body's ability to repair and renew tissues.

What it feels like: slower healing of injuries, reduced muscle recovery after exercise, thinning hair and skin changes.

Strategy: NAD+ supports stem cell function in animal models. Resistance training and adequate protein intake preserve muscle stem cell (satellite cell) function, which is particularly important for women over 40.

10. Altered Intercellular Communication

What it is: cells communicate through chemical signals. With age, these signaling networks become increasingly inflammatory, with aged cells sending distress signals that accelerate aging in neighboring cells.

What it feels like: systemic inflammation, increased allergy sensitivity, mood changes, hormonal signaling disruption.

Strategy: NAD+ and sirtuin activation reduce inflammatory signaling at the cellular level. Omega-3 fatty acids and polyphenol-rich foods also modulate intercellular inflammatory signaling.

11. Chronic Inflammation (Inflammaging)

What it is: unlike the acute inflammation that resolves an injury, inflammaging is a persistent, low-grade inflammatory state that accumulates with age. It drives multiple diseases including cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and neurodegeneration.

What it feels like: persistent low energy, aches and pains without clear cause, elevated inflammatory markers on blood tests, mood disruption.

Strategy: anti-inflammatory diet (Mediterranean pattern), consistent exercise, adequate sleep, stress reduction, and gut microbiome diversity all reduce inflammaging. This hallmark is directly connected to hallmark 12 below.

12. Dysbiosis

What it is: dysbiosis refers to imbalance in the gut microbiome, specifically a loss of diversity and protective bacterial species alongside an increase in inflammatory ones. This was added as a formal hallmark of aging in the 2023 Lopez-Otin paper (PMID 36599349).

What it feels like: bloating, digestive irregularity, reduced immune resilience, mood disruption, increased systemic inflammation, worsening of other hallmarks through the gut-immune axis.

Strategy: dietary fiber diversity, fermented foods, and synbiotic supplementation (probiotics plus prebiotics plus digestive enzymes) directly target this hallmark. For women over 40, gut microbiome support is one of the most accessible and high-leverage interventions available.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are all 12 hallmarks equally important?

The hallmarks are all interconnected, but some have broader downstream effects than others. Mitochondrial dysfunction, chronic inflammation, and dysbiosis are particularly high-leverage targets because they accelerate several other hallmarks simultaneously. Interventions that address these three tend to have the most visible and varied effects on overall healthspan.

At what age do the hallmarks of aging become clinically significant?

The hallmarks begin accumulating from early adulthood, but their rate of accumulation accelerates significantly in the 40s and 50s, particularly for women undergoing hormonal transitions. The 40s are often described in longevity research as the "inflection point" where proactive intervention has the highest return on investment.

Does NAD+ supplementation actually work in humans?

Human trials of NMN and NR show real effects on NAD+ levels in blood and muscle tissue. Yoshino et al. (PMID 34836809) demonstrated that NMN supplementation in prediabetic women improved muscle insulin sensitivity, a hallmark 6 outcome. Effects are more modest than in animal studies, and research is ongoing to identify optimal doses and long-term outcomes.

Which hallmarks does exercise address?

Exercise addresses at least 8 of the 12 hallmarks: genomic instability (through DNA repair activation), telomere attrition (through telomere length maintenance), epigenetic alterations (through sirtuin activation), loss of proteostasis (through protein turnover), mitochondrial dysfunction (through mitochondrial biogenesis), cellular senescence (through clearance of senescent cells), stem cell exhaustion (through stem cell activation), and chronic inflammation (through anti-inflammatory signaling). This is why exercise is the single most evidence-backed longevity intervention.

Can younger women benefit from targeting the hallmarks?

Yes, but the urgency and the leverage differ. Women in their 30s who build healthy habits are building a foundation that slows hallmark accumulation before it reaches the accelerated phase of the 40s. Women in their 40s and 50s are intervening during the period of fastest accumulation, where the return on consistent lifestyle changes and targeted supplementation is highest.
