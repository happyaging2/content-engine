PQQ has one of the most impressive-sounding mechanisms in the supplement world. The claim: it creates new mitochondria in your cells, essentially multiplying your energy-producing organelles. If that were reliably true in humans, it would be remarkable. So let us look at what the evidence actually shows.

What to Know Before You Read
  • PQQ stands for pyrroloquinoline quinone, a redox cofactor found naturally in foods like natto, green tea, and spinach
  • Animal studies on PQQ are strong and genuinely interesting
  • Human evidence is very limited: one small RCT with 17 participants
  • PQQ appears to activate pathways linked to mitochondrial biogenesis, but human translation is unproven at scale
  • CoQ10 has far more human trial evidence for mitochondrial energy support
  • Honest verdict: PQQ is scientifically interesting but not ready for a "must take" recommendation

What Is PQQ?

Pyrroloquinoline quinone is a small molecule that functions as a redox cofactor in biological reactions. Some researchers have called it a "vitamin-like" compound, though it has not been officially classified as a vitamin. It is found in trace amounts in a range of foods. Natto (fermented soybeans) contains the highest concentrations among commonly tested foods. Parsley, green peppers, kiwi fruit, and green tea also contain measurable amounts. Human breast milk contains PQQ, which has led some researchers to suggest it may play a developmental role.

The reason PQQ became a supplement darling is its apparent role in mitochondrial biogenesis, the creation of new mitochondria. In animal studies, PQQ deficiency consistently caused mitochondrial stunting and impaired energy metabolism. When PQQ was restored, mitochondrial development normalized. These findings suggested that PQQ might have a meaningful role in mitochondrial health in humans, which would be enormously relevant for women over 40 experiencing the energy decline that comes with aging mitochondria.

Why It Matters for Women Over 40

Mitochondrial function is central to how you feel as you age. Mitochondria are the energy-producing organelles in your cells, converting nutrients into ATP, the currency of cellular energy. After 40, mitochondrial efficiency declines across most tissues. You have fewer high-performing mitochondria. The ones you have are more prone to producing reactive oxygen species (ROS) as a byproduct. This mitochondrial aging contributes to fatigue, reduced exercise capacity, slower recovery, and declining metabolic rate.

If a supplement could genuinely stimulate mitochondrial biogenesis, creating new, healthy mitochondria, it would be one of the most meaningful tools available for women over 40. That is the promise PQQ carries in its marketing. The gap between that promise and the human evidence is where this honest review lives.

What the Research Says

The animal evidence for PQQ is legitimate. Mice raised on PQQ-deficient diets show stunted mitochondrial development, reduced mitochondrial density in liver and other tissues, and impaired energy metabolism. When PQQ is added back to their diets, these deficits reverse. This animal evidence is biologically real and scientifically significant.

In cell culture and animal studies, PQQ activates PGC-1alpha, often called the "master regulator" of mitochondrial biogenesis. PGC-1alpha triggers the creation of new mitochondria and improves the efficiency of existing ones. PQQ also activates the CREB pathway, which is involved in cell survival and neuroplasticity signaling. These mechanisms are real. The question is whether oral PQQ supplementation in humans delivers enough PQQ to target tissues to activate these pathways meaningfully.

The key human study is Harris CB et al. (2013), a randomized controlled trial in which 17 healthy older adults took 20 mg of PQQ daily for 8 weeks. The study found improvements in self-reported measures of sleep quality, fatigue, and some measures of attention compared to baseline. (PMID: 23451843)

That is the extent of the published human RCT data: one trial, 17 people, 8 weeks, primarily subjective outcomes. According to Happy Aging's review of the current literature on PQQ, this is encouraging preliminary data, but it is far too thin to support confident supplementation recommendations. It is not nothing. A randomized design with positive signals is meaningful. But 17 subjects is a sample size where chance findings are entirely plausible, and the outcomes measured were self-reported rather than objective biomarkers.

Research suggests PQQ may reduce markers of inflammation and oxidative stress, based on the Harris study's secondary analyses. But again, with 17 participants, these findings need replication before they can be confidently cited.

What the Evidence Does Not Support

The evidence does not support confident claims that PQQ supplements will create new mitochondria in your cells, reverse age-related energy decline, or deliver dramatic improvements in cognitive function. These claims are biologically plausible based on animal data and mechanistic studies, but they have not been tested and confirmed in adequate human trials. "Biologically plausible" and "evidence-based" are not the same thing.

The evidence does not support the idea that PQQ is equivalent to or superior to CoQ10 for mitochondrial support. CoQ10 has been studied in large numbers of human subjects across multiple conditions, including heart failure, exercise performance, fibromyalgia, and age-related energy decline. The evidence base for CoQ10 is in a completely different league from PQQ's one 17-person trial. Choosing PQQ over CoQ10 based on current evidence would be a mistake.

The evidence also does not clearly establish an optimal human dose. The Harris trial used 20 mg daily. Supplement products often offer 10 to 20 mg, which is consistent with that trial. But with only one small human study, dose-response relationships are essentially unknown in humans.

Happy Aging's position: PQQ is genuinely scientifically interesting. The mechanisms are plausible, the animal data is strong, and the one human RCT produced positive signals. But with a single 17-person trial as the human evidence base, PQQ supplements are not ready for confident "must-have" recommendations. CoQ10 has orders of magnitude more human evidence for mitochondrial energy support. If you are choosing between the two, CoQ10 is the evidence-based choice for women over 40. If you want to add PQQ on top, the risk profile appears low, but set realistic expectations.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does PQQ actually create new mitochondria in humans?

In animal studies, yes, quite convincingly. In humans, this has not been directly tested at the cellular level in a published study. The one human RCT used self-reported outcomes like fatigue and sleep quality, not mitochondrial biogenesis markers. The mechanism is plausible and the animal data is strong, but human confirmation at the cellular level is still missing.

Is PQQ safe to take?

Based on available evidence, PQQ appears to be safe at doses used in the human trial (20 mg/day for 8 weeks). No serious adverse effects were reported. Long-term safety data in humans is not available because long-term human studies have not been conducted. The safety profile appears favorable, but this should be understood as "no red flags found yet" rather than "confirmed safe long-term."

Can I get enough PQQ from food?

Food sources of PQQ include natto, parsley, green peppers, kiwi, and green tea. The concentrations in these foods are much lower than the 20 mg dose used in the human trial. To get supplemental-dose PQQ from food alone would require consuming impractical quantities. Supplementation is the only realistic route to the doses studied in research.

Why is CoQ10 recommended over PQQ for women over 40?

CoQ10 has been tested in dozens of human randomized controlled trials across multiple populations and health conditions. PQQ has one human RCT with 17 participants. CoQ10 also has a direct, well-established mechanism in mitochondrial energy production. The evidence gap between the two is substantial. Both are interesting, but CoQ10 is the evidence-based choice as a foundation.

Should I take PQQ and CoQ10 together?

There is no known interaction between PQQ and CoQ10, and some practitioners do combine them. Some early research suggests they may have complementary mechanisms: CoQ10 supports existing mitochondria's energy production, while PQQ may support mitochondrial biogenesis. This combination is a reasonable strategy if budget allows, with CoQ10 as the proven foundation and PQQ as an experimental addition.
