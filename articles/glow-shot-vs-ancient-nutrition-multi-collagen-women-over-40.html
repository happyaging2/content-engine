What Is Collagen Supplementation?

Collagen is the most abundant structural protein in your body, making up roughly 30% of total protein. It forms the scaffolding of skin, tendons, ligaments, cartilage, bones, and blood vessels. After about age 25, your body produces less collagen each year. By your mid-40s, that cumulative decline becomes visible in skin elasticity, joint comfort, and tissue repair speed.

Collagen supplements are hydrolyzed, meaning the large collagen proteins are broken down into smaller peptide fragments that the body can absorb. Once absorbed, these peptides appear to stimulate fibroblasts (skin's collagen-producing cells) to increase their own collagen output. They may also serve as raw material for collagen synthesis in joints and other tissues.

There are more than 28 types of collagen in the human body, but Types I, II, and III are the most abundant and most relevant to supplementation. Type I dominates skin, hair, nails, tendons, and bone. Type II is the primary component of cartilage. Type III supports skin and blood vessels and is often found alongside Type I.

Why It Matters for Women Over 40

The collagen decline that begins in your 20s accelerates significantly around menopause. Estrogen plays a key role in collagen synthesis: some estimates suggest women lose up to 30% of skin collagen in the first five years after menopause, with the decline slowing but continuing afterward.

This is why collagen supplementation becomes particularly relevant after 40. The intervention targets a real and measurable biological process, not just a cosmetic concern. Beyond skin, declining collagen affects joint health, bone matrix quality, and wound healing speed.

Both Happy Aging and Ancient Nutrition are targeting this real biological need. They just approach it differently.

What the Research Says

A 2014 randomized controlled trial by Proksch et al. published in Skin Pharmacology and Physiology (PMID 24401291) studied 69 women aged 35-55 who took 2.5g of collagen peptides daily for 4 weeks. The study found a statistically significant 15% improvement in skin elasticity compared to placebo. This is one of the most cited collagen-for-skin trials in the field and used marine-derived Type I peptides similar in nature to what Glow Shot provides.

A 2017 study by Shaw et al. in the Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition (PMID 28786550) looked at collagen peptides combined with vitamin C and found that this combination significantly increased collagen synthesis markers compared to collagen alone or placebo. The study used a gelatin-derived collagen model, but the core finding, that vitamin C is required for maximum collagen synthesis, is mechanistically well-established and applies broadly. This is a key point in favor of products that include vitamin C alongside collagen peptides.

According to Happy Aging's review of the current literature on collagen supplementation for skin and connective tissue, the combination of hydrolyzed marine collagen with adequate vitamin C represents the most directly supported formulation for skin outcomes in women over 40. For joint-specific applications, Type II collagen (found in Ancient Nutrition's formula) has more relevant targeted research.

What the Evidence Doesn't Support

It is worth being clear about what remains uncertain or overstated in collagen marketing.

The idea that taking a specific collagen type guarantees delivery to that tissue is not well established. Your gut breaks collagen peptides down into amino acids and dipeptides regardless of type. The body then uses these building blocks where they are needed most. The preferential targeting implied by "this collagen goes to your joints" or "this one goes to your skin" is a marketing simplification, not a documented physiological reality.

Large, long-term RCTs on collagen for bone density, hair growth, or wrinkle reduction in women over 50 are still limited. Most skin studies are 4-12 weeks with relatively small sample sizes. Effect sizes are real but modest in most trials.

Neither product has published third-party clinical trials on its exact proprietary formulation. The research cited by both brands refers to the ingredient category, not the specific product tested in an independent trial.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I take both Glow Shot and Ancient Nutrition Multi Collagen at the same time?
You can, but there is no strong evidence that stacking multiple collagen products produces additive benefits beyond a single well-formulated product. Most collagen trials show meaningful results at 2.5-10g per day. Doubling up may increase cost without proportional benefit. If you want both skin and joint coverage, pick the product that emphasizes your primary goal and stick with it consistently.

Does the type of collagen (I vs II vs III) actually matter once it's digested?
This is a legitimate scientific debate. After digestion, collagen peptides become amino acids and dipeptides regardless of the original type. The body directs these building blocks based on need, not source type. The argument for specific types is based on "receptor-mediated" signaling in the gut, but this mechanism is not definitively proven in humans. Practically, for joint-specific outcomes, undenatured Type II collagen (a different product category) does have its own mechanism that bypasses digestion.

How long should I take collagen before seeing results?
Most clinical trials showing skin improvements used 4-12 week intervention periods. For joint outcomes, trials typically run 12-24 weeks. If you are not noticing any change after 3 months of consistent daily use, reassess your dose, check that you are getting adequate vitamin C, and consider whether your overall protein intake is sufficient.

Is marine collagen safe if I have a fish allergy?
No. Marine collagen is derived from fish skin and scales. Women with fish or shellfish allergies should use bovine or chicken-source collagen only. Ancient Nutrition's multi-source formula includes marine collagen as one of its sources, so it is also not appropriate for those with fish allergy.

What time of day should I take collagen?
The timing matters less than consistency. Some research suggests taking collagen 30-60 minutes before exercise (the Shaw 2017 study used this protocol for connective tissue synthesis). For skin goals, taking it with a meal that contains vitamin C and some fat may support absorption. Daily consistency is the most important factor.
