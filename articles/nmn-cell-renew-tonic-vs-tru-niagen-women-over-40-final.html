NMN Cell Renew Tonic vs Tru Niagen: Which Is Better for Women Over 40?

NMN Cell Renew Tonic and Tru Niagen are both NAD+ precursor supplements, but they use different compounds to raise NAD+ levels, and the right choice depends on your specific goals, your budget, and how you weigh the evidence.

What to Know Before You Read
  • NMN Cell Renew Tonic uses NMN (nicotinamide mononucleotide), one step closer to NAD+ on the biosynthetic pathway.
  • Tru Niagen uses NR (nicotinamide riboside), studied in human trials since the mid-2010s.
  • Both products raise blood NAD+ levels.
  • The 2021 Yoshino study (PMID 33009199) is the most relevant sex-specific human evidence for NMN.
  • Tru Niagen has sponsored several NR human trials with a favorable safety profile.
  • Price, formulation quality, and health goals should all factor into the choice.

What the Research Says

For NMN, Yoshino et al. 2021 (PMID 33009199) in Science enrolled 25 postmenopausal women with prediabetes. At 250 mg NMN daily for 10 weeks, participants showed significantly improved skeletal muscle insulin sensitivity. For NR, a 2018 trial by Martens et al. in Cell Metabolism showed blood NAD+ levels rising 40-60% above baseline in healthy older adults taking 500 mg NR daily.

According to Happy Aging's review, NR has a broader and older human safety and efficacy database, while NMN has more specific and recent sex-relevant evidence in women. Neither has been compared head-to-head in a human trial.

The Happy Aging Recommendation

  1. Choose NMN Cell Renew Tonic if metabolic support and insulin sensitivity are your primary goals.
  2. Choose NR (Tru Niagen) if you are newer to NAD+ precursors and prefer the longest available safety record.
  3. Start at 250 mg per day and assess response over 8-12 weeks.
  4. Take in the morning with or without food.
  5. Track energy, exercise recovery, and metabolic markers.
  6. Combine with at least 150 minutes of moderate activity per week.
