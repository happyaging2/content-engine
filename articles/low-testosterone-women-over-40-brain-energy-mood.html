What to Know

  • Testosterone in women is produced in the ovaries and adrenal glands and declines gradually from the mid-30s onward, not just at menopause.
  • Low testosterone in women is associated with brain fog, reduced motivation, low libido, muscle loss, fatigue, and mood flattening, all symptoms that can easily be attributed to other causes or dismissed as "normal aging."
  • Most standard hormone panels do not include testosterone for women, and there is no universally agreed-upon definition of "low" testosterone in women, making this one of the most underdiagnosed hormonal issues in midlife health.
  • Davis and Wahlin-Jacobsen (2015, PMID 26358173) published a landmark review in The Lancet Diabetes and Endocrinology establishing testosterone as an essential hormone in women with legitimate physiological roles in brain, muscle, bone, and sexual function.
  • Lifestyle approaches including resistance exercise, adequate sleep, and zinc and vitamin D sufficiency may support healthy testosterone levels. Under medical supervision, DHEA and testosterone therapy are clinical options.
  • Testosterone supplementation without medical supervision and testing is not recommended. This article is educational.

When women think about hormone decline after 40, estrogen and progesterone come to mind. Testosterone rarely does. Yet testosterone is present in women at concentrations several times higher than estradiol throughout most of the reproductive years, and its gradual decline from the mid-30s onward contributes to changes in brain function, physical energy, mood, and body composition that millions of women experience without understanding the underlying cause.

What Is Testosterone in Women?

Testosterone is a steroid hormone belonging to the androgen class. In women, it is synthesized primarily in the ovaries and adrenal glands, with additional contribution from peripheral conversion of DHEA (dehydroepiandrosterone). It circulates in two forms: bound (attached to sex hormone-binding globulin, or SHBG, and albumin) and free (unbound and biologically active). It is free testosterone that exerts effects on tissues.

Contrary to the popular misconception that testosterone is exclusively a "male hormone," women's bodies rely on testosterone for a range of functions: maintaining muscle mass and strength, supporting bone density, driving libido and sexual response, contributing to mood stability and motivation, and supporting brain function, including dopamine pathways associated with drive and reward. Testosterone is also a precursor to estradiol in both sexes: it is converted to estrogen through a process called aromatization, meaning it contributes to estrogen levels indirectly as well.

Why It Matters for Women Over 40

A landmark 2015 review by Davis SR and Wahlin-Jacobsen S, published in The Lancet Diabetes and Endocrinology (PMID 26358173), established that testosterone in women declines gradually across the lifespan, beginning in the mid-30s, and that this decline is largely independent of the menopausal transition itself. By the time a woman reaches her 40s, she may already have testosterone levels roughly half of what she had in her mid-20s.

This is distinct from the sharp drop in estrogen that occurs at menopause. Testosterone's decline is slower and more insidious, which is one reason its effects are so often attributed to other causes: "I'm just tired," "I'm stressed," "I must be getting older." The symptoms of low testosterone in women do not come with a clear milestone event the way menopause does.

The brain connection is particularly relevant. Testosterone receptors are found in the hippocampus (the brain's primary memory center), the prefrontal cortex (executive function and decision-making), and in dopamine-producing regions associated with motivation and reward. Estrogen receives most of the attention in discussions of menopausal brain changes, but testosterone's role in cognitive energy, motivation, and mood is real and documented.

What the Research Says

The Davis and Wahlin-Jacobsen review (Lancet Diabetes Endocrinol. 2015;3(12):980-992, PMID 26358173) provides the most comprehensive synthesis of testosterone's physiological role in women. The authors note that testosterone deficiency in women is not formally recognized as a clinical diagnosis in the same way it is in men, in part because reference ranges for women are not well-standardized across laboratories and life stages. Yet the clinical reality of symptoms associated with low testosterone is well-documented in the literature.

A 9-year longitudinal study from the Melbourne Women's Midlife Health Project (Burger HG et al., Hum Reprod Update. 2007, PMID 17630397) provided important longitudinal data on hormone changes during the menopausal transition, showing that while total testosterone levels do not drop sharply at menopause the way estrogen does, the gradual age-related decline from the 30s onward means that by the postmenopausal years, testosterone levels are substantially below their peak reproductive-age values.

For sexual function, a 2019 systematic review and meta-analysis in The Lancet Diabetes and Endocrinology found that testosterone therapy was associated with significant improvements in sexual function, desire, arousal, and satisfaction in postmenopausal women across multiple randomized trials. This represents the strongest evidence base for testosterone therapy in women.

How to Get Tested Properly

Most standard annual wellness panels do not include testosterone for women. If you suspect low testosterone, ask your provider specifically for: total testosterone, free testosterone, and sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG). SHBG is important because high SHBG binds more testosterone, leaving less free and available for tissues, even if total testosterone appears adequate on a lab report. Testing should ideally be done in the morning (when testosterone peaks) and on day 8-10 of the menstrual cycle if you are still cycling.

Reference ranges for women vary significantly between laboratories and are not standardized. A result that is technically "within the lab's normal range" may still be in the lowest quartile for your age group and consistent with symptoms. Working with a provider who understands women's hormonal complexity, such as an integrative medicine physician, a reproductive endocrinologist, or a menopause specialist, gives you the best chance of a meaningful evaluation rather than a number comparison to a broad population average.

The Mitochondrial Connection

One reason NAD+ cellular health support is relevant to this conversation is that testosterone synthesis itself is a mitochondrial process. Testosterone is produced from cholesterol via a multi-step pathway that begins inside the mitochondria, specifically through the action of an enzyme called StAR (steroidogenic acute regulatory protein) that transports cholesterol into the inner mitochondrial membrane. When mitochondrial function declines with age, all steroid hormone synthesis, including testosterone, may be affected at the production level.

NAD+ is essential for mitochondrial energy production through its role in the electron transport chain. Supporting NAD+ levels, which decline with age, supports the cellular machinery that underlies hormone synthesis broadly. This is not a direct testosterone therapy, and it is not a substitute for clinical hormone assessment, but it is a physiologically coherent reason why cellular energy support can be a meaningful complement to a broader hormonal health strategy.

What the Evidence Doesn't Support

The herbal and supplement market is full of products claiming to "boost testosterone naturally" in women. Most of these claims are based on ingredients studied in men (such as tribulus terrestris and fenugreek) where evidence is modest at best, or on preclinical animal studies with no human clinical trial data. There is little to no robust evidence that these products meaningfully raise testosterone levels in women with clinically low values.

Self-treating with over-the-counter testosterone creams or compounded DHEA without laboratory testing and medical guidance is not recommended. Testosterone excess in women causes its own set of problems: acne, hair loss, voice deepening, and menstrual irregularities. The therapeutic window for testosterone in women is narrow, and appropriate dosing requires monitoring of blood levels.

Attributing low energy, mood flattening, and reduced drive entirely to low testosterone without ruling out other causes is also a clinical error. Thyroid dysfunction, iron deficiency anemia, depression, sleep disorders, and vitamin D deficiency produce overlapping symptoms and must be evaluated concurrently. A thorough panel is the starting point.

Who This Is NOT For

This article is educational. Women who are pregnant or trying to conceive should not pursue testosterone therapy without specialist guidance. Women with hormone-sensitive cancers, including estrogen-receptor positive breast cancer or endometrial cancer, should discuss any hormonal intervention with their oncologist before proceeding. Women with polycystic ovarian syndrome (PCOS), which is characterized by excess androgens rather than deficiency, should not assume low testosterone is their issue without laboratory confirmation. Testosterone therapy administered without medical supervision and monitoring carries risks; this article does not constitute medical advice or a recommendation to self-treat.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if my testosterone is low?

The only reliable way to know is laboratory testing. Symptoms alone are not sufficient for diagnosis because so many conditions overlap. Ask your provider for total testosterone, free testosterone, and SHBG. Symptoms that are most specifically associated with low testosterone in women (versus other hormonal issues) include low sexual desire that does not improve with relationship or psychological interventions, unexplained loss of muscle mass and strength, and a pervasive sense of reduced motivation or drive that is out of character for you. A thorough evaluation rules out other causes before attributing these to testosterone deficiency.

Can women use testosterone therapy?

Yes, and it is more widely used than most women realize. Testosterone therapy for women is not FDA-approved for any specific indication in the United States (as of 2026), but it is used off-label by menopause specialists and gynecologists, particularly for low libido in postmenopausal women. Compounded testosterone creams and gels, as well as low-dose formulations adapted from male products, are common approaches under medical supervision. A 2019 Lancet systematic review found significant benefits for sexual function in postmenopausal women. Risks at appropriate doses are generally low, but monitoring is essential.

Does DHEA help with low testosterone in women?

DHEA is a precursor hormone produced by the adrenal glands. It is converted in peripheral tissues to testosterone and estrogen. For women with low DHEA-S (DHEA sulfate, the measured form), supplementing DHEA under medical supervision may support testosterone and estrogen levels in tissues. Intravaginal DHEA (prasterone) is FDA-approved for treating pain during intercourse due to GSM and converts locally to both testosterone and estrogen in vaginal tissue. Oral DHEA supplements are available over the counter, but their effects are variable and should be monitored with testing. Do not start DHEA without a baseline DHEA-S level and medical guidance.

Does exercise increase testosterone in women?

Yes, and specifically resistance exercise. Multiple studies show that acute and chronic resistance training increases free testosterone in women. The magnitude is modest compared to effects in men, but meaningful over time, particularly for maintaining muscle mass, bone density, and cognitive vitality. Aerobic exercise also supports hormonal health broadly through its effects on cortisol regulation, insulin sensitivity, and sleep quality. Combining resistance training with adequate sleep and protein intake is the most effective lifestyle foundation for supporting healthy androgen levels in women over 40.

Is brain fog from low testosterone different from menopause brain fog?

In practice, the symptoms overlap substantially: difficulty retrieving words, reduced concentration, slower processing, and a general sense of mental cloudiness can all arise from estrogen decline, testosterone decline, poor sleep, high cortisol, or some combination. Clinically, the distinction matters because the treatment differs. Brain fog specifically associated with low motivation and reduced drive (as opposed to hot-flash-disrupted sleep or anxiety-driven concentration problems) is more likely to have a testosterone component. A full hormone panel alongside thyroid, iron, and vitamin D testing helps separate contributing factors.
