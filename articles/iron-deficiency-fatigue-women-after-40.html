Iron deficiency is the most common nutritional deficiency in the world, and women between 40 and 55 occupy a unique and often overlooked risk zone. During perimenopause, heavy and irregular periods can significantly increase iron losses, while shifts in gut absorption reduce how efficiently dietary iron is absorbed. Then, after menopause, the opposite concern emerges: iron stores can become elevated rather than depleted, creating a different set of problems. Understanding where you are in this transition matters for your energy, your brain, and your long-term health.

What to Know About Iron and Fatigue After 40

  • Iron deficiency without full anemia is called iron deficiency without anemia and can cause significant fatigue at normal hemoglobin levels
  • Perimenopausal women with heavy periods are at particularly high risk for iron depletion
  • The most reliable test is serum ferritin, not just hemoglobin; ferritin below 30 ng/mL commonly causes fatigue even without anemia
  • After menopause, iron overload becomes the more common concern for many women
  • Food sources and supplement form both matter: heme iron from animal foods absorbs 3x better than non-heme iron from plants

Why Iron Matters for Energy

Iron is central to energy metabolism in two critical ways. First, it is the core component of hemoglobin, the protein in red blood cells that carries oxygen from the lungs to every cell in the body. Without adequate iron, cells receive less oxygen, and energy production slows dramatically. The result is the characteristic fatigue, shortness of breath, and rapid heartbeat of anemia.

Second, iron is required for mitochondrial function independent of hemoglobin. The mitochondrial electron transport chain, which produces the vast majority of your body’s ATP energy, requires iron-sulfur clusters as essential cofactors. Even mild iron depletion that does not yet cause anemia can compromise mitochondrial energy output, which is why many women feel exhausted with “normal” hemoglobin but low ferritin.

Iron also participates in thyroid hormone synthesis and neurotransmitter production, including dopamine, serotonin, and norepinephrine. Low iron is associated with reduced motivation, mood disturbances, and cognitive slowing that go beyond physical fatigue.

The Perimenopausal Iron Window

The years between 40 and 55 represent a shifting and complex iron landscape for many women. Understanding the two phases of this transition is essential for knowing whether to focus on replenishment or moderation.

Phase 1: perimenopause, increased iron loss. Irregular and often heavier periods characterize perimenopause for many women. Menstrual blood loss is the primary cause of iron deficiency in premenopausal women globally. A woman losing more blood than typical due to hormonal fluctuations can rapidly deplete iron stores, particularly if dietary intake is low. This phase typically spans ages 40 to 52, though timing varies widely.

A study in Obstetrics and Gynecology (Munro et al., 2010, PMID: 20502296) found that heavy menstrual bleeding during perimenopause is significantly underdiagnosed and undertreated, contributing to silent iron depletion in millions of women.

Phase 2: postmenopause, shifting iron dynamics. After periods stop, iron is no longer lost through monthly bleeding. Iron stores rise over several years in most women. For the majority, this is not a concern. But for women with hereditary hemochromatosis, a common genetic condition affecting up to 1 in 200 people of Northern European descent, postmenopause can trigger iron overload that damages the liver, heart, and joints. This is one reason why routine serum ferritin testing is valuable for women after menopause.

Recognizing Iron Deficiency: The Symptoms Beyond Fatigue

Fatigue is the most common symptom of iron deficiency, but far from the only one. Recognizing the full symptom picture helps connect the dots when standard fatigue explanations do not fit.

Classic signs of iron deficiency include persistent tiredness that worsens through the day, pale or sallow skin, brittle nails with spoon-shaped deformities (koilonychia), hair thinning and increased shedding, restless legs syndrome, cold hands and feet, frequent infections, and brain fog with difficulty concentrating.

Two less-recognized symptoms are particularly common in perimenopausal women. Pica, the craving for non-food items such as ice, dirt, or chalk, is a documented manifestation of iron deficiency and is more common in women than men. Angular cheilitis, cracking at the corners of the mouth, also correlates with iron and B vitamin deficiency.

Restless legs syndrome (RLS) deserves special mention. Research from Johns Hopkins (Allen et al., 2013, PMID: 23294028) found that central nervous system iron deficiency, reflected by low cerebrospinal fluid ferritin, is a major driver of RLS even when blood ferritin appears normal. For women with RLS whose ferritin is below 75 ng/mL, iron repletion often produces significant symptom relief.

How to Test Iron Status Accurately

Standard blood panels often measure only hemoglobin and hematocrit, which identify overt anemia but miss earlier stages of iron depletion that cause fatigue. A complete iron assessment includes several distinct markers.

Serum ferritin measures stored iron. This is the most sensitive early indicator of iron depletion. Levels below 30 ng/mL are associated with fatigue and hair loss even without anemia. Some experts suggest that the optimal range for women’s energy and hair health is 50 to 100 ng/mL. Note that ferritin is an acute phase reactant, meaning it can appear falsely elevated during inflammation or illness.

Serum iron and total iron binding capacity (TIBC) together reveal how much iron is circulating and how much binding capacity remains. A low iron with high TIBC (greater than 400 mcg/dL) pattern is characteristic of iron deficiency.

Transferrin saturation below 16 percent indicates insufficient iron available to developing red blood cells, even before hemoglobin falls.

Reticulocyte hemoglobin content (CHr or Ret-He) is the most sensitive early marker, measuring iron availability specifically for new red blood cell production. Many labs now include this on complete iron panels.

Best Iron Sources and How to Improve Absorption

Not all dietary iron is equal. Heme iron from animal sources is absorbed at approximately 15 to 35 percent efficiency. Non-heme iron from plant sources absorbs at only 3 to 8 percent. For perimenopausal women with heavy periods who are not eating animal foods regularly, meeting iron needs from diet alone is genuinely challenging.

Highest heme iron sources: beef liver (6.5 mg per 3 oz), oysters (8 mg per 3 oz), beef (2.1 mg per 3 oz), dark chicken meat (1.1 mg per 3 oz), canned tuna (1.3 mg per 3 oz).

Highest non-heme iron sources: fortified cereals (variable), cooked lentils (3.3 mg per cup), canned white beans (8 mg per cup), tofu (3 mg per half cup), spinach (3.2 mg per cup cooked).

Vitamin C dramatically increases non-heme iron absorption, by up to 3-fold, when consumed in the same meal. A glass of orange juice with a lentil dish or bell peppers in a spinach salad leverages this synergy effectively.

Calcium, tannins in tea and coffee, and phytates in unsoaked grains all inhibit non-heme iron absorption. Avoid calcium supplements, coffee, and tea within 1 hour of meals designed to maximize iron intake. Soaking and cooking legumes significantly reduces their phytate content.

Iron Supplements: What to Know Before You Take Them

Iron supplements are highly effective for restoring depleted stores, but they come with considerations that make self-prescribing without testing inadvisable.

First, never supplement iron without confirming deficiency. Iron overload is as dangerous as deficiency, particularly for postmenopausal women and women with hemochromatosis genes. Excess iron generates free radicals through the Fenton reaction, accelerating oxidative damage to the heart and liver.

If testing confirms deficiency, ferrous bisglycinate (also called iron glycinate) is the most gentle and well-absorbed form. It causes significantly less gastrointestinal upset than ferrous sulfate, which is the standard pharmacy form. The standard therapeutic dose is 18 to 65 mg elemental iron per day under medical supervision.

Take iron supplements on an empty stomach or with vitamin C for best absorption. Do not take with calcium supplements, antacids, or within 1 hour of coffee or tea.

Retest ferritin 8 to 12 weeks after starting supplementation to verify that levels are rising. Do not stop supplementation when symptoms improve; ferritin stores need to be fully replenished, which takes 3 to 6 months in most cases.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I have iron deficiency fatigue with normal hemoglobin?

Yes. Iron deficiency without anemia is well-recognized in the medical literature. If ferritin is below 30 to 50 ng/mL and hemoglobin is normal, fatigue, hair loss, restless legs, and brain fog can still occur. Ask your doctor specifically for a ferritin test rather than relying on hemoglobin alone.

What ferritin level is too low for a woman over 40?

The laboratory “normal” range often starts at 12 ng/mL, but research consistently shows symptoms occur at ferritin below 30 ng/mL. For optimal energy, hair health, and cognitive function in women over 40, most practitioners who specialize in integrative medicine aim for ferritin of 50 to 100 ng/mL.

Is iron deficiency more common before or after menopause?

Deficiency is more common before and during perimenopause due to ongoing blood loss. After menopause, blood loss stops and iron stores typically rise over several years. Postmenopausal women are more likely to develop iron sufficiency or even overload than deficiency, though this depends on dietary habits and genetic factors.

What is the best iron supplement for women over 40?

Ferrous bisglycinate (iron glycinate) is the most bioavailable and best-tolerated iron supplement form. It causes less constipation and nausea than ferrous sulfate. Alternate-day dosing (every other day rather than daily) has been shown in research to improve absorption by allowing intestinal hepcidin levels to normalize between doses.

Does iron deficiency cause hair loss?

Yes. Low ferritin is one of the most common and treatable causes of diffuse hair shedding (telogen effluvium) in women. Hair follicles are highly sensitive to iron status because they are among the most rapidly dividing cells in the body. Restoring ferritin to above 70 ng/mL typically reduces shedding within 3 to 6 months.
