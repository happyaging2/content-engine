What Is Adrenal Health?

Adrenal health refers to how well your two adrenal glands, small triangle-shaped organs that sit on top of your kidneys, produce and regulate key hormones. These glands make cortisol (your stress hormone), DHEA (a precursor to sex hormones), adrenaline, and small amounts of estrogen and progesterone.

How the HPA Axis Controls Your Hormones

The HPA axis is the communication loop between your hypothalamus, pituitary gland, and adrenal glands. When you face stress, your hypothalamus signals the pituitary, which then tells the adrenals to pump out cortisol.

This system was designed for short bursts of stress, not the constant low-level pressure that many women over 40 carry daily. When the HPA axis stays activated for weeks or months, it starts to affect the systems around it, including the hormonal pathways that regulate your menstrual cycle and mood.

Research suggests that chronic HPA activation can suppress the production of LH and FSH, the hormones your pituitary sends to your ovaries. When those signals weaken, estrogen and progesterone production can become irregular. This is one reason why high stress periods often cause missed or irregular periods.

According to Happy Aging’s review of women’s health patterns in their community, adrenal-related symptoms are among the most frequently reported yet most frequently misdiagnosed issues for women in their 40s.

What Happens When Cortisol Stays High

When cortisol stays elevated for long stretches, a few things tend to happen at once.

First, cortisol competes with progesterone for the same receptors. Some research suggests that high cortisol can block progesterone signaling, which contributes to symptoms like anxiety, poor sleep, and heavy periods, because progesterone is naturally calming and cycle-regulating.

Second, the body begins to prioritize cortisol production over DHEA production. Since DHEA is a building block for both estrogen and testosterone, lower DHEA can contribute to fatigue, low libido, and mood changes.

Third, high cortisol disrupts sleep by keeping the nervous system in a light, alert state overnight. Poor sleep then raises cortisol further the next day. This feedback loop is one of the hardest cycles to break.

Signs of Adrenal-Driven Hormone Imbalance

The tricky part is that adrenal dysfunction symptoms look almost identical to perimenopause and thyroid problems. Here are the most common signs to watch for:

  • Fatigue that does not improve with sleep
  • Feeling wired at night but sluggish in the morning
  • Salt or sugar cravings, especially in the afternoon
  • Mood swings, irritability, or anxiety that feel disproportionate
  • Brain fog and difficulty concentrating
  • Irregular periods or worsening PMS
  • Low libido
  • Puffiness or weight gain around the midsection

A functional medicine doctor can order a salivary cortisol test (four-point diurnal cortisol panel) to see how your cortisol rises and falls throughout the day. This is more informative than a single blood cortisol draw.

The Role of Magnesium in Adrenal Support

Magnesium is involved in more than 300 enzymatic processes in the body, including the regulation of the HPA axis. Research suggests that magnesium deficiency can amplify the cortisol stress response, and that adequate magnesium may help moderate it.

Studies indicate that many adults are deficient in magnesium because modern food processing strips it from crops. Women over 40 are particularly at risk because estrogen declining naturally shifts magnesium distribution in the body.

Magnesium glycinate and magnesium malate tend to be the most well-absorbed oral forms. Magnesium oxide, which is cheap and common in grocery-store supplements, has very low bioavailability.

Liposomal magnesium is a newer delivery format that wraps magnesium in a fat-like shell, which research suggests improves absorption compared to standard oral forms. The evidence base is still growing, but early data is promising.

Adaptogen Support for the HPA Axis

Adaptogens are herbs that research suggests help the body adapt to stress more efficiently. The most studied ones for HPA axis support are ashwagandha and rhodiola.

Ashwagandha (Withania somnifera) has been studied in randomized controlled trials for its ability to reduce perceived stress and lower morning cortisol levels. Research suggests doses of 300 to 600 mg of a standardized root extract once or twice daily.

Rhodiola rosea has been studied for mental fatigue and stress resilience. It appears to work through a different pathway than ashwagandha, which is why some practitioners use them together.

Important: women who are pregnant, nursing, on thyroid medication, or managing autoimmune conditions should talk to their doctor before adding any adaptogen.

How Sleep Repairs the Adrenal-Hormone Loop

Your cortisol naturally drops to its lowest point around midnight and peaks around 6 to 8 AM. This rhythm is called the cortisol awakening response, and it is essential for energy, immune function, and hormone balance throughout the day.

Disrupted sleep (whether from night sweats, anxiety, or poor habits) breaks this rhythm. When cortisol does not drop low enough at night, the brain cannot fully shift into restorative sleep stages. And when you do not get enough deep sleep, HPA activity stays higher the next day.

Improving sleep is not optional for adrenal recovery. It is the foundation everything else builds on.

A Practical Adrenal Support Protocol

  1. Get a four-point salivary cortisol panel through a functional medicine doctor to confirm your cortisol pattern before starting anything.
  2. Prioritize 7 to 9 hours of sleep. Set a consistent bedtime and limit blue light for 60 minutes before bed.
  3. Take magnesium (glycinate or liposomal) at night, 200 to 400 mg, to support HPA calm and sleep quality.
  4. Add ashwagandha root extract (300 to 600 mg) in the morning or evening based on how your cortisol panel looks.
  5. Eat breakfast within 60 to 90 minutes of waking to prevent a cortisol spike from low blood sugar.
  6. Reduce high-intensity exercise if you are in a high-stress period. Swap one workout per week for a long walk or yoga.
  7. Work with your doctor to rule out thyroid dysfunction and check your sex hormone panel (FSH, estradiol, progesterone) so you have a full picture.

What the Evidence Doesn’t Support

“Adrenal fatigue” as a clinical diagnosis is not currently recognized by endocrinology organizations. The term is used in functional medicine circles to describe a spectrum of HPA dysregulation, but it is not the same as Addison’s disease (true adrenal insufficiency), which is serious and requires medical management.

Taking high-dose cortisol supplements (adrenal cortex glandulars) without confirmed lab deficiency is not evidence-supported and may actually worsen HPA function over time by suppressing your body’s own production.

There is also no evidence that any supplement, on its own, can “reset” adrenal function without lifestyle changes. Supplements work as support, not substitutes.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can adrenal problems cause perimenopause symptoms to feel worse?

Yes. When cortisol is chronically elevated, it can intensify symptoms like anxiety, poor sleep, and fatigue that also occur during perimenopause. The two conditions frequently overlap and can amplify each other.

How long does it take to see improvements in adrenal health?

Most women notice improvements in sleep and stress resilience within four to eight weeks of consistent lifestyle changes and magnesium supplementation. Full HPA axis recalibration can take three to six months of consistent effort.

Should I stop caffeine to support my adrenal glands?

Not necessarily, but if you rely on coffee to feel functional and crash hard by mid-afternoon, it is worth experimenting with reducing caffeine or shifting it earlier in the day. Caffeine does stimulate cortisol release.

What labs should I ask for if I suspect adrenal issues?

A four-point salivary cortisol panel, DHEA-S blood test, and a full thyroid panel (TSH, free T3, free T4) are a good starting point. Add FSH and estradiol to evaluate perimenopause if you are over 40 with cycle changes.

Is it safe to take magnesium every day long-term?

For most people, yes. Magnesium at standard doses (200 to 400 mg daily) is considered safe for long-term use. People with kidney disease should check with their doctor first, as kidneys regulate magnesium excretion.
