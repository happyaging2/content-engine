Magnesium is the fourth most abundant mineral in the body, required for over 300 enzymatic reactions including ATP production, protein synthesis, nerve transmission, and muscle relaxation. For women over 40, it is among the most impactful supplements available, with evidence supporting improvements in sleep quality, anxiety, blood pressure, bone density, and blood sugar regulation. The challenge is that “magnesium” is not a single thing. There are at least eight distinct forms on the market, each with different absorption rates, mechanisms, and best uses. This guide explains which forms actually work and for what.

What to Know About Magnesium Forms

  • At least 68 percent of Americans do not meet the daily recommended intake for magnesium from diet alone
  • Absorption rates vary dramatically by form: magnesium oxide absorbs at roughly 4 percent; glycinate and malate absorb at 50 to 80 percent
  • The best form depends on your primary goal: glycinate for sleep and anxiety, malate for energy and muscle pain, threonate for brain and memory, citrate for constipation
  • Standard recommended daily intake is 320 mg for women, but many integrative practitioners suggest 400 to 500 mg for symptomatic deficiency after 40
  • Magnesium does not cause a “high”; it works gradually over days to weeks rather than immediately

Why Magnesium Deficiency Is So Common After 40

Three interconnected factors make magnesium depletion increasingly common after 40. First, hormonal changes directly affect magnesium metabolism. Estrogen promotes magnesium retention in tissues; as estrogen declines, urinary magnesium excretion increases. Research in Magnesium Research (Muneyyirci-Delale et al., 1999, PMID: 10454264) documented significantly lower red blood cell magnesium in perimenopausal women compared to premenopausal women, independent of dietary intake.

Second, chronic stress depletes magnesium through the HPA axis. Cortisol, released during stress, increases urinary magnesium excretion. Women experiencing the elevated baseline cortisol of perimenopause are in a self-perpetuating cycle: stress depletes magnesium, low magnesium amplifies the stress response, which depletes more magnesium.

Third, many common medications used by women over 40 reduce magnesium status. Proton pump inhibitors (Prilosec, Nexium) reduce magnesium absorption significantly. Diuretics increase urinary excretion. Metformin for blood sugar management reduces magnesium absorption. Even certain blood pressure medications affect magnesium balance.

The Eight Main Forms of Magnesium: What They Do and Who They Are For

Magnesium Glycinate (Bisglycinate)

Magnesium glycinate is magnesium bound to two glycine molecules. It is the most widely recommended form for sleep and anxiety because of two distinct mechanisms. First, glycine is itself a calming neurotransmitter that acts on NMDA receptors in the brain to promote sleep onset and reduce anxiety. Second, the chelated form resists degradation in the gut, resulting in superior absorption with minimal laxative effect.

This is the best form for: sleep difficulty, anxiety, restless legs, muscle cramps, and women who have tried magnesium oxide and found it too harsh on digestion.

Dose: 200 to 400 mg elemental magnesium at night. Take 30 to 60 minutes before bed for sleep benefits.

Magnesium Malate

Malate is the salt of malic acid, a compound that participates directly in the Krebs cycle that mitochondria use to produce ATP. Magnesium malate therefore provides both magnesium and an energy substrate simultaneously. It is the preferred form for women with fibromyalgia, chronic fatigue, or muscle pain because it addresses both the magnesium deficiency and the underlying energy production deficit.

This is the best form for: fatigue, muscle pain, fibromyalgia, and women who need morning energy support.

Dose: 200 to 400 mg elemental magnesium taken in the morning or early afternoon to leverage the energy-supporting effects of malate.

Magnesium Threonate (L-Threonate)

Magnesium L-threonate was developed specifically to cross the blood-brain barrier, which most magnesium forms cannot do efficiently. Animal studies at MIT (Slutsky et al., 2010, PMID: 20152166) found that magnesium threonate increased brain magnesium levels by 15 percent and improved short-term and long-term memory significantly. Human trials have followed with promising results for cognitive function and sleep quality.

This is the best form for: brain fog, memory concerns, cognitive support, and women who prioritize neurological benefits.

Dose: 1,500 to 2,000 mg of magnesium L-threonate complex daily (providing approximately 140 to 200 mg elemental magnesium). More expensive than other forms.

Magnesium Citrate

Magnesium citrate combines magnesium with citric acid. It has good bioavailability (around 25 to 30 percent) and a notable osmotic laxative effect, drawing water into the intestine. This makes it effective for constipation but means it is not the first choice for sleep or anxiety when taken in higher doses.

This is the best form for: occasional constipation, general magnesium supplementation when GI effects are tolerable, and women who cannot find or afford glycinate.

Dose: 200 to 400 mg with water before bed; the GI effect is usually mild at lower doses.

Magnesium Oxide

Magnesium oxide is the cheapest and most common form in drugstore supplements. It has poor bioavailability (approximately 4 percent absorption) and acts primarily as an osmotic laxative. It is appropriate for short-term constipation relief but is not the right choice for supplementing magnesium status or addressing sleep, anxiety, or energy.

This is the best form for: nothing except short-term constipation. Avoid for long-term use.

Magnesium Taurate

Magnesium taurate combines magnesium with taurine, creating a molecule with combined cardiovascular benefits. Both magnesium and taurine independently lower blood pressure, regulate heart rhythm, and reduce arterial oxidative stress. The combination may be more effective than either alone for cardiovascular support.

This is the best form for: women with hypertension, heart palpitations, or cardiovascular risk factors.

Magnesium Orotate

Magnesium orotate combines magnesium with orotic acid, which supports heart and cellular energy metabolism. It is used in European cardiology to support heart failure recovery. Less accessible and more expensive than other forms but well-studied for specific cardiovascular applications.

This is the best form for: women with documented cardiac issues under medical supervision.

Magnesium Chloride (Topical)

Magnesium chloride applied topically as a spray or in a bath is claimed to absorb through the skin. The evidence for meaningful transdermal absorption is limited, but topical application does provide local muscle relaxation. It is a useful complement to oral supplementation rather than a replacement.

This is best used for: local muscle cramps, post-workout recovery, and women who cannot tolerate oral magnesium.

Magnesium for Sleep: The Evidence

The sleep benefits of magnesium are among the most consistently supported in the supplementation literature. A randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial in Journal of Research in Medical Sciences (Abbasi et al., 2012, PMID: 23144582) found that 500 mg magnesium daily for 8 weeks significantly improved sleep time, sleep efficiency, sleep onset latency, and serum melatonin in elderly subjects with insomnia. Cortisol levels also decreased.

The mechanism involves two pathways. Magnesium activates GABA receptors, the same inhibitory receptors targeted by benzodiazepines, though with much milder and safer effect. Magnesium also regulates melatonin production by supporting the enzymes that convert serotonin to melatonin. Low magnesium leads to impaired melatonin synthesis, which is why many women with low magnesium have normal-looking melatonin tests but still cannot sleep well.

For sleep, magnesium glycinate is the preferred form. Take 200 to 400 mg of elemental magnesium (not the total weight of the compound, which is always higher) 30 to 60 minutes before bed. Most women notice improvement within 2 to 3 weeks.

How to Choose the Right Form for You

Rather than a universal recommendation, here is a simple decision framework based on your primary symptom:

If your main concern is sleep difficulty or anxiety: magnesium glycinate, 300 to 400 mg at night.

If your main concern is fatigue, muscle pain, or fibromyalgia: magnesium malate, 300 to 400 mg in the morning.

If your main concern is brain fog or memory: magnesium L-threonate, 1,500 to 2,000 mg of the full compound (threonate complex) daily.

If your main concern is blood pressure or heart palpitations: magnesium taurate or magnesium glycinate, 300 mg twice daily.

If you want a liposomal form for maximum absorption: liposomal magnesium (as in calm-tonic) provides superior bioavailability by encasing magnesium in lipid particles that bypass the intestinal barriers that limit absorption of standard salt forms.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best magnesium for sleep after 40?

Magnesium glycinate is the most recommended form for sleep because it combines magnesium’s GABA-activating effect with glycine’s own sleep-promoting mechanism. Take 300 to 400 mg of elemental magnesium glycinate 30 to 60 minutes before bed for best results.

How much magnesium should a woman over 40 take?

The RDA for women is 320 mg per day from all sources. Given that dietary intake is often below this and absorption declines with age, many integrative practitioners recommend 300 to 500 mg supplemental magnesium daily. Start at the lower end and adjust based on tolerance, since higher doses can cause loose stools.

Can you take magnesium every day?

Yes. Magnesium is a dietary mineral that the body uses continuously; daily supplementation is both safe and appropriate for most women over 40. Unlike fat-soluble vitamins, excess magnesium is efficiently excreted by the kidneys in people with healthy kidney function.

What is the difference between magnesium glycinate and magnesium malate?

Glycinate is the better choice for sleep and anxiety because glycine itself is calming. Malate is the better choice for energy and muscle pain because malic acid directly supports mitochondrial ATP production. Some women take glycinate at night and malate in the morning to address both goals.

Does magnesium help with menopause symptoms?

Yes. Research supports magnesium’s benefit for several menopausal symptoms: it reduces hot flash frequency by modulating vasomotor tone, improves sleep quality disrupted by hormonal fluctuations, reduces anxiety by activating GABA receptors, and supports bone density by enhancing calcium absorption and reducing bone resorption markers.
