If you've been reading about longevity supplements and wondering what's actually happening inside your cells, NAD+ decline after 40 is one of the most important things to understand. NAD+ is not a trendy supplement ingredient. It's a molecule your body has always made, and every one of your trillions of cells depends on it to function. After 40, levels drop in ways that affect your energy, brain, skin, and ability to recover. The good news is that researchers have now mapped the five key pathways behind this decline, and understanding them tells you exactly what you can do about it.

What to Know About NAD+ Decline After 40

  • NAD+ is the molecule your cells use to produce energy and repair damage. It's sometimes called the "cellular energy currency," and without enough of it, your cells slow down.
  • Five distinct biological pathways drive NAD+ decline with age, including increased consumption by a rogue enzyme called CD38 and a drop in the body's main NAD+ recycling system.
  • According to Happy Aging's review of 18 peer-reviewed studies on NAD+ metabolism and aging, the decline is measurable by the mid-30s and accelerates in the decade after 40, making targeted support increasingly important.

What NAD+ Actually Is (In Plain English)

NAD+ stands for nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide. If that sounds like a chemistry class you didn't ask to be in, here's what it means in plain terms: NAD+ is a molecule found in every cell in your body. Its main job is to carry electrons during the process of making energy, specifically during a cellular process called the Krebs cycle and the electron transport chain. Think of it as the electrical wire that carries power through your cells.

But NAD+ does more than just energy work. It's also a required co-factor for a group of proteins called sirtuins, sometimes called your "longevity guardian proteins." Sirtuins regulate gene expression, control inflammation, repair DNA, and govern how your cells respond to stress. Without sufficient NAD+, sirtuins slow down and these protective functions decline. NAD+ is also used by enzymes called PARPs (poly ADP-ribose polymerases), which scan and repair your DNA. Every time a PARP fixes a strand of broken DNA (which happens thousands of times per day in a healthy cell), it consumes NAD+.

A landmark review by Eric Verdin published in Science (PMID: 26785468) summarized NAD+'s central role in aging biology and established it as a key target for longevity interventions. Verdin's analysis showed that declining NAD+ levels are consistently associated with hallmarks of aging across multiple tissue types and species. This is not a single study finding. It's a pattern that has been replicated across hundreds of experiments in the 10 years since that review was published.

Pathway 1: The CD38 Problem

CD38 is an enzyme that consumes NAD+. In a young, healthy immune system, CD38 plays a useful role in immune cell signaling. But as you age, CD38 activity increases substantially, and this increased activity becomes a significant drain on your NAD+ supply.

Research by Camacho-Pereira and colleagues (published in Cell Metabolism, DOI: 10.1016/j.cmet.2016.05.006) demonstrated that CD38 is responsible for the majority of NAD+ degradation that occurs with aging in mammals. In their study, mice lacking CD38 maintained much higher NAD+ levels as they aged and showed significantly better mitochondrial function compared to normal aging mice. CD38 activity was identified as a primary driver of the NAD+ decline seen in multiple tissues including liver, muscle, and adipose tissue.

Why does CD38 increase with age? Chronic, low-grade inflammation (which increases with age, a phenomenon sometimes called "inflammaging") is one driver: CD38 is expressed on immune cells, and as the immune system becomes more chronically activated, CD38 activity rises accordingly. This creates a feedback loop where inflammation depletes NAD+, lower NAD+ reduces sirtuin activity (which normally keeps inflammation in check), which leads to more inflammation, which drives more CD38 activity.

Pathway 2: DNA Damage Consumes NAD+

Every day, your DNA accumulates damage from UV radiation, oxidative stress, metabolic byproducts, and environmental exposures. A class of enzymes called PARPs are your cells' primary DNA repair machinery, and they require NAD+ to function. When PARPs detect a strand break in your DNA, they use NAD+ to build repair scaffolding and signal other repair proteins to gather at the damage site.

As you age, DNA damage accumulates faster (due to increased oxidative stress, declining antioxidant capacity, and other factors), and PARP activity increases in response. More PARP activity means more NAD+ consumption. Meanwhile, your cells' ability to synthesize new NAD+ is declining (see pathways 4 and 5). The result: NAD+ is being consumed at an increasing rate while production is decreasing. This mismatch becomes particularly acute after 40.

This is not a theoretical concern. PARP activity has been measured in aging tissues and shown to increase with age in multiple human and animal studies. The increased NAD+ consumption from heightened PARP activity is now considered one of the primary contributors to age-related NAD+ decline in actively dividing tissues like skin and gut.

Pathway 3: Chronic Inflammation Drains the Pool

Chronic low-grade inflammation, the persistent, low-level immune activation that increases after 40, drains NAD+ through multiple mechanisms simultaneously. CD38 (discussed above) is one. Another is the increased activity of inflammatory signaling molecules that require NAD+-consuming enzymes for their production. A third is the general increase in cellular stress and oxidative damage that inflammation produces, driving more PARP activation.

For women specifically, the hormonal shifts of perimenopause add an additional inflammatory layer. Estrogen has anti-inflammatory effects in multiple tissues, including the brain and gut lining. As estrogen declines, these protective effects weaken, and inflammatory signaling increases. This hormonal-inflammatory connection means that perimenopausal women often experience an accelerated NAD+ decline that goes beyond simple chronological aging.

Lifestyle factors compound this pathway. Poor sleep (which drives inflammatory cytokine production), a diet high in processed foods, excess body fat (which is itself inflammatory tissue), and chronic psychological stress (which elevates cortisol and drives inflammatory signaling) all accelerate NAD+ consumption through inflammation pathways. This is why two women of the same age can have meaningfully different NAD+ levels based on lifestyle differences.

Pathway 4: The Salvage Pathway Slows Down

Your body doesn't only make NAD+ from dietary precursors like tryptophan or niacin. It also recycles the byproducts of NAD+ metabolism back into new NAD+. This recycling system is called the salvage pathway, and it's responsible for the majority of NAD+ production in most tissues.

The key enzyme in the salvage pathway is NAMPT (nicotinamide phosphoribosyltransferase). NAMPT converts nicotinamide (a breakdown product of NAD+ use) back into NMN, which is then converted back into NAD+. Essentially, NAMPT is your NAD+ recycling machine.

Research on NAMPT in aging has consistently shown that its activity declines with age in multiple tissues. When NAMPT slows down, less NAD+ is recovered from the recycling pathway, and the net amount of NAD+ available in the cell decreases. This is also why NMN supplementation works as well as it does: by providing NMN directly, you bypass the NAMPT step and deliver the recycling pathway's product directly to cells that can immediately convert it to NAD+.

Pathway 5: NAMPT Expression Declines

Related to pathway 4 but worth separating: not only does NAMPT enzyme activity decline with age, but the gene that produces NAMPT is expressed less as cells age. This is partly driven by changes in gene regulatory systems that occur with aging, and partly by the reduced NAD+ availability itself. SIRT1, one of the key sirtuin proteins, helps regulate NAMPT gene expression. When NAD+ is low, SIRT1 is less active, NAMPT expression falls, NAMPT produces less NAD+, and the cycle continues downward.

This self-reinforcing decline is one of the reasons longevity researchers refer to NAD+ restoration as so valuable: the system doesn't correct itself naturally once it starts declining. Without intervention, the decline continues and accelerates. By supplementing with NAD+ precursors like NMN or NR (nicotinamide riboside), you break into this feedback loop by providing substrate that bypasses the slowing NAMPT step.

What Happens When NAD+ Drops

The practical effects of NAD+ decline are not subtle. Mitochondria (which depend on NAD+ for ATP production) become less efficient, producing less energy with more effort. Cells accumulate more unrepaired DNA damage. Sirtuin activity falls, reducing the cells' ability to regulate inflammation, respond to stress, and maintain healthy epigenetic patterns. The cumulative result shows up as fatigue that worsens with age, cognitive decline, slower physical recovery, changes in metabolism, and accelerated skin aging.

These are not inevitable features of aging that must be accepted. They are downstream effects of a specific biochemical deficit that can be at least partially addressed through targeted supplementation, lifestyle changes that reduce NAD+ consumption (reducing alcohol, managing chronic inflammation, improving sleep), and interventions that support NAD+ synthesis pathways.

Supporting NAD+ After 40: What the Research Points To

Understanding the five pathways gives you a clear picture of where interventions can help. Reducing inflammation (through diet, sleep, and stress management) slows the CD38 and inflammation-driven NAD+ consumption. Exercise supports NAMPT activity, which is one of the mechanisms behind exercise's well-documented anti-aging effects. Reducing alcohol removes a major acute NAD+ drain. And supplementing with NAD+ precursors like NMN or NR directly addresses the production-side deficit by bypassing the slowing salvage pathway.

No single intervention addresses all five pathways, which is why researchers in the longevity field consistently describe NAD+ restoration as requiring a multi-pronged approach. The supplement piece is important, but it works best alongside the lifestyle factors that reduce unnecessary NAD+ consumption.

Frequently Asked Questions

At what age does NAD+ start declining?

NAD+ levels begin declining measurably in the mid-30s, but the most significant drop occurs between 40 and 60. By mid-life, most people have roughly half the NAD+ they had in young adulthood. The rate of decline varies by individual and is influenced by lifestyle factors including sleep quality, alcohol consumption, chronic stress, and diet.

What is CD38 and why does it matter for NAD+?

CD38 is an enzyme that breaks down NAD+. In young tissues, it plays a useful role in immune signaling. But with age, CD38 activity increases substantially, turning it into a major NAD+ consumer. Research has shown that CD38 is responsible for much of the NAD+ decline seen in aging tissues. Reducing chronic inflammation (which drives CD38 activity) is one way to slow this pathway.

Can you test your NAD+ levels?

Yes, NAD+ levels can be measured through specialized blood tests, but these are not typically offered by standard labs. Some functional medicine providers offer whole-blood NAD+ testing. These tests are useful for establishing a baseline before supplementation and tracking changes over time, but they are not required to benefit from NAD+ precursor supplements.

Does exercise help maintain NAD+ levels?

Yes. Exercise increases NAMPT activity in muscle tissue, which is the rate-limiting enzyme in the NAD+ salvage pathway. Regular physical activity is one of the most consistent lifestyle-based interventions for supporting NAD+ production. This is one of the biological mechanisms behind exercise's anti-aging effects, and it's a complementary approach to NAD+ precursor supplementation rather than a replacement for it.

Does alcohol affect NAD+ levels?

Yes, significantly. When your liver metabolizes alcohol, it uses NAD+ to convert alcohol to acetaldehyde and then to acetate. This process consumes large amounts of NAD+ in liver cells and can deplete the local NAD+ pool for hours after drinking. Regular alcohol consumption therefore accelerates the same NAD+ depletion that aging produces, compounding the deficit. Reducing alcohol is one of the most direct lifestyle interventions for preserving NAD+ levels.

Can diet support NAD+ levels?

Yes, to a degree. Dietary niacin (vitamin B3, found in meat, fish, and legumes) is a precursor to NAD+ through a longer biosynthetic pathway. Foods containing tryptophan (an amino acid that can be converted to NAD+ through the de novo pathway) also provide raw materials. However, dietary precursors alone are not sufficient to meaningfully restore NAD+ levels in adults over 40 who have significant deficits. Supplementing with more direct precursors like NMN or NR is more efficient, while a nutrient-dense diet serves as a supportive baseline.
