NAD+ decline after 40 is not a single event. It is the result of five distinct biological processes that all accelerate with age, each consuming or reducing NAD+ through a different mechanism. Understanding these pathways is what separates a strategic approach to NAD+ support from simply swallowing a supplement and hoping for the best. This article covers the five pathways in plain language, what triggers each one, and what the research shows about addressing them.

What to Know About NAD+ Decline After 40

  • NAD+ does not just decline because production slows. It declines because five separate processes simultaneously reduce production, accelerate consumption, and impair recycling of the NAD+ molecule — all of which worsen with age.
  • CD38, an enzyme that becomes more active with inflammation and aging, is one of the largest consumers of NAD+ in the body and is a primary driver of the age-related NAD+ decline seen in human tissue studies.
  • Addressing NAD+ decline effectively requires supporting both NAD+ synthesis (through precursors like NMN) and reducing the pathways consuming it — particularly inflammation and DNA damage accumulation.

Pathway 1: CD38 Enzyme Overactivation

CD38 is an enzyme expressed on the surface of immune cells, and it is one of the most efficient NAD+-consuming enzymes in the body. For every molecule of NAD+ it processes, it produces only a fraction of useful output compared to other NAD+-consuming pathways like sirtuins. As we age, two things happen: inflammation increases (a phenomenon sometimes called inflammaging), and CD38 activity rises in response to that inflammatory signaling. The result is a relentless drain on the NAD+ pool from an enzyme that becomes more active precisely when we can least afford it.

Research published in Cell Metabolism by Camacho-Pereira et al. (2016, DOI: 10.1016/j.cmet.2016.05.006) demonstrated that CD38 knockout mice maintained significantly higher NAD+ levels as they aged compared to normal mice, directly implicating CD38 as a major driver of age-related NAD+ decline in mammals. The practical implication for humans: reducing the chronic inflammation that activates CD38 is as important as supplementing with NAD+ precursors. Anti-inflammatory lifestyle practices, including reducing processed food intake, regular moderate exercise, and adequate sleep, all help restrain CD38 activity.

Apigenin and quercetin, plant compounds found in parsley, celery, and onions, have been studied as natural CD38 inhibitors. While human evidence is still developing, animal studies suggest these flavonoids can partially block CD38 activity and raise NAD+ levels synergistically with NMN supplementation.

Pathway 2: DNA Damage and PARP Overactivation

PARPs (poly ADP-ribose polymerases) are a family of enzymes that use NAD+ as their primary substrate to repair damaged DNA. Every time a PARP enzyme seals a strand break or repairs an oxidative lesion in your DNA, it consumes NAD+. In youth, DNA damage is relatively controlled, PARP activity is proportionate, and NAD+ levels remain stable. After 40, the situation shifts: accumulated oxidative stress, environmental exposures, and reduced antioxidant capacity mean more DNA damage, more PARP activation, and a dramatically higher rate of NAD+ consumption through the repair pathway.

This creates a vicious cycle: lower NAD+ means less efficient DNA repair, which means more damage accumulates, which activates more PARPs, which depletes more NAD+. Research by Verdin (2015, PMID: 26785468) and others has identified PARP overactivation as a central feature of the aging cellular environment and a significant contributor to the 40-50% decline in tissue NAD+ observed between youth and midlife. Strategies that reduce oxidative DNA damage — including antioxidant-rich diets, reduction of alcohol and tobacco, and minimizing excessive UV exposure — reduce PARP demand and help preserve the NAD+ pool.

Pathway 3: Chronic Inflammation Consuming NAD+

Inflammation consumes NAD+ through multiple mechanisms simultaneously. Beyond CD38 activation (covered above), inflammatory cytokines suppress NAMPT, the rate-limiting enzyme in the salvage pathway that recycles NAD+ from its breakdown products. When NAMPT activity falls, the body’s ability to regenerate NAD+ from recycled components declines, reducing the total pool even if dietary intake of precursors remains constant.

Chronic low-grade inflammation is a defining feature of aging in general, and it becomes significantly more pronounced in women during perimenopause, when the anti-inflammatory effects of estrogen decline alongside sex hormone levels. This hormonal-inflammatory transition compounds the NAD+ depletion effect: estrogen normally suppresses certain pro-inflammatory cytokines, so as it declines, the inflammatory burden rises, NAMPT is further suppressed, and NAD+ falls faster than age-related changes alone would predict. According to Happy Aging’s review of the current literature on inflammation and NAD+ metabolism, managing systemic inflammation is one of the most underappreciated strategies for preserving cellular energy in women over 40.

Pathway 4: The Salvage Pathway Slows With Age

The NAD+ salvage pathway is the body’s primary system for recycling NAD+ from its metabolic byproducts back into usable NAD+. The key enzyme in this pathway is NAMPT (nicotinamide phosphoribosyltransferase), which converts nicotinamide (a breakdown product of NAD+) back into NMN, which is then converted to NAD+. NAMPT activity peaks in youth and declines progressively with age, particularly in metabolically active tissues like muscle, liver, and brain.

This slowdown in recycling means that even if NAD+ consumption stayed constant with age (which it does not — it increases), the body would produce less NAD+ from recycled precursors as salvage pathway efficiency falls. The combination of increased demand (more PARP and CD38 activity) and reduced recycling capacity is one of the most consistent mechanistic explanations for why tissue NAD+ levels fall so dramatically between the 20s and the 50s. NMN supplementation partially bypasses this bottleneck by providing a direct substrate for NAD+ synthesis that does not depend on NAMPT for its initial conversion step.

Pathway 5: NAMPT Expression Declines in Key Tissues

Beyond the functional slowdown of the salvage pathway, the gene expression of NAMPT itself decreases in certain tissues with age. This means fewer NAMPT enzyme molecules are being produced, not just less efficient existing molecules. The tissues where this is most pronounced include skeletal muscle (relevant for energy and exercise performance), adipose tissue (relevant for metabolic regulation), and the hypothalamus (relevant for circadian rhythm regulation, which in turn affects NAD+ rhythms throughout the body).

Research by Yoshino et al. (2011, DOI: 10.1016/j.cmet.2017.11.002) demonstrated that skeletal muscle NAMPT expression decreases with age and is associated with reduced exercise capacity and metabolic flexibility. Importantly, exercise itself upregulates NAMPT expression in muscle tissue, which is one of the molecular explanations for why regular physical activity is associated with better NAD+ levels in older adults. This creates a direct mechanistic link between the well-documented benefits of exercise for healthy aging and the specific biochemistry of NAD+ metabolism.

How NMN Addresses Multiple Pathways Simultaneously

Understanding the five pathways above clarifies why NMN supplementation works and what its limitations are. NMN directly addresses pathway 4 (salvage pathway slowdown) by providing a substrate that enters the NAD+ synthesis route downstream of the NAMPT bottleneck. It also partially compensates for pathway 5 (reduced NAMPT expression) because NMN to NAD+ conversion requires only NMNAT enzymes, which remain more active than NAMPT with age.

What NMN does not directly address is pathways 1, 2, and 3: the CD38 drain, PARP overconsumption from DNA damage, and inflammatory suppression of the salvage pathway. This is why lifestyle factors that reduce inflammation and DNA damage oxidative load remain essential even for women supplementing with NMN. Supplementing NAD+ precursors while ignoring chronic inflammation is like filling a bucket that has multiple holes in it — some benefit, but far less than if the holes were also addressed.

What the Evidence Doesn’t Support

The idea that NAD+ decline is simply a matter of not eating enough NAD+ precursor foods is not supported by the biology. Tryptophan (a dietary amino acid) can be converted to NAD+ through the de novo synthesis pathway, and nicotinamide is found in many foods, but the conversion efficiency of these dietary sources to tissue NAD+ is too low to compensate for the magnitude of decline driven by CD38, PARP activation, and NAMPT suppression. Dietary optimization helps at the margins but does not meaningfully reverse the 40-50% tissue NAD+ decline associated with aging. Supplemental NMN or NR at clinically studied doses (250-500mg daily) is necessary to produce measurable increases in tissue NAD+ in adults over 40.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why does NAD+ decline so much after 40?

NAD+ declines after 40 because five processes that consume or reduce NAD+ all accelerate with age simultaneously: CD38 enzyme overactivation (driven by inflammation), increased PARP activity (from DNA damage accumulation), inflammatory suppression of the salvage pathway, reduced NAMPT salvage enzyme activity, and decreased NAMPT gene expression in key tissues. These five pathways together explain the 40-50% tissue NAD+ decline observed between youth and midlife.

Is NMN enough to reverse NAD+ decline?

NMN addresses the supply side of NAD+ decline by providing a direct precursor that bypasses the NAMPT bottleneck in the salvage pathway. But it does not directly address the demand-side pathways — CD38, PARP, and inflammatory suppression of NAMPT. For the best results, NMN supplementation should be combined with lifestyle practices that reduce inflammation, DNA damage, and chronic stress.

Does alcohol accelerate NAD+ decline?

Yes, significantly. Alcohol metabolism consumes NAD+ in the liver through the enzyme alcohol dehydrogenase, which converts ethanol to acetaldehyde using NAD+ as a cofactor. Regular alcohol consumption is one of the fastest ways to deplete the NAD+ pool, particularly in liver cells. Women who drink regularly and supplement with NMN are partially counteracting the supplementation benefit with each drink. Reducing or eliminating alcohol is one of the highest-impact changes for NAD+ preservation alongside supplementation.

How long does it take to restore NAD+ levels after supplementation?

Human studies using blood NAD+ metabolite measurements show that NMN supplementation produces measurable increases in blood NAD+ markers within 2 to 4 weeks of daily use. Tissue-level NAD+ (in muscle, liver, and brain) takes longer to respond and is harder to measure non-invasively. Functional changes — energy, sleep, cognitive clarity — typically become noticeable within 4 to 8 weeks of consistent daily supplementation. The full benefit accumulates over months rather than days.
