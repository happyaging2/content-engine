EPA vs DHA: What Is the Difference and Which Do Women Over 40 Need?

If you have ever bought a fish oil supplement, you have seen EPA and DHA listed on the label, usually without explanation. These two long-chain omega-3 fatty acids are often grouped together as if they were interchangeable, but they have distinct biological functions, different concentrations in different tissues, and somewhat different health benefits. Understanding which one does what, and which you most need, helps you choose the right omega-3 supplement for your specific health priorities after 40.

What to Know

  • EPA (eicosapentaenoic acid) is primarily anti-inflammatory and has the strongest evidence for mood, cardiovascular, and joint health
  • DHA (docosahexaenoic acid) is the primary structural omega-3 in brain and retinal tissue and is most important for cognitive function and visual health
  • Most women over 40 benefit from supplements providing both EPA and DHA, but the optimal ratio depends on their primary health concern
  • For depression and inflammation: favor EPA-dominant supplements (EPA:DHA ratio of 2:1 or higher)
  • For cognitive health and brain fog: ensure adequate DHA (at least 500 to 1000 mg DHA daily)

What Is EPA and What Does It Do?

EPA stands for eicosapentaenoic acid, a 20-carbon omega-3 fatty acid. Unlike DHA, EPA is not significantly stored in tissue: it circulates in the blood and is rapidly metabolized into eicosanoids, signaling molecules that regulate inflammation, immune responses, blood clotting, and vascular tone. The anti-inflammatory eicosanoids derived from EPA (including prostaglandin E3, thromboxane A3, and leukotriene B5) directly compete with and counteract the pro-inflammatory eicosanoids derived from omega-6 arachidonic acid.

This is why EPA is especially relevant for reducing systemic inflammation, one of the central processes in accelerated aging after 40. As the omega-6 to omega-3 ratio in modern diets has skewed toward excessive omega-6 (with refined seed oils now dominating many diets), the pro-inflammatory eicosanoid pathway is chronically overactivated. Increasing EPA rebalances this signaling pathway.

EPA also converts in small amounts to DHA in the body, though conversion is inefficient (typically 5 to 10 percent). This is relevant for understanding why DHA status requires direct supplementation rather than relying on EPA conversion.

What Is DHA and What Does It Do?

DHA stands for docosahexaenoic acid, a 22-carbon omega-3 fatty acid. Unlike EPA, DHA is extensively incorporated into cell membranes throughout the body, particularly in the brain, retina, sperm cells, and heart tissue. In the brain, DHA makes up approximately 15 to 20 percent of total brain lipids and is the dominant omega-3 in synaptic membranes. DHA’s presence in these membranes is not just structural: it directly affects membrane fluidity, receptor function, and the efficiency of synaptic transmission.

DHA declines in brain tissue with normal aging. Post-mortem studies have shown that people with Alzheimer’s disease have lower DHA concentrations in prefrontal cortex and hippocampal tissue than age-matched controls without dementia. While DHA supplementation has not been definitively proven to prevent dementia, research consistently shows that higher DHA status is associated with better cognitive performance, larger brain volume, and slower cognitive decline in aging adults (Conklin et al., 2007, PMID: 17263897).

EPA vs DHA: Key Differences

Location in the body: EPA stays largely in circulation and is rapidly used in signaling. DHA is stored in tissue membranes, particularly the brain and retina, and accumulates gradually over weeks of supplementation.

Primary function: EPA is predominantly an anti-inflammatory signaling molecule. DHA is primarily a structural lipid that determines membrane composition and influences neurotransmission, vision, and cardiac electrical function.

Effect on mood: EPA has the stronger evidence for mood benefits. Meta-analyses of omega-3 supplementation for depression consistently show that supplements with a higher EPA:DHA ratio (at least 60 percent EPA of total EPA+DHA) produce the largest antidepressant effects. A 2011 meta-analysis by Martins et al. found that EPA-dominant supplements (but not DHA-dominant ones) significantly reduced depressive symptom scores in randomized controlled trials (PMID: 21069211).

Effect on cognition: DHA has the stronger structural evidence for cognitive maintenance and brain health. Studies of cognitive decline prevention have found that DHA, not EPA, is the primary omega-3 associated with better memory performance and brain volume maintenance in older adults. MIDAS (Memory Improvement with DHA Study) showed that 900 mg DHA daily for 24 weeks improved learning and memory in adults with age-related cognitive decline compared to placebo (Yurko-Mauro et al., 2010, PMID: 20434961).

Cardiovascular effects: Both EPA and DHA reduce triglycerides, but through somewhat different mechanisms. EPA reduces the liver’s production of VLDL (the triglyceride-carrying lipoprotein), while DHA alters the composition of HDL particles. Both forms reduce cardiovascular inflammation, but the REDUCE-IT trial (Bhatt et al., 2019, PMID: 30415628), which demonstrated significant cardiovascular event reduction with high-dose omega-3, used pure EPA (icosapentaenoic acid as the active drug), suggesting EPA may have stronger cardiovascular protective effects at high doses.

Which Omega-3 Should Women Over 40 Prioritize?

For most women over 40, a supplement providing both EPA and DHA is the most practical and comprehensive choice, as both offer important benefits for the health concerns most common at midlife. The question is which ratio to prioritize based on your primary concerns.

If mood and anxiety are primary concerns: Choose a supplement with a higher EPA:DHA ratio, ideally 2:1 or 3:1 EPA:DHA. Look for supplements providing at least 1,000 to 1,500 mg EPA per serving. Pure EPA supplements (marketed for mood support) are available and appropriate for women whose primary concern is depression or anxiety.

If cognitive health and brain fog are primary concerns: Ensure adequate DHA intake, at least 500 to 1,000 mg per day. Algae-based DHA supplements are a non-fish source appropriate for vegetarians and those who are sensitive to fish oil. DHA-dominant supplements (commonly marketed for prenatal or brain health) provide higher DHA relative to EPA.

If inflammation and joint pain are primary concerns: An EPA-forward supplement (2:1 EPA:DHA or higher) provides the most direct anti-inflammatory benefit through eicosanoid signaling. Total omega-3 dose at 2,000 to 3,000 mg EPA+DHA daily supports meaningful anti-inflammatory effects.

If heart health and triglycerides are primary concerns: Both EPA and DHA reduce triglycerides at 2,000 to 4,000 mg per day. The REDUCE-IT evidence specifically favors high-dose EPA for event reduction, but the omega-3-triglyceride effect applies broadly to combined EPA+DHA supplements at adequate doses.

How Much EPA and DHA Do Women Over 40 Need?

Most health authorities recommend a minimum of 250 to 500 mg combined EPA+DHA daily for general health maintenance. For specific therapeutic goals, research doses are higher: 1,000 to 2,000 mg combined for general anti-inflammatory support, 1,500 to 3,000 mg for mood support, and 2,000 to 4,000 mg for significant triglyceride reduction.

Two to three servings of fatty fish weekly (salmon, mackerel, sardines, herring, anchovies) provide approximately 1,000 to 2,000 mg combined EPA+DHA. Women who do not eat fatty fish regularly, or who eat it fewer than twice weekly, benefit from supplementation to reach anti-inflammatory and cognitive support doses. Check the supplement label: “total omega-3” includes ALA (from plant sources) and other omega-3s that are not EPA or DHA; look specifically for the EPA and DHA milligrams listed separately.

EPA and DHA in Perimenopause Specifically

Estrogen has a profound effect on DHA metabolism. Premenopausal women convert EPA to DHA far more efficiently than men or postmenopausal women, due to estrogen’s upregulation of desaturase enzymes involved in this conversion. As estrogen falls in perimenopause, this conversion efficiency drops, meaning postmenopausal women require a higher direct DHA intake to maintain brain DHA status (Childs et al., 2010, PMID: 19857064).

This makes perimenopause a critical time to ensure adequate omega-3 supplementation, particularly DHA. Women who relied on their body’s efficient DHA conversion during their reproductive years may notice cognitive changes, dry eyes (DHA is critical for tear film and retinal health), and increased inflammatory symptoms as estrogen falls and DHA synthesis decreases.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it better to take EPA and DHA together or separately?

For most women, a combined EPA+DHA supplement is the most practical and effective approach. Combined supplements address both inflammation (via EPA) and structural brain/tissue health (via DHA) simultaneously. Separate, single-form supplements (pure EPA or pure DHA) are more appropriate for specific therapeutic purposes where one form is strongly indicated over the other, such as pure EPA for clinical-level depression management.

What is a good EPA:DHA ratio for women over 40?

There is no single universal ideal ratio. A 2:1 EPA:DHA ratio is a common recommendation for general health and anti-inflammatory support. For cognitive health focus, a balanced 1:1 or 1:2 EPA:DHA ratio ensures adequate DHA for brain membrane support. For mood and anxiety, 3:1 or higher EPA:DHA is supported by the antidepressant omega-3 meta-analyses. Standard fish oil supplements typically provide approximately 2:1 EPA:DHA, which works well for general use.

Can I get enough EPA and DHA from walnuts and flaxseeds?

No. Walnuts, flaxseeds, chia seeds, and hemp seeds provide ALA (alpha-linolenic acid), a plant-form omega-3 that the body must convert to EPA and DHA. Conversion rates are very low: typically 1 to 10 percent of ALA converts to EPA, and less than 1 percent converts to DHA. This is insufficient to maintain adequate EPA and DHA levels, particularly for brain health and anti-inflammatory effects at therapeutic doses. Fish, seafood, and algae (which produce EPA and DHA directly) are the reliable sources.

Is algae omega-3 as effective as fish oil?

Yes. Fish accumulate EPA and DHA by eating microalgae, which are the original source of these fatty acids in the marine food chain. Algae-derived omega-3 supplements provide the same EPA and DHA molecules as fish oil. They are appropriate for vegetarians and vegans, and they carry none of the fishy smell, taste, or concerns about marine contaminants that some women have with fish oil. Research supports comparable bioavailability of algae-based DHA to fish-derived DHA.

Does omega-3 help with perimenopause hot flashes?

The evidence is mixed but modestly positive. Some research suggests that EPA and DHA may reduce hot flash frequency and severity, potentially through prostaglandin modulation affecting the hypothalamic thermoregulatory center. A small RCT published in Menopause found that omega-3 supplementation reduced hot flash severity (not frequency) compared to placebo. The effect is not strong enough to rely on as a primary hot flash intervention, but for women already taking omega-3 for other reasons, it may provide a welcome additional benefit.
