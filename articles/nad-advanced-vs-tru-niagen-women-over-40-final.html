Why NAD+ Matters for Women Over 40

NAD+ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide) is a coenzyme found in every cell in your body. It is essential for energy production, DNA repair, and the activity of sirtuins, a family of proteins linked to longevity. After your mid-30s, NAD+ levels begin a steady decline, and by your 50s, some estimates suggest levels may be less than half of what they were in your 20s.

For women, this matters because NAD+ also interacts with estrogen metabolism. Research suggests that SIRT1, a sirtuin that depends on NAD+, plays a role in regulating inflammatory pathways that become more active as estrogen declines during perimenopause and menopause.

The goal of NAD+ supplementation is not to flood your system with NAD+. The molecule itself does not absorb well when taken directly. Instead, you take precursors: molecules your body can convert into NAD+. Both products use this precursor approach. They just use different precursors.

What the Research Says

The most important thing to say upfront is that NAD+ precursor research is real and growing, but most of the strongest evidence is still in animal models or small human studies. Here is what the published literature supports.

For NR (the ingredient in Tru Niagen), a 2019 study published in Cell Reports found that nicotinamide riboside supplementation increased NAD+ levels in the skeletal muscle of older adults and was associated with transcriptomic signatures suggesting reduced inflammation (PMID: 31533038). That is meaningful. It is a human study with measurable results in a tissue that matters for aging.

For NMN, a 2021 study published in Science found that NMN supplementation increased muscle insulin sensitivity in prediabetic women (PMID: 34730967). That study enrolled women specifically, which makes it especially relevant to Happy Aging's target audience.

NMNH, the reduced form of NMN included in Happy Aging's formula, has emerging data suggesting it may be a more efficient precursor in some tissues, though human RCT data is still limited compared to NR and NMN.

According to Happy Aging's review of the current literature on NAD+ precursors, the combination of NMNH and liposomal NAD+ alongside sirtuin activators like resveratrol and pterostilbene may provide a more complete support system than a single precursor alone, though head-to-head human trials comparing these approaches do not yet exist.

A foundational 2008 review in Annual Review of Nutrition evaluated all major NAD+ precursor vitamins in human nutrition, including NR, and established NR as a bioavailable and effective precursor for NAD+ synthesis (PMID: 18429691). This remains one of the most cited papers in this field.

What the Evidence Does Not Support

Neither product has been proven to reverse aging, extend human lifespan, or cure any age-related condition. That claim does not appear in either product's marketing, but it is worth stating clearly because the broader category of "longevity supplements" attracts a lot of hype.

The evidence for NR raising blood NAD+ levels is solid. What is less clear is whether raising blood NAD+ translates directly into the clinical outcomes people care about, such as improved muscle strength, better cognitive function, or reduced disease risk. Most human studies measure NAD+ levels as a biomarker, not hard clinical endpoints.

For multi-ingredient formulas like Happy Aging's, the complexity cuts both ways. Combining ingredients that each have some individual evidence is logical. But combinations have not been tested as a whole in published clinical trials. You are extrapolating from individual ingredient studies, which is a reasonable clinical approach but not the same as a clinical trial of the formula itself.
