Methylation sounds technical, but it touches nearly every system in the body: gene expression, neurotransmitter production, detoxification, and cellular energy recycling. As of 2026, TMG has become one of the most-discussed additions to NAD+ and longevity protocols, and for good reason. Here is what the evidence actually shows about whether it lives up to that interest.

What to Know Before Reading
  • TMG (trimethylglycine) is found naturally in beets, spinach, and whole grains. It is sometimes called betaine.
  • Its primary role is as a methyl donor in the one-carbon metabolism cycle, which influences homocysteine regulation, DNA methylation, and neurotransmitter synthesis.
  • Elevated homocysteine is associated with cardiovascular risk and cognitive decline. TMG may support homocysteine reduction.
  • TMG also supports the methylation pathway that recycles NAD+, making it relevant alongside NMN and NR supplementation.
  • Standard supplement doses (500 mg to 3g/day) appear to be safe in published studies.

What Is TMG (Trimethylglycine)?

Trimethylglycine is an amino acid derivative found naturally in beets (which is why it is also called betaine, from the Latin word for beet). It consists of the amino acid glycine with three methyl groups attached. These methyl groups are its main functional contribution: TMG donates them to biochemical reactions that require methylation throughout the body.

As women age through their 40s and beyond, methylation capacity can decline due to lower dietary quality, reduced absorption of B vitamins (which are key methylation co-factors), and increasing demand from cellular processes like NAD+ recycling.

Why It Matters for Women Over 40

Homocysteine is one of the most clinically relevant connections between methylation and women's health. Elevated homocysteine levels are associated with increased cardiovascular disease risk and have been linked to cognitive decline. Estrogen has a protective effect on homocysteine metabolism, which means that as estrogen declines during perimenopause, homocysteine levels can rise.

TMG's primary function in the methylation cycle is converting homocysteine back into methionine. The connection to NAD+ is equally relevant: when the body makes NAD+ through the salvage pathway, a byproduct called nicotinamide is produced. Clearing nicotinamide requires methylation. TMG supplementation addresses this bottleneck directly.

Recent research through 2026 suggests that women taking NMN or NR for NAD+ support may benefit meaningfully from pairing those supplements with TMG.

What the Research Says

According to Happy Aging's review of the published research on TMG and betaine, the evidence is strongest in the areas of homocysteine regulation and methylation support.

Research associated with PMID: 17513435 examined TMG supplementation and homocysteine metabolism. The findings support that betaine/TMG can lower elevated homocysteine levels. Research associated with PMID: 19828901 explored betaine's role in the methylation cycle and confirmed TMG functions as an osmolyte and methyl donor.

What the Evidence Doesn't Support

Homocysteine reduction is not the same as preventing cardiovascular disease. TMG does not have proven anti-aging effects in humans in the sense of extending lifespan. TMG is also not a replacement for dietary adequacy or for addressing B12 and folate deficiencies.

Happy Aging's position: TMG is a practical, well-tolerated methyl donor that provides meaningful homocysteine support and fills a specific gap in NAD+ recycling protocols.

Who Should NOT Take TMG

If you have bipolar disorder or are prone to anxiety, start with a low dose. Women who are pregnant or nursing should consult their healthcare provider. If you are on medications that affect the liver, discuss TMG with your physician.

A Practical Protocol for TMG in a Longevity Stack

  1. Start with 500 mg of TMG daily for the first two weeks to assess tolerance and mood effects.
  2. If well-tolerated, increase to 1,000-2,000 mg daily, split between morning and midday.
  3. Take TMG with food to minimize the slight fishy body odor that can occur in some individuals at higher doses.
  4. Pair with your NMN or NR supplement to support the full NAD+ recycling cycle.
  5. Ensure your B12 and folate levels are adequate.
  6. Consider a yearly homocysteine blood test if you have cardiovascular risk factors.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does TMG actually do for longevity?
TMG supports longevity through two primary pathways: donating methyl groups to convert homocysteine to methionine, and supporting methylation of nicotinamide (a NAD+ recycling byproduct) to maintain efficient NAD+ production from NMN and NR.

How is TMG different from betaine?
They are the same molecule. Betaine is the naturally occurring dietary form found in beets and other plants. The terms are used interchangeably in research and supplement labeling.

Do I need TMG if I already take B12 and folate?
B12 and folate work through the MTHFR pathway, not the BHMT pathway that TMG primarily supports. These two methylation routes are parallel and complementary. Adding TMG still provides additional methylation support through a different biochemical route.
