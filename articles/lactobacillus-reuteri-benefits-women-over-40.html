Lactobacillus Reuteri for Women Over 40: What This Specific Probiotic Strain Can and Cannot Do

Lactobacillus reuteri is one of the few probiotic strains with genuine strain-specific clinical evidence, but the outcomes most relevant to women over 40 are more modest than the internet would have you believe.

What You Need to Know
  • L. reuteri is a naturally occurring gut and breast milk bacterium that declines with age and antibiotic use
  • Two specific strains have the strongest human evidence: DSM 17938 (gut motility, infant colic) and ATCC PTA 6475 (bone density, immune function)
  • The strongest evidence for L. reuteri is in infants (colic relief) and H. pylori co-treatment; evidence for general gut health in healthy adult women is more limited
  • ATCC PTA 6475 has been studied for bone density in postmenopausal women, with early positive results from small trials only
  • The popular claim that L. reuteri boosts testosterone and longevity is based on a mouse study using a very specific yogurt preparation; human evidence does not support this outcome
  • L. reuteri produces reuterin (an antimicrobial compound) and synthesizes vitamin B12 locally in the gut, which are real and documented biological activities

What Is Lactobacillus Reuteri?

Lactobacillus reuteri is a species of lactic acid bacteria that is naturally found in the gastrointestinal tracts of many mammals, including humans. Unlike many probiotic species that exist primarily in the colon, L. reuteri colonizes multiple segments of the digestive tract, including the small intestine. It is also naturally present in human breast milk, where it is thought to play a role in establishing the infant microbiome.

L. reuteri is one of the few probiotic species where researchers have taken strain identification seriously. In probiotic research, "strain" matters enormously: different strains of the same species can have completely different effects. Two well-characterized strains of L. reuteri have generated the most clinical evidence. The first is L. reuteri DSM 17938 (sold under the brand name BioGaia Protectis), which has been extensively studied for infant colic and gut motility. The second is L. reuteri ATCC PTA 6475 (sold under the brand name BioGaia Osfortis), which has been studied primarily for bone density and immune function, including in postmenopausal women.

L. reuteri produces several biologically active compounds during its residence in the gut. The most notable is reuterin (3-hydroxypropionaldehyde), an antimicrobial compound that inhibits the growth of pathogenic bacteria, including Salmonella, Listeria, and H. pylori. L. reuteri also synthesizes vitamin B12 locally in the gut, produces short-chain fatty acids, and modulates local immune signaling through several well-characterized pathways.

Why It Matters for Women Over 40

Several factors make L. reuteri particularly relevant to consider for women over 40, even though the direct evidence for this age group remains limited.

First, L. reuteri levels decline naturally with age, particularly with repeated courses of antibiotics. Many women over 40 have taken multiple courses of antibiotics over their lifetimes, and each course disrupts L. reuteri populations in ways that do not always recover fully. Replenishing a strain that is genuinely depleted is biologically different from adding a strain the body never had in high abundance.

Second, the estrogen connection is real, though indirect. The gut microbiome influences estrogen metabolism through the estrobolome (the collection of gut bacteria that produce beta-glucuronidase, the enzyme that deconjugates estrogens for reabsorption). While L. reuteri is not primarily an estrobolome organism, maintaining overall microbial diversity and a healthy gut environment supports estrogen metabolism generally. This has implications for hormonal balance during the perimenopause and menopause transition.

Third, bone density is a legitimate concern for postmenopausal women, and this is where L. reuteri ATCC PTA 6475 has generated the most scientifically interesting data specifically relevant to older women.

According to Happy Aging's review of strain-specific probiotic evidence for women over 40, L. reuteri stands out among consumer probiotics because the research is strain-specific rather than generic. Most probiotic marketing claims apply vague species-level evidence to proprietary strains that have not been studied. L. reuteri is an exception where the strain matters and has been characterized.

What the Research Says

A 2022 systematic review (Sanders et al., PMID 35538310) examined the clinical evidence for L. reuteri across multiple health outcomes. The review confirmed the strongest evidence base for infant colic (DSM 17938) and H. pylori co-treatment, with more modest but emerging evidence for adult gut and immune applications.

For bone density in older women, a small randomized controlled trial found that L. reuteri ATCC PTA 6475 supplementation over 12 months significantly slowed bone mineral density loss at the tibia in older women compared to placebo. The trial was conducted in Sweden and included postmenopausal women with low but not osteoporotic bone density. The effect was meaningful in the context of the rapid bone loss that accompanies menopause, and the safety profile was excellent.

Important caveats: this bone density trial was small (about 90 participants), was conducted in a specific population, and has not yet been replicated in larger trials. It establishes a plausible mechanism and an early positive signal, but it is not yet sufficient evidence to say definitively that L. reuteri prevents osteoporosis in postmenopausal women. That research is ongoing.

For immune function, L. reuteri ATCC PTA 6475 has shown immunomodulatory effects in several studies, reducing inflammatory markers and influencing cytokine profiles. This is consistent with its known mechanism of action through Toll-like receptor signaling and regulatory T-cell induction. For women over 40, whose immune systems are increasingly driven toward low-grade systemic inflammation, anti-inflammatory immune support is a relevant target.

L. reuteri's local production of reuterin and vitamin B12 are well-documented biological activities, though the clinical significance of locally synthesized B12 (as opposed to dietary B12) for adults with intact absorption is uncertain.

What the Evidence Doesn't Support

The popular claim circulating since around 2021 that L. reuteri supplementation boosts testosterone, promotes longevity, and produces dramatic physical changes in humans deserves honest scrutiny.

The origin of this claim is a mouse study from researchers at MIT (Erdman et al., originally published 2013, with follow-up work through 2020). In that research, mice fed a specific L. reuteri yogurt preparation (using strain ATCC PTA 6475 at high doses, in a fermented yogurt matrix) showed increased testosterone, improved muscle mass, faster wound healing, and a range of other positive effects. These were real findings in the mouse model.

However, several important limitations apply to extrapolating this to humans. First, the dose used in the mouse studies was extraordinarily high relative to what a human would receive from a standard supplement. Second, the delivery vehicle (a specific yogurt fermented with L. reuteri) may matter: the live bacterial matrix and fermentation byproducts are not replicated in a freeze-dried capsule. Third, mouse physiology differs substantially from human physiology in hormonal regulation. Fourth, to date, no adequately powered human clinical trial has demonstrated testosterone increases or the longevity effects seen in mice. Multiple podcasters and influencers have popularized a self-experiment approach, but anecdotes are not evidence.

This does not mean the mouse findings are unimportant: they are interesting and warrant further human research. But women over 40 who purchase L. reuteri specifically for testosterone or anti-aging effects seen in mice are getting ahead of the current human evidence base.

L. reuteri is also not a treatment for gastrointestinal infections, IBD, SIBO, or any specific GI condition in women. Any therapeutic use requires medical supervision with a specified clinical-grade product.

Who This Is NOT For

L. reuteri supplementation in any form is not appropriate as a standalone intervention for women with significant bone loss (osteoporosis). Diagnosed osteoporosis requires medical management, which may include bisphosphonates, denosumab, or other prescription therapies. L. reuteri might be a complementary strategy discussed with a physician, but it is not a substitute for prescription osteoporosis treatment.

Women who are immunocompromised, undergoing chemotherapy, or have had recent bowel surgery should discuss any probiotic use with their physician before starting. Live bacterial supplements carry a small but real risk in immunocompromised individuals.

Women looking for a dramatic, fast-acting intervention for menopausal symptoms will not find that in L. reuteri. The effects studied are modest, slow-developing, and specific. This is a long-game strategy, not a quick fix.

Frequently Asked Questions

Which L. reuteri product should I look for?

Look for products that specify the strain: BioGaia Protectis tablets contain DSM 17938 (best evidence for gut motility); BioGaia Osfortis contains ATCC PTA 6475 (bone density, immune function; the strain most directly studied in postmenopausal women). If a product's label does not list the strain designation (a letter-number code), it is not possible to apply the published research to that product.

How long does it take L. reuteri to work?

In clinical trials, measurable effects on gut symptoms begin within 2 to 4 weeks. The bone density trial ran for 12 months before significant differences from placebo were detectable. This reflects the different timescales of gut microbiome changes (weeks) versus bone remodeling (months to years). For digestive comfort outcomes, expect several weeks before clear assessment is possible.

Can I get L. reuteri from food?

L. reuteri is present in some traditionally fermented foods, including certain fermented dairy products. However, the concentration in commercial food products is generally far below the doses used in clinical trials. Some researchers have made L. reuteri yogurt at home using specific starter cultures (a trend popularized by Dr. William Davis), but the resulting bacterial counts are variable and the preparations are not standardized. Food sources are supportive but are not reliable delivery vehicles for therapeutic-level doses.

Is L. reuteri the same as the probiotic in Activia or other yogurts?

No. Activia's probiotic is Bifidobacterium animalis subsp. lactis (strain DN-173 010 or CNCM I-2494). This is a completely different genus, species, and strain from L. reuteri. These are separate organisms with separate evidence bases and separate mechanisms. The fermented food and probiotic market uses many different species, and the differences between them are scientifically meaningful.

Does L. reuteri help with vaginal health?

This is a common question, but L. reuteri is not a vaginal probiotic. Vaginal health is primarily supported by Lactobacillus crispatus, L. jensenii, L. gasseri, and L. iners. The gut-vaginal connection exists (bacteria from the gut can influence vaginal microbiome composition), but taking oral L. reuteri is not an evidence-based strategy for vaginal health specifically. Vaginal probiotics are a different category requiring products formulated and tested for vaginal application.
