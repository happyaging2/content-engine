What to Know

  • Collagen peptides and vitamin C are not competing supplements. They work through different mechanisms and support each other in the collagen production process.
  • Vitamin C is a required cofactor for the enzymes that stabilize collagen's triple helix structure. Without enough vitamin C, newly made collagen is structurally weak.
  • Hydrolyzed collagen peptides (particularly Type I from marine or bovine sources) are absorbed and appear in circulation as bioactive peptides that stimulate fibroblast activity.
  • After menopause, skin collagen content drops by roughly 30 percent in the first five years. Both approaches target this decline through different pathways.
  • The human trial evidence is stronger for collagen peptides than for oral vitamin C on skin outcomes specifically, though vitamin C's role in collagen synthesis is essential and well-established.
  • Taking both together provides complementary and likely synergistic support for skin elasticity.

Walk into any supplement store and you will find entire shelves dedicated to skin from the inside: collagen powders, vitamin C serums in capsule form, and combination products promising both. For women over 40, where skin changes are happening faster than at any previous decade, choosing wisely matters. Here is what the research actually shows about each, how they interact, and why the comparison framing misses the point.

What Are Collagen Peptides?

Collagen is the most abundant protein in the human body, making up roughly 70 percent of skin's dry weight. It provides the structural scaffolding that gives skin its firmness and elasticity. Type I collagen is the predominant form in skin and is also the main collagen type found in marine (fish-derived) and bovine (cow-derived) collagen supplements.

Collagen peptides, also called hydrolyzed collagen, are collagen that has been broken down through enzymatic hydrolysis into shorter chain peptides of 2,000-5,000 daltons. This size reduction is critical: intact collagen is too large to be absorbed through the digestive tract intact. Hydrolyzed peptides can be absorbed through the intestinal wall and enter circulation as intact dipeptides and tripeptides, particularly proline-hydroxyproline (Pro-Hyp) and hydroxyproline-glycine sequences, which are the peptide fragments unique to collagen.

These circulating peptides appear to act as signaling molecules for fibroblasts, the cells in the dermis responsible for manufacturing new collagen. Research suggests they stimulate fibroblast proliferation and upregulate collagen gene expression, essentially telling the skin "more collagen is needed here." Multiple human trials have measured skin outcomes including elasticity, hydration, wrinkle depth, and dermal density with oral collagen peptide supplementation.

Why It Matters for Women Over 40

The hormonal changes of perimenopause and menopause accelerate skin aging in ways that go beyond sun exposure and normal chronological aging. Estrogen directly stimulates fibroblast activity and collagen synthesis. When estrogen levels fall, collagen production slows significantly. Research published in the British Journal of Dermatology found that women lose approximately 30 percent of dermal collagen in the first five years after menopause, at a rate of roughly 2 percent per year over the following two decades (Brincat et al., PMID: 3695930). This is a faster rate of loss than age-related decline alone would produce.

The skin changes women over 40 notice most: increased skin laxity, deeper nasolabial folds, thinner and more crepey skin texture, and slower wound healing, all reflect this underlying collagen deficit. Both collagen peptides and vitamin C address this deficit through different but complementary mechanisms.

Vitamin C's role is as a necessary cofactor for the enzymes prolyl hydroxylase and lysyl hydroxylase, which attach hydroxyl groups to the proline and lysine amino acids in collagen chains. These hydroxyl groups are required for collagen's triple helix to form and stabilize. Without adequate vitamin C, the collagen produced is structurally weak and degrades faster. This is why severe vitamin C deficiency causes scurvy: the classic symptoms (skin breakdown, poor wound healing, gum bleeding) all reflect inadequate collagen integrity.

Even subclinical vitamin C inadequacy, which is more common than most people assume, may impair collagen quality without causing obvious deficiency symptoms. An analysis of NHANES data found that 7 percent of the U.S. population is vitamin C deficient and many more have suboptimal levels, particularly smokers and older adults with limited fruit and vegetable intake.

What the Research Says

Collagen peptides for skin elasticity: The evidence base has grown substantially. A systematic review published in the Journal of Drugs in Dermatology examined 19 studies on oral collagen supplementation for skin aging and found consistent improvements in skin elasticity, hydration, and wrinkle reduction compared with placebo (Choi et al., PMID: 31834864). Doses ranged from 2.5g to 10g per day in trials, with most showing benefit at 5-10g daily after 8-12 weeks. One well-designed randomized controlled trial published in Skin Pharmacology and Physiology found that 2.5g of specific bioactive collagen peptides daily for 8 weeks significantly improved skin elasticity in women aged 35-55 compared with placebo (Proksch et al., PMID: 23949208).

Vitamin C for skin outcomes: The evidence for oral vitamin C on skin outcomes specifically (as opposed to its essential role in collagen biochemistry) is more mixed. In populations with adequate vitamin C status, additional supplementation has modest effects on skin appearance in most trials. Where vitamin C shows clearer benefit is in combination with collagen peptides: a study published in the Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition found that 15g of collagen peptides combined with vitamin C produced greater improvements in connective tissue markers compared with placebo, supporting the synergistic hypothesis (Shaw et al., PMID: 28978672).

Dose ranges from actual trials: Collagen peptides: 2.5-10g/day, with most trials showing meaningful effects at 5g. The most studied bioactive peptide forms include VERISOL (Rousselot) and the specific di- and tripeptide fractions in marine-derived hydrolysates. Vitamin C: the trials that showed skin benefits used doses ranging from 250mg to 1,000mg daily. The tolerable upper limit for vitamin C is 2,000mg/day; above 500mg, absorption efficiency decreases and excess is excreted.

What the Evidence Doesn't Support

The topical collagen myth is one of the most persistent misconceptions in skincare. Collagen molecules are far too large to penetrate the skin barrier topically. Intact collagen applied to the skin surface sits on top of the stratum corneum and does not reach the dermis where fibroblasts live. Products that claim to deliver collagen "deep into the skin" via topical application are misleading about mechanism. Topical products containing collagen can provide temporary moisturization at the surface, but they do not build dermal collagen. This is why oral supplementation, which delivers absorbable peptides to the bloodstream, is the relevant delivery route for dermal collagen support.

The marine vs. bovine debate is largely a marketing distinction for skin purposes. Both marine collagen (typically from fish scales or skin) and bovine collagen (from cow hides) are predominately Type I collagen after hydrolysis. The peptide sequences that matter for fibroblast signaling (Pro-Hyp, Hyp-Gly) are present in both forms. Marine collagen is sometimes marketed as superior for skin due to smaller peptide size and faster absorption, but head-to-head trials comparing skin outcomes for marine versus bovine collagen hydrolysates at equivalent doses have not consistently favored one over the other. Both have evidence for Type I collagen outcomes, which is the relevant form for skin.

The idea that you must choose between collagen peptides and vitamin C is a false premise created by the structure of supplement marketing. They do not compete. Vitamin C is a cofactor for the enzymatic machinery that processes newly synthesized collagen, while collagen peptides provide the stimulus and the substrate. Taking both is more physiologically logical than choosing one.

Very high doses of collagen (above 20-30g per day) have not been shown to produce proportionally greater skin benefits compared with 5-10g doses. Once fibroblast stimulation is saturated, additional collagen peptides do not appear to drive further synthesis. More is not better above evidence-based dose ranges.

The Happy Aging Recommendation

According to Happy Aging's review of the skin elasticity supplement literature, the case for taking both collagen peptides and vitamin C is stronger than the case for either alone. They work at different steps in the same biological process: vitamin C supports the quality of collagen being made, while collagen peptides stimulate the process and provide the building blocks. Separating them into a competition misses the synergy.

For women over 40 managing postmenopausal collagen loss, the evidence-supported protocol is 5-10g of hydrolyzed collagen peptides daily alongside 250-500mg of vitamin C, taken consistently for at least 8-12 weeks before evaluating results. The form matters: look for hydrolyzed (not intact) collagen with documented peptide size.

Who This Is NOT For

Women with fish or shellfish allergies considering marine collagen. Marine collagen is typically derived from fish (scales, skin, or bones) and is not appropriate for women with fish allergies. Bovine collagen is a well-studied alternative with equivalent evidence for Type I collagen outcomes in skin. Read labels carefully, as "marine collagen" sources vary.

Women with a history of kidney stones (calcium oxalate type). High-dose vitamin C supplementation (above 1,000mg/day) can increase urinary oxalate excretion, a risk factor for calcium oxalate kidney stones. Women with a history of this stone type should keep vitamin C supplementation at or below 250-500mg/day and discuss with their physician.

Women expecting immediate results. Collagen synthesis is a biological process that unfolds over weeks. The trials that showed skin improvements used supplementation periods of 8-12 weeks. If you are evaluating at two weeks and not seeing changes, you are not giving the biology enough time. Commit to a 12-week consistent protocol before drawing conclusions.

Women who smoke. Smoking dramatically accelerates collagen degradation through oxidative stress and reduces vitamin C levels in the bloodstream. No supplement regimen will fully counteract the skin damage from smoking. Addressing smoking is more impactful on skin aging than any supplement combination.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a specific type of collagen for skin?

Type I collagen is the relevant type for skin. Both marine and bovine hydrolyzed collagen are predominantly Type I after hydrolysis. Type II collagen is the joint-specific form and is less relevant for skin outcomes. If your primary goal is skin elasticity and hydration, look for Type I hydrolyzed peptides specifically.

Can I get enough collagen support from food alone?

You can obtain collagen amino acids (glycine, proline, hydroxyproline) from bone broth, chicken skin, and other animal connective tissue foods. However, the specific bioactive peptide sequences (Pro-Hyp, Hyp-Gly) that appear in circulation after supplementation and have been studied for fibroblast stimulation are harder to reliably deliver from whole foods. Supplementation with hydrolyzed collagen peptides provides a standardized, concentrated dose of these specific sequences.

Should I take collagen in the morning or at night?

Timing does not significantly affect efficacy based on available evidence. Consistency matters more than timing. Some women prefer adding collagen powder to their morning coffee or smoothie; others take capsules at night. Choose the time that makes daily consistency most achievable for you.

How much vitamin C do I need alongside collagen?

The RDA for vitamin C is 75mg/day for women. Many experts in connective tissue health suggest 250-500mg/day as a meaningful target for collagen synthesis support, which covers both the baseline requirement and provides a buffer for times of higher oxidative stress. Doses above 500mg are largely excreted and add limited additional benefit for most women.
