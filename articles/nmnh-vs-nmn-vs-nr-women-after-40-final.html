If you've been researching NAD+ supplements, you've probably run into three acronyms: NR, NMN, and NMNH. They all raise NAD+ levels, but they do it differently, and the evidence behind each is not equal. Here is what you actually need to know as a woman over 40.

What to Know Before You Read
  • NAD+ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide) is a coenzyme essential for energy metabolism, DNA repair, and circadian rhythm regulation that declines with age.
  • NR (nicotinamide riboside) has the longest clinical trial history of the three and is the most extensively studied in humans.
  • NMN (nicotinamide mononucleotide) is a more direct NAD+ precursor and has a growing body of human data, including a study specifically in women.
  • NMNH (dihydronicotinamide mononucleotide, or "reduced NMN") is the newest form, with preclinical data suggesting high efficiency but limited published human trials.
  • All three raise blood NAD+ levels in humans. The differences are in conversion pathway, tissue specificity, and depth of human evidence.
  • No precursor has been shown in a human trial to definitively outperform the others on clinically meaningful outcomes for women over 40.

What Are NAD+ Precursors and Why Do They Matter?

NAD+ does not absorb well when taken directly as a supplement. So instead of taking NAD+ itself, you take a molecule that your body can convert into NAD+ through one of several metabolic pathways. These are called precursors.

Think of it like making bread. You cannot consume flour in the same way you consume bread. But give your body the right ingredients in the right form, and it builds what you need. NR, NMN, and NMNH are three different "pre-bread" molecules your body can use to build NAD+.

The reason this matters after 40 is that NAD+ production naturally slows with age. The enzymes that synthesize NAD+ from scratch (via the de novo pathway) become less efficient. The salvage pathway, which recycles NAD+ precursors, also slows. So you end up with lower cellular NAD+, which affects everything from mitochondrial energy production to DNA repair speed to how well your body manages inflammation.

Why It Matters for Women Over 40

The hormonal shifts of perimenopause create additional demands on NAD+-dependent systems. SIRT1, a sirtuin enzyme that helps regulate inflammatory signaling and circadian rhythm, is NAD+-dependent. PARP1, a DNA repair enzyme that becomes more active as oxidative stress increases (which happens as estrogen declines), also consumes NAD+.

The result is a double pressure: less NAD+ being made, and more NAD+ being consumed by stress-response systems. For women navigating the biological transition of perimenopause, supporting NAD+ production from the right precursor may provide meaningful cellular support during a period when the body's needs are elevated.

A 2021 study published in Science (PMID: 34730967) enrolled specifically prediabetic women and found that NMN supplementation improved muscle insulin sensitivity. This is one of the few NAD+ precursor studies to focus on women as a cohort, and it highlights that the metabolic benefits of NAD+ support may be particularly relevant for women in midlife, when insulin sensitivity often declines alongside hormonal shifts.

What the Research Says

Here is the honest breakdown of where each precursor stands in the published human evidence.

NR has the strongest and most extensive human trial record of the three. A 2019 study by Elhassan et al. in Cell Reports found that NR supplementation raised NAD+ metabolites in aged skeletal muscle and induced gene expression patterns associated with reduced inflammation (PMID: 31533038). Multiple other human trials have confirmed that oral NR reliably raises blood NAD+ levels. A foundational 2008 review by Bogan and Brenner in Annual Review of Nutrition established NR as a bioavailable and metabolically active NAD+ precursor (PMID: 18429691).

NMN has growing human evidence. The 2021 Yoshino et al. Science paper (PMID: 34730967) is among the most significant. NMN enters the NAD+ pathway more directly than NR: NR must be converted to NMN inside the cell before becoming NAD+. NMN skips that step. A transporter called Slc12a8 has been identified in mouse small intestine that actively takes up NMN directly, though its role in human gut absorption is still being studied.

NMNH (dihydronicotinamide mononucleotide) is the most recently developed form. It is the reduced form of NMN, meaning it carries an extra hydrogen, which theoretically allows it to enter NAD+ synthesis through a slightly different and potentially more efficient route in some tissues. Preclinical data is promising, but peer-reviewed human randomized controlled trials for NMNH specifically are still limited. It is the most speculative of the three from a strict evidence standpoint.

What the Evidence Does Not Support

None of the three precursors has been shown in a published human clinical trial to produce dramatically different clinical outcomes compared to the others. All three raise NAD+ levels in blood. What they do not yet tell us with certainty is which tissues are most affected, which form produces the best outcomes for specific populations like perimenopausal women, and what the ideal dose and timing looks like over a lifetime of supplementation.

The idea that NMNH is categorically superior to NMN or NR is not yet supported by human clinical trial data. It may be more efficient in some tissue types based on preclinical models, but human trials that compare NMNH head-to-head with NR or NMN and measure hard clinical endpoints have not been published as of this writing. Choosing NMNH involves accepting a degree of biological plausibility extrapolated from preclinical science.

It is also worth noting that raising blood NAD+ levels, which all three precursors reliably do in humans, does not automatically mean that tissue-specific NAD+ in the brain, muscle, or skin is raised to the same degree. NAD+ is not easily transported across cell membranes, so where the precursor is absorbed and converted matters. This is an active area of research, not a settled question.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is NMNH the same as NMN?

Not exactly. NMNH is the reduced form of NMN, meaning it has an additional hydrogen atom. This chemical difference changes the pathway by which it enters NAD+ synthesis. Both are related to NMN, but NMNH is not the same molecule and enters a slightly different metabolic route. Think of them as close relatives with different door keys into the same house.

Can I take NR and NMN together?

Some practitioners recommend stacking NAD+ precursors, and there is no known safety concern with combining NR and NMN. However, the synergistic benefit of combining them has not been studied in published human trials. If you are already using a formula that contains NMN at studied doses, adding additional NR may provide diminishing returns and add unnecessary cost. Consult your physician before stacking NAD+ precursors.

Which NAD+ precursor is easiest on the stomach?

All three are generally well-tolerated in published trials. Some people report mild nausea with high-dose NR or NMN, particularly when taken on an empty stomach. Taking NAD+ precursors with food may reduce GI discomfort. NMNH has limited human tolerability data. If stomach sensitivity is a concern, starting with NR (the most extensively safety-tested form) at a lower dose is a sensible approach.

Does liposomal delivery actually improve NMN absorption?

Liposomal encapsulation is designed to protect molecules from degradation in the gut and improve transport across intestinal cell membranes. This approach is well-established for some nutrients (like liposomal vitamin C). Whether liposomal NMN meaningfully outperforms standard NMN in humans has not been confirmed in a large published RCT, but the mechanism is biologically plausible. It is a reasonable formulation choice while head-to-head data matures.
