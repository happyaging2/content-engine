If your libido has quietly disappeared after 40, you are not imagining it, and you are not alone. Most conversations about female sexual health focus on estrogen, but testosterone plays an equally important role in how you feel in your body, your desire for intimacy, your energy, and your sense of self. Testosterone and libido in women are deeply connected, and understanding how this hormone shifts after 40 is the first step toward doing something about it. This article breaks down what testosterone actually does for women, how and why it declines, and what science says about supporting healthy levels naturally.

What to Know

  • Women produce testosterone in the ovaries and adrenal glands. It peaks in the late 20s and declines by roughly 50 percent by the time of menopause.
  • Testosterone is closely tied to libido, muscle tone, energy, mood, and bone density in women, not just men.
  • Low testosterone in women is common after 40 but is often overlooked in standard lab panels.
  • SHBG (sex hormone-binding globulin) rises with age and binds free testosterone, reducing how much your body can actually use.
  • Natural strategies including strength training, sleep optimization, stress reduction, and targeted nutrition can support healthier testosterone levels.

Why Testosterone Matters More Than You Think for Women

Testosterone is often framed as a male hormone, but women produce it too, just in smaller amounts. It is made primarily in the ovaries and adrenal glands, and it plays a central role in several areas of female health that have nothing to do with masculinity. In women, testosterone contributes to sex drive and arousal, skeletal muscle maintenance, bone mineral density, mood stability, and mental sharpness. Research published in the Lancet Diabetes and Endocrinology confirmed that testosterone therapy in postmenopausal women led to significant improvements in sexual function, desire, and satisfaction compared to placebo, establishing a clear clinical link between the hormone and female sexual health. Beyond libido, low testosterone is associated with fatigue, increased body fat, loss of muscle tone, and low motivation. These are symptoms that many women in midlife accept as "just aging" when they may actually reflect a correctable hormonal shift. Understanding this distinction opens the door to meaningful action rather than passive acceptance.

How Testosterone Levels Change After 40

Testosterone in women follows a gradual downward trajectory throughout adult life. Levels are at their highest in the mid-to-late 20s and begin declining steadily from there. By the time a woman reaches her mid-40s, her testosterone is typically around half what it was at peak. The transition through perimenopause accelerates this process. As ovarian function declines, the ovaries produce less testosterone directly. At the same time, the adrenal glands are left carrying a larger share of the production burden. If the adrenals are already under strain from chronic stress, their contribution to testosterone output diminishes further. Compounding this is a rise in sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG). SHBG is a protein that binds to circulating testosterone, making it unavailable to tissues. As estrogen fluctuates in perimenopause, SHBG levels often rise, which effectively reduces the amount of free, biologically active testosterone even when total levels appear normal on a blood test. This means standard lab results may underreport the functional deficit many women are experiencing.

Signs Your Testosterone May Be Low

Because testosterone affects so many systems, low levels in women produce a wide range of symptoms that are easy to attribute to other causes. The most commonly reported sign is a noticeable reduction in sexual desire, specifically a decrease in spontaneous libido and reduced pleasure from physical intimacy. But other signs are equally telling. Many women with low testosterone describe a persistent flatness in motivation and mood, sometimes described as feeling emotionally "blunted" or indifferent to things that used to be enjoyable. Physical changes often include increased difficulty building or maintaining muscle even with regular exercise, accelerated fat accumulation around the abdomen and hips, and a general sense of physical weakness. Cognitive symptoms such as difficulty concentrating, slower mental processing, and what feels like a reduction in confidence and drive are also associated with low testosterone in women. If several of these symptoms are present simultaneously, a discussion with a healthcare provider about measuring free testosterone levels, not just total testosterone, is warranted. Standard panels often test only total testosterone, which can be misleading when SHBG is elevated.

The Testosterone-Libido Connection Explained

Sexual desire in women is regulated by a complex interaction between brain chemistry, hormones, and emotional state. Testosterone plays a specific role by acting on androgen receptors in the brain, particularly in the areas associated with reward, motivation, and arousal. When testosterone is sufficient, these circuits are more sensitive and responsive. When it is low, the threshold for arousal rises, meaning more stimulation is needed to produce the same response, and in many cases the response becomes absent altogether. This is not a psychological failure. It is a physiological change with a hormonal basis. A large meta-analysis published in the Journal of Sexual Medicine found that low free testosterone was a consistent predictor of female sexual dysfunction across age groups, with the strongest association in women between 40 and 65. The research also found that interventions targeting testosterone, whether pharmacological or through lifestyle, produced measurable improvements in desire and satisfaction. Understanding libido as partly hormonal removes the stigma and shame that many women carry when they notice this change, and redirects attention to biology as a starting point for solutions.

Natural Ways to Support Healthy Testosterone After 40

Several evidence-informed lifestyle strategies can support the body's ability to maintain healthier testosterone levels. Resistance training is one of the most reliable. Studies consistently show that strength-based exercise stimulates testosterone production in women, with the effect being most pronounced in high-intensity compound movements like squats, deadlifts, and kettlebell work. Adequate sleep is equally important. Testosterone is released in pulses during deep sleep, and chronic sleep deprivation measurably suppresses output. Aim for seven to nine hours in a cool, dark room. Managing chronic stress matters because cortisol and testosterone compete for the same precursor hormone, pregnenolone. When cortisol is chronically elevated, pregnenolone is preferentially diverted toward stress hormone production at the expense of sex hormone synthesis. Nutritionally, zinc is critical for testosterone synthesis and is commonly deficient in women eating plant-heavy diets. Vitamin D acts as a steroid hormone and supports androgen receptor sensitivity. Adequate dietary fat, particularly from sources like avocados, nuts, olive oil, and fatty fish, provides the cholesterol backbone from which testosterone is made. Avoiding alcohol and managing body composition also help, as excess adipose tissue converts testosterone to estrogen via aromatase activity.

NMN, NAD+, and Hormonal Energy

One emerging area of interest for women navigating hormonal transitions is the role of NAD+ in supporting cellular energy and hormonal signaling. NAD+ is a coenzyme found in every cell that powers mitochondrial function and supports enzyme pathways involved in DNA repair, inflammation regulation, and circadian rhythm management. Research suggests that declining NAD+ levels with age may contribute to reduced ovarian function and altered hormone production. NMN (nicotinamide mononucleotide) is a direct precursor to NAD+ and has shown promise in animal models for preserving ovarian reserve and mitochondrial health. A study in Cell Reports demonstrated that NMN supplementation in aging female mice improved oocyte quality and energy production in ovarian tissue. While human trials are ongoing, the mechanistic rationale for NMN supporting hormonal health is strong. For women over 40 who are experiencing the combined effects of low testosterone, fatigue, and hormonal disruption, addressing cellular energy production is a foundational strategy that complements other hormonal support interventions.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do women actually need testosterone?

Yes. Women produce testosterone naturally and rely on it for libido, muscle maintenance, energy, mood, and bone density. It declines significantly with age and is often an overlooked piece of the hormonal health picture for women over 40.

How do I know if my testosterone is low?

Common signs include reduced or absent libido, fatigue, loss of muscle tone, low mood, and difficulty concentrating. A blood test measuring free testosterone (not just total) will give a clearer picture, since high SHBG can make total levels appear normal when bioavailable levels are low.

Can lifestyle changes really raise testosterone in women?

Yes, within the natural physiological range. Resistance training, adequate sleep, stress management, zinc and vitamin D intake, and reducing alcohol have all shown measurable effects on testosterone levels and on symptoms associated with low testosterone in women.

Is NMN related to testosterone?

NMN supports NAD+ production, which powers mitochondrial function across all hormone-producing tissues including the ovaries and adrenal glands. While NMN does not directly raise testosterone, supporting cellular energy in these tissues may help maintain healthier hormonal output over time.

At what age does testosterone start declining in women?

Testosterone in women begins declining gradually from the late 20s onward. By the mid-40s, levels are typically around 50 percent of their peak, and the transition through perimenopause further accelerates this decline as ovarian contribution decreases.

Tracking Progress: How to Know If Your Approach Is Working

When addressing low testosterone and declining libido after 40, it helps to have clear markers of progress beyond just subjective feelings. Most women notice improvements in energy and motivation before libido specifically, often within four to six weeks of consistent lifestyle changes. Sleep quality frequently improves as cortisol patterns normalize and stress-management practices take hold. Physical indicators including improved muscle tone and reduced belly fat accumulation suggest improving hormonal balance. Libido itself tends to lag behind these other markers by several weeks. This is normal and expected: the brain's reward and motivation circuits take time to recalibrate once the hormonal substrate improves. Tracking these signs systematically, perhaps in a simple daily journal noting energy, motivation, sleep quality, and mood, helps maintain perspective during the weeks when change feels slow. If after three months of consistent implementation you have not noticed meaningful improvement in energy, mood, or libido, requesting a comprehensive hormonal panel from your healthcare provider including free testosterone, SHBG, DHEA-S, and progesterone levels is a reasonable next step. Some women benefit from targeted hormonal support that goes beyond lifestyle, and understanding your specific hormonal picture helps direct that conversation with clinical precision.
