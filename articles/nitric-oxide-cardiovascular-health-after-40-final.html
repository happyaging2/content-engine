Nitric oxide is a molecule so small and simple (just one nitrogen and one oxygen atom) that it seems implausible as a cornerstone of cardiovascular health. Yet the discovery of its role in blood vessel function earned a Nobel Prize in 1998, and the research since has only deepened appreciation for how central nitric oxide is to the vascular health of women over 40. Nitric oxide is the primary signal that tells blood vessels to relax and dilate, and its decline after menopause is a key driver of the increased blood pressure, reduced circulation, and elevated cardiovascular risk that makes heart disease the leading cause of death in postmenopausal women. Understanding nitric oxide and how to support its production is one of the most important things women over 40 can do for long-term heart health.

What to Know

  • Nitric oxide (NO) is produced by the endothelial cells lining blood vessels and is the primary signal that causes vessels to relax and widen (vasodilation)
  • Estrogen directly stimulates nitric oxide production through activation of eNOS (endothelial nitric oxide synthase); as estrogen declines after menopause, NO production falls
  • Low nitric oxide is associated with high blood pressure, reduced cardiovascular blood flow, increased cardiovascular event risk, and impaired exercise capacity
  • Dietary nitrates from leafy greens and beets are converted to nitric oxide in the body and are among the most accessible NO-boosting strategies
  • L-arginine and L-citrulline are the amino acid precursors to nitric oxide and have been studied in clinical trials for blood pressure and exercise performance

What Nitric Oxide Does in the Cardiovascular System

Nitric oxide is synthesized by endothelial cells (the cells lining the inside of blood vessels) from the amino acid L-arginine through an enzyme called endothelial nitric oxide synthase (eNOS). Once produced, NO diffuses into the underlying smooth muscle of blood vessel walls, where it activates guanylyl cyclase, increases cyclic GMP (cGMP), and causes smooth muscle relaxation. This relaxation allows the vessel to dilate, increasing its diameter and reducing resistance to blood flow, which lowers blood pressure and improves blood delivery to all tissues.

Beyond vasodilation, nitric oxide plays multiple protective cardiovascular roles: it inhibits platelet aggregation (reducing clot risk), inhibits the adhesion of white blood cells to vessel walls (reducing the arterial inflammation that drives atherosclerosis), reduces LDL oxidation (reducing the formation of the oxidized LDL that initiates arterial plaque), and stimulates vascular smooth muscle cells to produce less collagen (reducing arterial stiffening). These effects collectively make NO one of the most important endogenous molecules for cardiovascular protection.

The pathological opposite of sufficient nitric oxide is endothelial dysfunction: a state in which the endothelium produces insufficient NO, blood vessels fail to dilate appropriately, and the blood vessel environment becomes more pro-inflammatory and pro-thrombotic. Endothelial dysfunction is considered the earliest stage of cardiovascular disease development, preceding clinically detectable atherosclerosis by years or decades. It is both preventable and reversible through lifestyle and nutritional interventions.

Why Estrogen Loss Reduces Nitric Oxide After Menopause

The connection between estrogen and nitric oxide is direct and mechanistically well-established. Estrogen binds to estrogen receptor alpha (ERalpha) on endothelial cells and activates a rapid, non-genomic pathway that stimulates eNOS through phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase (PI3K) and Akt kinase signaling. This eNOS activation increases NO production within minutes of estrogen binding, contributing to the vasodilatory effects that give premenopausal women their cardiovascular protection advantage over age-matched men.

In the Women's Health Initiative and other large studies, cardiovascular disease risk in women begins rising sharply in the decade following menopause, tracking with the progressive decline in endogenous estrogen. The reduction in eNOS-driven NO production is one of the primary mechanisms behind this risk increase, alongside the changes in lipid profiles (reduced HDL, increased LDL and triglycerides) and inflammatory marker elevations that accompany estrogen loss.

The practical implication for women over 40 is that maintaining robust dietary nitrate intake, regular aerobic exercise, and NO-supporting nutrients becomes more important, not less, as estrogen declines. These interventions partially compensate for the loss of estrogen-driven eNOS stimulation through alternative NO production pathways.

How to Boost Nitric Oxide Naturally After 40

There are two main pathways through which the body produces nitric oxide, and each can be supported through diet and lifestyle.

The L-arginine to NO pathway: eNOS converts the amino acid L-arginine into NO. L-arginine is found in protein-rich foods including poultry, seafood, legumes, nuts, and seeds. L-citrulline (found in watermelon and supplements) is converted to L-arginine in the kidneys more efficiently than supplemental L-arginine itself and is a more effective way to raise L-arginine availability for NO synthesis. Clinical trials of L-citrulline at 3 to 6g per day have shown significant reductions in arterial stiffness and blood pressure in adults with hypertension or endothelial dysfunction.

The dietary nitrate to NO pathway: green leafy vegetables (particularly arugula, spinach, and Swiss chard) and beetroot are exceptionally rich in dietary nitrates. In the mouth, nitrate-reducing bacteria convert dietary nitrate to nitrite, which is then converted to NO in the stomach and blood under conditions of low oxygen. This bacteria-mediated pathway is entirely independent of eNOS and provides an alternative route to NO production that becomes increasingly important as eNOS efficiency declines with age. A glass of beetroot juice (approximately 400mg nitrate) raises blood NO to levels measurable within 2 to 3 hours and has been shown to reduce blood pressure by 4 to 10 mmHg in randomized trials.

Aerobic exercise is perhaps the most powerful sustained stimulator of eNOS activity. Exercise increases shear stress (the physical drag of blood moving past vessel walls), which is the primary mechanical stimulus for eNOS activation. Regular aerobic exercise training upregulates eNOS expression in endothelial cells and increases the baseline sensitivity of the eNOS system to stimulation, producing a lasting improvement in vascular NO production that goes beyond the acute exercise session.

Foods, Supplements, and Habits That Support NO After 40

Beyond dietary nitrates and L-citrulline, several other nutrients and practices influence NO production and longevity in the endothelium.

Polyphenols from berries, dark chocolate, green tea, and red wine activate eNOS through SIRT1-mediated pathways and also reduce the breakdown of existing NO by reactive oxygen species. Quercetin, resveratrol, and epicatechins (from cocoa) have all shown endothelium-protective effects in clinical trials.

Vitamin D activates the vitamin D receptor on endothelial cells and upregulates eNOS expression; vitamin D deficiency (extremely common in women over 40 in northern latitudes) is associated with endothelial dysfunction and cardiovascular risk, suggesting that correcting deficiency is an important cardiovascular intervention.

Avoiding mouthwash is a lesser-known but evidence-supported recommendation: antiseptic mouthwashes kill the nitrate-reducing bacteria in the mouth that are essential for converting dietary nitrate to nitrite and then to NO. Studies have shown that mouthwash use reduces the blood pressure benefit of dietary nitrate consumption by eliminating this critical bacterial step. If cardiovascular health is a priority, using an antiseptic mouthwash routinely is worth reconsidering.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the symptoms of low nitric oxide?

Low nitric oxide often produces no obvious symptoms until cardiovascular effects become clinically significant. Early signs include slightly elevated blood pressure that does not respond well to standard lifestyle changes, reduced exercise capacity and earlier fatigue during aerobic exertion, cold hands and feet from reduced peripheral circulation, and in some cases, cognitive fog from reduced cerebral blood flow. Because NO's primary roles are vascular and occur at a microscopic level in vessel walls, its deficiency is typically detected through blood pressure measurement, flow-mediated dilation testing (a research measure of endothelial function), or through cardiovascular risk factor accumulation rather than through specific symptoms.

Does beetroot juice really lower blood pressure?

Yes, with consistent evidence from multiple randomized trials. A meta-analysis by Siervo and colleagues published in the Journal of Nutrition found that inorganic nitrate and beetroot juice consumption reduced systolic blood pressure by 4.4 mmHg and diastolic by 1.1 mmHg on average. The effect is meaningful: a 4 to 5 mmHg reduction in systolic blood pressure is associated with roughly 10 to 15 percent reduction in cardiovascular event risk at a population level. The blood pressure effect peaks 2 to 3 hours after consumption and lasts 12 to 24 hours, meaning daily consumption is needed for sustained benefit.

Is L-arginine or L-citrulline better for nitric oxide?

L-citrulline is generally more effective for raising blood L-arginine levels and NO production than L-arginine supplementation directly. This is the "arginine paradox": despite the fact that blood arginine levels in healthy adults are above the Km (saturation point) of eNOS, L-arginine supplements often do not increase NO production further, while L-citrulline, converted to arginine in the kidneys, does. L-citrulline at 3 to 6g per day has more consistent clinical evidence for NO-mediated benefits than L-arginine. The one exception is people with argininosuccinate synthetase (ASS) deficiency, where L-arginine supplementation is medically indicated.

How does CoQ10 support nitric oxide?

CoQ10 supports NO production indirectly through mitochondrial function. eNOS activity requires NADPH as a cofactor, and CoQ10 is essential for maintaining the redox balance (NADPH/NADP+ ratio) in endothelial cell mitochondria. CoQ10 also reduces oxidative stress in blood vessels, protecting NO from being rapidly degraded by superoxide radicals, which react with NO to form peroxynitrite (a damaging compound rather than a protective one). The combination of CoQ10 and dietary nitrate or L-citrulline may be particularly effective for cardiovascular NO support because they address different limiting factors simultaneously.

Can stress reduce nitric oxide?

Yes. Stress and cortisol reduce NO production through multiple mechanisms. Cortisol directly reduces eNOS expression in endothelial cells and increases the production of asymmetric dimethylarginine (ADMA), a naturally occurring compound that competes with L-arginine for eNOS and inhibits NO synthesis. ADMA is elevated in women with cardiovascular risk factors and is considered an independent predictor of cardiovascular events. Stress management is therefore a cardiovascular intervention as well as a psychological one, partly through this ADMA-mediated NO suppression mechanism.

Does nitric oxide affect brain health and cognitive function after 40?

Yes, significantly. The brain is extremely dependent on precisely regulated blood flow, and nitric oxide is the primary mediator of cerebral blood flow autoregulation. Brain regions increase their local blood flow in proportion to their metabolic activity (a phenomenon called neurovascular coupling), and this response is primarily mediated by neuronal nitric oxide synthase (nNOS) producing NO in response to neural activity, which then dilates local arterioles. When NO production is impaired, neurovascular coupling is reduced, meaning active brain regions do not receive proportional blood flow increases, limiting their function and nutrient delivery. Research in older adults has found that vascular NO availability correlates with cognitive performance scores, and interventions that improve NO (including dietary nitrate from vegetables and exercise) also improve cognitive outcomes in several trials. For women over 40 concerned about both cardiovascular and cognitive health, supporting NO production through diet and lifestyle addresses both systems through a shared vascular mechanism.
