Does Ashwagandha Actually Work for Women Over 40? What Research Shows

Does ashwagandha work for women over 40? This question comes up constantly in wellness circles, and for good reason. Ashwagandha has been used in Ayurvedic medicine for thousands of years and has attracted growing scientific attention over the past two decades. For women navigating the hormonal, energetic, and emotional challenges of midlife, the promise of an adaptogenic herb that supports stress resilience, hormonal balance, and cognitive function is genuinely appealing. But does the evidence hold up? This article takes a grounded look at what the research actually shows, how ashwagandha works in the body, what it does particularly well for women over 40, and where expectations should be managed.

What to Know at a Glance

  • Ashwagandha is a well-studied adaptogen with real, documented effects on cortisol, stress, and cognitive function
  • Clinical trials have shown meaningful reductions in perceived stress and cortisol levels in women using ashwagandha
  • Research suggests some benefit for sexual function, thyroid support, and menopausal symptom relief in women
  • Ashwagandha does not directly replace estrogen or progesterone and is not a hormone therapy
  • Quality, dose, and form of ashwagandha matter significantly for effectiveness
  • Combining ashwagandha with NAD+ support may address both the hormonal and cellular energy dimensions of aging after 40

What Is Ashwagandha and How Does It Work?

Ashwagandha (Withania somnifera) is a small shrub native to India, North Africa, and parts of the Mediterranean. Its root and sometimes leaves are used medicinally. In Ayurvedic tradition, it is classified as a rasayana, a rejuvenating herb used to promote longevity, vitality, and resistance to disease. In modern evidence-based medicine, it is classified as an adaptogen, a category of herbs and botanicals that support the body's ability to adapt to stress without causing harm.

The primary active compounds in ashwagandha are withanolides, a class of steroidal lactones found predominantly in the root. Withanolides have been shown in laboratory and clinical studies to modulate inflammation, influence hormone receptor activity, and affect the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, which controls the body's stress response.

Ashwagandha's mechanisms are multiple and intersecting. It downregulates the HPA axis, reducing the output of cortisol and related stress hormones. It has demonstrated anti-inflammatory properties through inhibition of inflammatory signaling pathways. It exerts neuroprotective effects, supporting brain cell health and cognitive function. And it appears to influence thyroid hormone conversion, which has implications for energy and metabolism.

Unlike pharmaceuticals that work through a single, specific target, adaptogens like ashwagandha tend to work systemically and gently, nudging multiple physiological systems toward homeostasis rather than forcing a single pathway in a single direction. This is both a strength (fewer side effects, broader benefits) and a limitation (effects may be modest compared to pharmaceutical interventions for specific conditions).

The quality and standardization of ashwagandha preparations vary enormously across commercial products. Extracts standardized to withanolide content, particularly those using the KSM-66 or Sensoril extraction methods, have the strongest clinical evidence and are the most comparable to the preparations used in published research.

What the Research Says About Ashwagandha for Women

The clinical evidence for ashwagandha has grown substantially over the past decade, and several well-designed randomized controlled trials are now available.

A double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled study published in Medicine (2019) examined the effects of KSM-66 ashwagandha root extract (300 mg twice daily) in 60 adults experiencing chronic stress. Participants showed significant reductions in perceived stress scores, cortisol levels, and anxiety compared to the placebo group. Importantly, the trial included women, and subgroup analyses showed similar benefits across genders.

A study published in the Journal of Ethnopharmacology (2021) specifically examined ashwagandha in perimenopausal women and found significant improvements in menopausal symptoms including hot flash frequency, sleep quality, and mood compared to placebo. The trial used 300 mg of standardized root extract twice daily for 8 weeks.

Research published in BioMed Research International found that ashwagandha supplementation improved sexual function in women, including arousal, lubrication, satisfaction, and number of successful sexual encounters, compared to placebo. This is particularly relevant for perimenopausal women, for whom sexual dysfunction is a common and often undertreated concern.

Cognitive function research has also shown promise. A randomized controlled trial published in the Journal of Dietary Supplements found that ashwagandha root extract improved memory and cognitive function in adults with mild cognitive impairment. This research is relevant to perimenopausal women experiencing brain fog, though more targeted studies in this specific population are needed.

Thyroid research in animal and some human studies suggests ashwagandha may support T4 and T3 thyroid hormone levels, which is potentially meaningful for women whose thyroid function is borderline low, a common and often underdiagnosed finding in women over 40. However, women with autoimmune thyroid conditions (Hashimoto's or Graves' disease) should exercise caution and consult their doctor before using ashwagandha, as immune-modulating herbs can occasionally exacerbate autoimmune conditions.

How Ashwagandha Affects Hormones After 40

The hormonal effects of ashwagandha in women over 40 deserve careful discussion, because this is an area where both overselling and underselling are common.

Ashwagandha is not estrogenic in the traditional sense. It does not contain phytoestrogens and does not directly bind to estrogen receptors the way compounds like isoflavones in soy do. However, it may influence the hormonal environment in meaningful indirect ways.

Its primary hormonal effect is through cortisol reduction. Cortisol and sex hormones compete for precursors (the building blocks from which they are synthesized), particularly pregnenolone. When the body is chronically stressed and producing high levels of cortisol, less pregnenolone is available for conversion to DHEA, progesterone, testosterone, and ultimately estrogen. This is sometimes called the "pregnenolone steal." By reducing cortisol output, ashwagandha may free up hormonal precursors for sex hormone production. While this mechanism is biologically plausible and supported by some preclinical data, direct evidence in perimenopausal women showing measurable increases in estrogen or progesterone from ashwagandha alone is limited.

DHEA, the adrenal precursor hormone that declines significantly with age, has been shown in some studies to increase modestly with ashwagandha supplementation. Since DHEA serves as a building block for both estrogen and testosterone, this may have downstream hormonal benefits.

Testosterone appears to increase modestly in women using ashwagandha in some studies. This is relevant because testosterone in women is important for libido, energy, muscle strength, and mood. A small but growing body of research in women supports a link between ashwagandha and improved testosterone levels and their functional effects.

The thyroid connection is also worth noting. Thyroid hormones regulate metabolic rate and energy. Research suggests ashwagandha may improve T4 to T3 conversion, which would support energy levels and metabolism. For women over 40 who notice fatigue and metabolic changes that are not fully explained by sex hormone shifts, thyroid function and its potential support through ashwagandha is a relevant consideration.

Stress, Cortisol, and Ashwagandha: What Science Shows

The best-documented effect of ashwagandha, and likely its most clinically relevant one for women over 40, is its ability to reduce cortisol and improve the body's management of stress.

Chronic stress is a defining feature of midlife for many women. Whether the stress comes from work, caregiving, relationship demands, or the physiological stress of hormonal change itself, the result is sustained elevated cortisol that interferes with sleep, mood, metabolism, immune function, and sex hormone production.

Multiple randomized controlled trials have measured serum cortisol levels before and after ashwagandha supplementation and found consistent, statistically significant reductions. A landmark study published in the Indian Journal of Psychological Medicine (2012) found that participants taking 300 mg of high-concentration full-spectrum ashwagandha root extract twice daily for 60 days had significantly lower serum cortisol levels, reduced stress and anxiety scores, and better sleep quality compared to placebo. The average reduction in cortisol in the treatment group was approximately 27.9 percent.

This cortisol-reducing effect has meaningful downstream consequences for perimenopausal women. Lower cortisol means better sleep. Better sleep means more effective emotional regulation and less anxiety. Lower cortisol also means less interference with sex hormone production and metabolism. The sympathetic nervous system (fight-or-flight) is dialed back, and the parasympathetic (rest-and-repair) system has more influence. This creates conditions in which other aspects of hormonal health are more likely to stabilize.

Ashwagandha also appears to reduce C-reactive protein (a marker of inflammation) and may support immune regulation, both of which are relevant since chronic low-grade inflammation is increasingly understood to contribute to fatigue, brain fog, and metabolic changes in midlife women.

What Ashwagandha Does NOT Do (Managing Expectations)

In the wellness industry, adaptogens are frequently presented as near-miraculous solutions. It is important to set accurate expectations about what ashwagandha can and cannot do for women over 40, because unrealistic expectations lead to disappointment and, sometimes, to avoiding the interventions that would actually help most.

Ashwagandha is not a hormone replacement therapy. It does not replace estrogen, progesterone, or testosterone. If perimenopause symptoms are severe and directly driven by significant hormone deficiency, ashwagandha alone is unlikely to be sufficient. It may support the endocrine system and reduce the burden of stress on hormonal balance, but it does not supply hormones.

Ashwagandha is not a cure for perimenopause or menopause. It may reduce the severity of some symptoms, particularly those related to stress, sleep, mood, and cognitive function, but it will not stop the natural progression of hormonal transition or prevent any of its defining biological changes.

Ashwagandha does not work immediately. Most clinical trials showing meaningful effects used 8 to 12 weeks of consistent supplementation. If you try ashwagandha for a week and feel no different, that does not mean it is not working. The effects are gradual and cumulative.

Ashwagandha may not be appropriate for everyone. Women who are pregnant or breastfeeding should avoid it. Women with autoimmune conditions should consult their doctor. Women on thyroid medications, sedatives, immunosuppressants, or other medications that may interact should seek medical guidance before adding ashwagandha.

Ashwagandha is a supplement, not a substitute for foundational health behaviors. No herb will compensate for chronically poor sleep, a highly inflammatory diet, no physical activity, and unmanaged stress. Ashwagandha works best as an addition to a strong foundation, not as a replacement for one.

How to Take Ashwagandha and What to Look For

The gap between a clinical-grade ashwagandha product and a poorly formulated one is large. Understanding what to look for helps ensure you are getting a product that actually reflects the evidence.

Dosage in well-designed clinical trials typically ranges from 300 mg to 600 mg of standardized root extract daily, often divided into two doses. Lower doses (below 150 mg) used in many commercial products may not deliver sufficient withanolide content to produce measurable effects.

Standardization matters enormously. Look for products standardized to at least 2.5 to 5 percent withanolides. Products that list only "ashwagandha root powder" without standardization may contain highly variable concentrations of active compounds. Branded extracts with published clinical evidence behind them, including KSM-66 (which uses only root, not root and leaves) and Sensoril (full-spectrum extract), are the best-supported options.

Root versus leaf matters. Some cheaper products use ashwagandha leaf, which has a different withanolide profile from the root. Most clinical research on stress, cortisol, and menopausal symptom reduction has been done on root extracts. Root-only preparations are generally preferred.

Ashwagandha can be taken with food to reduce the mild GI effects some people experience. It can be taken in the morning or evening. Some women prefer taking it at night given its mildly calming properties, which may support sleep. Taking it consistently at the same time each day supports stable serum levels.

Synergistic combinations are worth considering. Ashwagandha works well alongside other stress-regulating and energizing nutrients. Pairing it with NAD+ support addresses cellular energy metabolism, which declines with both age and chronic stress, providing complementary benefits across the energy, hormonal, and cognitive dimensions of midlife health. The combination supports both the adaptogenic (top-down stress regulation) and the cellular bioenergetics (bottom-up energy production) aspects of healthy aging.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does ashwagandha take to work for women over 40?

Most clinical trials showing significant effects used supplementation periods of 8 to 12 weeks. Some women notice initial improvements in sleep and stress within 2 to 4 weeks, but full benefits for cortisol regulation and hormonal support typically develop with consistent use over 2 to 3 months.

Can ashwagandha affect estrogen levels in women?

Ashwagandha does not directly supply estrogen or bind to estrogen receptors like phytoestrogens do. It may support the hormonal environment indirectly by reducing cortisol and freeing up hormonal precursors. Some women report reduced menopausal symptoms with ashwagandha use, but it is not a substitute for estrogen therapy when estrogen deficiency is the primary concern.

Is ashwagandha safe to take every day for women over 40?

Clinical trials have used ashwagandha daily for periods of up to 12 weeks without significant adverse effects in healthy adults. Long-term daily use is generally considered safe at recommended doses, though cycling (taking breaks every 2 to 3 months) is sometimes recommended. Women with specific health conditions or on medications should consult a healthcare provider.

Can ashwagandha help with perimenopause hot flashes?

Research published in the Journal of Ethnopharmacology found that perimenopausal women taking standardized ashwagandha extract reported significant reductions in hot flash frequency compared to placebo over 8 weeks. While not all women respond equally, this is a promising area with real clinical data behind it.

Does ashwagandha interact with any medications?

Ashwagandha may interact with thyroid medications, immunosuppressants, sedatives, and medications for blood pressure or blood sugar. Women taking any of these should discuss ashwagandha with their healthcare provider before starting supplementation.
