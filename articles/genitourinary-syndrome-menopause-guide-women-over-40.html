What to Know

  • Genitourinary Syndrome of Menopause (GSM) is the official clinical term adopted in 2014 to describe the full range of estrogen-deficiency changes affecting the vagina, vulva, and lower urinary tract after menopause.
  • An estimated 27% to 84% of postmenopausal women experience GSM symptoms, yet fewer than 25% seek treatment because many believe these changes are simply a normal, unavoidable part of aging.
  • GSM symptoms include vaginal dryness, burning, painful intercourse, vaginal pH changes, urinary urgency, and increased susceptibility to urinary tract infections.
  • Unlike hot flashes and night sweats, GSM symptoms do not improve on their own over time. Without support, they typically worsen progressively.
  • Effective options exist, ranging from non-hormonal moisturizers and lubricants to prescription local estrogen and DHEA. Treatment should be personalized with a healthcare provider.
  • Nutritional support, including phytoestrogen-rich foods and anti-inflammatory dietary patterns, may complement medical treatment.

Half of all postmenopausal women live with symptoms they have never been given a proper name for. Dryness. Burning. Pain during intimacy. Recurring bladder infections. These are not isolated complaints. They are connected expressions of a single underlying process, and they have a clinical name: Genitourinary Syndrome of Menopause.

What Is Genitourinary Syndrome of Menopause?

Genitourinary Syndrome of Menopause (GSM) is the umbrella term that replaced the older, narrower term "vaginal atrophy" in 2014. It was introduced by a consensus panel convened by the International Society for the Study of Women's Sexual Health (ISSWSH) and the North American Menopause Society (NAMS), led by Portman and Gass, and published in Menopause and the Journal of Sexual Medicine (PMID 25179577). The change was not just linguistic. "Vaginal atrophy" failed to capture the urinary symptoms that many women experience, and the word "atrophy" carried a stigma that discouraged women from discussing symptoms with their doctors.

GSM encompasses all estrogen-deficiency related changes to the vulva, vagina, and lower urinary tract, including: thinning and loss of elasticity of vaginal tissue, decreased vaginal lubrication and secretions, rising vaginal pH (from the normal acidic range below 4.5 to a more alkaline range above 5.0), loss of vaginal folds (rugae), urethral thinning and loss of support, increased urinary frequency and urgency, heightened UTI susceptibility, and vulvar irritation and dryness. Because these tissues all share estrogen sensitivity, they all respond to the same hormonal decline that occurs naturally at menopause.

Why It Matters for Women Over 40

The vaginal and vulvar tissues, the urethra, and the bladder trigone are all rich in estrogen receptors. During the reproductive years, estrogen maintains the thickness, elasticity, blood flow, and lubrication of these tissues, and supports a healthy vaginal microbiome dominated by Lactobacillus bacteria. These Lactobacillus species produce lactic acid, which keeps vaginal pH low and creates an environment hostile to pathogens.

When estrogen declines at menopause, this entire ecosystem shifts. Vaginal walls thin. Blood flow decreases. Lubrication diminishes. The pH rises, and the Lactobacillus population drops, making the vagina more susceptible to bacterial vaginosis, yeast overgrowth, and ascending urinary infections. The urethra and bladder trigone lose their estrogen-maintained tone, contributing to urinary urgency, frequency, and incomplete bladder emptying.

An important distinction from vasomotor symptoms (hot flashes, night sweats): those typically improve over time as the body adjusts to lower estrogen levels. GSM does not. Without intervention, GSM symptoms tend to worsen progressively, because the underlying tissue changes are not temporary adaptations but ongoing structural shifts driven by a permanently lower estrogen environment.

What the Research Says

The landmark 2014 consensus statement by Portman and Gass (PMID 25179577) established that GSM impacts an estimated 27% to 84% of postmenopausal women, depending on the symptom definition used and the population studied. Despite this prevalence, surveys consistently show that fewer than one in four affected women discusses these symptoms with a healthcare provider. The most common reason given: believing the symptoms are normal and that nothing can be done.

For treatment, local estrogen therapy (vaginal estrogen cream, tablet, or ring) has the most robust evidence base, with multiple randomized controlled trials demonstrating improvement in vaginal tissue health, lubrication, pH normalization, and reduction of dyspareunia. Because the dose is local and systemic absorption is minimal, local estrogen is generally considered safe even for many women who are not candidates for systemic hormone therapy.

Prasterone (intravaginal DHEA) was FDA-approved in 2016 for treating dyspareunia due to GSM, with clinical trials showing improvements in vaginal epithelial health and reduction in pain during intercourse. Ospemifene, a selective estrogen receptor modulator (SERM) taken orally, is FDA-approved for moderate to severe dyspareunia and vaginal dryness in women with GSM.

Non-hormonal options, including vaginal moisturizers (used regularly, not just during intercourse) and lubricants (used specifically during sexual activity), have good evidence for improving dryness and reducing discomfort. These are appropriate first-line options for women who prefer non-hormonal approaches or cannot use estrogen.

What the Evidence Doesn't Support

One of the most persistent myths about GSM is that it is simply "natural" and must be accepted. While the hormonal changes driving GSM are natural, the tissue damage they cause and the symptoms that result are not something women need to endure without recourse. There are safe, evidence-supported interventions available for most women, including those who cannot or prefer not to use estrogen.

The notion that over-the-counter soy isoflavone supplements reliably replicate the benefits of local estrogen for GSM is not supported by current evidence. While phytoestrogens, including isoflavones from soy and red clover, have been studied for menopausal symptoms broadly, evidence for significant improvement in vaginal tissue changes is limited and inconsistent. A 2016 review in the American Journal of Obstetrics and Gynecology noted that phytoestrogens do not produce the tissue-level changes that local estrogen does. They may be a reasonable complementary dietary choice, but they are not a substitute for clinical treatment of moderate to severe GSM.

Using heavily scented soaps, feminine hygiene sprays, or douching to manage GSM symptoms can worsen the condition significantly. These products disrupt the vaginal microbiome further, raise pH, and can cause contact dermatitis on already fragile tissue. The vagina's self-cleaning mechanisms are supported by, not improved by, these products.

Happy Aging's position: GSM deserves to be taken seriously as a medical condition. Women should feel empowered to discuss these symptoms openly with their healthcare providers and to ask specifically about the full range of treatment options, including local estrogen, which is under-prescribed relative to its evidence base and safety profile for most women.

Use that term with your provider. Ask about local estrogen therapy as a first-line option, and ask whether you are a candidate. Who This Is NOT For

This article is educational and does not substitute for individual medical evaluation. Women who experience bleeding from the vagina after menopause, a new mass or lesion in the vaginal or vulvar area, or symptoms that came on suddenly and intensely should seek prompt medical evaluation, as these can indicate conditions that require diagnosis before treatment. Women with a personal history of estrogen-sensitive cancers (including certain breast cancers) should discuss any hormone-containing options, including local estrogen, with their oncologist before use. The safety profile of local vaginal estrogen in breast cancer survivors is an active area of medical discussion, and decisions should be individualized.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between GSM and vaginal atrophy?

Vaginal atrophy was the older term used to describe thinning and drying of vaginal tissue after menopause. GSM is the newer, more comprehensive clinical term adopted in 2014 by ISSWSH and NAMS. GSM is preferred because it captures not just vaginal changes but also vulvar and lower urinary tract symptoms, and because "atrophy" carried stigma that discouraged women from seeking care. Clinically, they overlap substantially, but GSM is the term you are more likely to encounter in current medical literature and conversations with providers.

Does GSM go away on its own?

Unlike hot flashes, which often diminish over the first few years after menopause, GSM symptoms do not resolve on their own. They are driven by persistently low estrogen levels, which remain low for the rest of a woman's life after menopause. Without some form of support (local estrogen, regular use of vaginal moisturizers, or other interventions), GSM symptoms typically worsen progressively over time. This is one of the most important reasons to begin addressing them early.

Is local estrogen safe if I have had breast cancer?

This is a nuanced question that should be discussed with an oncologist, not answered with a blanket rule. For many breast cancer survivors, particularly those not on aromatase inhibitors, the minimal systemic absorption of low-dose vaginal estrogen may be acceptable. Major oncology organizations have issued statements acknowledging that non-hormonal approaches should be tried first, and that the decision to use vaginal estrogen in breast cancer survivors is individualized. Your oncology team is the right partner for this conversation.

What is the difference between a vaginal lubricant and a vaginal moisturizer?

A vaginal lubricant is used immediately before or during sexual activity to reduce friction. It provides temporary relief and is not absorbed into tissue. A vaginal moisturizer is applied regularly (typically 2-3 times per week) and is designed to restore moisture to vaginal tissue over time, similar to how a face moisturizer works on facial skin. Both are useful for GSM, and they serve different functions. Moisturizers are the longer-term tissue support; lubricants are the situational solution during intimacy. Look for fragrance-free, paraben-free formulations with a pH close to the natural vaginal range (around 3.5-4.5).

Can diet help with GSM?

Diet alone will not reverse the tissue-level changes of GSM, but dietary patterns matter as a complement to other approaches. Foods rich in phytoestrogens (flaxseeds, edamame, tofu, tempeh) may offer modest support for hormonal balance. An anti-inflammatory dietary pattern, rich in omega-3 fatty acids (salmon, sardines, walnuts) and antioxidants (leafy greens, berries, olive oil), supports tissue health broadly. Adequate hydration also supports mucosal tissue throughout the body. These are supportive strategies, not replacements for clinical treatment of moderate to severe GSM.
