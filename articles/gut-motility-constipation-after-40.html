Constipation affects approximately 33 percent of adults over 60 and is significantly more common in women than in men throughout adult life. For women in the menopausal transition, constipation and sluggish digestion often worsen noticeably, frequently at the same time that other perimenopausal symptoms emerge. Most women are not told that estrogen and progesterone directly regulate gut motility, which means the same hormonal shifts driving hot flashes and sleep disruption are also slowing the digestive system. Understanding the mechanism makes it possible to address constipation more intelligently than the standard advice of drinking more water and eating bran.

What to Know About Gut Motility After 40

  • Estrogen and progesterone both regulate gastrointestinal motility; as progesterone rises in the second half of the menstrual cycle, it relaxes smooth muscle including gut muscle, which is why constipation often worsens premenstrually
  • In perimenopause, erratic progesterone fluctuations and eventual decline disrupt the hormonal regulation of motility in a less predictable way
  • Colonic transit time increases with age in women; the gut becomes less efficient at moving contents forward
  • Thyroid function, which often declines subtly in women after 40, is another major regulator of gut motility; hypothyroidism causes constipation even in subclinical ranges
  • Targeted interventions including specific fiber types, magnesium, movement timing, and microbiome support address the distinct mechanisms of age-related constipation more effectively than generic laxatives

Why Gut Motility Changes After 40: The Hormonal Drivers

Gastrointestinal motility is the coordinated movement of smooth muscle throughout the digestive tract that propels food through digestion. This coordination depends on the enteric nervous system (ENS), the “gut brain,” as well as systemic hormones including estrogen and progesterone.

Progesterone is a smooth muscle relaxant. This is well understood in the context of pregnancy, where rising progesterone prevents uterine contractions. The gut is also smooth muscle, and progesterone slows peristalsis (the wave-like contractions that move intestinal contents forward) in the same way. This is why many women experience constipation in the luteal phase of their menstrual cycle (days 15-28), when progesterone is highest, and why constipation improves with menstruation as progesterone drops. During perimenopause, progesterone fluctuates erratically and eventually declines, leading to an irregular pattern of intestinal motility that can present as alternating constipation and looser stools.

Estrogen’s role is more complex. Estrogen receptors are present in colon tissue, and estrogen generally supports intestinal motility and water secretion in the gut. As estrogen declines, the gut tends toward slower transit and reduced water secretion, contributing to harder, less frequent stools. The combined loss of both hormonal regulatory inputs creates the gut motility changes many women notice acutely in perimenopause.

The Thyroid Connection

The thyroid plays a critical but underrecognized role in gut motility. Thyroid hormone (T3 and T4) regulates the activity of the interstitial cells of Cajal, the pacemaker cells of the gut that drive rhythmic peristaltic contractions. In hypothyroidism, these pacemaker cells slow down, and intestinal transit time increases significantly.

Women are five to ten times more likely than men to develop hypothyroidism, with the highest incidence in women over 40. Subclinical hypothyroidism (TSH elevated but below clinical treatment thresholds) is associated with constipation, bloating, and reduced gastric emptying even when T3 and T4 remain in reference ranges. Research by Chang et al. (2012, PMID: 22236946) found that even subclinical hypothyroid states produced measurable increases in colonic transit time and stool consistency changes consistent with constipation.

Women over 40 who experience worsening constipation without clear dietary explanation should have a comprehensive thyroid panel (TSH, free T3, free T4, and thyroid antibodies) as part of investigating the root cause, particularly if constipation is accompanied by fatigue, cold sensitivity, weight changes, or hair thinning.

Fiber: The Most Evidence-Supported Intervention

Dietary fiber remains the foundational intervention for constipation, but the type of fiber matters more than most recommendations acknowledge. The relevant distinction is between insoluble fiber (which adds bulk and speeds colonic transit) and soluble fiber (which forms a gel, feeds the microbiome, and softens stool).

Insoluble fiber sources including wheat bran, corn bran, and cellulose from vegetables accelerate colonic transit by increasing stool bulk and stimulating stretch receptors in the colon that trigger peristaltic contractions. This mechanism is most effective in women with slow-transit constipation where the physical bulk stimulus is reduced.

Soluble fiber, including psyllium husk, oat beta-glucan, and legume fiber, absorbs water and forms a viscous gel that softens stool and reduces straining. A meta-analysis by Suares and Ford (2011, PMID: 21230770) in the American Journal of Gastroenterology found that psyllium husk supplementation significantly improved stool frequency and consistency in adults with chronic constipation. Psyllium at 10 to 20 grams daily is one of the most evidence-supported single interventions for functional constipation.

The combination of both fiber types, which a diverse whole-food diet provides naturally, is more effective than either alone. The practical target is 25 to 35 grams of total dietary fiber daily, with adequate hydration (at least 1.5 to 2 liters of water) to allow soluble fiber to function appropriately. Taking fiber without adequate fluid can worsen constipation rather than improve it.

Magnesium: The Underrated Osmotic Support

Magnesium has a dual mechanism for relieving constipation that makes it particularly useful for women over 40. Magnesium citrate and magnesium oxide draw water into the colon through osmotic effects, softening stool and stimulating contractions. Separately, magnesium’s role in smooth muscle relaxation and nerve signaling may improve the coordinated peristaltic activity that drives colonic transit.

A randomized controlled trial by Mori et al. (2016, PMID: 27306418) in the European Journal of Nutrition examined low-dose magnesium oxide supplementation in adults with functional constipation and found significant improvements in stool frequency and consistency at 1.5 grams of magnesium oxide daily compared to placebo. This is a relatively high dose of magnesium oxide (which has low absorption but high osmotic activity in the gut); for women who also want systemic magnesium benefits (sleep, muscle function), magnesium citrate at 300 to 400 mg daily provides moderate osmotic support alongside better systemic absorption than oxide.

Magnesium-based support for constipation works best as a consistent daily supplement rather than an as-needed laxative. The dose-response relationship is nonlinear: below the threshold needed for osmotic effect, magnesium may not improve constipation, but above it, the osmotic effect can become diarrhea. Starting at 200 to 300 mg of magnesium citrate with dinner and adjusting based on response allows individual calibration.

Movement and the Gut

Physical activity is consistently associated with improved gut motility through multiple mechanisms. Exercise stimulates colonic smooth muscle activity directly through mechanical stimulation and activates the sympathetic-enteric nervous system interface that drives peristalsis. A systematic review by Chey et al. (2016, PMID: 26480550) found that physical activity interventions significantly improved self-reported constipation symptoms and objective stool frequency compared to sedentary controls.

Timing matters as well. Morning exercise, particularly walking, stimulates the gastrocolic reflex, the physiological connection between stomach filling and colonic motility that produces the urge to defecate after meals. A morning walk of 20 to 30 minutes before or after breakfast capitalizes on the natural gastrocolic reflex timing and can significantly improve regularity for women with sluggish morning motility.

The sedentary patterns that many women fall into during desk-based work and evening relaxation reduce the stimulation of gut motility throughout the day. Breaking sitting time with brief walking every hour, consistent with general metabolic health recommendations, also benefits gut transit time.

Probiotics and the Microbiome

The gut microbiome plays an active role in intestinal motility through several mechanisms. Certain bacterial species produce short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) that stimulate enteroendocrine cells in the gut lining to release serotonin (5-HT). The gut contains approximately 95 percent of the body’s total serotonin, and gut serotonin is the primary neurotransmitter driving peristaltic motor patterns in the enteric nervous system. Women with dysbiosis, reduced microbial diversity, or depleted SCFA-producing bacteria have reduced serotonergic stimulation of gut motility and often experience constipation as a result.

A systematic review and meta-analysis by Miller et al. (2017, PMID: 27765705) found that probiotic supplementation significantly increased stool frequency (by an average of 1.3 bowel movements per week) and improved stool consistency in adults with constipation. The most effective strains for constipation in the literature are Bifidobacterium lactis (multiple studies), Lactobacillus casei Shirota, and Bifidobacterium longum. Multi-strain formulas including these species alongside prebiotic fiber substrates show more consistent benefits than single-strain products.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why did I suddenly become constipated in my 40s?

Hormonal changes are the most common explanation. The erratic progesterone fluctuations of perimenopause, combined with declining estrogen, disrupt the hormonal regulation of gut smooth muscle that maintained more regular motility earlier in life. Thyroid changes (hypothyroidism becomes more common after 40), reduced physical activity, lower dietary fiber intake, and changes in gut microbiome diversity also contribute. Worsening constipation in the 40s without obvious dietary changes warrants evaluation of thyroid function and consideration of hormonal factors.

Is it safe to use laxatives long-term?

It depends on the type. Stimulant laxatives (senna, bisacodyl) are intended for occasional use and can cause dependence and reduced intrinsic gut motility with prolonged daily use. Osmotic laxatives like polyethylene glycol (PEG/Miralax) and magnesium-based products are considered safer for longer-term use when lifestyle measures are insufficient. Bulk-forming laxatives (psyllium, methylcellulose) are essentially high-dose fiber supplements and are safe for ongoing daily use. For chronic constipation, addressing root causes (fiber, hydration, movement, thyroid, microbiome) is preferable to long-term stimulant laxative dependence.

How much water do I need to drink to help with constipation?

Hydration is essential for fiber to work properly and for stool to remain soft enough to pass comfortably. Most evidence supports a minimum of 1.5 to 2 liters (50 to 70 ounces) of water daily for women with constipation, with more needed in hot weather or during exercise. Dehydration significantly worsens constipation by increasing the reabsorption of water from stool in the large intestine, producing harder, drier stools. Coffee and tea count toward fluid intake but have mild diuretic effects; plain water and herbal teas are the most reliable hydration sources.

Can stress cause constipation in women over 40?

Yes, significantly. Chronic psychological stress activates the sympathetic nervous system, which inhibits the parasympathetic (rest and digest) input that drives gut motility. High cortisol directly reduces peristaltic activity and can cause the enteric nervous system to shift from motility-promoting to motility-inhibiting signaling patterns. Women over 40 experiencing high stress alongside constipation often find that stress management interventions (yoga, sleep improvement, adaptogenic herbs) improve both conditions simultaneously, reflecting their shared autonomic nervous system driver.

What position helps with constipation?

Defecation posture matters more than most people realize. Research has shown that a squatting position (or the use of a toilet footstool to raise the knees above hip height while seated) straightens the anorectal angle, reducing the resistance to stool passage and decreasing the muscular effort required. A randomized study by Sakakibara et al. (2010, PMID: 20346679) found that using a footstool to raise the knees significantly improved subjective straining and stool consistency in adults with functional constipation. This simple mechanical change addresses one structural factor that compounds the motility issues driving constipation after 40.
