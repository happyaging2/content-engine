Why It Matters for Women Over 40

The gut changes that come with perimenopause and menopause are real, underappreciated, and often frustrating. Women who never had significant digestive issues in their 30s frequently report increased bloating, more food sensitivities, slower digestion, and changes in bowel regularity starting in their mid-40s.

Several mechanisms drive this. First, estrogen and progesterone both affect gut motility: the gut has estrogen receptors throughout the enteric nervous system, and as estrogen fluctuates and eventually declines, gut transit time slows. This is why constipation becomes more common in perimenopause. Second, the gut microbiome itself changes with hormonal status. The estrobolome (the subset of gut bacteria involved in estrogen metabolism) shifts, affecting both estrogen recycling and overall microbial diversity. Third, stomach acid production and digestive enzyme output tend to decline with age, making it harder to fully break down proteins and complex carbohydrates. The result is more fermentation in the gut, which means more gas and bloating.

This context matters for choosing between these two products. A probiotic alone addresses one piece of the puzzle: microbiome support. Digestive enzymes address a different piece: efficient food breakdown before fermentation occurs. For women whose bloating is primarily driven by undigested food reaching the large intestine (very common after 40), enzymes may provide relief that probiotics alone do not.

According to Happy Aging's review of gut health interventions for women over 40, the women who see the most meaningful improvement typically combine prebiotic fiber, probiotic support, and digestive enzyme optimization rather than relying on any single intervention.

What the Research Says

Bacillus coagulans, the probiotic strain in Olly, has genuine research support. It is one of the better-studied spore-forming strains in the market, with evidence for reducing IBS symptoms, improving stool consistency, and supporting digestive comfort. Its spore-forming nature means it survives the manufacturing process for gummies (which involves heat) and the acidic environment of the stomach better than most lactobacillus strains. For general probiotic support, Bacillus coagulans is a credible choice.

The evidence for digestive enzymes in age-related digestive decline is also meaningful. Exocrine pancreatic insufficiency, where the pancreas produces insufficient digestive enzymes, is a recognized medical condition that becomes more prevalent with age. Even subclinical enzyme insufficiency, which is harder to diagnose but very common, contributes to bloating and incomplete digestion. Supplemental digestive enzymes (particularly lipase, amylase, and protease) have clinical evidence for reducing bloating and digestive discomfort in adults with enzyme insufficiency.

The research on inulin as a prebiotic is well-established. Inulin selectively feeds bifidobacteria and promotes short-chain fatty acid production. Both Olly and Happiest Gut include prebiotic fiber; the question is the breadth of the prebiotic blend, which affects which bacterial populations it supports.

Research suggests that a diverse prebiotic fiber intake (multiple fiber types rather than a single fiber source) better supports microbial diversity, because different bacterial species ferment different fiber types. A blend of prebiotic fibers tends to be more effective than a single source for women aiming to support a broad, diverse microbiome.

What the Evidence Doesn't Support

Neither product should be marketed or understood as a treatment for irritable bowel syndrome, inflammatory bowel disease, or any specific gastrointestinal condition. Probiotics in general have highly variable evidence across conditions, and consumer supplement probiotics are not the same as the specific strains tested in clinical trials for specific conditions.

The idea that more CFUs automatically means a better product is not supported by the evidence. What matters is strain identity (which specific strain it is), whether the strain survives to the gut, and whether it is appropriate for the target outcome. A gummy probiotic with 1 billion CFU of a well-studied, shelf-stable spore former can outperform a capsule with 50 billion CFU of a fragile lactobacillus strain that does not survive manufacturing and digestion.

Neither Olly nor Happiest Gut will produce meaningful results without dietary support. Probiotic bacteria need fermentable fiber to survive and thrive in the gut. Women eating a low-fiber, highly processed diet will see limited benefit from either supplement. Diet is the foundation; supplements are additions to a solid foundation, not replacements for one.

Who This Is NOT For

Neither product is a treatment for diagnosed gastrointestinal conditions such as Crohn's disease, ulcerative colitis, celiac disease, or small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO). If you have a diagnosed GI condition, your probiotic and enzyme decisions should be made with a gastroenterologist, as some probiotic strains can worsen certain conditions.

If you are immunocompromised, probiotics carry a small but real risk and should be discussed with your physician before starting. This applies to both Olly and Happiest Gut.

Women with significant bloating that is new, progressive, or accompanied by unintended weight loss, rectal bleeding, persistent pain, or changes in bowel habits should see a physician before attributing symptoms to menopause-related gut changes. New, progressive digestive symptoms in women over 40 warrant medical evaluation to rule out structural causes.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to see results from either product?

Digestive enzyme supplements typically produce noticeable results within the first week, often within days, because they work mechanically on each meal rather than needing to colonize and grow over time. Probiotics have a slower timeline: most research shows meaningful microbiome changes and symptom improvement over 4 to 8 weeks of consistent use. If you choose Happiest Gut, expect the enzyme component to work quickly and the probiotic/prebiotic component to build over weeks.

Can I take both Olly and Happiest Gut together?

There is no known safety concern with taking both, but it would not be cost-effective or necessary. The supplement categories overlap significantly. If you already have Olly, you could start there and transition to Happiest Gut if symptoms persist. Starting fresh with Happiest Gut is the more comprehensive approach.

Does Olly use enough CFUs to actually work?

One billion CFU is on the lower end of what many clinical trials use, but it is important to understand that Bacillus coagulans is a spore-forming strain with exceptional survival compared to fragile lactobacillus strains. A smaller dose of a surviving, well-studied strain often delivers more benefit than a larger dose of a strain that does not survive to the gut. The 1 billion CFU dose in Olly is clinically relevant for this specific strain.

I've heard Olly is not as serious as pharmaceutical-grade probiotics. Is that fair?

Olly is a consumer supplement, not a medical-grade probiotic. There is a meaningful difference between supplements used in clinical trials (which specify exact strains, doses, and manufacturing standards) and consumer products. That said, Bacillus coagulans is a legitimate, well-studied strain, and Olly's manufacturing standards are above average for the mass-market category. It is a credible consumer product, not a clinical-grade therapeutic.

Is the powder form of Happiest Gut a disadvantage for travel?

Powder supplements require planning for travel: you need a container or pre-measured packets. For short trips, many women use a small reusable container. Some women switch to a simpler probiotic capsule while traveling and return to Happiest Gut at home. The powder format is a minor inconvenience that most users adapt to quickly.
