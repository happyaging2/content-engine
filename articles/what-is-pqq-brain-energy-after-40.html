PQQ is a nutrient that most women over 40 have never heard of, yet it performs a function that no other known compound can replicate: it stimulates the growth of entirely new mitochondria in existing cells, a process called mitochondrial biogenesis. At a time in life when mitochondria are declining in both number and function, and when brain energy is one of the first places women feel that decline as brain fog and cognitive fatigue, PQQ offers a genuinely differentiated mechanism for supporting mental clarity and energy. Here is what the research actually shows.

What to Know

  • PQQ (pyrroloquinoline quinone) is a redox-active compound found in many foods that acts as a cofactor for a class of bacterial enzymes and serves antioxidant and mitochondria-stimulating functions in human cells.
  • PQQ is the only known dietary compound that stimulates mitochondrial biogenesis: the growth of new mitochondria in existing cells. It does this by activating PGC-1alpha, the master regulator of mitochondrial growth.
  • Mitochondria decline in number and function with age, and this decline is a primary driver of the cognitive fatigue, reduced processing speed, and mental fog many women notice after 40.
  • Human clinical trials show PQQ supplementation improves cognitive performance, reduces fatigue, and improves sleep quality in older adults at doses of 20 mg per day.
  • PQQ and CoQ10 work through complementary mechanisms and are often taken together for synergistic mitochondrial support.

What Is PQQ and Where Does It Come From?

Pyrroloquinoline quinone (PQQ) is a small, redox-active quinone compound that was first identified in bacteria as a cofactor for certain oxidoreductase enzymes. It was initially proposed as a new B vitamin in the early 1990s, but this designation was later contested because the body does not synthesize it endogenously and definitive proof of a specific enzyme-cofactor role in mammals was not established. Despite the B-vitamin classification being withdrawn, PQQ is present in virtually all foods tested (plants, breast milk, fermented foods) and produces clear biological effects in mammals at nutritional levels.

The richest food sources of PQQ include fermented soybean products like natto, green peppers, parsley, kiwi fruit, and papaya. Green tea and oolong tea provide meaningful amounts. Human breast milk has among the highest PQQ concentrations of any tested fluid, suggesting it is important during early development. Typical dietary intake of PQQ in Western diets is estimated at 0.1 to 1 mg per day, which is roughly 10 to 100 times below the 10 to 20 mg daily doses used in human clinical trials showing cognitive and mitochondrial benefits.

PQQ functions as a redox catalyst, meaning it can cycle between oxidized and reduced forms, donating and accepting electrons repeatedly without being consumed. This cycling capacity gives it a potency as an antioxidant that far exceeds single-use antioxidants like vitamin C. PQQ can cycle through thousands of redox cycles before being degraded, making a very small amount capable of neutralizing many times its molecular weight in reactive oxygen species. In the electron-dense environment of mitochondria, this catalytic antioxidant activity is particularly valuable for protecting the mitochondrial membrane from the oxidative damage that is a constant byproduct of ATP production.

PQQ and Mitochondrial Biogenesis: The Unique Mechanism

The most remarkable property of PQQ is its ability to stimulate the creation of entirely new mitochondria in existing cells through mitochondrial biogenesis. This is distinct from simply improving the function of existing mitochondria, which CoQ10 and other compounds do. PQQ literally promotes the growth of additional mitochondria, expanding the cell's energy production capacity rather than just optimizing existing capacity.

The mechanism involves PQQ's activation of CREB (cyclic AMP response element-binding protein), a transcription factor that upregulates PGC-1alpha, the master regulatory protein of mitochondrial biogenesis. PGC-1alpha, when activated, coordinates the expression of hundreds of genes involved in mitochondrial construction, fuel utilization, and antioxidant defense. It is the same pathway activated by endurance exercise (which famously increases mitochondrial density in muscle cells) and caloric restriction (associated with increased mitochondrial efficiency and longevity in animal models).

Research by Stites and colleagues at UC Davis confirmed that dietary PQQ modulates mitochondrial density and function in animal models, finding that animals raised on PQQ-deficient diets had abnormal mitochondria with reduced capacity, while PQQ supplementation reversed these changes. This mechanistic research established that PQQ is genuinely required for optimal mitochondrial development and maintenance.

For women over 40, who experience progressive mitochondrial decline as a key driver of fatigue, cognitive slowdown, and cellular aging, the ability of PQQ to stimulate new mitochondrial growth represents a physiologically meaningful intervention. Exercise does the same thing in muscle cells, but in brain cells, where the neuronal mitochondria that support cognition and mental energy are particularly difficult to reach through exercise alone, PQQ offers a complementary systemic approach.

PQQ and Brain Function in Human Clinical Trials

Several human clinical trials have examined PQQ's effects on cognitive function and brain energy specifically, providing the most directly relevant evidence for women over 40 concerned about mental clarity and focus.

A double-blind, placebo-controlled trial by Itoh and colleagues published in Functional Foods in Health and Disease examined PQQ supplementation at 20 mg daily in adults aged 50 to 70 with subjective cognitive decline. After 12 weeks, the PQQ group showed significant improvements in composite cognitive scores compared to placebo, with the most pronounced benefits in attention, working memory, and cognitive processing speed. These are precisely the cognitive domains that women over 40 most frequently report declining in the early stages of age-related cognitive change.

A study by Ohwada and colleagues published in the Journal of Clinical Biochemistry and Nutrition examined PQQ supplementation in older adults and found improvements in cognitive measures including the Stroop color word test (a measure of attention and cognitive flexibility) and the Digit Symbol substitution test (working memory and processing speed). These standardized cognitive assessment tools are sensitive to the mild cognitive changes of normal aging, making them appropriate measures for a study designed to detect brain energy improvements rather than treatment of clinical cognitive impairment.

A study by Harris and colleagues at UC Davis (published in the Journal of Nutritional Biochemistry) examined the effects of dietary PQQ on inflammation and mitochondrial-related metabolism in human subjects. The research found that PQQ supplementation reduced circulating IL-6 (a primary inflammatory cytokine elevated in chronic inflammation) and improved mitochondrial metabolism markers. The anti-inflammatory effect is relevant for brain health because neuroinflammation is a primary driver of the cognitive decline and brain fog that accelerate with aging.

PQQ and CoQ10: A Powerful Combination

PQQ and CoQ10 are among the most scientifically rational nutrient combinations for mitochondrial support because they address different but complementary aspects of mitochondrial health.

CoQ10 works within the electron transport chain as an electron carrier between complexes, directly supporting the ATP synthesis process in existing mitochondria. It is the fuel efficiency compound: it ensures the existing mitochondrial machinery runs efficiently.

PQQ works upstream by stimulating the growth of new mitochondria through PGC-1alpha activation and protecting mitochondrial DNA and membranes from oxidative damage through its catalytic antioxidant cycling. It is the capacity expansion compound: it increases the number of mitochondria available to produce energy.

Research examining the combination found greater improvements in cognitive performance and energy measures when PQQ and CoQ10 were given together compared to either alone, consistent with their mechanistically additive effects. The combination is particularly logical for women over 40 who are experiencing both declining mitochondrial number (the PQQ target) and declining CoQ10 concentrations in existing mitochondria (the CoQ10 target).

Standard dosing for the combination is typically 20 mg PQQ plus 200 to 300 mg CoQ10 (or 100 to 200 mg ubiquinol) daily, taken with a fat-containing meal for optimal absorption of both fat-soluble compounds.

Practical Guidance for Using PQQ After 40

PQQ supplements are available as standalone products and in combination formulas with CoQ10. The doses used in human clinical trials showing cognitive benefits are consistently in the 20 mg per day range. Lower doses may provide some antioxidant benefit, but the mitochondrial biogenesis and cognitive effects appear dose-dependent and most pronounced at or above 20 mg.

PQQ is water-soluble, unlike CoQ10, and absorbs reasonably well whether taken with or without food. However, taking it with a meal is generally preferable for tolerability. It is stable at room temperature and does not require refrigeration. The most common supplement form is a disodium salt (BioPQQ), which is the form used in most human trials and is well tolerated with no significant adverse effects documented at doses up to 60 mg daily.

Results in human trials appear within 8 to 12 weeks of consistent supplementation. The effects are subtle compared to stimulants: PQQ does not produce an immediate energy boost but rather a gradual improvement in the baseline cognitive sharpness, sustained mental stamina, and fatigue resistance that reflects better mitochondrial function over time. Women who start PQQ typically report noticing the difference most clearly after stopping it for a week and comparing how they feel.

PQQ has no known significant drug interactions and is not contraindicated alongside other supplements. It pairs well with NAD+ precursors (NMN or NR), CoQ10, and omega-3 fatty acids as part of a comprehensive brain health protocol.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does PQQ do for the brain specifically?

PQQ supports brain function through two primary mechanisms. First, it stimulates mitochondrial biogenesis (growth of new mitochondria) in neurons and supporting brain cells, increasing the energy production capacity available for cognitive processes. Second, it reduces neuroinflammation by lowering pro-inflammatory cytokines like IL-6, which impair synaptic function and cognitive processing at elevated levels. Human trials show improvements in attention, working memory, and processing speed at 20 mg daily.

Is PQQ a vitamin?

PQQ was initially proposed as a new B vitamin (vitamin B14) when it was first characterized, but the scientific consensus moved away from this designation because its specific enzymatic role in mammals was not conclusively established and the body does not synthesize it endogenously. It is currently classified as a dietary compound with vitamin-like properties: essential for optimal mitochondrial function, present in all tested foods, and producing deficiency-like effects in animals raised without it.

Can PQQ help with menopause brain fog?

Yes, with realistic expectations. Menopause brain fog involves several overlapping mechanisms including mitochondrial energy decline in neurons, neuroinflammation, sleep disruption, and hormonal changes in serotonin and dopamine signaling. PQQ addresses the mitochondrial energy and neuroinflammation components directly, which are among the most significant contributors to the processing speed and memory difficulties of menopause. It does not address the hormonal components (estrogen/progesterone effects on neurotransmitter systems), so it works best as part of a comprehensive approach.

How is PQQ different from other brain supplements?

Most brain supplements work by enhancing neurotransmitter levels, increasing blood flow to the brain, or providing antioxidant protection. PQQ's distinguishing feature is its mitochondrial biogenesis effect: it is the only known dietary compound that stimulates neurons to grow new mitochondria. This makes it uniquely targeted at the underlying energy production deficit rather than just the signaling environment. This is why PQQ is often combined with other brain supplements rather than replacing them: it addresses a different layer of brain health than most nootropics.

What foods contain PQQ and can I get enough from diet?

PQQ is found in many foods but at low concentrations. The richest sources are natto (fermented soybeans), green and red peppers, parsley, kiwi fruit, papaya, and green tea. Typical Western dietary intake is estimated at 0.1 to 1 mg per day. The doses used in human clinical trials showing cognitive and mitochondrial benefits are 10 to 20 mg daily, which is 10 to 100 times higher than typical dietary intake. Getting meaningful therapeutic amounts from food alone is not practical without supplementation.
