Lion's mane mushroom has become one of the most talked-about brain supplements for women over 40, and for good reason: it is one of very few botanical compounds with documented mechanisms for supporting nerve growth factor (NGF), a protein essential to the health and repair of brain cells. But it is not the only brain supplement with real evidence behind it. If you are trying to decide whether lion's mane is worth prioritizing for brain health, focus, and memory after 40, comparing it directly to the other well-researched options gives you a much clearer picture than looking at any one supplement in isolation.

What to Know

  • Lion's mane supports nerve growth factor (NGF) production, which promotes the growth and repair of neurons, making it unique among common brain supplements.
  • Phosphatidylserine has the most consistent clinical trial evidence for memory improvement in aging adults, including FDA-qualified health claims.
  • Bacopa monnieri has strong evidence for reducing cognitive decline with age and improving verbal learning, but requires longer supplementation periods for effect.
  • Omega-3 DHA is the most foundational brain supplement because it is a structural component of brain cell membranes, not just a signaling molecule.
  • Ginkgo biloba has the largest body of human clinical evidence among botanicals but the results are inconsistent across trial designs.

Lion's Mane Mushroom: The Neurogenesis Angle

Hericium erinaceus (lion's mane) is a culinary and medicinal mushroom native to Asia, North America, and Europe. It contains two classes of compounds unique to this mushroom: hericenones (found in the fruiting body) and erinacines (found in the mycelium). Both compounds cross the blood-brain barrier and stimulate the production of nerve growth factor and brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF).

NGF is a protein that promotes the growth, survival, and differentiation of neurons, and supports the maintenance of synaptic connections. NGF production declines with age, and low NGF activity is associated with accelerated cognitive aging and neurodegenerative conditions. The ability of lion's mane compounds to stimulate NGF production from within neurons is genuinely mechanistically novel compared to most other botanical brain supplements, which work primarily through antioxidant or neurotransmitter-modulating pathways.

The human clinical evidence for lion's mane includes a double-blind, placebo-controlled trial published in Phytotherapy Research in 2009, which studied 50 to 80-year-old men and women with mild cognitive impairment who received 3 grams per day of lion's mane powder for sixteen weeks. The lion's mane group showed significantly higher scores on cognitive function scales compared to placebo, with scores declining again after supplementation ended. A second double-blind trial in 2020 found similar improvements in cognitive function scores in adults aged 50 to 80 over twelve weeks at 750 mg three times daily of a standardized extract.

Phosphatidylserine: The Memory Gold Standard

Phosphatidylserine (PS) is a phospholipid that is a structural component of every cell membrane in the body, with the highest concentrations in the brain. It is found in the inner leaflet of neuronal membranes, where it plays roles in cell signaling, neurotransmitter release, and synaptic plasticity.

PS supplementation has been studied in controlled clinical trials for cognitive aging since the 1980s, making it one of the longest-studied brain supplements for older adults. The evidence base includes multiple randomized controlled trials showing improvements in memory, learning, and cognitive flexibility in older adults with age-associated memory impairment.

In 2003, the FDA allowed qualified health claims for phosphatidylserine, stating that "consumption of phosphatidylserine may reduce the risk of dementia in the elderly." This is a qualified claim (not a definitive one), but it represents a level of evidentiary review that very few brain supplements have undergone. The FDA's review noted that the scientific evidence was suggestive but not conclusive.

In head-to-head mechanistic comparison, PS works at the membrane structure level (maintaining membrane fluidity and receptor function), while lion's mane works at the growth factor signaling level. These are complementary rather than redundant mechanisms, which is why many formulations include both.

Bacopa Monnieri: Long-Term Memory Support

Bacopa monnieri is an Ayurvedic herb with a substantial clinical trial record for cognitive function, particularly for verbal memory and the rate of learning new information. Its active compounds, bacosides, appear to support synaptic communication by protecting dendrites from damage and promoting dendritic branching.

A key feature of bacopa's research profile is that it requires a longer supplementation period to show effects than most brain supplements: studies typically show significant cognitive improvements at twelve weeks or more of consistent use, with minimal effects at shorter time points. This is likely because bacopa's mechanism involves structural neuronal changes (dendritic growth) rather than acute neurotransmitter effects.

For women over 40 dealing with the slower processing speed and word retrieval difficulties that often emerge during perimenopause, bacopa's specific strength in verbal memory and learning rate is particularly relevant. A meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials found that bacopa significantly improved memory free recall and speed of information processing in older adults with normal age-related cognitive decline.

Omega-3 DHA: The Foundation

Docosahexaenoic acid (DHA), an omega-3 fatty acid found in fatty fish and algae, is not a botanical supplement in the traditional sense. It is a structural component of brain cell membranes. Approximately 60 percent of the brain's dry weight is fat, and DHA makes up a substantial portion of the polyunsaturated fatty acids in neuronal membranes. Adequate DHA is not a brain-optimizing supplement. It is a brain-maintenance requirement.

After 40, women who do not consume oily fish regularly are likely to have suboptimal DHA status, which affects membrane fluidity, neurotransmitter receptor function, and the brain's capacity for the synaptic remodeling that underlies learning and memory.

Research shows that DHA supplementation improves cognitive performance in adults with mild memory complaints who have low baseline omega-3 status, with the strongest effects seen in verbal memory tasks. The MIDAS study found that DHA supplementation for six months significantly improved memory and learning rates in healthy adults with age-related memory complaints.

For women who eat little or no oily fish, getting DHA status adequate before adding specialized botanical supplements like lion's mane or bacopa is logically the right starting sequence. DHA is the foundation that all other cognitive support builds on.

Ginkgo Biloba: Established but Inconsistent

Ginkgo biloba has the longest history and largest body of human clinical research among botanical brain supplements. It works primarily by increasing cerebral blood flow, inhibiting platelet aggregation, and providing antioxidant protection to neuronal tissues.

The evidence on ginkgo for cognitive aging is mixed. Some large randomized controlled trials, including the Ginkgo Evaluation of Memory (GEM) study, found no significant reduction in dementia risk with ginkgo supplementation. Other smaller studies have found improvements in cognitive function and memory in adults with age-associated memory impairment.

Ginkgo's blood-thinning properties (it inhibits platelet-activating factor) require caution for women taking anticoagulants or antiplatelet medications, and it should be discontinued before surgery. Its safety profile is otherwise acceptable, but women on blood thinners or SSRI antidepressants should discuss ginkgo with their physician before using it.

How to Choose and Stack for Women Over 40

The question of which brain supplement is best after 40 is really a question about which combination best addresses the specific cognitive profile you want to support and where your nutritional baseline stands.

A practical hierarchy: Start with omega-3 DHA (at least 1 gram per day of DHA specifically), because without adequate brain structural support, no botanical supplement is as effective. If memory and verbal fluency are your primary concerns, adding phosphatidylserine (100 mg, three times per day) has the longest and most consistent clinical evidence base. If neurogenesis and neuroprotection are your goals, or if you have a family history of cognitive decline, lion's mane's NGF-stimulating mechanism offers a unique and scientifically credible approach.

Lion's mane and phosphatidylserine are complementary (different mechanisms, no known interactions), and many women over 40 use them together. Bacopa is best for women specifically focused on verbal memory and learning rate, with the patience for twelve or more weeks of consistent use before expecting significant results.

The most important variables: quality and standardization of the extract, consistency of daily use, and the supporting lifestyle of adequate sleep, cardiovascular exercise, and stress management, all of which have more impact on brain aging than any supplement.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is lion's mane better than phosphatidylserine for brain health after 40?

They work through different mechanisms and are not directly comparable. Phosphatidylserine has more consistent human clinical trial evidence for memory improvement. Lion's mane offers a unique neurogenesis pathway through NGF stimulation. Most formulas and protocols that include both are doing so for good mechanistic reasons, not just to include more ingredients.

How long does lion's mane take to work?

Human trials showing cognitive improvements use lion's mane for twelve to sixteen weeks of consistent daily use. Anecdotal reports of improved focus and mental clarity appear more quickly (two to four weeks), likely from the anti-inflammatory effects. Set a three-to-four-month commitment before judging the full cognitive benefit.

Can I take all these brain supplements together?

Most combinations are safe, but more is not always better. Omega-3 DHA, phosphatidylserine, and lion's mane can be used together safely. Adding bacopa creates a comprehensive stack. Ginkgo requires additional caution if you take blood thinners. Start with one or two supplements and add gradually, noting changes before adding more.

Does lion's mane help with brain fog specifically?

Research suggests lion's mane may reduce neuroinflammation and support BDNF, both of which contribute to the brain fog experience. Anecdotal reports from women in perimenopause using lion's mane for brain fog are generally positive. Direct clinical trials focused specifically on perimenopausal brain fog have not been published, but the mechanistic evidence supports its plausibility.

What is the best form of lion's mane to take?

Look for a dual-extracted lion's mane product that includes both the fruiting body and mycelium, since the bioactive hericenones are in the fruiting body and erinacines are in the mycelium. A standardized extract with stated beta-glucan content (at least 20 to 30 percent) ensures therapeutic potency. Avoid products that use only mycelium-on-grain, which may contain more starch than active compounds. Third-party certificate of analysis testing for heavy metals and microbial contamination is also worth verifying, since mushroom products concentrate whatever is in their growing substrate.
