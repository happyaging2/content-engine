In recent longevity research, a molecule called urolithin A has attracted serious scientific attention for its ability to do something most compounds cannot: trigger the cleaning and renewal of mitochondria, the energy-producing structures inside every cell. The remarkable thing about urolithin A is that your body does not make it directly from food. It is created by specific gut bacteria that metabolize polyphenols found in pomegranates, walnuts, and certain berries. This gut-to-cell pathway makes it one of the most compelling intersections between gut health and whole-body aging. For women over 40, understanding urolithin A means understanding how your gut microbiome may be one of the most important levers for maintaining muscle strength, cellular energy, and longevity.

What to Know

  • Urolithin A is produced by gut bacteria from ellagitannins found in pomegranates, walnuts, and certain berries
  • It triggers mitophagy, the cellular process that removes damaged mitochondria and stimulates renewal of healthy ones
  • Clinical trials show urolithin A improves muscle endurance, reduces fatigue, and enhances mitochondrial gene expression in adults

  • Urolithin A is available as a supplement (Mitopure is the commercially studied form) and is generally well tolerated

What Is Urolithin A and Where Does It Come From?

Urolithin A is a compound in the urolithin family, a group of molecules produced when intestinal bacteria metabolize ellagitannins and ellagic acid. These polyphenol precursors are found in pomegranate seeds and juice, walnuts, raspberries, strawberries, and cloudberries.

The conversion process works as follows: when you eat a pomegranate or handful of walnuts, ellagitannins pass into your large intestine. Specific bacterial species, primarily from the Gordonibacter and Ellagibacter genera, break down these polyphenols through a series of metabolic steps into urolithins, including urolithin A, urolithin B, and isourolithin A. Urolithin A is the most bioactive and most studied of this group.

The catch is that not everyone produces urolithin A from food. Research has identified three distinct "urolithin metabotypes" in the human population. In Metabotype A (about 40% of adults), the gut microbiome efficiently produces urolithin A. In Metabotype B, partial production occurs along with other urolithins. In Metabotype 0, which affects roughly 25-40% of adults, little or no urolithin A is produced regardless of polyphenol intake. This variability explains why supplemental urolithin A was developed as a direct delivery route that bypasses gut conversion.

How Urolithin A Activates Mitophagy

The primary mechanism through which urolithin A supports health is mitophagy, a word combining "mitochondria" and "autophagy" (the cellular cleaning process). Mitophagy specifically refers to the selective removal of damaged or dysfunctional mitochondria.

As cells age, damaged mitochondria accumulate. They produce less energy, generate more damaging reactive oxygen species, and can trigger inflammation. The body has a quality-control system that is supposed to identify and eliminate these faulty mitochondria, but this mitophagy process becomes less efficient with age.

Urolithin A directly activates mitophagy pathways, in particular through the PINK1-Parkin signaling cascade that tags damaged mitochondria for removal. By enhancing this process, urolithin A helps cells clear out dysfunctional mitochondria and stimulates the biogenesis (creation) of new, healthy ones. The result is a cellular energy upgrade from within.

This mechanism matters enormously for muscle function, which is driven by mitochondrial efficiency. It also matters for brain cells, immune cells, and virtually every other tissue where mitochondria are central to performance.

What Human Research Shows

Urolithin A moved from animal studies to human trials relatively quickly, and the results have been encouraging.

A landmark 2019 study published in Nature Metabolism tested urolithin A (as the Mitopure formulation) in healthy older adults over four weeks. Compared to placebo, the supplementation group showed significant improvements in mitochondrial gene expression in muscle tissue, suggesting enhanced mitochondrial function at the cellular level. Importantly, the compound was safe and well-tolerated with no adverse effects.

A follow-up randomized controlled trial in 2022, also published in a peer-reviewed journal, found that 500 mg of urolithin A per day for four months significantly improved muscle endurance (measured by grip strength and hand grip duration) in adults over 65. Subjects also showed favorable changes in plasma acylcarnitine levels, a biomarker of mitochondrial fatty acid metabolism.

Animal research has consistently shown that urolithin A extends lifespan in C. elegans (nematode worm), improves running endurance in mice, and reduces age-related muscle decline. While human data is still accumulating, the mechanistic evidence is strong and the early clinical results are consistent with the proposed mitophagy mechanism.

Why This Matters for Women Over 40

Women over 40 face two converging challenges that urolithin A directly addresses: declining muscle mass and declining mitochondrial efficiency.

Sarcopenia risk. After 40, women lose approximately 3-8% of muscle mass per decade, with the rate accelerating after menopause due to declining estrogen. Loss of muscle mass reduces metabolic rate, impairs balance, and increases fracture risk. Mitophagy enhancement supports the quality of muscle mitochondria, which is a key determinant of muscle power and endurance independent of muscle volume.

Cellular energy decline. Many women over 40 notice a shift in energy levels, not just from sleep or lifestyle, but from a deep cellular inefficiency that feels different from simple tiredness. This often reflects mitochondrial aging: cells that generate less ATP from the same fuel. Urolithin A addresses this at the source by facilitating mitochondrial renewal.

Gut microbiome shifts. The gut microbiome changes significantly during perimenopause and after menopause, with reductions in beneficial bacterial diversity that directly affect urolithin-producing species. Women who previously produced adequate urolithin A from food may find their conversion capacity declining precisely when they need it most.

How to Increase Urolithin A: Food and Supplementation

For women in Metabotype A, dietary strategies work well. Focus on regular intake of:

Pomegranate (whole seeds or 100% pomegranate juice): the richest source of ellagitannins. One cup of pomegranate arils provides approximately 1,000 mg of ellagitannins.

Walnuts: a handful (1 ounce) provides ellagic acid and other polyphenols alongside beneficial omega-3 fatty acids.

Raspberries, strawberries, and cloudberries: good sources of ellagic acid that pair well with daily meals or yogurt.

For women in Metabotype 0 or B, or those with altered gut microbiomes due to antibiotics, stress, or menopause-related microbiome shifts, direct supplementation with urolithin A (typically 250-500 mg per day) bypasses the conversion step entirely. The most well-studied supplement form is Mitopure (by Amazentis), which has been used in the published human clinical trials and demonstrated bioavailability and tolerability.

Improving overall gut microbiome diversity through prebiotic fiber, fermented foods, and a probiotic supplement also supports the bacterial species involved in urolithin production, creating better conditions for natural conversion over time.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is urolithin A and what does it do?

Urolithin A is a compound produced by gut bacteria from polyphenols in pomegranates and certain other foods. Its primary action is activating mitophagy, the process that removes damaged mitochondria and stimulates renewal of healthy ones, supporting muscle function and cellular energy production.

Can everyone produce urolithin A from food?

No. About 25-40% of adults lack the specific gut bacteria needed to convert dietary polyphenols into urolithin A, a group classified as Metabotype 0. These individuals benefit most from direct urolithin A supplementation because eating more pomegranates will not compensate for the missing bacterial conversion step.

Is urolithin A a supplement?

Yes. Urolithin A is available as a standalone dietary supplement, most commonly under the brand name Mitopure at doses of 250-500 mg per day. It is also found in some multi-ingredient longevity formulas. Human clinical trials have demonstrated safety and efficacy at these doses.

How long does urolithin A take to work?

In human studies, measurable improvements in mitochondrial gene expression were observed within four weeks of daily supplementation. Improvements in muscle endurance and physical function in older adults were detected over four months. Mitophagy is a slow biological process; consistent daily use over months produces the most meaningful results.

Does urolithin A help with weight management?

Indirectly, yes. By improving mitochondrial efficiency in muscle cells, urolithin A supports the metabolic capacity of muscle tissue, which is the body's primary site of fat oxidation. Better mitochondrial function in muscle means more efficient energy usage, though urolithin A is not a direct fat-burning compound.

Urolithin A and Healthy Aging: The Broader Longevity Picture

While the mitochondrial and muscle benefits of urolithin A are the most well-studied, emerging research suggests its effects extend across multiple longevity-related pathways that are particularly relevant for women over 40.

Neuroprotection. The brain's neurons are among the most mitochondria-dependent cells in the body, requiring enormous amounts of ATP to maintain electrical gradients and neurotransmitter recycling. Mitophagy enhancement by urolithin A has been shown in preclinical studies to protect neurons from amyloid-beta and tau accumulation, the hallmarks of Alzheimer's pathology. While human trials on cognition have not yet been published, the mechanism is compelling given the brain's dependence on mitochondrial quality control.

Gut microbiome improvements. There is a bidirectional relationship between urolithin A and gut health. The gut microbiome produces urolithin A, but urolithin A also appears to influence the gut environment in return, particularly by reducing intestinal inflammation through its effects on the NF-kB pathway. Women who supplement with urolithin A and also prioritize microbiome diversity through prebiotic foods may find the benefits compound over time: a healthier microbiome produces more urolithin A naturally, and urolithin A in turn supports the gut environment.

Skin and wound healing. Mitophagy enhancement in skin fibroblasts supports the cellular quality control needed for collagen synthesis and skin repair. Preclinical evidence suggests that urolithin A may support skin barrier function and reduce the oxidative damage to skin cells that accelerates visible aging. This makes it relevant not just for muscle and longevity but for the skin changes that women over 40 commonly experience alongside hormonal shifts.

Taken together, urolithin A is emerging as one of the most pleiotropic longevity compounds: one that supports the same cellular quality-control pathway (mitophagy) across multiple organ systems simultaneously, making it highly relevant as a daily longevity supplement for women over 40. Daily supplementation at 500 mg is the dose most consistently used in the published human trials and represents the most practical and evidence-grounded approach for women whose gut microbiome conversion is uncertain or insufficient.
