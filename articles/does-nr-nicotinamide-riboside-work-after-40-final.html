What to Know Before Reading
  • NR is a form of vitamin B3 that the body can convert into NAD+, a molecule critical for cellular energy and DNA repair.
  • Human trials confirm NR raises blood NAD+ levels. Whether that improves how you feel or function is still being studied.
  • NR and NMN are both NAD+ precursors. They work through slightly different pathways and have different research profiles.
  • No human trial has proven that NR extends lifespan or reverses biological aging in women.
  • NR appears to be well-tolerated in short-term studies at standard doses (250-500 mg/day).

What Is NR (Nicotinamide Riboside)?

Nicotinamide riboside is a naturally occurring form of vitamin B3. It belongs to a group of molecules called NAD+ precursors, meaning the body uses it as a raw material to produce NAD+ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide). NAD+ is found in every cell and plays a central role in converting food into energy, repairing damaged DNA, and regulating proteins called sirtuins that influence how cells age.

The important detail is that NAD+ levels decline with age. Research suggests that by the time a woman reaches her 40s, her cellular NAD+ levels may be meaningfully lower than they were in her 20s. The theory behind NR supplementation is that restoring NAD+ levels could support the cellular processes that depend on it.

Why It Matters for Women Over 40

NAD+ decline in the 40s coincides with several shifts that women commonly experience: lower energy, slower recovery from exercise, changes in metabolic rate, and early signs of cognitive change. Mitochondria, the energy-producing organelles in cells, are particularly dependent on NAD+.

What the Research Says

According to Happy Aging's review of the available human trial literature on NR, the evidence falls into a few consistent categories.

NR raises blood NAD+ levels. A 2018 study by Martens et al. (PMID: 28832581) found that NR supplementation at 500 mg twice daily raised whole blood NAD+ levels in healthy middle-aged and older adults.

NR appears safe in short-term use. A 2017 safety study by Airhart et al. (PMID: 29459476) examined NR supplementation in healthy adults and found it to be well-tolerated at doses up to 1,000 mg/day.

Older adults show measurable responses. A 2019 study by Elhassan et al. (PMID: 31032835) found that NR supplementation increased NAD+ metabolites in muscle tissue in older adults.

What the Evidence Doesn't Support

NR has not been shown to reverse biological age. No peer-reviewed human trial has demonstrated that NR supplementation meaningfully changes biological age markers in a clinically significant way.

Happy Aging's position: NR is a well-tolerated NAD+ precursor with consistent evidence for raising blood NAD+ levels in humans. It works best as part of a broader approach to cellular health.

Who Should NOT Take NR

If you have a history of cancer, consult your oncologist. Women who are pregnant or nursing should avoid NR supplements. If you are on any prescription medication, discuss NAD+ supplementation with your prescribing physician before starting.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take for NR to work?
Most studies measure NAD+ levels after 2-8 weeks of consistent supplementation. Users typically report noticing changes within 4-8 weeks of daily use.

Does NR cause any side effects?
Short-term studies report NR is well-tolerated. Some users report mild nausea or flushing at higher doses. Taking NR with food can reduce gastrointestinal discomfort.

Can NR help with perimenopause symptoms?
Some researchers hypothesize that NAD+ support may help with energy, sleep quality, and metabolic health during perimenopause, but no clinical trial has been specifically completed to evaluate NR as a perimenopause intervention.
