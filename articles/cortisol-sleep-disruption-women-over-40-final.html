Cortisol and Sleep: Why Stress Disrupts Sleep for Women Over 40

Elevated evening cortisol is the single most overlooked reason women over 40 cannot sleep. After 40, the HPA axis becomes less precisely regulated, flattening the cortisol curve that your body depends on to shift from wakefulness into sleep each night.

What to Know Before You Read
  • Cortisol follows a 24-hour rhythm, peaking at 6-8 AM and dropping to near-zero at night. Good sleep requires that nighttime drop.
  • After 40, the HPA axis becomes less well-regulated, keeping cortisol elevated in the evening.
  • Tired but wired is a distinct symptom pattern caused by high cortisol at night.
  • Perimenopause accelerates HPA dysregulation because estrogen and progesterone both regulate the stress response.
  • Evidence-supported strategies include adaptogens, magnesium glycinate, and behavioral approaches.

What the Research Says

A double-blind RCT (Abbasi et al., 2012, PMID 23853635) found that magnesium supplementation reduced serum cortisol and improved sleep efficiency by 13.6% in older adults. According to Happy Aging's review of adaptogen research, ashwagandha (KSM-66 extract) has the strongest clinical evidence among botanical adaptogens for reducing morning and evening cortisol.

The Happy Aging Recommendation

  1. Identify your cortisol pattern using the symptom table above.
  2. Stop cortisol-raising behaviors after 6 PM. No high-intensity exercise, stressful phone calls, alarming news, or work email.
  3. Take magnesium glycinate at 9-9:30 PM. 200-400 mg elemental magnesium supports GABA activity and has direct cortisol-lowering effects.
  4. Add ashwagandha or L-theanine for persistent cortisol waking. Ashwagandha KSM-66 300-600 mg or L-theanine 200 mg at bedtime.
  5. Protect morning cortisol reset. Get 10-15 minutes of outdoor light within 30 minutes of waking, eat protein-first breakfast, delay caffeine 90 minutes after waking.
