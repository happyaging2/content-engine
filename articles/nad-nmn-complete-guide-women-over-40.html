What NAD+ Is and Why It Declines After 40

NAD+ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide) is a molecule your mitochondria use to convert food into usable energy. It also serves as a fuel source for DNA repair enzymes and the sirtuin proteins that regulate cellular aging. The problem is that NAD+ production slows as you age and consumption of existing NAD+ rises — because more DNA damage requires more repair activity, which burns through NAD+ faster. According to Happy Aging's review of the current literature on NAD+ biology, this dual-pressure decline (less production, more consumption) is one of the most consistent biochemical signatures of aging identified across cell types. By the time most women reach their mid-40s, their NAD+ levels are already substantially lower than they were at 20, which affects everything from afternoon energy to sleep quality to how quickly they recover after exercise. How NMN Raises NAD+ Levels

NMN enters cells and is converted directly to NAD+ through a two-step enzymatic process, making it one of the most efficient NAD+ precursors available. Unlike NAD+ itself, which cannot cross cell membranes easily, NMN is taken up by specific transporters in the small intestine and gut tissue, raising blood and tissue NAD+ levels within hours of a dose. The question of how much this matters for how you feel day-to-day is answered in part by looking at the timing of supplementation — your body makes the most NAD+ during the first half of the day, in sync with your circadian rhythm, which is why morning dosing tends to produce the clearest results. Happy Aging's position: NMN at 250-500mg taken in the morning with food is the protocol most consistent with the current timing research. When You Will Feel a Difference

Most women notice something within the first two to four weeks of consistent NMN supplementation — typically more stable energy through the afternoon, slightly better sleep, or sharper mental clarity. Deeper changes, like improved exercise recovery or skin quality, tend to become apparent between months two and three, when NAD+-dependent repair processes have had enough time to make measurable differences at the cellular level. The timeline is not the same for everyone: women with the lowest baseline NAD+ levels (often indicated by significant fatigue, poor sleep, and slow post-exercise recovery) tend to notice changes fastest, while those who are already in good metabolic shape may notice subtler improvements over a longer timeframe. The Sirtuin Connection

Sirtuins are a family of seven proteins (SIRT1 through SIRT7) that use NAD+ as a required cofactor to do their job — which is to regulate gene expression, DNA repair, inflammation, and metabolic efficiency. When NAD+ falls, sirtuins slow down, and the biological programs they regulate become less active. SIRT1 is the most studied: it activates genes that protect mitochondria, suppress inflammation, and maintain insulin sensitivity. SIRT3 manages mitochondrial efficiency directly. Without adequate NAD+ to fuel them, these proteins cannot keep up with the repair demands of an aging cell. This is the molecular link between NAD+ levels and visible aging effects like fatigue, brain fog, and slower recovery. NAD+, Autophagy, and Cellular Cleanup

Autophagy is your body's cellular recycling system — it identifies damaged proteins, worn-out mitochondria, and other cellular debris, and breaks them down so the raw materials can be reused. After 40, autophagy slows significantly, which means damaged components accumulate inside cells rather than being cleared. NAD+ supports autophagy indirectly by fueling the sirtuin SIRT1, which activates several of the genes that run the autophagy process. Raising NAD+ through NMN supplementation therefore supports both direct energy production and the cellular housekeeping that prevents the buildup of cellular "garbage" associated with aging. Fasting, exercise, and certain plant compounds like resveratrol also activate autophagy through overlapping but distinct pathways. Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between NAD+ and NMN?

NAD+ is the active coenzyme your cells use for energy and repair. NMN is a precursor molecule that your body converts into NAD+ — taking NMN is a way to raise NAD+ levels because NAD+ itself is poorly absorbed when taken orally.

How much NMN should women over 40 take?

Most clinical research uses doses between 250mg and 500mg per day. Start at 250mg in the morning and assess how you feel after 4 weeks before increasing the dose.

Can you take NMN and NAD+ at the same time?

There is no evidence of harm in combining them, but it is generally not necessary — NMN converts to NAD+ efficiently, so adding NAD+ orally provides little additional benefit given its poor oral bioavailability.

Are there side effects from NMN?

NMN is well tolerated in the doses used in human studies. A small number of people report mild nausea or flushing at higher doses; taking NMN with food reduces both effects.
