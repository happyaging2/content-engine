If you have ever had hormonal blood work done, you may have seen the acronym SHBG on your results without receiving much explanation. SHBG stands for sex hormone-binding globulin, and it is one of the most important but least discussed factors in how your hormones actually function after 40. Your total estrogen and testosterone levels may look perfectly normal, but if your SHBG is too high or too low, the hormones that are bound to it cannot do their job. Understanding SHBG explains why women with "normal" hormone levels can still have all the symptoms of hormonal imbalance.

What to Know About SHBG

  • SHBG is a protein produced by the liver that binds to estrogen and testosterone, making them temporarily inactive
  • Only "free" (unbound) hormones are biologically active and can enter cells to produce effects
  • High SHBG reduces the availability of estrogen and testosterone, causing low-hormone symptoms even when total hormone levels are normal
  • Low SHBG makes more testosterone and estrogen available, increasing both androgenic and estrogenic effects
  • SHBG levels are strongly influenced by thyroid function, insulin resistance, liver health, and body composition

What SHBG Does in the Body

SHBG is a glycoprotein produced in the liver. It circulates in the bloodstream and acts as a transport vehicle and regulator for sex hormones. SHBG binds to three sex steroids with different affinities: dihydrotestosterone (DHT) with the highest affinity, testosterone with moderate affinity, and estradiol (the primary active estrogen) with lower but still meaningful affinity.

When a hormone is bound to SHBG, it cannot enter cells. Hormone receptors on cell surfaces respond only to free, unbound hormones. The amount of free hormone is therefore the relevant variable for how you actually feel and function, not the total hormone level that standard blood tests typically report.

Approximately 98 percent of testosterone and 60 to 80 percent of estradiol in a typical woman's bloodstream are bound to either SHBG or albumin (a secondary carrier protein with lower binding affinity). Only about 2 to 3 percent of testosterone and 1 to 2 percent of estradiol are truly "free." This narrow free fraction determines everything from energy to libido to mood.

How SHBG Changes After 40

SHBG levels are not static; they shift significantly with the hormonal changes of perimenopause and menopause, and these shifts have substantial consequences for how women experience their hormones.

During perimenopause, fluctuating estrogen levels cause corresponding fluctuations in SHBG, since estrogen stimulates SHBG production in the liver. When estrogen levels drop or fluctuate erratically, SHBG follows, changing the ratio of free to bound hormones unpredictably. This is one reason why perimenopause is hormonally chaotic rather than a simple linear decline.

After menopause, estrogen production from the ovaries ceases, but SHBG often remains elevated for years due to residual stimulation from adrenal estrogens and because the liver, without the signal to reduce SHBG production, maintains elevated levels. The result is that postmenopausal women may have both low total estrogen and high SHBG, creating a double reduction in free estrogen availability that amplifies symptoms even more than the total hormone decline alone would suggest.

Testosterone availability is particularly affected. As SHBG remains elevated after menopause while total testosterone declines, free testosterone levels fall substantially below what total testosterone tests show. This explains why some women with "normal range" testosterone experience reduced libido, low motivation, fatigue, and muscle loss characteristic of low-testosterone states.

High SHBG: Symptoms and Causes

When SHBG is elevated, less free estrogen and testosterone are available, producing symptoms of hormonal insufficiency despite potentially normal total hormone levels.

Symptoms of high SHBG include: low libido, vaginal dryness, mood flatness or depression, fatigue, difficulty building muscle, joint pain, brain fog, and increased sensitivity to hormonal fluctuations. These are the same symptoms associated with low estrogen and low testosterone, because functionally, high SHBG produces the same end result.

Common causes of elevated SHBG in women over 40:

Hyperthyroidism or excessive thyroid hormone replacement significantly elevates SHBG because thyroid hormones stimulate liver SHBG production. Women on thyroid medication who feel low despite normal TSH should have SHBG checked.

Oral estrogen therapy (pills rather than patches or gels) is metabolized through the liver on first pass and strongly stimulates SHBG production. Women on oral HRT often have dramatically elevated SHBG compared to those using transdermal estrogen, which bypasses the liver. Switching to transdermal formulations can substantially reduce SHBG and increase free hormone availability.

Low-calorie diets and low fat intake increase SHBG because the liver increases its production when caloric or fat intake is restricted. Women following very low-fat or severely calorie-restricted diets may find that their hormonal symptoms worsen despite no change in total hormone levels.

Low SHBG: Symptoms and Causes

Low SHBG makes more estrogen and testosterone available in free form, which sounds desirable but comes with its own set of issues.

Symptoms of low SHBG include: acne or oily skin (from excess free testosterone), excess facial or body hair, irregular periods, mood volatility, and in premenopausal women, signs consistent with polycystic ovarian syndrome (PCOS). In women who are estrogen dominant, low SHBG also allows more free estrogen to act on tissues, amplifying estrogenic effects.

Common causes of low SHBG:

Insulin resistance is the most common cause of low SHBG in women over 40. Elevated insulin directly suppresses SHBG synthesis in the liver. Research in Diabetes Care (Plymate et al., 1988, PMID: 3056407) established that insulin inhibits SHBG production in dose-dependent fashion. For women with metabolic syndrome or prediabetes, improving insulin sensitivity (through diet, exercise, and berberine or metformin) reliably raises SHBG.

Obesity, particularly abdominal adiposity, reduces SHBG through multiple pathways including insulin resistance and direct suppression from adipokines. Losing 5 to 10 percent of body weight reliably increases SHBG in overweight women.

Hypothyroidism reduces SHBG synthesis because thyroid hormone is needed to stimulate its production. Women with low-normal thyroid function often have low SHBG alongside low-free-hormone symptoms.

How to Test and Interpret SHBG

SHBG is measured with a standard blood test and most doctors will include it on a hormonal panel if asked. The key is to interpret it in context with total hormone levels to calculate free hormone availability.

Normal SHBG ranges for women vary by laboratory, but generally fall between 18 and 144 nmol/L, a range so wide as to be nearly uninformative. What matters clinically is whether SHBG, in combination with total testosterone and total estradiol, produces a free hormone level consistent with your symptoms.

The calculated free testosterone is the most clinically useful derived value. It can be calculated from total testosterone, SHBG, and albumin using the Vermeulen formula. Many online calculators are available. For reference, free testosterone below 1 to 2 pg/mL in women over 40 is associated with libido decline, fatigue, and reduced muscle response to exercise.

Request from your physician: total testosterone, free testosterone (or calculated free testosterone using SHBG + total T + albumin), SHBG, and estradiol. Interpret these as a set, not individually.

Natural Ways to Optimize SHBG

If SHBG is too high, strategies to reduce it include improving insulin sensitivity (lower-carbohydrate diet, resistance training, berberine), optimizing thyroid function (selenium, zinc, iodine for T4-to-T3 conversion), switching from oral to transdermal estrogen if on HRT, and adding moderate dietary fat (particularly polyunsaturated and monounsaturated fats, which are associated with lower SHBG in population studies).

If SHBG is too low, strategies to raise it include improving insulin sensitivity (counterintuitively, also relevant here because insulin resistance and obesity cause low SHBG), maintaining a healthy weight, and ensuring adequate dietary fiber (fiber intake is positively associated with SHBG in multiple epidemiological studies, possibly through effects on estrogen enterohepatic recirculation).

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a normal SHBG level for women over 40?

The laboratory reference range (18 to 144 nmol/L) is broad and clinically imprecise. What matters more is whether free hormone levels calculated from your SHBG and total hormone levels match your symptoms. Most integrative practitioners aim for free testosterone in the 1 to 3 pg/mL range for women over 40, and use SHBG as one input in that calculation rather than as a standalone value.

Can high SHBG cause low libido even with normal testosterone?

Yes. This is one of the most common scenarios mismanaged in conventional medicine. If SHBG is elevated, total testosterone can appear normal while free testosterone is insufficient to support libido, energy, and muscle function. Testing only total testosterone without SHBG misses this pattern entirely.

Does insulin resistance lower SHBG?

Yes. Elevated insulin directly suppresses SHBG synthesis in the liver in a dose-dependent relationship. Improving insulin sensitivity through dietary changes, exercise, and supplements like berberine or inositol reliably raises SHBG in women with insulin resistance.

Does oral contraceptive use affect SHBG?

Yes, significantly. Combined oral contraceptives containing ethinyl estradiol and progestins substantially elevate SHBG, sometimes by 3 to 4 times baseline. This dramatically reduces free testosterone. Some women experience persistent low libido and fatigue even after stopping oral contraceptives because SHBG remains elevated for months or years. This is a clinically recognized phenomenon called post-pill SHBG elevation.

Does weight loss change SHBG?

Yes. Losing 5 to 10 percent of body weight reliably raises SHBG in overweight women, improving the balance of free hormones. The mechanism involves improved insulin sensitivity and reduced adipokine suppression of SHBG synthesis. For women with low SHBG due to obesity, weight management is the most sustainable SHBG-raising strategy.
