Urolithin A and Mitophagy: What Women Over 40 Need to Know

Urolithin A is a compound your gut bacteria can produce from certain foods, and it is one of the few substances shown in human trials to trigger mitophagy, the cellular process that clears out damaged mitochondria. As of 2026, the research is promising but still early, and most women over 40 face a gut microbiome change that limits how much urolithin A they can actually make from diet alone.

What to Know Before You Read
  • Urolithin A is produced when gut bacteria convert ellagic acid from foods like pomegranates, walnuts, and strawberries.
  • It activates mitophagy, the process of removing and recycling damaged mitochondria.
  • Most women over 40 do not produce urolithin A efficiently from food due to microbiome changes associated with aging.
  • Human trials are limited but show improved muscle endurance and mitochondrial markers at supplemental doses.
  • Urolithin A supplements bypass the gut conversion step entirely.
  • This supplement is not FDA-approved to treat any condition and should not replace medical care.

What Is Urolithin A?

Urolithin A is a postbiotic compound produced when specific bacteria in the colon metabolize ellagitannins and ellagic acid from pomegranates, walnuts, raspberries, and certain other plant foods. The challenge is that urolithin A production requires a particular gut bacterial ecosystem. Studies suggest that roughly 30 to 40 percent of the adult population cannot produce meaningful amounts even when eating urolithin precursor foods regularly.

What Is Mitophagy and Why Does It Matter?

Mitophagy is a specific type of autophagy that targets damaged mitochondria for recycling. When mitophagy slows down, damaged mitochondria accumulate, energy output drops, and affected cells generate more oxidative stress. After 40, mitophagy activity tends to decline, contributing to the fatigue and slower recovery many women notice in their 40s and 50s.

What the Research Says

A human trial published in Nature Metabolism found that at 1,000 mg of purified urolithin A daily for four months, participants showed a 12 percent increase in muscle endurance compared to placebo, along with elevated mitochondrial gene expression markers. Earlier phase 1 work (Andreux et al., Nature Metabolism 2019) found oral urolithin A was safe and well tolerated at doses up to 2,000 mg per day in older adults.

According to Happy Aging's review of this clinical data, urolithin A has the most direct human evidence for mitophagy activation among currently available supplements.
