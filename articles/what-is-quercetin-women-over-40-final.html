What Is Quercetin?

Quercetin (3,3',4',5,7-pentahydroxyflavone) is a flavonoid pigment found in many plants, where it serves as a UV filter and defense compound against pathogens. In human nutrition, it is one of the most widely consumed flavonoids in the Western diet, appearing in significant quantities in onions (particularly red onions), apples (especially the skin), capers, berries (blueberries, cranberries, elderberries), broccoli, kale, and red grapes.

Despite being so common in the diet, meaningful therapeutic amounts of quercetin from food alone are difficult to achieve. Bioavailability from food is highly variable (typically 0 to 50 percent depending on the food matrix), and the amounts needed for documented biological effects are typically higher than even a quercetin-rich diet provides. This is where supplementation becomes relevant.

Quercetin's biological activity is broad. It is classified as an antioxidant (neutralizing free radicals), an anti-inflammatory agent (inhibiting multiple inflammatory signaling pathways), an antiviral compound (inhibiting viral entry and replication for several virus types), an antihistamine (stabilizing mast cells and reducing histamine release), and most recently a senolytic (selectively inducing apoptosis in senescent cells). This unusual combination of activities across multiple biological systems is what makes quercetin particularly interesting for midlife women.

How Quercetin Fights Inflammation After 40

Chronic low-grade inflammation, what researchers call inflammaging, is one of the primary biological drivers of accelerated aging and the cluster of conditions that become more common after 40: metabolic syndrome, cardiovascular disease, cognitive decline, and autoimmune flares. After menopause, the anti-inflammatory protection provided by estrogen is withdrawn, and inflammatory markers (CRP, interleukin-6, TNF-alpha) tend to rise.

Quercetin targets inflammation through multiple pathways that work together to reduce inflammatory signaling more comprehensively than compounds that act through a single mechanism.

Its primary anti-inflammatory action involves inhibition of NF-kB (nuclear factor kappa-light-chain-enhancer of activated B cells), the central transcription factor that drives production of pro-inflammatory cytokines. By preventing NF-kB from entering the cell nucleus, quercetin reduces the expression of TNF-alpha, interleukin-1 beta, and interleukin-6, the same cytokines elevated in chronic inflammaging.

Quercetin also inhibits NLRP3 inflammasome activation. The NLRP3 inflammasome is a protein complex that triggers the production of IL-1 beta and IL-18 in response to cellular stress and danger signals. NLRP3 activation is elevated in postmenopausal women and is implicated in the chronic inflammation associated with metabolic disease, joint inflammation, and neurodegeneration. Blocking NLRP3 is an active target of pharmaceutical research, and quercetin's documented ability to do this through a natural mechanism is significant.

Research supports quercetin's anti-inflammatory effects in human studies: supplementation at 500 to 1,000 mg per day reduces CRP, TNF-alpha, and IL-6 in clinical trials in metabolically compromised adults and older populations. For women over 40 whose inflammatory burden is increasing with age and hormonal transition, these are meaningful targets.

Quercetin as a Senolytic

One of the most exciting developments in quercetin research came from the longevity science community, where it has been identified as a senolytic compound. Senolytics selectively induce apoptosis (programmed cell death) in senescent cells while leaving healthy cells unaffected.

The most famous research on quercetin as a senolytic comes from Mayo Clinic, where researchers discovered that the combination of dasatinib (a cancer drug) and quercetin (DQ) reduces senescent cell burden in both animal models and human pilot studies. In a 2019 study published in EBioMedicine, researchers at Mayo Clinic found that three days of DQ treatment in patients with idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis reduced senescence markers (p16, p21, MMP-3) in adipose tissue and skin, the first human evidence of senolytic activity from any compound.

While most of the DQ senolytic research uses quercetin at pharmaceutical concentrations alongside dasatinib, quercetin alone also shows senolytic activity in cell culture and animal models, just at higher concentrations. Fisetin shows greater standalone senolytic activity than quercetin alone, which is why the combination of the two flavonoids is increasingly studied as a natural senolytic protocol.

For women over 40, the relevance is that the accumulation of senescent cells in tissues like adipose tissue, skin, and muscle accelerates around menopause, when the immune system's capacity to clear senescent cells (through natural killer cells and macrophages) declines. Quercetin's senolytic activity adds a longevity dimension to its anti-inflammatory benefits that is particularly relevant for midlife women.

Quercetin and Immune Support

Quercetin has well-documented antiviral and immune-modulating properties that make it relevant beyond longevity contexts.

As an antihistamine, quercetin inhibits histamine release from mast cells and basophils, which underlies its traditional use for allergy symptoms. It stabilizes mast cell membranes, reducing the cascade of inflammatory mediators released in allergic reactions. For women over 40 who notice worsened allergies (a common perimenopausal complaint, possibly related to estrogen's mast cell-stabilizing effects), quercetin offers a natural antihistamine approach.

Quercetin also has antiviral activity that has received significant research attention. It inhibits the early stages of viral infection by interfering with viral entry (by binding to spike proteins and receptors that viruses use to enter cells) and viral replication (by inhibiting viral RNA polymerase activity). Research has documented inhibitory effects against multiple respiratory viruses in cell culture and animal models. Additionally, quercetin may act as a zinc ionophore, helping transport zinc into cells where it can inhibit viral RNA replication. This combination of mechanisms makes it a compound of ongoing interest in antiviral research.

Bioavailability: Why Liposomal Quercetin Matters

Standard quercetin supplements have notoriously poor and variable bioavailability. Quercetin is poorly water-soluble, and its absorption from the gut depends on conversion by intestinal bacteria, food matrix effects, and the presence of other compounds. Studies comparing plasma quercetin levels after different formulations find highly variable absorption, with some individuals absorbing relatively little from standard capsule forms.

Several strategies improve quercetin bioavailability significantly. Taking it with fat improves absorption of this lipophilic flavonoid. Quercetin phytosome (complexed with sunflower phosphatidylcholine) has been shown in clinical trials to improve bioavailability 20-fold compared to standard quercetin. Liposomal quercetin (encapsulated in phospholipid vesicles) similarly dramatically improves absorption by protecting the compound through the digestive environment and enhancing cellular uptake.

Quercetin also benefits from co-administration with bromelain (a pineapple enzyme) or vitamin C, both of which improve quercetin absorption and may enhance its anti-inflammatory effects. The combination of quercetin with vitamin C is particularly well-studied, as the two compounds have complementary antioxidant and anti-inflammatory actions.

The practical implication: when evaluating quercetin supplements, the delivery form is as important as the dose. 250 mg of liposomal quercetin may deliver more bioactive quercetin to tissues than 1,000 mg of standard quercetin powder at lower cost per effective dose.

How to Use Quercetin After 40

For general anti-inflammatory and antioxidant benefit, 500 to 1,000 mg of quercetin per day (from a bioavailable form) is the range supported by most clinical trials. Taking it with a fat-containing meal improves absorption from any formulation.

For seasonal immune support during periods of high exposure risk, some practitioners recommend higher doses (1,000 to 2,000 mg per day) for shorter periods. The compound is well-tolerated at these doses in published safety data, with headache and tingling as the most commonly reported side effects at doses above 1 gram per day, both of which resolve with dose reduction.

For senolytic applications, the DQ protocol uses quercetin at very high doses over short intermittent periods (1,000 to 1,500 mg alongside dasatinib), which is a medical intervention requiring physician oversight. A purely nutraceutical senolytic approach combining fisetin and quercetin at standard supplement doses is being explored in research but does not yet have a validated human protocol.

Quercetin can interact with certain medications: it inhibits CYP3A4 and P-glycoprotein, meaning it can raise blood levels of some drugs that are metabolized by these enzymes, including some antibiotics, antifungals, and blood thinners. If you take prescription medications, disclose quercetin use to your physician.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is quercetin safe to take every day after 40?

Quercetin has an excellent safety profile in clinical research at doses up to 1,000 mg per day for up to twelve weeks. Long-term daily supplementation is commonly practiced with a good safety record. The most important consideration is potential drug interactions for women taking prescription medications. Always inform your physician of quercetin use if you take other medications.

What is the difference between quercetin and quercetin phytosome?

Quercetin phytosome is quercetin complexed with phosphatidylcholine (from sunflower or soy), creating a more stable, fat-soluble form that absorbs significantly better than standard quercetin. Studies show quercetin phytosome achieves plasma levels approximately 20 times higher than standard quercetin at the same dose. Liposomal quercetin works through a similar principle of lipid-based delivery for improved absorption.

Can quercetin help with histamine intolerance after menopause?

Yes. Quercetin stabilizes mast cells and reduces histamine release, which can be beneficial for women with histamine intolerance, a condition where the body struggles to break down histamine from food. Menopause may worsen histamine sensitivity because estrogen, when excessive or when processed through the wrong pathway, can itself stimulate mast cell histamine release. Quercetin's mast cell-stabilizing effect addresses this pathway directly.

Does quercetin work better combined with other supplements?

Several combinations show synergy in research. Quercetin plus vitamin C is a classic pairing that improves quercetin absorption and provides complementary antioxidant coverage. Quercetin plus zinc (as quercetin is a zinc ionophore) supports antiviral immune defense. Quercetin plus fisetin may provide more complete senolytic coverage. Quercetin plus bromelain improves bioavailability and may enhance anti-inflammatory effects.

How long does it take for quercetin to work for inflammation?

Anti-inflammatory effects from quercetin supplementation appear in clinical trials at four to eight weeks of consistent daily use, measured through blood markers like CRP and IL-6. Subjective improvements in joint comfort and energy may be noticed sooner or later depending on the individual and the extent of baseline inflammation. Consistent daily use over two to three months provides the most reliable assessment of benefit.
