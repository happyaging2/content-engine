What to Know

  • Urolithin A is a compound produced when gut bacteria convert ellagitannins from foods like pomegranates, walnuts, and berries into a bioactive molecule
  • Only 30 to 40% of adults have the gut bacteria needed to produce meaningful amounts of Urolithin A from food; the majority cannot convert ellagitannins efficiently regardless of diet
  • Urolithin A activates mitophagy, the cellular recycling process that removes damaged mitochondria and allows cells to replace them with healthier ones
  • Human clinical trials have shown that supplemental Urolithin A increases muscle endurance and reduces inflammation in older adults even in non-producers
  • For women over 40, mitophagy is especially relevant because mitochondrial quality declines with age and hormonal transition, contributing to fatigue, muscle loss, and metabolic slowdown
  • According to Happy Aging's review of the research, direct Urolithin A supplementation bypasses the gut bacteria conversion gap and is the most reliable way to access its cellular benefits

You may have heard that pomegranate is a longevity superfood, or that eating more walnuts supports healthy aging. Both claims trace back, in part, to a single compound: Urolithin A. But the story has a significant twist that most supplement marketing skips over entirely. Whether you actually benefit from pomegranates or walnuts, in terms of Urolithin A specifically, depends almost entirely on which gut bacteria you happen to have. And for most people over 40, the answer may be: not the right ones.

What Is Urolithin A?

Urolithin A is a compound in the urolithin family, a class of molecules produced when specific gut bacteria metabolize ellagitannins and ellagic acid. Ellagitannins are polyphenols found in pomegranates, walnuts, raspberries, strawberries, and some oak-aged wines. The conversion pathway goes: ellagitannins in food are first broken down to ellagic acid in the gut, then specific bacteria convert ellagic acid into urolithins including Urolithin A (UA), Urolithin B, and several isomers.

Urolithin A itself is not found in food in meaningful amounts. It is a product of microbial biotransformation that happens inside the gut. This is a critical distinction because it means the health benefits associated with ellagitannin-rich foods are not universal. They depend on the specific microbial composition of your gut, which varies enormously between individuals and changes with age, antibiotic use, diet patterns, and health status.

Why It Matters for Women Over 40

Mitophagy is one of the most important cellular maintenance processes for aging well, and it is one that begins to fail in midlife. The word comes from combining "mitochondria" with "autophagy" (cellular self-eating), and it describes the process by which cells identify damaged or dysfunctional mitochondria, tag them for removal, and recycle their components. When this process works well, cells maintain a pool of healthy, efficient mitochondria. When it fails, damaged mitochondria accumulate, produce excessive reactive oxygen species (ROS), and impair cellular energy production.

For women over 40, the stakes are high. Estrogen directly supports mitochondrial function through multiple mechanisms, including regulating the mitochondrial antioxidant defense system and promoting mitochondrial biogenesis. As estrogen declines through perimenopause and menopause, mitochondrial quality control becomes more vulnerable, and the cellular cost of failing mitophagy is paid in the currency of fatigue, muscle weakness, brain fog, and accelerated tissue aging.

Urolithin A activates the PINK1/Parkin pathway, which is the primary molecular switch for mitophagy. When UA is present, damaged mitochondria are flagged for autophagosomal clearance with greater efficiency. This is one of the few known natural compounds that directly activates this pathway in human cells at concentrations achievable through supplementation.

What the Research Says

The human evidence for Urolithin A is more developed than for most longevity compounds, owing largely to a rigorous clinical program from the research group that developed Timeline's Mitopure (a pharmaceutical-grade UA product) and published in peer-reviewed journals.

A 2019 study published in Nature Metabolism (Ryu et al., DOI: 10.1038/s42255-019-0073-4) conducted the first randomized controlled trial of Urolithin A in humans. Middle-aged and older adults received 500 or 1,000 mg UA per day for four weeks. Results showed that UA significantly increased mitochondrial gene expression in muscle tissue and reduced plasma inflammatory markers (notably IL-6 and CRP), even in participants who were classified as non-producers based on their gut bacteria profile. This was a landmark finding: it demonstrated that supplemental UA could bypass the gut conversion barrier entirely.

A 2022 randomized controlled trial published in JAMA Network Open (Singh et al., DOI: 10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2022.18232) tested 1,000 mg UA per day for 4 months in older adults with low muscle endurance. The UA group showed significantly greater improvement in muscle endurance (measured by hand grip and walking endurance tests) compared to placebo. Muscle gene expression analysis showed increased mitophagy markers, consistent with the proposed mechanism.

A 2023 study in the same population confirmed improvements in mitochondrial biomarkers and muscle strength with sustained supplementation, with a favorable safety profile and no significant adverse events at standard doses.

What the Evidence Does Not Support

The evidence base for Urolithin A is real but still developing, and some of the claims circulating in the wellness space outpace what the trials can actually support.

Urolithin A has not been shown to reverse sarcopenia (age-related muscle loss) in clinically meaningful ways on its own. The improvements in muscle endurance observed in trials are real but modest in magnitude. UA supports cellular quality control; it does not rebuild muscle without the resistance training stimulus that signals muscle protein synthesis. Women who are not doing regular resistance exercise will not see the muscle-related benefits that UA can complement.

The anti-cancer and anti-inflammatory benefits of pomegranates and ellagitannin-rich foods, which are cited in epidemiological studies, cannot be attributed solely to Urolithin A. These foods contain hundreds of bioactive compounds, and isolating any single mechanism is premature. The longevity associations with pomegranate-heavy diets in Mediterranean and Middle Eastern populations reflect a dietary pattern, not a single supplement effect.

There is no human evidence that Urolithin A extends lifespan, despite strong findings in C. elegans and rodent models. The cellular mechanisms observed in human trials are promising and directionally aligned with healthy aging, but the leap to human longevity extension is not supported by any human data yet.

Who This Is NOT For

Urolithin A is not a replacement for the lifestyle foundations of healthy aging. Women who are sedentary and looking to UA to compensate for lack of physical activity will see minimal benefit. The mitophagy pathway is most relevant in the context of active muscle use; the quality control signal is most powerful when muscles are actually being challenged through exercise.

Women with a diverse, whole-foods diet rich in pomegranates, berries, and nuts who happen to be high-producers of UA from gut bacteria may already have reasonable baseline UA levels and may see less incremental benefit from supplementation than low-producers. Without testing gut microbiome UA production status (which is not widely available clinically), it is not possible to know your producer status without supplementation or specialized testing.

Women who are pregnant, breastfeeding, or have active inflammatory bowel disease should consult their physician before starting UA supplementation, as the clinical trials have not included these populations and the effects on gut barrier function in these states are not characterized.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if I am a UA producer or non-producer?

There is no widely available consumer test for UA producer status. Some specialized microbiome testing companies include Urolithin A production prediction in their reports, but the accuracy varies. The practical alternative is to consume a controlled amount of pomegranate juice (one cup) and measure urinary Urolithin A output, but this requires laboratory testing not available to most consumers. For most women over 40, assuming non-producer status and using supplemental UA is the most reliable approach.

What does mitophagy actually feel like? Will I notice when it improves?

Mitophagy itself is invisible, occurring at the cellular level. What women notice when mitophagy and mitochondrial quality improve are the downstream effects: improved energy levels that feel more sustained throughout the day, better recovery after exercise, and reduced feelings of physical fatigue. These changes tend to develop gradually over 8 to 12 weeks rather than appearing suddenly.

Can I get enough Urolithin A from eating pomegranates every day?

If you are a high producer, daily pomegranate consumption may provide a meaningful but still lower UA dose than used in clinical trials. If you are not a high producer (which applies to the majority of adults), pomegranates will not meaningfully raise your UA levels regardless of consumption. The food-based approach is pleasant and provides other nutritional benefits, but it is not a reliable substitute for direct UA supplementation.

Are there any interactions between Urolithin A and medications?

No significant drug interactions with Urolithin A have been identified in published clinical trials. UA is primarily metabolized in the gut and liver, and no CYP450 enzyme interactions have been reported. Women on immunosuppressant medications should consult their physician, as UA affects autophagy pathways that are active in immune cells. Otherwise, current evidence suggests a clean safety profile.
