DHEA for Women Over 40: Benefits, Risks, and What the Research Says

DHEA is a hormone precursor that declines sharply after age 30, and by 40 most women have lost a third or more of their peak DHEA output. It remains one of the most studied and most misunderstood supplements in women's hormonal health, with real RCT evidence for specific benefits and real risks that get glossed over in popular wellness coverage.

What to Know Before You Read
  • DHEA is a hormone precursor produced mainly by the adrenal glands. It converts to both estrogen and testosterone depending on your body's needs.
  • DHEA peaks in your mid-20s and declines by 1-3% per year after that.
  • Clinical evidence supports DHEA for bone density, libido, vaginal health, and mood in postmenopausal women at low doses (25-50 mg).
  • Higher doses (50-100 mg) are associated with androgenic side effects including acne, facial hair, and voice changes.
  • Women with hormone-sensitive cancers should not take DHEA without oncologist guidance.
  • DHEA is available over the counter in the US but is a prescription drug in Canada, Australia, and the UK.

What the Research Says

The clinical evidence for DHEA in women over 40 is strongest in three areas. Bone density: several RCTs in postmenopausal women found low-dose DHEA (50 mg/day) improves lumbar spine and hip bone mineral density. Vaginal health: intravaginal DHEA (prasterone) has FDA approval as Intrarosa for dyspareunia in postmenopausal women. Mood: a double-blind crossover NIMH trial found DHEA at 90 mg/day improved wellbeing, energy, and mood in adults aged 40-70 with low DHEAS levels.

According to Happy Aging's review, benefits are most meaningful when DHEAS levels are confirmed to be low and doses are kept at or below 50 mg per day.

Who Should NOT Take DHEA

  • Personal history of breast, ovarian, uterine, or other hormone-sensitive cancers
  • Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) where androgen levels are already elevated
  • Active acne or hirsutism that is already significant
  • Pregnancy or active breastfeeding
  • Use of insulin, anticoagulants, or corticosteroids

The Happy Aging Recommendation

  1. Get your DHEAS level tested before starting. A morning serum DHEAS test is inexpensive and widely available.
  2. Confirm with your provider that DHEA is appropriate for your health history.
  3. Start low at 10-25 mg per day, not 50-100 mg.
  4. Retest at 3 months. Check DHEAS, total testosterone, and free testosterone.
  5. Do not combine DHEA with other hormonal supplements without medical guidance.
