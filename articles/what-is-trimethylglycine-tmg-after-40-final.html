Trimethylglycine (TMG) has been quietly gaining attention in the longevity and anti-aging community, and for good reason. Also known as betaine, TMG is a naturally occurring compound found in beets, spinach, quinoa, and seafood. It plays a central role in methylation, one of the most important biochemical processes in the body, and it has emerged as a meaningful complement to NAD+ precursors like NMN for women over 40 pursuing a comprehensive cellular health strategy. Understanding what TMG is, what it does in the body, and why its role becomes more relevant after 40 is valuable for anyone looking beyond basic supplements into the more nuanced landscape of longevity biochemistry.

What to Know

  • TMG (trimethylglycine) is a methyl donor: it donates methyl groups to dozens of biochemical reactions including homocysteine metabolism, DNA methylation, and creatine synthesis.
  • Methylation efficiency often declines with age, and TMG supplementation supports its restoration.
  • TMG lowers homocysteine, an amino acid that rises with age and is associated with cardiovascular disease, cognitive decline, and bone loss when elevated.
  • When taking NMN or other NAD+ precursors, TMG is often recommended as a co-supplement because NAD+ synthesis consumes methyl groups, and TMG replenishes this supply.
  • Food sources include beets, spinach, quinoa, wheat germ, and shellfish. Most people need supplemental TMG to reach the amounts used in research.

What Is Methylation and Why It Matters After 40

Methylation is the addition of a methyl group (one carbon and three hydrogen atoms: CH3) to DNA, proteins, or small molecules. It sounds technical but its effects are profoundly biological. Methylation of DNA regulates gene expression, switching genes on or off without changing the underlying genetic code. This process is central to immune function, inflammation control, detoxification in the liver, neurotransmitter production, and hormone metabolism. Methylation patterns in DNA are one of the most reliable biological clocks: the Horvath epigenetic clock, for example, measures aging by changes in DNA methylation patterns across hundreds of sites in the genome. As methylation capacity declines with age, this contributes to dysregulated gene expression, increased cancer risk, impaired detoxification, elevated inflammation, and rising homocysteine levels. After 40, several factors compound this decline: declining B12 levels (a key methylation cofactor), hormonal shifts that alter estrogen metabolism (which is heavily methylation-dependent), and increased metabolic demand on methyl groups from stress, inflammation, and poor diet. TMG directly addresses this by providing the methyl groups that the methylation cycle consumes.

The Homocysteine Connection

Homocysteine is an amino acid produced during methionine metabolism. In a healthy methylation cycle, homocysteine is quickly re-methylated back to methionine using methyl groups donated by TMG, folate, or vitamin B12. When methylation is impaired or methyl donor supply is insufficient, homocysteine accumulates. Elevated homocysteine is not benign. It is an independent risk factor for cardiovascular disease, directly damaging the endothelium (inner lining of blood vessels) through multiple mechanisms including oxidative stress, impaired nitric oxide production, and promotion of platelet aggregation. Elevated homocysteine is also strongly associated with cognitive decline and dementia risk: a meta-analysis published in Neurology found that high homocysteine levels were associated with a significantly increased risk of Alzheimer's disease. For bone health, homocysteine interferes with collagen cross-linking in bone matrix, reducing structural integrity. Homocysteine levels naturally rise with age, and post-menopausal women show particularly significant increases. TMG supplementation consistently lowers homocysteine in clinical trials: a meta-analysis published in the European Journal of Nutrition found that betaine supplementation reduced plasma homocysteine by an average of 1.3 mmol/L, a clinically meaningful reduction.

TMG as a Complement to NMN and NAD+ Supplementation

One of the most practical reasons women taking NMN or nicotinamide riboside (NR) add TMG to their protocol is the methyl group consumption issue. When NMN is converted to NAD+ and NAD+ is subsequently consumed by the sirtuin and PARP enzymes it powers, the metabolic byproduct is nicotinamide. Nicotinamide is then methylated (consuming methyl groups) and excreted as N-methylnicotinamide in urine. At higher doses of NMN or NR, this process can consume a significant amount of methyl groups, potentially contributing to methyl donor depletion and rising homocysteine if not replenished. TMG supplementation (typically 500 mg to 1,000 mg per day) alongside NMN provides the methyl group supply needed to maintain healthy methylation while supporting NAD+ levels. Several longevity researchers and clinicians who use NAD+ precursors routinely recommend TMG as a co-supplement for this reason. The combination is synergistic: NMN raises cellular energy capacity while TMG maintains the methylation infrastructure that cellular health depends on.

Other Benefits of TMG After 40

Beyond homocysteine reduction and methylation support, TMG has several additional benefits relevant to women over 40. Athletic performance research initially drove much of the TMG literature: studies consistently show that TMG supplementation (2 to 2.5 grams per day) improves muscle endurance, strength, and body composition in active individuals, with effects attributed to its role in creatine synthesis (creatine requires methyl groups, and TMG supplies them) and its osmolyte function (TMG helps cells maintain proper hydration under stress). For women working to preserve muscle after 40, this is relevant alongside resistance training. Liver health is another area: TMG is classified as a lipotropic agent, meaning it supports the liver's ability to process and export fat. Studies show that betaine supplementation reduces liver fat accumulation and improves markers of non-alcoholic fatty liver disease, which becomes more common in women after menopause due to metabolic changes. TMG also appears to reduce markers of oxidative stress and support healthy blood pressure in people with elevated cardiovascular risk. Its broad safety profile and multiple overlapping benefits make it a practical addition to a longevity supplement protocol.

How to Take TMG: Sources, Dosage, and Practical Guidance

TMG is found naturally in beets (the richest food source), spinach, quinoa, wheat germ, and shellfish including shrimp and prawns. However, the amounts in food are modest compared to the doses used in research, which typically range from 500 mg to 6 grams per day depending on the target outcome. For general methylation support and homocysteine management, 500 mg to 1,500 mg per day is a reasonable range. For athletic performance benefits, studies used 2 to 2.5 grams per day. For liver health, doses of 1 to 2 grams per day showed benefit. TMG supplements are available in powder form (often included in NAD+ precursor formulas or greens powders) and capsules. Powder forms may have a slightly sweet taste due to the glycine component. TMG is well-tolerated by most people, with occasional mild digestive discomfort at higher doses. It should be taken with food to minimize this. TMG for Athletic Performance and Muscle Preservation After 40

Beyond methylation and longevity applications, TMG has a well-established evidence base in exercise science that is directly relevant to women over 40 working to preserve muscle and physical performance. The athletic performance literature on TMG (mostly published under its synonym "betaine") consistently shows improvements in strength, power output, and body composition. A review of betaine supplementation studies by Cholewa et al. found that the majority of controlled trials showed significant improvements in muscle strength (bench press, squat), muscle power (vertical jump, sprint performance), and changes in body composition favoring lean mass over fat mass. The mechanism involves several pathways: creatine synthesis (TMG donates methyl groups required for creatine production in the liver), osmotic cell protection (TMG acts as an intracellular osmolyte, helping cells maintain hydration under exercise stress and reducing exercise-induced cellular damage), and indirect support for nitric oxide availability through homocysteine lowering. For women over 40 who are engaged in resistance training programs to counter muscle loss and maintain metabolic health, TMG provides meaningful support that complements protein intake and creatine supplementation. The doses used in athletic performance research (2 to 2.5 grams per day) are higher than those used for methylation support alone, and taking TMG with meals as part of a pre- or post-workout nutrition approach is practical for active women incorporating it into a training protocol.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is TMG the same as betaine?

Yes. TMG and betaine are the same compound. Betaine was the original name (named after beets, where it was first discovered). TMG is the chemical name that describes its structure: glycine with three methyl groups attached. Both names refer to the same molecule.

Should I take TMG with NMN?

Many longevity researchers recommend combining TMG with NMN. NAD+ metabolism consumes methyl groups, and TMG replenishes them, potentially preventing the homocysteine rise that could otherwise accompany high-dose NAD+ precursor supplementation. A dose of 500 mg to 1,000 mg of TMG per day is commonly used alongside NMN.

How quickly does TMG lower homocysteine?

Studies show measurable reductions in homocysteine within four to six weeks of consistent TMG supplementation. The reduction is dose-dependent and most significant in people with initially elevated homocysteine levels (above 10 mmol/L).

What foods are highest in TMG?

Beets and beet juice are the richest common food sources of TMG. Other good sources include spinach, quinoa, wheat germ, and shellfish like shrimp. Beet powder and beet root supplements also provide concentrated TMG.

Can TMG improve energy levels?

Indirectly, yes. TMG supports cellular methylation, creatine synthesis, and the methyl donor pool that NAD+ metabolism relies on. Women who add TMG to an NMN protocol often report improved energy, which may reflect the synergistic effect of supporting both NAD+ production and the methylation infrastructure it depends on.

Does TMG support liver health after 40?

TMG has a well-established role in liver metabolism, where it is a primary source of methyl groups for hepatic methylation reactions. Research shows that TMG supplementation reduces liver fat accumulation, lowers liver enzyme markers (ALT and AST) in people with non-alcoholic fatty liver disease, and supports bile acid metabolism. Women over 40 experiencing metabolic changes that affect liver function may find TMG particularly valuable as a dual-purpose methyl donor and hepatoprotective agent. The liver's methylation demand increases substantially during hormonal transitions, making adequate TMG status important for detoxification and estrogen metabolism.
