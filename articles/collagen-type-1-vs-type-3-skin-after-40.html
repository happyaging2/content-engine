If you have ever shopped for collagen supplements and noticed that some products emphasize "Type I and III collagen" while others focus on "Type II," you have encountered a distinction that matters more than most supplement marketing acknowledges. Different types of collagen serve different structural roles in the body, and for women over 40 who are primarily concerned with skin firmness, elasticity, and wrinkle reduction, understanding which types dominate skin architecture and which supplements deliver those types is essential for making an effective choice. This article breaks down the key collagen types, explains why Types I and III are the most important for skin after 40, and covers what the research shows about restoring them through diet and supplementation.

What to Know

  • Type I collagen makes up approximately 80% of the skin's collagen and provides tensile strength and structural support
  • Type III collagen provides the elasticity and suppleness of young skin and is found alongside Type I in the dermis

  • Marine collagen (from fish skin) is predominately Type I and is the most bioavailable and skin-targeted collagen supplement source

The Collagen Family: A Brief Taxonomy

Collagen is the most abundant protein in the human body, making up approximately 30% of total body protein. It forms the structural scaffolding of skin, tendons, bones, cartilage, blood vessels, and organs. Researchers have identified 28 distinct types of collagen, each characterized by the specific chains of amino acids that form its triple-helix structure and the tissues in which it predominantly occurs.

Type I collagen is the most abundant collagen in the body, found in skin, bone, tendons, ligaments, and the cornea. In the dermis (the deep layer of skin), Type I collagen fibers are tightly bundled and provide tensile strength: the structural rigidity that resists pulling forces and prevents sagging. When Type I declines, skin loses its structural support and begins to droop and fold.

Type III collagen is the second most common type in the dermis and is found alongside Type I in a flexible reticular (net-like) fiber arrangement. It is the collagen of elasticity: Type III is responsible for the bounce and spring of young skin, the property that allows it to return to its original position after being pressed or stretched. It is also found in large amounts in blood vessel walls, giving them flexibility. Early in life, Type III collagen is relatively more abundant; as we age and Type III declines faster than Type I, skin loses its springiness before it loses its bulk.

Type II collagen is found almost exclusively in cartilage and is the primary target of joint-support collagen supplements. It has minimal presence in the skin and is not the relevant type for skin anti-aging purposes.

Type IV collagen forms the basement membrane that anchors the dermis to the epidermis. It is synthesized in the skin but is not well replicated by oral supplementation due to its non-fibrillar structure.

Why Both Type I and Type III Decline After 40

Skin collagen synthesis is driven primarily by fibroblasts, the specialized cells in the dermis that produce and maintain the extracellular matrix. After 40, fibroblast activity declines, and the balance between collagen synthesis and degradation shifts toward net loss.

The rate of decline is approximately 1% of collagen content per year after age 30, accelerating to approximately 2-3% per year in the first decade after menopause due to the loss of estrogen, which normally stimulates fibroblast activity and inhibits collagenase (the enzyme that breaks down collagen).

Type III collagen tends to decline proportionally faster than Type I. This creates a characteristic aging pattern: skin becomes less springy and elastic before it fully loses volume and firmness. Women in their 40s often describe this as skin that "doesn't bounce back" the way it used to, even before obvious sagging is apparent.

Additional factors accelerating Type I and III collagen decline include UV radiation (which activates matrix metalloproteinases that degrade collagen fibers), smoking, high sugar intake (through glycation of collagen fibers), and chronic inflammation (which degrades extracellular matrix through inflammatory proteases).

Which Collagen Supplement Is Best for Skin After 40?

The most important factor for collagen supplementation is not the type listed on the label, but the bioavailability of the collagen peptides. When you consume hydrolyzed collagen (collagen that has been broken down into small peptides), it is absorbed from the gut as proline-hydroxyproline and glycine-proline dipeptides, which then circulate to skin fibroblasts and stimulate local collagen production.

The fibroblasts themselves determine what type of collagen to produce based on local tissue signals. Oral collagen peptides do not directly "become" Type I or III collagen; they act as signaling molecules that prompt fibroblasts to upregulate collagen synthesis in general.

Marine collagen (fish skin and scales) is predominantly Type I collagen with smaller amounts of Type III. Its peptides have a molecular weight of approximately 500-1,500 Da (smaller than bovine collagen peptides), which may explain why some research finds higher bioavailability from marine sources. A 2019 meta-analysis of collagen peptide studies found consistent improvements in skin hydration, elasticity, and wrinkle depth, with marine collagen studies showing robust results across multiple endpoints.

Bovine collagen (from cow hides) contains both Type I and Type III and is the most commonly used collagen source in the supplement industry. It is effective and less expensive than marine collagen. For women concerned specifically with skin elasticity restoration (Type III decline), bovine collagen's Type III content makes it a logical choice, though the absolute amounts in supplements vary by manufacturer.

Vitamin C co-supplementation. This is non-negotiable: vitamin C is the essential cofactor for prolyl hydroxylase and lysyl hydroxylase, the enzymes that stabilize newly synthesized collagen chains. Without adequate vitamin C, collagen synthesis cannot proceed to produce mature, stable collagen fibers. Supplementing collagen without ensuring adequate vitamin C is significantly less effective than the combination.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between Type I and Type III collagen?

Type I collagen provides tensile strength and structural support to skin, bones, and tendons. It makes up about 80% of skin collagen. Type III collagen provides the elastic, springy quality of young skin. Both types are found in the dermis and both decline with age, with Type III declining faster, which is why skin loses its bounce before it loses its bulk.

Which collagen is best for skin?

For skin anti-aging purposes, supplements providing Type I collagen (marine collagen) or Type I and III combined (bovine collagen) are most relevant. Type II collagen is primarily for joint support and has minimal skin benefits. Marine collagen has slightly higher bioavailability due to smaller peptide size and is the preferred choice for skin-focused supplementation.

How long does collagen supplementation take to work for skin?

Most clinical trials show measurable improvements in skin hydration and elasticity after 8-12 weeks of daily supplementation (typically 2.5-10 g per day of hydrolyzed collagen peptides). Wrinkle depth reductions are often detected in studies using objective skin measurement tools. Subjective appearance improvements are typically noticed by women at 8-10 weeks.

Does vitamin C actually help collagen production?

Yes. Vitamin C is an essential cofactor for the enzymes that stabilize newly synthesized collagen chains. Without it, procollagen cannot be properly hydroxylated, resulting in unstable, non-functional collagen fibers. Studies confirm that vitamin C and collagen peptides together produce significantly greater improvements in skin metrics than collagen peptides alone.

Can you get enough collagen from food?

Bone broth, slow-cooked meat with connective tissue, and fish with skin all provide collagen precursors. However, the amounts of collagen-stimulating peptides in typical dietary portions are lower than the doses used in clinical trials (2.5-10 g per day). For women specifically targeting measurable skin improvements, supplementation at clinical doses provides a more reliable approach than diet alone.

Optimizing Your Collagen Strategy: The Complete Protocol for Women Over 40

Collagen supplementation works, but the evidence also shows that the context in which you take collagen matters as much as the dose. Getting the most from collagen peptides after 40 involves layering the nutritional, lifestyle, and timing factors that maximize fibroblast activity and minimize the forces that degrade newly synthesized collagen.

Vitamin C timing. Collagen supplementation is significantly more effective when taken with vitamin C. For women who take collagen in the morning as part of a coffee or smoothie routine, ensuring that vitamin C is present simultaneously (either from fruit or a vitamin C supplement of 500-1,000 mg) is a simple but important optimization. Post-exercise timing is another consideration: resistance training creates controlled micro-damage in connective tissue that upregulates collagen synthesis for 24-48 hours afterward. Taking collagen with vitamin C in the 30-60 minute window after exercise may enhance the targeted delivery of collagen precursors to the tissues most actively remodeling.

Managing the factors that degrade collagen. Collagen synthesis is a losing battle if the rate of degradation exceeds the rate of production. The three most significant accelerators of Type I and III collagen degradation in women over 40 are advanced glycation end-products (AGEs) from high-sugar diets, UV radiation without sun protection, and chronic systemic inflammation driven by gut dysbiosis, excess visceral fat, or poor sleep quality. Addressing these three factors creates a permissive environment for supplemental collagen to rebuild rather than merely replace what is being continuously lost.

Collagen for connective tissue beyond skin. Type I and III collagen are not only skin proteins. They are the primary structural proteins in tendons, ligaments, and fascia. Women over 40 who are physically active often notice connective tissue vulnerability alongside skin changes. A collagen supplementation protocol designed for skin also benefits joint connective tissue integrity, reducing the risk of tendon injury during exercise and supporting the flexibility and resilience of ligaments that become less tolerant of mechanical stress with estrogen decline.

Consistency for at least 8-12 weeks is the minimum timeline for measurable change. Women who report that collagen did not work for them typically used it for less than six weeks or did not pair it with vitamin C and adequate protein intake to support the full biosynthesis pathway. The combination of marine collagen, vitamin C, and adequate dietary protein creates the complete substrate for the fibroblast-mediated collagen renewal that visibly improves skin firmness and elasticity after 40.
