Copper is one of the most essential and most overlooked minerals in the human body. It is needed for iron absorption, collagen synthesis, nerve function, and the production of a key antioxidant enzyme called superoxide dismutase. Despite its importance, copper deficiency is more common than most women realize, particularly after 40, when the factors that deplete copper tend to compound quietly over time. If you have been experiencing unexplained fatigue, frequent illness, brittle hair, or tingling in your limbs, copper is a mineral worth knowing more about. This guide covers the most telling signs of copper shortfall, why it becomes more likely after 40, and how to restore your levels effectively.

What to Know

  • Copper supports iron metabolism, collagen production, antioxidant defense, and nerve conduction
  • Signs of deficiency include fatigue, premature grey hair, pale skin, numbness, and impaired immunity
  • Excessive zinc supplementation is a leading cause of copper deficiency because zinc and copper compete for absorption
  • The recommended daily intake is 0.9 mg for adult women, but many women fall below this threshold
  • Liver, shellfish, nuts, seeds, and dark chocolate are the richest dietary sources of copper

What Does Copper Do in the Body?

Copper functions as a cofactor for a range of enzymes that govern some of the body's most vital processes.

Iron metabolism. One of copper's most critical jobs is enabling the ferroxidase enzyme ceruloplasmin, which converts iron into its transportable form. Without adequate copper, iron cannot be properly loaded into transferrin, the protein that carries iron through the blood. This means copper deficiency can mimic iron deficiency anemia, even when dietary iron intake is sufficient.

Collagen and elastin cross-linking. The enzyme lysyl oxidase, which requires copper, is responsible for cross-linking collagen and elastin fibers, giving your skin, blood vessels, and bones their structural strength. After 40, when collagen production is already declining, copper deficiency accelerates the loss of skin firmness and connective tissue integrity.

Antioxidant defense. Copper is a core component of superoxide dismutase (SOD), one of the body's most powerful antioxidant enzymes. SOD neutralizes superoxide radicals that are generated during normal cellular energy production. Without enough copper, oxidative stress accelerates throughout the body.

Nervous system function. Copper is required for myelin synthesis, the insulating layer that protects nerve fibers and enables fast signal transmission. Low copper impairs myelin integrity, leading to neurological symptoms that can be mistaken for other conditions.

7 Signs You May Be Low in Copper After 40

1. Persistent fatigue and weakness. Because copper is needed to process iron and support mitochondrial energy production, deficiency often manifests as deep tiredness that does not respond to rest. This is especially confusing because standard iron blood tests may come back normal.

2. Frequent infections and slow recovery. Copper is needed for the maturation and activity of neutrophils and natural killer cells. Studies consistently show that copper-deficient individuals have impaired immune responses, catching infections more easily and taking longer to recover.

3. Premature grey hair or hair thinning. Copper is essential for melanin production, the pigment that gives hair its color. A decline in copper can accelerate greying. Copper also supports the integrity of hair follicles, so deficiency is associated with increased hair loss and brittleness.

4. Pale or sallow skin. Since copper is needed for iron transport (via ceruloplasmin) and for melanin synthesis, low copper can leave the skin looking pallid and dull. The skin may lose its natural warmth and pigmentation evenness.

5. Numbness, tingling, or balance problems. Copper deficiency myelopathy is a well-recognized neurological syndrome that presents exactly as described: numbness and tingling in the hands and feet, unsteady gait, and muscle weakness. It is often misdiagnosed as vitamin B12 deficiency because the presentation is nearly identical.

6. Increased bruising or slow wound healing. Because copper-dependent lysyl oxidase is required for collagen cross-linking, a shortfall weakens blood vessel walls and skin repair. This leads to easy bruising, fragile skin, and slower healing after cuts or scrapes.

7. Joint pain and bone fragility. Copper supports bone matrix formation through collagen and plays a role in osteoblast (bone-building cell) activity. Deficiency is associated with reduced bone mineral density, a concern that overlaps significantly with postmenopausal bone loss.

Why Copper Deficiency Is Underdiagnosed After 40

One of the biggest reasons copper deficiency goes unrecognized is that its symptoms overlap so closely with those of iron deficiency, vitamin B12 deficiency, and general aging. A standard blood panel typically checks for iron and B12 but rarely tests serum copper or ceruloplasmin levels.

The zinc-copper competition. Zinc and copper are absorbed through the same intestinal transporter (metallothionein). When you take high doses of zinc, copper absorption is blocked. With the rise in zinc supplementation for immune health, copper deficiency from zinc competition has become increasingly common. Women taking more than 25-40 mg of zinc per day without balancing it with copper are at particular risk.

Gut absorption changes. Conditions that impair gut absorptive surface, including celiac disease, Crohn's disease, and post-bariatric surgery, significantly reduce copper absorption. Age-related changes in gut motility and secretion also reduce mineral absorption efficiency over time.

High-dose vitamin C supplements. Very high doses of supplemental vitamin C (above 2,000 mg per day) can impair copper absorption by reducing copper to a less absorbable form. Women taking megadose vitamin C should be mindful of their copper status.

Best Food Sources of Copper

The body cannot produce copper, so dietary intake is essential. The richest sources include:

Organ meats: Beef liver is the single most concentrated source, with a 3-ounce serving providing about 14 mg of copper, far exceeding the daily requirement. Chicken liver is also an excellent source.

Shellfish: Oysters are exceptionally rich in copper. A small serving of oysters can provide several times the daily recommended intake in a highly bioavailable form.

Nuts and seeds: Cashews, almonds, sunflower seeds, and sesame seeds are good plant-based sources. A quarter cup of cashews provides roughly 0.6 mg.

Dark chocolate: An ounce of dark chocolate (85% cacao or higher) contains about 0.5 mg of copper alongside its antioxidant flavanols.

Legumes: Lentils, chickpeas, and black beans provide modest copper with the added benefit of fiber for gut health.

Aim to include at least two copper-rich foods in your daily diet. If you supplement zinc, pair it with 1-2 mg of copper to maintain the balanced ratio your body requires.

How to Test and Restore Your Copper Levels

The most reliable way to assess copper status is through a serum copper and ceruloplasmin test, available through any standard blood lab. Serum copper levels below 70 mcg/dL are generally considered low; the normal range is approximately 70-140 mcg/dL for adult women.

If your levels are low, or if you have multiple symptoms and your lifestyle fits the risk profile, a supplemental dose of 1-2 mg of elemental copper daily is typically sufficient to restore levels within four to eight weeks. Copper bisglycinate and copper gluconate are the most bioavailable and well-tolerated forms.

Higher therapeutic doses (above 3 mg per day) should be medically supervised, as copper toxicity is a real concern at excessive levels. The tolerable upper intake level for copper is set at 10 mg per day, providing a wide safety margin at typical supplemental doses of 1-3 mg.

The most practical approach for most women is to ensure a comprehensive longevity formula includes copper in a properly balanced ratio alongside any zinc and other trace minerals, rather than supplementing copper in isolation.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the most common causes of copper deficiency in women over 40?

The most frequent causes are high-dose zinc supplementation without copper balance, poor gut absorption, and inadequate dietary intake from low organ meat and shellfish consumption. Conditions like celiac disease and gastric bypass also significantly increase risk.

Can copper deficiency cause anemia?

Yes. Copper is required for iron transport via ceruloplasmin. Without adequate copper, iron cannot move effectively from storage to the bloodstream, producing an anemia that looks like iron deficiency but does not respond to iron supplementation. Testing both copper and iron together is important when evaluating persistent fatigue.

How much copper does a woman over 40 need daily?

The RDA is 0.9 mg per day. Women taking supplemental zinc or with digestive absorption issues may benefit from 1-2 mg per day to ensure adequate copper status. The tolerable upper limit is 10 mg per day, so modest supplementation is well within a safe range.

Does copper help with skin and hair after 40?

Yes. Copper is directly involved in collagen cross-linking (through lysyl oxidase) and melanin production (through tyrosinase). Correcting copper deficiency can support skin firmness and help slow premature greying, though it is not a standalone anti-aging treatment.

Is copper in a multivitamin enough?

Most standard multivitamins contain 0.9-2 mg of copper, which is adequate for women without elevated needs. If you are taking supplemental zinc at doses above 15-20 mg, verify your multivitamin includes copper at a ratio of approximately 10:1 zinc-to-copper to offset the competition for absorption.

Signs Your Copper Levels Are Improving After Supplementation

One of the practical challenges with correcting copper deficiency is that recovery is gradual and the signs of improvement can be subtle at first. Knowing what to look for helps you assess whether your supplementation strategy is working before you invest in follow-up blood testing.

Energy improvements usually come first. Because copper is needed for iron transport and mitochondrial energy production, the fatigue associated with copper deficiency tends to be one of the earlier symptoms to improve once levels begin recovering. Women often notice less afternoon energy crashes and more consistent energy through the day within four to six weeks of consistent supplementation at therapeutic doses.

Immune resilience is a slower but reliable indicator. If you previously caught every cold going around and recovery took longer than expected, tracking your illness frequency over a three-to-six month period provides a meaningful measure of improving immune function. Copper-dependent neutrophil and natural killer cell activity normalizes over this timeframe.

Hair quality changes take longer. Hair growth cycles are long, and changes in follicle health driven by nutritional correction take three to six months to manifest in visibly healthier new growth. Early signs include reduced hair shedding (which often precedes improved regrowth) and improved hair texture.

Confirm with re-testing. After eight to twelve weeks of consistent supplementation, a repeat serum copper and ceruloplasmin test will confirm normalization. Most women with a diagnosed deficiency see levels normalize within this window at appropriate supplemental doses of 1-2 mg daily elemental copper.
