What to Know

  • Fisetin is a flavonoid found naturally in strawberries, apples, and onions, now being researched for its ability to clear senescent "zombie" cells from the body.
  • Senolytics are compounds that selectively destroy senescent cells, which accumulate with age and release inflammatory signals that accelerate aging in surrounding tissues.
  • Animal studies on fisetin are genuinely impressive, showing extended lifespan and reduced frailty markers in mice. Human evidence is much more limited.
  • As of 2026, most published human trials on fisetin have focused on safety and tolerability, not on measuring longevity or cellular aging outcomes. Results from larger trials are pending.
  • The dose used in animal studies does not translate directly to humans in a straightforward way, and the optimal human dosing protocol remains unknown.
  • Fisetin has a strong theoretical rationale but is still an emerging compound. Women over 40 interested in longevity currently have stronger evidence-based options while fisetin research matures.

Fisetin has become one of the most discussed longevity compounds in the past few years, showing up in anti-aging circles, podcasts, and supplement stacks with increasing frequency. The excitement is not baseless: the animal data is striking, and the biology makes sense. But the honest picture of where the science actually stands is considerably more nuanced than the hype suggests. This article covers what fisetin is, what it may do, and exactly how confident we should be right now.

What Is Fisetin?

Fisetin (3,3',4',7-tetrahydroxyflavone) is a polyphenol flavonoid found naturally in a variety of fruits and vegetables. Strawberries contain the highest concentration of any common food, with approximately 160 micrograms per gram. It is also found in apples, grapes, cucumbers, onions, and mangoes, though at lower levels.

Fisetin has been studied for decades as an antioxidant and anti-inflammatory compound, but more recent research has focused on its potential as a senolytic, meaning a compound that selectively triggers cell death in senescent cells. This represents a shift from fisetin being viewed as a general antioxidant to being considered part of a more specific anti-aging strategy targeting the fundamental biology of cellular aging.

What makes fisetin potentially different from general antioxidants is its proposed mechanism. Unlike antioxidants that scavenge free radicals, senolytics work by activating apoptosis pathways specifically in cells that are already senescent but resisting normal cell death. Healthy cells are not significantly affected. This selectivity is what makes the concept so appealing from a longevity standpoint.

Why It Matters for Women Over 40

Cellular senescence accumulates faster after 40, and the rate of accumulation appears to accelerate around the hormonal changes of perimenopause. Senescent cells are characterized by their resistance to programmed cell death (apoptosis) and their secretion of a pro-inflammatory combination of cytokines, proteases, and growth factors collectively called the senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP). The SASP damages neighboring cells, contributes to chronic low-grade inflammation, and has been linked to accelerated tissue aging, metabolic dysfunction, and reduced regenerative capacity.

Research published in Nature Medicine (Kirkland et al., 2017, PMID 28481326) established that senescent cells have a causal role in driving aging phenotypes in mice, not just a correlational one. When senescent cells were cleared in mouse models, animals showed improvements in physical function, reduced frailty, and extended healthspan. This mechanistic clarity is why senolytics became a serious research focus and why fisetin attracted interest as a potentially accessible, food-derived option.

For women over 40, the accumulation of senescent cells in key tissues including adipose tissue, muscle, bone, and the ovaries has specific implications. Research suggests that ovarian senescence accelerates reproductive aging, and that adipose tissue senescence contributes to the metabolic changes and inflammation associated with menopause. A compound that could reduce this cellular burden is compelling in theory.

What the Research Says

The foundational fisetin study that generated significant attention was published in EBioMedicine in 2018 by Yousefzadeh et al. (PMID 30279143). Researchers at the Mayo Clinic found that fisetin was the most potent senolytic among 10 flavonoids tested in cell culture. In mice, both intermittent and continuous fisetin treatment reduced senescent cell burden across multiple tissues. Treated mice showed improvements in health metrics and extended median lifespan compared to controls. This was a landmark finding and appropriately generated significant scientific interest.

Subsequent animal research has continued to show encouraging results. A 2021 study published in Aging Cell (Kim et al.) found that fisetin treatment in aged mice improved cognitive function and reduced neuroinflammation. Other animal studies have examined fisetin's effects on Alzheimer's models, adipose tissue senescence, and lung aging with generally positive results.

The critical limitation is the translation to humans. Animal senolytic studies use doses that, when scaled by body weight, would be extremely high in human terms (sometimes cited as 20 to 100 mg/kg). A 60 kg woman scaled at 20 mg/kg would need 1,200 mg per day. Getting 1,200 mg from strawberries would require eating approximately 7 to 8 kg of strawberries daily. The supplement doses commonly sold (100 to 500 mg/day continuous, or 1,000 to 1,500 mg in pulsed protocols) are below these scaled animal doses, and whether any human dose actually achieves meaningful senolytic effect in tissue has not been clearly established.

What the Evidence Does Not Support

The most important caveat about fisetin is that, as of 2026, there are no published large-scale randomized controlled trials demonstrating that fisetin reduces senescent cell burden, extends healthspan, or improves longevity-related outcomes in healthy women over 40. The Mayo Clinic Translational Clinical Research Team has registered several trials (NCT numbers for fisetin in various conditions including frailty and osteoporosis), but peer-reviewed results from adequately powered trials have not been published in full.

The Phase 1 safety trials published so far have primarily confirmed that fisetin is generally well-tolerated at the doses tested and does not produce significant adverse events. That is meaningful for safety, but safety confirmation is not efficacy confirmation. A compound can be safe and still not work.

It is also important to distinguish between fisetin and the senolytic combination most rigorously studied in humans: dasatinib plus quercetin (D+Q). Dasatinib is an FDA-approved cancer drug (a tyrosine kinase inhibitor), not a supplement. The D+Q combination has more advanced human trial data than fisetin, including a small but published trial in idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis patients (Kirkland et al., 2019, EBioMedicine, PMID 31076309) showing reduced senescent cell markers in peripheral blood after intermittent D+Q treatment. Fisetin is often positioned as the accessible, supplement-based alternative to D+Q, but it has not yet demonstrated equivalent human evidence.

Additionally, the optimal human dosing protocol for fisetin remains genuinely unknown. Continuous low-dose supplementation and intermittent high-dose pulsing are both discussed in longevity communities, but these are extrapolated from mouse protocols, not validated human regimens. Neither approach has been demonstrated to produce meaningful senolytic activity in human tissue.

The Happy Aging Recommendation

According to Happy Aging's review of the fisetin literature, the compound is genuinely one of the most interesting emerging longevity research areas, but it is not yet ready to be the centerpiece of a longevity supplement strategy for women over 40. The animal evidence is compelling, the safety profile in early human trials looks acceptable, and the biological rationale is sound. The gap is efficacy evidence in humans, specifically women in the 40 to 60 age range.

For women who want to support cellular health and longevity pathways with the strongest available human evidence, NAD+ precursor supplementation has a larger and more mature evidence base for supporting mitochondrial function, DNA repair, and cellular energy metabolism. The decline of NAD+ with age is well-documented, and multiple human trials support supplementation with NMN or NR as a way to meaningfully raise NAD+ levels in tissue. That is a distinct but complementary approach to cellular longevity.

Fisetin is worth watching. If you are interested in supplementing it, the available safety data suggests it is unlikely to cause harm at common supplement doses. But approach it as an emerging compound you are monitoring, not as a proven longevity intervention. Revisit the evidence in 12 to 18 months as larger trials report results.

Who This Is NOT For

  • Women expecting proven longevity benefits from current supplement doses: The human evidence for fisetin's efficacy does not yet support it as a reliable longevity intervention. If you need something with a stronger clinical track record, look elsewhere for now.
  • Women on chemotherapy or immunosuppressive medications: Fisetin's mechanisms may interact with certain cancer therapies. Consult your oncologist before adding any polyphenol supplements during treatment.
  • Women taking blood thinners: Flavonoids including fisetin have some theoretical anticoagulant activity. Discuss with your doctor if you take warfarin, clopidogrel, or similar medications.
  • Women looking for a substitute for medical evaluation of aging concerns: If you have significant concerns about cognitive decline, physical frailty, or metabolic dysfunction, get proper clinical assessment first. Fisetin is not a treatment for medical conditions.
  • Anyone considering high-dose pulsed protocols without medical supervision: The very high doses sometimes discussed in longevity communities have no validated safety or efficacy data in humans. Self-experimenting with multi-gram doses is not evidence-based practice.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much fisetin is in strawberries?

Strawberries contain approximately 160 micrograms of fisetin per gram of fresh fruit. A standard serving of strawberries (about 100g) provides roughly 16 mg of fisetin. To reach even the lowest studied supplement doses of 100 mg per day from food alone would require eating close to 625 grams of strawberries daily. Supplementation is the only practical way to reach doses relevant to the research literature.

Is fisetin the same as quercetin?

No. Fisetin and quercetin are both flavonoids with senolytic research interest, but they are distinct compounds with different structures and potency profiles. In the 2018 Mayo Clinic cell culture study, fisetin was shown to be a more potent senolytic than quercetin. Quercetin's human senolytic data comes primarily from its combination with dasatinib (a prescription drug), not from quercetin alone.

What are the early human safety findings for fisetin?

Early phase 1 human trials have generally found fisetin to be well tolerated at doses up to 1,000 mg per day over short periods. No serious adverse events have been reported in the published safety literature. Mild gastrointestinal symptoms are the most commonly noted side effect. These are safety findings, not efficacy findings.

Is there a best time to take fisetin?

No consensus exists on timing for fisetin. Fisetin has low water solubility and is better absorbed when taken with a meal containing fat. Some longevity researchers advocate for intermittent high-dose pulsed protocols (for example, taking fisetin on 2 consecutive days per month at higher doses) based on mouse study designs, but this approach has not been validated in human trials.

Does fisetin interact with NAD+ supplements?

No known negative interactions between fisetin and NAD+ precursors have been documented. They target different cellular mechanisms (senescent cell clearance vs. NAD+ pathway support), so they are often discussed as complementary approaches in longevity research. If combining both, standard supplement doses of each appear safe based on available data, though combined human trials have not been conducted.
