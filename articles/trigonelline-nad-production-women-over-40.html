What Is Trigonelline?

Trigonelline (scientific name: 1-methylnicotinic acid) is an alkaloid produced naturally in many plants as a derivative of nicotinic acid. You likely encounter it daily: it is one of the compounds in coffee that breaks down during roasting into other flavor-active molecules, and it is found in significant amounts in fenugreek seeds, a plant long used in traditional medicine.

For women over 40, the relevance of trigonelline comes from its potential role in NAD+ production. NAD+ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide) is the coenzyme that powers cellular energy, DNA repair, and sirtuin activity. As research by Gomes et al. in Cell (PMID 23140366) documented, NAD+ levels decline significantly with age, disrupting mitochondrial communication and accelerating cellular aging. Finding new ways to support NAD+ production is therefore an active and important area of longevity research.

Why NAD+ Decline Matters for Women Over 40

NAD+ does not decline at the same rate for everyone. Research on the enzyme CD38 by Camacho-Pereira et al. (PMID 27304503) showed that CD38 activity increases with age and actively degrades NAD+, creating a consumption problem on top of the production problem. For women, estrogen's role in NAD+ metabolism means that hormonal changes during perimenopause may accelerate NAD+ loss beyond what happens in men of the same age.

NAD+ also plays a role in regulating your body's internal clock. Research by Nakahata et al. published in Cell (PMID 19460348) demonstrated that circadian rhythm regulation depends on NAD+ availability, which helps explain why sleep disruption becomes more common as NAD+ levels decline. This circadian link is particularly relevant for women over 40 who notice sleep becoming lighter or less restorative.

These mechanisms set the context for understanding why any new potential NAD+ precursor, including trigonelline, deserves serious scientific attention, and why the bar for recommending it should be high.

What the Research Says About Trigonelline

The most significant finding driving interest in trigonelline came from research published in 2023 identifying trigonelline as a NAD+ precursor in animal models. The proposed mechanism runs through the Preiss-Handler pathway, where nicotinic acid and related compounds are converted stepwise into NAD+. Trigonelline, as a methylated form of nicotinic acid, appears to enter this pathway under certain conditions.

Research suggests that in animal models, trigonelline administration raised tissue NAD+ levels, including in muscle, which is significant because skeletal muscle NAD+ content is closely linked to metabolic health and energy production. According to Happy Aging's review of the emerging trigonelline literature through 2026, the preclinical data is genuinely promising and represents a plausible new avenue for NAD+ support.

However, the human evidence is limited. A small number of early human studies have looked at blood trigonelline levels in relation to health outcomes, and at least one study found that higher circulating trigonelline was associated with lower all-cause mortality in older adults. This is an association, not proof of a mechanism, and it does not tell us whether supplementing with trigonelline produces the same effect as having naturally higher levels.

The effective supplemental dose for raising NAD+ in humans has not been established. Coffee provides trigonelline, but most of it degrades during roasting. Fenugreek supplements contain higher amounts, but standardization is inconsistent across products.

What the Evidence Does Not Support

The evidence does not yet support using trigonelline as a primary or standalone NAD+ strategy for women over 40. There are no published large-scale human randomized controlled trials demonstrating that trigonelline supplementation raises blood or tissue NAD+ levels in women, improves energy, reduces biological aging markers, or provides any of the other outcomes women are typically seeking when they invest in a NAD+ protocol.

The animal-to-human translation gap is real and important here. Many compounds that raise NAD+ in mice do not show the same effects in humans at the doses and delivery forms available in supplements. Until human RCT data is available for trigonelline at defined doses, it remains a compound of scientific interest rather than a validated supplementation strategy.

The idea that drinking more coffee will meaningfully raise your NAD+ levels is also not supported by the current evidence. The amount of trigonelline that survives roasting and is bioavailable from a typical cup of coffee is likely well below what researchers used in preclinical experiments.

Happy Aging's position: trigonelline is an exciting emerging compound, but the evidence is too preliminary to rely on it as a primary NAD+ strategy. Women over 40 who want validated support for NAD+ levels are better served by established precursors like NMNH or NR combined with ingredients that address the consumption side of the NAD+ equation, such as CD38 inhibitors and sirtuin activators.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does drinking coffee raise NAD+ levels because of trigonelline?

This is unlikely in any meaningful way. Most trigonelline in coffee degrades during roasting. The amount that reaches your bloodstream from a typical cup is probably far below what was used in preclinical experiments showing NAD+ effects. Coffee has many other benefits, but counting it as a NAD+ supplement is not supported by current evidence.

Should I try a fenugreek supplement for trigonelline?

Fenugreek contains trigonelline in higher amounts than roasted coffee, but standardization of trigonelline content in fenugreek supplements is inconsistent, and the effective dose for NAD+ support in humans has not been established. Fenugreek may support blood sugar balance and has a reasonable safety record for that use, but as a NAD+ strategy, it is speculative at this stage.

How does trigonelline compare to NMN for women over 40?

NMN has the advantage of published human RCT data specifically in women (PMID 34836809), while trigonelline is still in the preclinical and early observational phase. For women over 40 making purchasing decisions today, NMN and NR have a substantially stronger evidence base.

Is trigonelline safe?

Trigonelline from dietary sources like coffee and fenugreek has a long history of safe consumption. Supplemental trigonelline at higher doses has not been extensively studied for safety in humans. Research suggests it is well tolerated at the doses found in food, but supplemental dosing safety data in women is not yet available.

Will trigonelline supplements be available in the future?

Research suggests that as the preclinical findings are confirmed in human trials, trigonelline may become a common addition to longevity supplement formulas. Several research groups are currently investigating effective doses and delivery forms. This is a space worth watching through 2026 and beyond.
