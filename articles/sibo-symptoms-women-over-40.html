What to Know

  • SIBO (small intestinal bacterial overgrowth) occurs when bacteria that normally live in the colon migrate into and colonize the small intestine, where they ferment food before it can be properly absorbed.
  • Women over 40 are at higher risk because gastric acid production declines with age and because estrogen decline slows gut motility, allowing bacteria to travel upward from the colon.
  • SIBO bloating tends to occur within 90 minutes of eating (faster than typical food intolerance), is located in the upper abdomen, and is often accompanied by belching rather than lower gas.
  • SIBO requires a clinical diagnosis (breath test) and is treated medically, typically with targeted antibiotics or herbal antimicrobials. Self-treating with standard probiotics can worsen symptoms in some cases.
  • After SIBO treatment, gut microbiome support is genuinely useful for preventing recurrence by rebuilding a healthy microbial environment.
  • Many women with SIBO have been told they have IBS for years. The distinction matters because the treatments are completely different.

You eat a normal-sized meal and within an hour your abdomen is distended and uncomfortable. The bloating sits high, under your ribs. You may notice belching, nausea, or a "full" feeling that seems disproportionate to what you ate. These are not typical post-meal bloating patterns. They are the signature of a different problem, one that is frequently missed in women over 40: small intestinal bacterial overgrowth, commonly called SIBO. Understanding whether your bloating might be SIBO is genuinely important, because the approach to treating it is completely different from treating ordinary digestive discomfort.

What Is SIBO?

The small intestine is where most nutrient absorption happens. Under normal conditions, it contains relatively few bacteria: far fewer than the colon, which hosts the dense microbial population most people think of as the "gut microbiome." The small intestine stays relatively sterile because of several protective mechanisms: stomach acid kills most ingested bacteria, bile salts are antibacterial, the ileocecal valve physically separates the small intestine from the bacteria-rich colon, and the migrating motor complex (MMC) sweeps bacteria downward between meals.

SIBO occurs when these protective mechanisms break down and colonic bacteria (or in some cases, archaea) colonize the small intestine in abnormally high numbers. Once there, these bacteria begin fermenting food before it can be absorbed, producing hydrogen gas, methane gas, or both. The result is rapid, severe bloating that begins before food even reaches the colon.

SIBO is not a single disease but rather a pattern of bacterial overgrowth that can vary by which organisms are dominant. Hydrogen-dominant SIBO tends to cause diarrhea-predominant symptoms. Methane-dominant SIBO (technically called intestinal methanogen overgrowth or IMO) tends to cause constipation-predominant symptoms. Hydrogen sulfide SIBO is less well understood but associated with a "rotten egg" gas quality and loose stools.

Why It Matters for Women Over 40

SIBO is significantly more common in older women than the mainstream conversation about gut health acknowledges. Several age-related changes converge to increase risk.

Gastric acid production (hydrochloric acid) declines with age, a condition called hypochlorhydria. Adequate stomach acid is one of the primary defenses against bacterial migration into the small intestine. Research has found that hypochlorhydria is associated with a substantially increased risk of SIBO (PMID: 21396499). Women who take proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) like omeprazole for acid reflux are at even higher risk, as PPIs further suppress acid production.

Gut motility slows with age and is further affected by declining estrogen in perimenopause. The migrating motor complex (the "housekeeping wave" that sweeps the small intestine between meals) requires adequate motility to function. When motility slows, bacteria have more time to accumulate in the small intestine rather than being swept toward the colon. Research has linked hypothyroidism (which becomes more common in women over 40) with impaired MMC activity and increased SIBO risk (PMID: 22735028).

Ileocecal valve dysfunction is another contributing factor that becomes more relevant with age. The ileocecal valve separates the small intestine from the colon. When it becomes incompetent (often related to abdominal adhesions from prior surgeries or inflammatory processes), colonic bacteria can more easily migrate upward.

According to Happy Aging's review of gastrointestinal research in midlife women, the most important reason SIBO is underrecognized in this population is that it mimics IBS so closely. Women with SIBO are often managed for IBS for years with fiber, low-FODMAP diets, and standard probiotics, none of which address the underlying bacterial overgrowth. In fact, high-fiber diets can worsen SIBO symptoms by feeding the misplaced bacteria.

What the Research Says

Research using lactulose and glucose breath testing (the standard diagnostic approach for SIBO) has found that SIBO is common in people with diagnosed IBS: a systematic review found a SIBO prevalence of approximately 54% in IBS patients versus 32% in controls (PMID: 10499488). More recent meta-analyses using stricter diagnostic criteria still find substantially elevated SIBO prevalence in functional GI disorders.

The clinical relevance is significant: a subset of people diagnosed with IBS actually have SIBO as a primary driver, and treating the SIBO (rather than managing symptoms with dietary restriction) can produce dramatic and lasting improvement.

A 2000 study published in the American Journal of Gastroenterology by Pimentel et al. found that treating SIBO with rifaximin (a non-absorbed antibiotic) produced normalization of breath tests in 48% of IBS patients and elimination of IBS symptoms in 75% of those who normalized (PMID: 11050066). Subsequent research has refined and validated this relationship.

What the Evidence Doesn't Support

SIBO has attracted a significant wellness-industry response that sometimes outpaces the science. It is important to be honest about what the evidence does and does not show.

Self-diagnosing SIBO based on symptoms alone is not reliable. The symptom overlap with IBS, food intolerances, gastroparesis, and other functional GI disorders is too high. Several online "SIBO symptom quizzes" circulate and give high-confidence assessments based on nothing more than self-reported symptoms. These should not be used as diagnostic tools. The only validated diagnostic approach is a breath test administered under proper clinical protocols (lactulose or glucose substrate, with appropriate preparation including dietary restrictions for 24 to 48 hours before testing).

Standard probiotics during active SIBO treatment may worsen symptoms in some patients. This is counterintuitive because probiotics are generally beneficial for gut health. However, if bacterial overgrowth is occurring in the small intestine, adding more live bacteria via a probiotic can temporarily increase the fermentation burden in the small intestine. Several clinicians and researchers recommend avoiding standard multi-strain probiotics during the active antimicrobial treatment phase of SIBO, though Saccharomyces boulardii (a yeast) appears to be better tolerated during this period.

The low-FODMAP diet is commonly recommended for SIBO, but it is more accurately a symptom management tool than a SIBO treatment. It reduces fermentable substrates that feed the bacteria, which can reduce symptoms, but it does not eliminate the underlying overgrowth. After months on a strict low-FODMAP diet, the microbiome can become further depleted of beneficial bacteria. It should be used as a bridge, not a long-term solution.

Herbal antimicrobial protocols (oregano oil, berberine, allicin, neem) are increasingly popular as alternatives to antibiotic treatment and do have some supporting research. A 2014 study in Global Advances in Health and Medicine found comparable outcomes between herbal antimicrobial protocols and rifaximin in breath test normalization (PMID: 25317262). However, these protocols require clinical guidance regarding doses, duration, and combination. Self-prescribing high-dose antimicrobial herbs without testing or clinical oversight is not recommended.

What to Do If You Suspect SIBO

If the SIBO pattern described above resonates with your experience, the appropriate next step is to see a gastroenterologist or a physician with specific experience in functional GI disorders and request a lactulose or glucose breath test. Make sure to ask for proper preparation instructions: inaccurate test results are common when patients eat the wrong foods in the 24 to 48 hours before testing.

Once tested, treatment decisions should be made with your physician. Standard first-line treatment is rifaximin (for hydrogen-dominant SIBO) with or without neomycin (for methane-dominant SIBO). Herbal antimicrobial protocols are a reasonable alternative for patients who prefer to avoid antibiotics, but should still be guided by a practitioner who understands SIBO management.

After completing antimicrobial treatment, rebuilding the gut microbiome is genuinely important for preventing recurrence. A healthy, diverse colonic microbiome helps maintain the microbial competition that prevents bacterial migration back into the small intestine. This is where targeted gut support supplements play a real and evidence-backed role, not as SIBO treatment, but as SIBO post-treatment maintenance.

Who This Is NOT For

This article is educational and is not a substitute for medical evaluation. If you have severe abdominal pain, blood in your stool, unexplained weight loss, fever, or vomiting, seek medical attention immediately. These are not SIBO symptoms and require urgent evaluation to rule out serious conditions.

If you have a history of bowel surgery, Crohn's disease, or other structural GI conditions, your risk factors for SIBO are elevated and you should discuss this specifically with a gastroenterologist rather than relying on general guidance.

If you are currently taking proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) for acid reflux and also have bloating symptoms, it is worth discussing SIBO testing with your doctor, as PPI use is a documented risk factor for small intestinal bacterial overgrowth and the two conditions frequently co-occur.

Frequently Asked Questions

How common is SIBO in women over 40?

Precise prevalence data in this specific demographic is limited, but SIBO is estimated to affect 6 to 15 percent of healthy individuals and a much higher proportion of people with GI symptoms. Given the age-related risk factors (reduced gastric acid, slowed motility, hormonal changes) that converge in women in their 40s and 50s, the prevalence in this group is likely at the higher end of the range. Research suggests that the prevalence in women with IBS symptoms specifically may be as high as 54 percent (PMID: 10499488).

Is a low-FODMAP diet helpful for SIBO?

A low-FODMAP diet can provide symptomatic relief during active SIBO by reducing the fermentable substrates available to overgrown bacteria. However, it does not treat the underlying overgrowth and, if followed long-term, can reduce overall microbial diversity. It is best used as a temporary symptom management tool while pursuing diagnosis and treatment, not as a standalone intervention.

Can SIBO go away on its own?

Rarely, mild SIBO may resolve if the underlying contributing factor (such as a temporary motility disruption from a GI illness) is corrected. However, chronic SIBO in the context of age-related gastric acid decline or structural abnormalities does not typically resolve without targeted treatment. Waiting and hoping carries the risk of ongoing nutrient malabsorption (particularly of fat-soluble vitamins B12 and iron) and worsening dysbiosis.

What does a SIBO breath test involve?

The standard SIBO breath test requires dietary preparation (typically a low-fermentation diet for 24 to 48 hours before the test and a 12-hour fast immediately before). You drink a measured amount of lactulose or glucose solution, and breath samples are collected over 2 to 3 hours and measured for hydrogen and methane gas output. Elevated gas levels at specific time points indicate bacterial fermentation in the small intestine. The test is non-invasive and can be done at many gastroenterology offices or via home test kits with clinical oversight.

Should I take probiotics before or after my SIBO breath test?

Stop probiotic supplements at least 2 to 4 weeks before a SIBO breath test. Probiotics can temporarily alter the bacterial population in the small intestine and may affect test results. Discuss the specific preparation protocol with your ordering physician.
