Word-finding problems during perimenopause are a real neurological symptom driven by estrogen's decline and its effect on the brain chemical acetylcholine, not a sign that something is seriously wrong with your memory. As of 2026, researchers understand this connection well enough to offer practical guidance on what can help.

What to Know at a Glance

  • The "tip-of-the-tongue" phenomenon spikes in perimenopause due to estrogen's role in acetylcholine production.
  • This is different from early dementia. Perimenopause-related word-finding problems are typically temporary and situation-specific.
  • Choline-rich foods and supplements, lion's mane mushroom, and bacopa may support verbal fluency and memory recall.
  • Sleep, stress reduction, and blood sugar stability are as important as any supplement.
  • Persistent or worsening memory problems should always be evaluated by a doctor.

What Is the Tip-of-the-Tongue Phenomenon?

The tip-of-the-tongue phenomenon is the experience of knowing a word or name exists but being temporarily unable to retrieve it from memory. You know you know it. It is right there. And then it slips away. This is a normal feature of how verbal memory works, but it becomes noticeably more frequent during perimenopause.

Why Estrogen Affects Word Recall

Estrogen is not just a reproductive hormone. It acts throughout the brain, and one of its key functions is supporting acetylcholine production and receptor sensitivity. Acetylcholine is your primary neurotransmitter for learning, attention, and verbal memory.

When estrogen levels fluctuate or decline during perimenopause, acetylcholine signaling becomes less consistent. The hippocampus (your brain's memory-filing center) and the prefrontal cortex (which handles word retrieval and executive function) both have dense estrogen receptors, so both are sensitive to these changes.

Research suggests that women in perimenopause show changes in verbal memory and processing speed that largely stabilize after menopause, once estrogen settles at a lower but consistent level. This is reassuring: for most women, this is a transitional phase, not a permanent decline.

According to Happy Aging's review of cognitive health patterns in women over 40, word-finding difficulty is consistently the number-one cognitive complaint reported during the perimenopause transition, ahead of focus problems and forgetfulness.

What Choline Has to Do With It

Choline is the precursor molecule your body uses to make acetylcholine. Without enough choline, your brain cannot produce adequate acetylcholine, and verbal memory and attention suffer.

Most women do not get enough choline from food. The recommended daily intake is 425 mg for adult women, but surveys suggest average intake falls well below that. Eggs are the richest dietary source (one whole egg provides about 147 mg of choline). Liver, salmon, and some legumes also contribute.

Alpha-GPC (glycerophosphocholine) is a form of choline that crosses the blood-brain barrier efficiently. It is one of the most studied choline supplements for cognitive function. Research suggests it may support memory and verbal learning in adults.

Citicoline (CDP-choline) is another well-absorbed form that provides both choline and cytidine (which converts to uridine, a building block for brain cell membranes). Some studies suggest it may support attention and verbal memory in adults with mild cognitive symptoms.

Lion's Mane: Nerve Growth Factor Support

Lion's mane mushroom (Hericium erinaceus) contains compounds called hericenones and erinacines that research suggests can stimulate nerve growth factor (NGF) production in the brain. NGF supports the survival and function of neurons, including cholinergic neurons (the ones that use acetylcholine).

A randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial published in Phytotherapy Research found that lion's mane supplementation improved cognitive function scores in adults with mild cognitive impairment compared to placebo (PMID: 18844328). More studies in perimenopausal women specifically are needed, but the mechanism is relevant.

Bacopa Monnieri for Verbal Memory

Bacopa is an Ayurvedic herb with several randomized controlled trials supporting its use for verbal learning and memory acquisition speed. Research suggests bacopa works partly by supporting serotonin and acetylcholine systems and reducing oxidative stress in brain tissue.

The typical studied dose is 300 to 450 mg of a standardized extract (45% bacosides). Bacopa takes eight to twelve weeks to show its full effect, which is longer than most people expect.

Lifestyle Factors That Matter as Much as Supplements

Sleep deprivation is the most reliable way to worsen word-finding. The brain consolidates verbal memory during deep sleep. When sleep is cut short, interrupted by night sweats, or fragmented by anxiety, verbal recall suffers the next day. If hot flashes are disrupting your sleep, addressing them is a cognitive intervention, not just a comfort measure.

Blood sugar instability also directly affects word retrieval. When glucose dips in the afternoon, mental fog and word-finding gaps often worsen. Eating protein and fiber at each meal stabilizes glucose and supports more consistent cognitive performance throughout the day.

Chronic stress raises cortisol, which suppresses hippocampal function. Even brief daily stress-reduction practices, like five minutes of deep breathing or a short walk, have been shown in research to support working memory and verbal fluency.

A Practical Protocol for Word-Finding Support

  1. Increase dietary choline: aim for at least two whole eggs per day, or add 300 to 600 mg of alpha-GPC.
  2. Add lion's mane mushroom extract (500 to 1000 mg/day) for nerve growth factor support.
  3. Consider bacopa monnieri (300 mg standardized extract) once daily, noting it takes 8 to 12 weeks for full effect.
  4. Prioritize sleep: if night sweats are the issue, address temperature regulation (cooling mattress pad, light bedding, magnesium before bed).
  5. Eat every 3 to 4 hours to keep blood sugar stable. Do not skip breakfast.
  6. Add a short daily movement session (even 20 minutes of walking). Physical activity has consistent evidence for supporting hippocampal health and verbal memory.
  7. Track your symptoms for 60 days. If word-finding problems are worsening rather than fluctuating, schedule a cognitive assessment with your doctor.

What the Evidence Doesn't Support

Brain training apps have not been shown to improve real-world verbal recall or transfer meaningfully to daily cognitive tasks. They improve at the specific task practiced, not general cognition.

High-dose B12 injections are sometimes marketed for brain fog. Unless you are actually B12 deficient (which your doctor can test for), supplementing will not improve word-finding.

There is no evidence that any single supplement eliminates perimenopause-related cognitive symptoms entirely. The goal is meaningful support during a transitional period, not complete symptom reversal.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is word-finding difficulty in perimenopause permanent?

For most women, no. Research suggests that verbal memory often stabilizes or improves after menopause, once estrogen settles at a consistent (though lower) level. The transition period is when fluctuations are most disruptive to brain chemistry.

At what age do word-finding problems typically start in perimenopause?

Most women notice the change in their mid-to-late 40s, but some women report subtle changes as early as their early 40s, especially during high-stress periods or after disrupted sleep. The timing varies widely based on when perimenopause begins.

Can hormone replacement therapy (HRT) help with word-finding?

Some research suggests that estrogen therapy, started close to menopause onset, may help preserve verbal memory and processing speed. This is an active area of research. Discuss with your doctor whether HRT is appropriate for your situation.

How long does it take for lion's mane to work?

Most studies have run for 8 to 16 weeks. Anecdotally, many people notice subtle improvements in mental clarity within four to six weeks, but the NGF-stimulating effects build over time. Consistency matters more than dose.

Should I be worried about early Alzheimer's if I have word-finding problems?

Word-finding problems during perimenopause are not, on their own, a sign of Alzheimer's. Alzheimer's typically involves progressive changes across multiple areas including orientation, recognition, and daily function, not just occasional word-finding gaps. If you are concerned, speak with your doctor for proper evaluation and peace of mind.
