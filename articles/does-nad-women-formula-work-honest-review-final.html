What the Science Says About NAD+ Supplementation for Women

The science behind NAD+ supplementation has matured significantly over the past decade. Several well-designed human trials have now confirmed what animal studies suggested, which is that NAD+ precursor supplementation can meaningfully raise cellular NAD+ levels in adults, including middle-aged and older women.



A landmark 2021 study published in Science by Yoshino and colleagues found that NMN supplementation improved muscle insulin sensitivity in postmenopausal prediabetic women, a particularly relevant finding for a demographic at elevated metabolic risk. This was one of the first high-quality human trials to demonstrate a clinically meaningful benefit of NMN specifically in women. The study used 250mg of NMN daily for 10 weeks.



Earlier research by Martens and colleagues published in Nature Communications in 2018 examined the closely related NAD+ precursor NR (nicotinamide riboside) in middle-aged and older adults, finding that it was safe, well-tolerated, and successfully elevated blood NAD+ levels in a dose-dependent manner. While NMN and NR are different molecules, both feed the same NAD+ biosynthesis pathway and evidence suggests comparable efficacy.



A 2018 review by Rajman, Chwalek, and Sinclair in Cell Metabolism comprehensively evaluated the therapeutic potential of NAD+-boosting molecules across multiple systems, concluding that the evidence base for NAD+ precursor supplementation in aging is strong and growing. The review highlighted energy metabolism, DNA repair, circadian rhythm regulation, and immune function as the key domains of benefit.



For women specifically, the NAD+ decline that begins around age 35 to 40 coincides with the hormonal transition of perimenopause. This timing is not coincidental. NAD+ plays a key role in the function of sirtuins, enzymes that regulate gene expression, stress responses, and estrogen metabolism. When NAD+ levels fall, sirtuin activity declines alongside them, contributing to hormonal dysregulation, increased inflammation, and reduced cellular resilience. Supporting NAD+ levels during this window appears to provide a meaningful cellular buffer against the effects of hormonal change.
