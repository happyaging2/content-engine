If you want to know what to eat to fight inflammation after 40, you are in the right place. The anti-inflammatory diet is not a fad or a restrictive protocol. It is a way of eating built around real food that research consistently links to lower levels of CRP, IL-6, TNF-alpha, and other markers of chronic systemic inflammation. For women navigating the hormonal transitions of midlife, food is one of the most accessible and powerful tools available for reducing the inflammatory burden that contributes to fatigue, joint pain, brain fog, and long-term disease risk.

What to Know

  • The most anti-inflammatory foods are omega-3 rich fish, polyphenol-rich berries and olive oil, cruciferous vegetables, and spices like turmeric and ginger.
  • Foods that drive inflammation include refined sugar, trans fats, processed meats, alcohol, and refined grains.
  • The anti-inflammatory plate model provides a simple framework for building every meal around foods that reduce, not amplify, inflammatory signaling.
  • Quercetin, found in onions, apples, and capers, is one of the most studied anti-inflammatory plant compounds and can be concentrated through supplementation.
  • A consistent 7-day eating pattern matters more than any single superfood.

Omega-3 Rich Fish: The Most Potent Anti-Inflammatory Food Category

Of all dietary changes that reduce systemic inflammation, increasing omega-3 fatty acid intake from fatty fish has the most robust and consistent evidence base. DHA (docosahexaenoic acid) and EPA (eicosapentaenoic acid) are the active forms of omega-3 that the body uses directly. They work by competing with arachidonic acid (the pro-inflammatory omega-6) in the synthesis of prostaglandins and leukotrienes, effectively shifting the body's inflammatory signaling balance toward resolution rather than perpetuation.

Clinical trials consistently show that increasing EPA and DHA intake reduces CRP, IL-6, and TNF-alpha. A study examining overweight women aged 40 to 70 years confirmed that omega-3 intake measurably shifted immune-related gene expression toward an anti-inflammatory profile (PMC4882690). The most DHA and EPA-rich foods are salmon, mackerel, sardines, herring, and anchovies. Canned sardines and wild salmon are practical, affordable ways to hit two to three servings of fatty fish per week.

For women who do not eat fish, algae-based omega-3 supplements provide DHA directly from its original marine source and bypass the inefficient ALA-to-DHA conversion pathway. This is the most evidence-backed plant-based alternative. Walnuts, flaxseeds, and chia seeds provide ALA omega-3, which is beneficial but does not substitute fully for preformed DHA and EPA from fatty fish or algae.

Polyphenol-Rich Berries and Olive Oil: Targeting NF-kB at the Source

Polyphenols are plant compounds that work partly by inhibiting NF-kB, the same master inflammatory transcription factor that curcumin targets. Berries are among the most polyphenol-dense foods available. Blueberries, blackberries, raspberries, and strawberries are all rich in anthocyanins, flavonoids shown in human trials to reduce oxidative stress and inflammatory markers. Their effects on brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) and cerebral blood flow are an added cognitive benefit.

Extra-virgin olive oil is the other standout anti-inflammatory food supported by the strongest dietary research. Its oleocanthal component inhibits COX-1 and COX-2 enzymes (the same targets as ibuprofen) at nutritional doses. Its high oleic acid content also reduces expression of pro-inflammatory genes in immune cells. The Mediterranean diet's consistent association with reduced inflammatory markers and chronic disease risk is substantially driven by high extra-virgin olive oil consumption. Using it as your primary cooking oil and salad dressing base is a practical first step.

Other high-polyphenol foods include dark chocolate (70 percent cacao and above), green and black tea, red wine in small amounts, pomegranate, red cabbage, and herbs such as oregano, rosemary, and thyme. Variety across this category maximizes the anti-inflammatory benefit since different polyphenol subtypes act through different pathways.

Cruciferous Vegetables and Sulforaphane: Activating the Body's Own Defense System

Cruciferous vegetables, including broccoli, kale, cauliflower, Brussels sprouts, arugula, and cabbage, contain sulforaphane, an isothiocyanate with a unique anti-inflammatory mechanism. Rather than directly blocking inflammatory enzymes, sulforaphane activates Nrf2, the cell's own master antioxidant and cytoprotective regulator. When Nrf2 is activated, it switches on a battery of protective genes that reduce oxidative stress and lower the inflammatory signaling environment.

Research published in PMC (Dinkova-Kostova and Abramov, 2015, PMC5725197) confirmed that sulforaphane activates KEAP1-Nrf2 target genes and reduces pro-inflammatory cytokines including IL-1 beta, IL-6, and inducible nitric oxide synthase in brain and immune cells. Additionally, a study (PMID: 18755157) showed sulforaphane suppressed LPS-induced inflammation in macrophages through the Nrf2-dependent pathway.

To maximize sulforaphane content, cruciferous vegetables are best eaten lightly cooked or raw. Overcooking destroys the myrosinase enzyme needed to activate sulforaphane from its precursor glucoraphanin. Chopping or chewing cruciferous vegetables and letting them rest for 40 minutes before cooking, or adding raw mustard seed, which contains myrosinase, to cooked broccoli, both significantly increase the sulforaphane yield.

Turmeric, Ginger, and Anti-Inflammatory Spices

Spices are not flavor accessories; they are some of the most concentrated sources of anti-inflammatory phytochemicals available. Turmeric contains curcumin, which as reviewed in detail elsewhere, inhibits NF-kB and reduces CRP, IL-6, and TNF-alpha in clinical trials. Culinary use of turmeric, while below therapeutic supplemental doses, is a meaningful daily contribution especially when consumed consistently and with black pepper (piperine enhances curcumin absorption).

Ginger contains gingerols and shogaols, compounds that inhibit COX-2 and 5-LOX enzymes, the same targets as many pharmaceutical anti-inflammatory drugs. Clinical trials show that ginger supplementation reduces CRP and other markers of inflammation, particularly in women with conditions like osteoarthritis and metabolic syndrome. Fresh ginger in cooking, ginger tea, and ginger added to smoothies are practical ways to include it daily.

Other anti-inflammatory spices worth building into your routine include cinnamon (which supports blood sugar regulation, reducing insulin-driven inflammation), rosemary (rich in rosmarinic acid, a potent antioxidant), and cloves (extremely high in eugenol, an anti-inflammatory polyphenol). Using a wide variety of herbs and spices is one of the easiest ways to dramatically increase your daily polyphenol intake without changing your eating patterns dramatically.

Foods That Drive Inflammation: What to Minimize

Knowing what to reduce is as important as knowing what to add. Refined sugar is the single most impactful dietary driver of inflammation for most Western women. Sugar spikes blood glucose, triggers insulin release, drives advanced glycation end-product (AGE) formation, and feeds pro-inflammatory gut bacteria at the expense of beneficial species. Liquid sugar, from sodas, fruit juices, sports drinks, and sweet coffees, is particularly problematic because it bypasses satiety signals and enters the bloodstream rapidly.

Trans fats, still present in some commercial baked goods, fried fast foods, and partially hydrogenated oils, are directly incorporated into cell membranes where they impair membrane function and amplify inflammatory signaling. Even small amounts have been linked to elevated CRP and increased cardiovascular inflammatory risk. Reading ingredient labels for "partially hydrogenated oil" is still necessary since they persist in some packaged products.

Processed meats (bacon, hot dogs, sausages, deli meats) are high in AGEs from their processing methods, saturated fats, and pro-inflammatory heme iron in forms that promote oxidative stress. Alcohol disrupts gut barrier integrity even at moderate consumption levels, directly contributing to LPS translocation and systemic inflammation. Refined grains, white bread, white rice, and commercial pastries, cause rapid blood glucose spikes and provide the substrate for AGE formation without the fiber and nutrients that buffer inflammation in whole grains.

The Anti-Inflammatory Plate Model

Rather than counting nutrients or tracking macros, the anti-inflammatory plate model gives a simple visual framework for building every meal. Half the plate is vegetables, with a strong emphasis on non-starchy options: dark leafy greens, cruciferous vegetables, colorful peppers, tomatoes, cucumber, zucchini, and beets. The richer and more varied the colors, the broader the polyphenol spectrum. One quarter of the plate is protein: prioritizing fatty fish, eggs, poultry, legumes, or small amounts of high-quality red meat. The remaining quarter is whole grains, legumes, or root vegetables like sweet potato.

Every meal includes a source of anti-inflammatory fat: olive oil on the salad, avocado alongside the protein, or fatty fish as the protein itself. Every meal includes some form of anti-inflammatory spice or herb. Berries, nuts, or dark chocolate serve as snacks or dessert. This framework does not require perfection. It provides a structure that, when followed most of the time, measurably reduces the inflammatory load carried by the body.

A 7-Day Anti-Inflammatory Eating Template

Day 1: Breakfast: Berry smoothie with spinach, flaxseed, and almond butter. Lunch: Wild salmon salad with arugula, avocado, cherry tomatoes, and olive oil dressing. Dinner: Baked mackerel with roasted broccoli and quinoa.

Day 2: Breakfast: Overnight oats with blueberries, walnuts, and cinnamon. Lunch: Lentil soup with kale, garlic, and turmeric. Dinner: Chicken thighs with roasted cauliflower and sweet potato.

Day 3: Breakfast: Two scrambled eggs with sauteed spinach and sardines on rye toast. Lunch: Large mixed greens salad with chickpeas, shredded beets, pumpkin seeds, and tahini dressing. Dinner: Shrimp stir-fry with bok choy, ginger, garlic, and brown rice.

Day 4: Breakfast: Greek yogurt with pomegranate seeds, mixed nuts, and a drizzle of honey. Lunch: Tuna salad on sourdough with sliced tomato and cucumber. Dinner: Lamb and vegetable stew with plenty of rosemary and garlic.

Day 5: Breakfast: Chia pudding with mango, coconut milk, and hemp seeds. Lunch: Roasted vegetable and white bean soup. Dinner: Baked salmon with asparagus and a side of sauteed kale with olive oil and lemon.

Day 6: Breakfast: Smoked salmon on sourdough with sliced avocado and capers. Lunch: Caprese salad with extra-virgin olive oil and fresh basil, alongside a cup of lentil soup. Dinner: Turkey meatballs with zucchini noodles and tomato sauce with oregano.

Day 7: Breakfast: Frittata with broccoli, mushrooms, and goat cheese. Lunch: Nioise-style salad with tuna, olives, green beans, and hard-boiled egg. Dinner: Miso-glazed cod with roasted Brussels sprouts and sesame seeds.

How Quercetin Rounds Out an Anti-Inflammatory Protocol

Quercetin is a flavonoid found naturally in onions (especially red onions), apples, capers, kale, grapes, and green tea. Research published in Nutrients (2016, PMC4808895) confirmed that quercetin inhibits NF-kB activation, suppresses production of pro-inflammatory cytokines including TNF-alpha and IL-6, and directly scavenges free radicals. Its anti-inflammatory mechanism is complementary to omega-3 fatty acids and curcumin, covering pathways that the others do not.

Dietary quercetin from whole foods is a meaningful contribution but is difficult to concentrate to therapeutic levels through food alone. Capers are the highest dietary source (around 230 mg per 100g), followed by red onions (around 20 mg per 100g). Quercetin supplementation allows consistent delivery of clinical amounts, typically 500 to 1000 mg per day, and research suggests it is well-absorbed and has a relatively long half-life of 11 to 28 hours, supporting consistent anti-inflammatory action.

For women over 40 who are already eating an anti-inflammatory whole food diet and want to further support their inflammatory regulation, quercetin as a supplement adds a distinct and well-researched anti-inflammatory pathway. It is particularly relevant for women with seasonal allergies (quercetin is a natural mast cell stabilizer), cardiovascular inflammatory risk factors, or those managing conditions with an NF-kB-driven inflammatory component.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take for an anti-inflammatory diet to work?

Some improvements in blood sugar and energy are noticeable within the first week, while measurable reductions in CRP and other inflammatory markers typically show up after 4 to 8 weeks of consistent anti-inflammatory eating.

What is the single most important food to add for inflammation after 40?

Fatty fish like salmon or sardines, eaten two to three times per week, provides DHA and EPA that directly reduce prostaglandin production and inflammatory cytokines, making it the most evidence-backed single addition for systemic inflammation reduction.

Is the Mediterranean diet anti-inflammatory?

Yes. The Mediterranean diet is one of the most rigorously studied dietary patterns for inflammation reduction, consistently associated in large cohort studies and trials with lower CRP, IL-6, and reduced risk of inflammatory chronic diseases. Its emphasis on olive oil, fish, vegetables, legumes, and herbs directly overlaps with the anti-inflammatory plate model.

Can quercetin from food provide the same benefit as supplements?

Foods rich in quercetin like red onions, capers, and apples are valuable daily sources, but reaching clinical dosages through food alone is difficult. Supplementation offers a practical way to consistently access the anti-inflammatory levels used in research trials.

Does dairy increase inflammation after 40?

For most people, dairy does not significantly increase inflammation and fermented dairy like yogurt and kefir may actually reduce it through probiotic effects. However, women with lactose intolerance or dairy sensitivity may experience individual inflammatory responses, and in those cases reducing or eliminating dairy is worth trialing.
