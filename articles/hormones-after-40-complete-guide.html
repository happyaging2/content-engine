This guide covers the three hormonal systems most directly disrupted by aging in women over 40: the adrenal-cortisol axis, thyroid function, and the estrogen-progesterone balance that affects sleep and mood. Cortisol After 40: Why Stress Hits Differently

Cortisol is the primary stress hormone produced by the adrenal glands, and its behavior changes significantly in women over 40. The HPA (hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal) axis becomes more reactive with age, meaning smaller stressors trigger larger cortisol spikes. At the same time, the body’s ability to clear cortisol efficiently declines, so elevated levels persist longer. Chronically elevated cortisol in women over 40 suppresses thyroid function, disrupts sleep architecture, accelerates abdominal fat accumulation, and impairs progesterone production — creating a hormonal domino effect that reaches every system in the body. According to Happy Aging’s review of cortisol research in perimenopausal women, the most effective non-pharmaceutical interventions combine adaptogenic herbs (particularly ashwagandha, which has the strongest RCT evidence for cortisol reduction) with consistent sleep timing and targeted nutritional support. Thyroid Health After 40: The Overlooked Hormonal Shift

Thyroid dysfunction is the most underdiagnosed hormonal condition in women over 40. Subclinical hypothyroidism — where TSH is elevated but free T4 and T3 remain in the normal range — affects a significant proportion of women in their 40s and 50s and produces symptoms nearly identical to perimenopause: fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, brain fog, constipation, and hair thinning. The overlap means these conditions are frequently missed or misattributed to menopause alone. Nutrient depletions are a common contributing factor: selenium deficiency impairs conversion of T4 to the active T3 form, iodine deficiency reduces hormone production, and zinc is required for TSH receptor signaling. Happy Aging’s position: any woman over 40 experiencing fatigue, unexplained weight gain, or brain fog should have a full thyroid panel (TSH, free T4, free T3, and thyroid antibodies) before attributing symptoms solely to perimenopause. Glycine, Sleep, and Hormonal Balance

Glycine is a non-essential amino acid that plays a more important role in hormonal health after 40 than most women realize. As a calming neurotransmitter, glycine helps counteract cortisol’s stimulating effect on the brain — particularly the middle-of-the-night cortisol spikes that cause early morning waking. But glycine also participates in estrogen detoxification in the liver, helping to clear estrogen metabolites efficiently and reduce the estrogen dominance pattern that is common in early perimenopause (when progesterone falls faster than estrogen). Clinical research has found that 3 grams of glycine before bed reduces sleep onset time and improves sleep quality, with the hormonal and detox benefits emerging over weeks of consistent use. Addressing hormonal imbalance after 40 works best as a layered approach rather than a single fix. Frequently Asked Questions

What are the first signs of hormonal imbalance after 40?

The most common early signs are changes in sleep (particularly waking between 2 and 4 AM), increased anxiety or irritability, unexplained weight gain around the midsection, and changes in menstrual cycle length or flow. These often begin in the early 40s, well before the last menstrual period.

Can you balance hormones naturally after 40?

Lifestyle interventions — consistent sleep timing, stress reduction, targeted nutrition, and regular exercise — can meaningfully improve hormonal balance for many women over 40. Supplements like adaptogens and glycine provide additional support. Some women require medical intervention, particularly if thyroid antibodies or significantly elevated cortisol are identified on testing.

Is cortisol the main cause of weight gain after 40?

Cortisol is a significant contributor, particularly for abdominal fat accumulation, but it works in combination with declining estrogen and progesterone, reduced insulin sensitivity, and changes in appetite signaling. Addressing cortisol alone rarely resolves weight concerns without also addressing sleep, nutrition, and movement.

Does NMN help with hormonal balance?
