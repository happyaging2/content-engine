What Is Nattokinase and What Does It Do for Heart Health After 40?

Nattokinase is an enzyme derived from natto, a traditional Japanese fermented soybean dish that has been consumed for centuries and is associated with Japan’s exceptionally low rates of cardiovascular disease. Discovered in 1987 by researcher Hiroyuki Sumi, nattokinase has since been studied for its ability to support healthy blood flow, reduce the risk of clot formation, and lower blood pressure, three mechanisms directly relevant to the cardiovascular changes women face after 40.

What to Know

  • Nattokinase is a serine protease enzyme that breaks down fibrin, a protein that forms blood clots
  • It has been studied in randomized controlled trials for reducing blood pressure and improving blood viscosity
  • Clinical trials show nattokinase may reduce systolic blood pressure by 5 to 8 mmHg and lower fibrinogen levels
  • Nattokinase is NOT appropriate for women taking blood thinners (warfarin, heparin) or before surgery due to anticoagulant effects
  • Standard supplemental doses range from 2,000 to 4,000 FU (fibrinolytic units) per day

What Is Nattokinase?

Nattokinase is a serine protease enzyme produced by the bacterium Bacillus subtilis natto during the fermentation of soybeans to make natto. It belongs to the subtilisin family of enzymes, which are proteolytic (protein-degrading) enzymes capable of breaking down fibrin, the protein that forms the scaffold of blood clots.

Fibrinolysis (the breakdown of fibrin) is one of the body’s natural mechanisms for preventing blood clots from persisting or growing after they form. The body produces its own fibrinolytic enzymes, primarily plasmin (activated from plasminogen by tissue plasminogen activator, tPA). Nattokinase enhances fibrinolysis through a complementary mechanism: it directly degrades fibrin, activates tPA, and may increase plasminogen activator activity. This makes it a multi-pronged support for healthy blood flow and clot prevention.

After 40, several factors increase cardiovascular risk in women: estrogen decline reduces the cardioprotective effects of estrogen on blood vessel function, vascular stiffness increases, and the coagulation-fibrinolysis balance shifts toward greater clotting tendency. Blood pressure also tends to rise post-menopause. Nattokinase addresses several of these changes through its fibrinolytic and blood pressure-lowering mechanisms.

What Does the Research Show for Blood Pressure?

The most clinically significant evidence for nattokinase in cardiovascular health comes from blood pressure trials. A randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial by Kim et al. (2008, PMID: 18971533) found that 2,000 FU of nattokinase daily for 8 weeks reduced systolic blood pressure by 5.55 mmHg and diastolic blood pressure by 2.84 mmHg in subjects with prehypertension and stage 1 hypertension. This represents a clinically meaningful reduction: each 5 mmHg reduction in systolic blood pressure corresponds to approximately a 10 to 15 percent reduction in stroke risk.

A larger clinical trial by Jensen et al. (2016, PMID: 27221594) confirmed these findings, showing significant reductions in systolic blood pressure at 12 weeks with nattokinase supplementation. The mechanism may involve nattokinase’s effect on bradykinin (a vasodilatory peptide) through its interaction with the kallikrein-kinin system, in addition to its direct fibrinolytic effects on blood vessel wall fibrin deposits.

Fibrin and Blood Viscosity: The Clot Prevention Angle

Elevated fibrinogen (the precursor to fibrin) is an independent cardiovascular risk factor. High fibrinogen levels make blood more viscous and increase the likelihood of clot formation. Fibrinogen levels naturally increase with age, and post-menopause the decline in estrogen (which has anti-thrombotic effects) compounds this risk.

A study by Suzuki et al. (2003, PMID: 12657494) found that nattokinase supplementation reduced fibrinogen and Factor VIII (another coagulation protein) in healthy adults, suggesting a direct role in improving the coagulation-fibrinolysis balance. A follow-up study found that nattokinase reduced fibrinogen levels by approximately 8 percent in a 26-week trial.

Importantly, nattokinase does not appear to prevent appropriate clot formation at recommended doses, making its risk profile distinct from pharmaceutical anticoagulants, which can produce dangerous bleeding at therapeutic doses. However, women with clotting disorders or who are taking anticoagulant medications should not add nattokinase without medical supervision due to its fibrinolytic effects.

The Atherosclerosis Angle: Beyond Blood Pressure

Emerging research suggests nattokinase may have effects on atherosclerotic plaques beyond blood pressure and coagulation. A 2017 Chinese randomized controlled trial by Ren et al. found that nattokinase supplementation over 26 weeks significantly reduced carotid intima-media thickness (a measure of atherosclerotic plaque burden) compared to placebo, and reduced LDL cholesterol and triglycerides (PMID: 27798533). Carotid IMT reduction is a clinically meaningful endpoint representing genuine reversal of plaque accumulation.

This potential anti-atherosclerotic effect would make nattokinase one of the few supplements with evidence for addressing not just cardiovascular risk factors but actual plaque dynamics, though the Ren et al. study needs replication in larger trials before firm conclusions can be drawn.

How Is Nattokinase Different from Eating Natto?

Natto is the traditional food source of nattokinase, and eating it provides the enzyme in its natural food matrix along with vitamin K2 (particularly MK-7, the most bioavailable form). Japan’s low cardiovascular disease rates have long been associated with natto consumption in epidemiological research. However, natto has a strong, fermented flavor and sticky texture that many Westerners find challenging to eat regularly.

Nattokinase supplements provide the enzyme in standardized doses (measured in FU, fibrinolytic units) without the strong taste or texture of natto, and without the very high vitamin K2 content of natto (which can interfere with warfarin therapy, unlike standard nattokinase supplements). The enzyme’s activity is measured in FU rather than milligrams, because it is the enzymatic activity, not the raw weight, that determines effectiveness. Standard research doses range from 2,000 to 4,000 FU daily.

Safety Considerations After 40

Nattokinase is well tolerated in the research literature at standard doses in healthy adults without blood-thinning medications. The most important safety consideration is its blood-thinning effect: women taking warfarin, heparin, clopidogrel, aspirin at therapeutic doses, or newer anticoagulants (rivaroxaban, apixaban) should not add nattokinase without medical supervision, as the combined anticoagulant effect can increase bleeding risk.

Nattokinase should be discontinued at least 2 weeks before any planned surgical procedure for the same reason. Women with known clotting disorders (factor V Leiden, protein C or S deficiency) should consult their physician before use. For healthy women without these considerations, nattokinase at 2,000 FU daily is generally well tolerated.

How Nattokinase Fits Into a Cardiovascular Health Strategy After 40

Nattokinase is most effective as part of a comprehensive cardiovascular support approach rather than as a standalone intervention. The lifestyle foundations for cardiovascular health after menopause include regular aerobic exercise (150 minutes weekly at moderate intensity), a diet reducing refined carbohydrates and emphasizing omega-3-rich fish, colorful vegetables, and olive oil, and stress management that addresses the cortisol-driven vascular stiffness that accelerates in perimenopause. Nattokinase adds fibrinolytic support and blood pressure lowering on top of these foundations, addressing mechanisms that diet and exercise alone do not specifically target.

Magnesium (supporting vasodilation and calcium channel function in vessel walls), omega-3 fatty acids (reducing platelet aggregation and triglycerides), and coenzyme Q10 (supporting mitochondrial energy production in cardiac muscle) are among the supplements with complementary cardiovascular mechanisms that pair well with nattokinase in a coherent strategy for women managing post-menopausal cardiovascular risk. None of these replace medical management of significant hypertension or established cardiovascular disease, but together they address the multiple pathways through which the cardiovascular risk profile shifts after 40.

Women should expect nattokinase’s blood pressure effects to emerge gradually over 6 to 8 weeks of consistent daily use, mirroring the timeline seen in the clinical trials. Measuring blood pressure consistently at the same time of day before and 8 weeks after starting nattokinase provides the clearest picture of individual response. Some women will see larger reductions than others, particularly those starting with higher systolic readings or greater fibrinogen levels, which appear to be associated with stronger fibrinolytic responses to the enzyme.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can nattokinase replace blood pressure medication?

No. Nattokinase has modest blood pressure lowering effects (5 to 8 mmHg systolic in clinical trials) that are meaningful but not sufficient to replace prescription antihypertensives in women with significant hypertension (blood pressure above 140/90 consistently). It may be a useful complement to lifestyle modifications (diet, exercise, salt reduction) for women with prehypertension or mild high blood pressure, and should be discussed with a physician before use alongside medications.

Is nattokinase safe to take every day?

For healthy women without clotting disorders or blood-thinning medications, daily nattokinase at 2,000 FU is generally well tolerated. Clinical trials have run for up to 26 weeks without significant adverse effects in healthy adults. Long-term daily use beyond 26 weeks has not been as thoroughly studied, though the enzyme is derived from a traditional food with centuries of human consumption without documented harm at food-based doses.

When should I take nattokinase for best results?

Most clinical research administers nattokinase in the morning or at bedtime, as fibrinolytic activity may benefit from spacing away from large meals (though this is not definitively established). Some practitioners suggest taking it between meals to maximize absorption of the enzyme before food proteins in the gut compete for digestion. Consistency of daily use matters more than precise timing within the day.

Does nattokinase raise vitamin K levels?

No. Nattokinase supplements do not contain meaningful amounts of vitamin K2, unlike whole natto which is one of the richest dietary sources of vitamin K2 (MK-7). Women taking warfarin (which requires careful vitamin K monitoring) can generally take nattokinase supplements safely from a vitamin K perspective, but should still consult their physician about the anticoagulant effects of nattokinase itself.

What is the difference between nattokinase and serrapeptase?

Both are proteolytic enzymes with fibrinolytic effects, but they come from different sources and have different substrate specificities. Nattokinase is derived from Bacillus subtilis natto fermentation and has the most robust clinical evidence for blood pressure and fibrinolysis. Serrapeptase comes from a silkworm gut bacterium (Serratia marcescens) and has been studied primarily for anti-inflammatory and mucus-clearing effects. Some cardiovascular support formulas combine both, though nattokinase has the stronger specifically cardiovascular clinical evidence base.
