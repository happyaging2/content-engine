What Is Quercetin?

Quercetin is a flavonoid, a type of polyphenol found naturally in many plants. It belongs to the flavonol subclass and is one of the most abundant flavonoids in the human diet, found in onions (especially red onions), apples, capers, kale, broccoli, and berries.

In supplement form, quercetin is concentrated to doses far higher than what food provides, allowing for more consistent and measurable anti-inflammatory effects. It acts through several mechanisms including inhibition of pro-inflammatory enzymes (COX-2 and lipoxygenase), reduction of inflammatory cytokine production (including TNF-alpha and IL-6), and antioxidant activity through free radical neutralization.

What Is Inflammaging and Why It Matters After 40

Inflammaging is a term coined by researchers to describe the chronic, low-grade, systemic inflammation that increases with biological aging. It is not the acute inflammation you feel when you cut your finger or get a cold. It is a background level of immune activation that persists over years and decades.

This persistent inflammation is driven by multiple factors that converge around midlife: accumulation of senescent cells (cells that have stopped dividing but continue to secrete inflammatory signals), declining estrogen (which had anti-inflammatory effects), increasing adipose tissue (which produces inflammatory cytokines), and reduced antioxidant capacity.

According to Happy Aging’s review of the inflammaging research, women over 40 are at a particular inflection point where multiple pro-inflammatory factors converge simultaneously due to hormonal shifts, making targeted anti-inflammatory support more impactful in this age window than at any earlier stage of life.

Elevated inflammatory markers are associated with fatigue, joint stiffness, cognitive fog, skin changes, and increased susceptibility to illness. They are not symptoms to simply accept as “part of aging.” They are addressable biological signals.

How Quercetin Works as an Anti-Inflammatory and Senolytic

Quercetin works through several overlapping pathways:

Anti-inflammatory enzyme inhibition: Quercetin inhibits COX-2 and 5-LOX, the same enzymes targeted by common anti-inflammatory drugs. Unlike drugs, it does this at doses achievable through supplementation without the GI risks associated with NSAIDs.

Cytokine reduction: Research shows quercetin reduces production of key inflammatory signaling molecules including TNF-alpha, IL-1beta, and IL-6. These are the same cytokines elevated in chronic inflammatory conditions and measured in inflammaging research.

Senolytic activity: Perhaps most exciting for the longevity field, quercetin (especially when combined with dasatinib in research settings, or with fisetin in natural supplement contexts) shows evidence of senolytic activity, meaning it may help clear senescent cells. Senescent cells accumulate with age and are a major driver of inflammaging. Natural senolytic approaches are one of the most actively researched areas in longevity science.

Antioxidant activity: Quercetin directly scavenges reactive oxygen species (ROS) and also activates the Nrf2 pathway, which upregulates the body’s own antioxidant enzyme systems including superoxide dismutase and catalase.

What the Research Shows

For inflammation markers: multiple trials show quercetin supplementation reduces circulating levels of CRP (C-reactive protein), IL-6, and TNF-alpha, particularly in individuals with elevated baseline inflammatory markers. A meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials found that quercetin significantly reduced CRP levels, a primary marker of systemic inflammation.

For immune function: research suggests quercetin has antiviral properties, inhibiting the replication of several respiratory viruses in laboratory studies. Observational data suggests populations with higher flavonoid intake have lower rates of upper respiratory infections.

For exercise recovery: several trials have looked at quercetin for reducing delayed-onset muscle soreness and inflammation after exercise, with generally positive findings particularly in older adults.

For senolytic effects: the strongest senolytic evidence uses quercetin in combination with the prescription drug dasatinib, which is not a supplement protocol. Research on natural quercetin alone as a senolytic is promising but less established than the drug combination. This is an area of active investigation as of 2026.

How to Take Quercetin Effectively

Absorption is the biggest challenge with quercetin. Standard quercetin has low bioavailability and most of it passes through without being absorbed. Several strategies improve this:

Take with fat: quercetin is lipophilic, meaning it dissolves in fat. Taking it with a fat-containing meal (eggs, avocado, olive oil) can improve absorption compared to taking it on an empty stomach.

Choose phytosome or bromelain-enhanced forms: quercetin phytosome has the best documented bioavailability of any oral quercetin form. Quercetin with bromelain is a cost-effective middle ground.

Pair with vitamin C: some research suggests vitamin C (ascorbic acid) may help maintain quercetin’s antioxidant activity in circulation and support its recycling.

A Quercetin Protocol for Women Over 40

  1. Choose your form: quercetin phytosome (500mg/day) for maximum bioavailability, or quercetin with bromelain (500 to 1000mg/day) as a well-researched alternative.
  2. Take with your largest meal of the day (ideally one containing fat).
  3. Pair with 250 to 500mg of vitamin C in the same meal to support antioxidant recycling.
  4. Take consistently for at least 4 to 6 weeks before assessing results. Anti-inflammatory effects are cumulative, not immediate.
  5. Track relevant symptoms: joint comfort, energy levels, post-exercise recovery time, frequency of minor illness.
  6. If using for senolytic support, research protocols in human studies use cyclical dosing (a few days on, then a break). Discuss with your provider before using a senolytic dosing approach.
  7. Combine with dietary quercetin sources (red onions, capers, apples) for additional baseline flavonoid intake.

What the Evidence Doesn’t Support

The evidence does not support using quercetin as a standalone senolytic therapy without medical supervision. The senolytic research is exciting but most of the human evidence uses quercetin combined with a prescription drug (dasatinib) in specific clinical conditions, not standard supplement doses. Using quercetin for senolytic purposes is an emerging and unproven application in healthy adults without medical guidance.

The evidence also does not support quercetin as a treatment for diagnosed autoimmune conditions, chronic inflammatory diseases, or cancer. It is a wellness support supplement with anti-inflammatory properties, not a medical treatment.

Standard quercetin powder at low doses is not well-supported for meaningful anti-inflammatory outcomes. If you are taking a 250mg standard quercetin capsule without a bioavailability enhancer, the evidence suggests you are getting very little systemic benefit. Dose and form both matter significantly.

The evidence does not support using quercetin in place of prescribed anti-inflammatory medications. If you are managing a condition with NSAIDs or other anti-inflammatory drugs, speak with your healthcare provider before adding quercetin or attempting to reduce your medication based on supplement use.

FAQ

Is quercetin safe to take every day?

Research suggests daily quercetin at doses up to 1000mg is well-tolerated in healthy adults. Some people report mild headaches or digestive discomfort at higher doses. If you experience any side effects, reduce the dose or switch forms.

Can quercetin interact with medications?

Yes. Quercetin inhibits certain cytochrome P450 enzymes involved in drug metabolism and may interact with antibiotics, blood thinners, and thyroid medications. Always discuss with your provider if you are taking prescription medications before adding quercetin.

How long does quercetin take to work for inflammation?

For measurable changes in inflammatory markers, studies typically see effects at the 4 to 8 week mark with consistent daily use. Joint comfort and energy improvements are often noticed before blood markers change.

Can I get enough quercetin from food alone?

Diet provides roughly 5 to 40mg of quercetin per day for most people, far below the 500 to 1000mg used in anti-inflammatory research. Supplements are necessary to reach therapeutic levels.

Is quercetin beneficial for perimenopause symptoms?

Research is limited but suggests quercetin’s anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties may support some perimenopause-related concerns including energy and immune resilience. It is not a hormonal intervention and should not be expected to directly reduce hot flashes or night sweats.
