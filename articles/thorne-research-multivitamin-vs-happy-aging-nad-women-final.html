Why It Matters for Women Over 40

Women over 40 face two distinct but related health challenges. The first is nutritional: the demands of perimenopause and menopause, combined with the reality that most women do not eat a perfectly varied, nutrient-dense diet every day, mean that micronutrient gaps are common and consequential. Gaps in folate, B12, vitamin D, magnesium, and iron affect energy, mood, cognition, and cardiovascular health. A quality multivitamin addresses this.

The second challenge is cellular aging: the biological processes that make cells less functional, tissues less resilient, and organs less efficient over time. These are not primarily nutritional deficiencies. They are failures of cellular maintenance systems: mitochondria that produce less energy and more oxidative waste, accumulating senescent cells that trigger chronic inflammation, declining NAD+ levels that impair hundreds of enzymatic processes including DNA repair, and dysregulation of the sirtuins (proteins that coordinate aging responses). A multivitamin does not address these mechanisms.

This is why framing the choice as Thorne versus the NAD Women's Formula is somewhat artificial: they are designed for different jobs. The question is which job is more urgent for your situation.

For a woman in her early 40s who has never taken a multivitamin and has a generally good diet: starting with a quality multivitamin like Thorne is entirely reasonable. Filling nutritional gaps should come before adding longevity-targeted compounds.

For a woman in her mid-to-late 40s or early 50s who is already eating well and noticing the classic signs of cellular aging (declining energy, longer recovery from exercise, brain fog, skin changes, sleep disruption), the NAD Women's Formula addresses mechanisms that no multivitamin can reach.

What the Research Says

The evidence supporting Thorne's formulation choices is strong at the ingredient level. Methylfolate supplementation is clearly superior to folic acid for women with MTHFR variants, and prevalence of MTHFR polymorphisms in the population is high enough that this matters for a significant percentage of users. Methylcobalamin for B12 is better retained in brain tissue than cyanocobalamin. Highly bioavailable mineral chelates (like bisglycinate forms of magnesium, zinc, and iron) are demonstrably better absorbed than oxide or sulfate forms. These are not marketing claims; they are basic nutritional biochemistry with solid research support.

The evidence base for NAD+ precursors (NMN and NR) is growing. Human trials have demonstrated that oral NMN and NR supplementation measurably increases blood NAD+ levels. What is more contested is which downstream effects are clinically meaningful in healthy adults. Animal studies (particularly in rodents) show striking improvements in muscle function, metabolism, cognitive aging, and longevity with NAD+ restoration. Human trials are showing improvements in muscle strength, endurance, and some metabolic markers, particularly in older adults. The field is moving quickly and the evidence is more mature than critics acknowledge, even if it falls short of the dramatic claims sometimes made in the longevity media space.

Resveratrol and quercetin have well-characterized sirtuin-activating properties in laboratory and animal studies. Human clinical evidence for quercetin is stronger, with evidence for anti-inflammatory effects and quercetin's role as a senolytic compound (helping clear senescent cells). CoQ10 has strong evidence for mitochondrial support and cardiovascular benefits, particularly relevant for women over 40.

Liposomal delivery, which the NAD Women's Formula uses for key ingredients, is supported by pharmacokinetic research showing meaningfully higher bioavailability for compounds that otherwise have poor intestinal absorption, including certain fat-soluble antioxidants and phospholipid-bound actives.

What the Evidence Doesn't Support

No supplement can reverse biological aging or guarantee longevity. Claims that any formula "reverses" aging are not supported by current evidence and violate FDA structure-function claim requirements. The NAD Women's Formula supports healthy cellular function; it does not undo decades of cellular wear.

The case for Thorne as the best multivitamin on the market is strong, but it should be noted that several competitors, including Pure Encapsulations, MegaFood, and Garden of Life, also produce high-quality, well-formulated multivitamins. Thorne is excellent but not uniquely irreplaceable. The principles that make Thorne good (methylated B vitamins, bioavailable minerals, third-party testing) are the principles to look for in any multivitamin.

The "12 hallmarks of aging" framework is a genuine scientific framework developed by leading geroscience researchers. However, the connection between a supplement ingredient and addressing a specific hallmark is often more theoretical than proven in humans. The hallmarks are well-characterized in laboratory models; the ability of specific supplements to measurably influence those hallmarks in living humans, at supplement doses, is still an active area of research.

Who This Is NOT For

Women who have not established basic dietary habits, regular exercise, and adequate sleep should prioritize those fundamentals before investing in a premium longevity supplement. No supplement, including either of these, produces meaningful results in the context of a high-stress lifestyle, chronic sleep deprivation, and a processed food-heavy diet.

Women who are pregnant, breastfeeding, or planning to become pregnant should not use the NAD Women's Longevity Formula without discussion with their OB-GYN. Several ingredients have not been studied in pregnant women, and the formula is designed for perimenopause and post-menopause, not reproductive years.

Women who are taking prescription medications, particularly blood thinners, immunosuppressants, or medications metabolized by the cytochrome P450 system, should discuss both products with their physician before starting. Resveratrol and quercetin in particular have known interactions with several common medications.

How does NAD+ actually decline, and does it matter?

NAD+ is a coenzyme found in every cell. It is required for over 500 enzymatic reactions, including the activity of sirtuins (longevity regulators), PARP enzymes (DNA repair), and the electron transport chain (cellular energy production). Research shows that NAD+ levels in human tissue decline by approximately 50% between ages 20 and 60. The cellular consequences include impaired DNA repair, reduced mitochondrial efficiency, accumulated oxidative damage, and dysregulated immune and metabolic function. This decline is measurable, meaningful, and is considered one of the key mechanisms of biological aging.

Why does the formula use liposomal delivery?

Many longevity compounds, including resveratrol and certain fat-soluble antioxidants, have poor bioavailability in standard capsule or tablet form because they are either poorly water-soluble, rapidly metabolized in the gut wall, or extracted before reaching target tissues. Liposomal delivery encases these compounds in phospholipid vesicles that mimic cell membrane structure, protecting them through digestion and improving absorption into systemic circulation. Pharmacokinetic studies show significantly higher blood levels of liposomal formulations compared to equivalent standard doses.
