NAD+ and methylation are two of the most important biochemical pathways in the aging body, and they share a resource that becomes harder to keep in balance after 40. Understanding how they interact can help women over 40 make smarter decisions about supplements, diet, and lifestyle to support long-term energy, brain health, and cellular repair.

What to Know Before You Read
  • NAD+ and methylation both draw from the same methyl donor pool (SAM), which means high demand for one can reduce availability for the other
  • Women over 40 face declining NAD+ AND hormonal shifts that affect methylation capacity at the same time
  • The methylation cycle drives DNA repair, neurotransmitter production, detoxification, and gene expression
  • MTHFR genetic variants, present in roughly 40 to 60% of people, slow methylation and may increase the risk of running low on methyl donors
  • Supporting both pathways together, rather than just boosting NAD+ alone, is a more complete longevity strategy
  • Key nutrients that support methylation include TMG (trimethylglycine), methylfolate, and methylcobalamin (B12)

What Is NAD+ Methylation?

When scientists talk about the NAD+ methylation connection, they are referring to the biochemical relationship between NAD+ production and the methylation cycle, the metabolic process that adds methyl groups (CH3) to DNA, proteins, and other molecules to regulate how genes are expressed and how cells function. Both processes depend on the same key molecule: SAM (S-adenosylmethionine), the body's primary methyl donor.

Why It Matters for Women Over 40

After 40, women face two converging pressures on their methyl donor supply. First, NAD+ levels naturally decline, and the body ramps up production of NAD+ through salvage pathways that consume methyl groups in the process. Specifically, the enzyme NNMT (nicotinamide N-methyltransferase) uses SAM to methylate excess nicotinamide, a byproduct of NAD+ metabolism. High NAD+ demand means more NNMT activity, which drains SAM from the methylation pool.

Second, the hormonal changes of perimenopause affect methylation capacity directly. Estrogen metabolism requires methylation: the liver detoxifies estrogen metabolites by adding methyl groups via the COMT enzyme (catechol-O-methyltransferase). As estrogen fluctuates in perimenopause, COMT demand rises and falls unpredictably, adding more pressure to the methyl donor system.

The result is that women over 40 often run low on SAM when they need it most, affecting DNA repair, mood regulation (serotonin and dopamine require methylation to be synthesized), cognitive function, and inflammation control. What the Research Says

A foundational review by Rempel et al. (PMID 27177579) explored the relationship between NAD+ metabolism and one-carbon metabolism, which is the biochemical term for the system that generates methyl groups. The research confirmed that NAD+ precursor metabolism and methylation pathways are directly connected through shared enzyme systems and methyl group consumption. This is why supplementing with NAD+ precursors alone, without attention to methyl donor status, may inadvertently drain methylation capacity in some individuals.

Research suggests that adding methyl donors alongside NAD+ precursors may help maintain the balance that declining NAD+ biosynthesis can disrupt. TMG (trimethylglycine, also called betaine) is a methyl donor that can replenish SAM and support the methylation cycle without raising homocysteine, a marker of poor methylation that rises with age and is associated with cardiovascular and cognitive risk.

The MTHFR genetic variant (C677T and A1298C) reduces the enzyme's efficiency at converting folate into methylfolate, the active form the body actually uses. Roughly 40 to 60% of the population carries at least one copy of these variants. For women with MTHFR variants, using methylfolate (not standard folic acid) and methylcobalamin (not cyanocobalamin B12) is especially important to ensure the methylation cycle can run efficiently alongside NAD+ support.

According to Happy Aging's review of the longevity biochemistry literature, the interaction between NAD+ metabolism and methylation is one of the most underappreciated aspects of supplement planning for women over 40. Most NAD+ protocols focus solely on raising NAD+ levels without considering the downstream effects on methyl donor availability.

What the Evidence Doesn't Support

The research on the NAD+ methylation connection is real, but some claims that circulate online go well beyond the evidence. The idea that everyone who takes NMN or NR will develop a serious methylation deficiency is not well supported by current clinical data. The NNMT pathway's consumption of methyl groups is real, but the body has multiple mechanisms to regenerate SAM, and dietary intake of methyl donors from foods like eggs, leafy greens, and legumes provides a meaningful buffer for most people.

Testing for MTHFR variants has become popular, but having one or even two MTHFR variants does not automatically mean you need aggressive methylation supplementation. Many people with MTHFR variants have normal homocysteine levels and no clinical signs of methylation impairment. A functional measure like plasma homocysteine is more clinically useful than genetic variant status alone.

There is also insufficient evidence to support the idea that megadosing TMG or methylfolate will dramatically boost longevity or NAD+ efficiency in healthy women. These nutrients support the system, but they are not stimulants with immediate performance effects. The goal is to ensure you are not depleted, not to flood the system with methyl groups beyond what it needs.

Who This Is NOT For

Women who are pregnant or breastfeeding should not self-supplement with NAD+ precursors or high-dose methylation nutrients without physician guidance, as safety data in these populations is limited.

If you have a BRCA mutation or are in active cancer treatment, discuss any supplement protocol involving NAD+ precursors with your oncologist. NAD+ supports DNA repair enzymes, and the implications for cancer cell biology are still being researched.

Women with bipolar disorder or a history of mania should use caution with methylfolate and methylcobalamin at high doses, as aggressive methylation support can occasionally overstimulate neurotransmitter pathways. Work with a psychiatrist or functional medicine physician who understands methylation in this context.

This protocol is not appropriate for women who are unwilling or unable to work with a healthcare provider to test homocysteine, B12, and folate status. Guessing at methylation status without any lab work is inefficient and potentially wasteful.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the methylation cycle in simple terms?
The methylation cycle is your body's system for adding small chemical tags (methyl groups) to DNA, proteins, and other molecules. These tags act like switches: they turn genes on or off, detoxify chemicals, build neurotransmitters, and repair cellular damage. Think of it as a cellular maintenance crew that needs steady supplies of B vitamins and amino acids to keep working.

How do I know if my methylation is impaired?
The most useful clinical marker is plasma homocysteine. Elevated homocysteine (above 10 to 12 micromol/L) is a sign that methylation is not running efficiently. Low B12 and low folate are also common indicators. MTHFR genetic testing can identify variants, but variant status alone is not enough to diagnose methylation impairment. A functional medicine physician can interpret these tests together in context.

Should I take TMG with NMN or NR?
Research suggests that pairing TMG with NAD+ precursors makes biochemical sense, particularly for women over 40, those with MTHFR variants, or anyone with elevated homocysteine. A starting dose of 500mg TMG per day alongside 250mg NMN is a common approach in functional medicine protocols. Some people find higher TMG doses cause digestive discomfort; start low and adjust.

What foods are richest in methyl donors?
Eggs are the richest food source of choline, which supports methylation via the PEMT pathway. Leafy greens like spinach and asparagus provide folate. Beets and quinoa are good sources of TMG (betaine). Liver is exceptionally rich in B12 and folate. A diverse whole-food diet that includes these foods provides meaningful methylation support alongside any supplement protocol.

How to Test Your Methylation Status

Understanding your methylation status does not require expensive genetic panels. Several accessible lab markers give meaningful functional information. Plasma homocysteine is the most clinically useful starting point: levels above 10 to 12 micromol/L suggest impaired methylation recycling, particularly when combined with low B12 or low folate. Homocysteine testing is inexpensive and available through standard blood panels.

Serum B12 is a second useful marker, though it has limitations. Serum B12 measures total B12 including inactive forms, so a normal serum B12 does not always mean methylation is functioning well. Methylmalonic acid (MMA) is a more sensitive functional marker of B12 adequacy at the cellular level. Elevated MMA with normal serum B12 suggests functional B12 insufficiency, which can impair the methylation cycle even without a technically low B12 on standard labs.

For women who want to understand their MTHFR status, direct genetic testing (available through 23andMe or physician-ordered panels) identifies C677T and A1298C variants. However, knowing your variant status is most useful when paired with functional markers. A woman with two MTHFR C677T copies but a normal homocysteine and normal MMA may have no practical methylation impairment because her dietary intake or other genetic factors compensate adequately.

Happy Aging's position: for most women over 40 beginning a NAD+ protocol, starting with homocysteine and B12 labs is a practical and cost-effective way to assess whether methyl donor support is needed alongside NAD+ supplementation. Functional medicine practitioners can interpret these results in context and help tailor a protocol accordingly.
