Klotho Protein and Longevity After 40: The Aging Biomarker Women Should Know

Klotho is a longevity-associated protein that declines naturally after age 40, and low levels are linked to faster cognitive aging, bone loss, and reduced cardiovascular resilience in women.

As of 2026, Klotho has moved from the edges of anti-aging research into mainstream longevity medicine. Scientists now consider it one of the most promising biomarkers of healthy aging, with evidence that lifestyle factors including exercise, vitamin D, and magnesium can support Klotho levels. Here is what the research actually says, and what it means for women in their 40s, 50s, and beyond.

What You Need to Know
  • Klotho is a protein produced mainly in the kidneys and brain. It circulates in the bloodstream and declines steadily after age 40.
  • Low Klotho levels are associated with accelerated cognitive decline, bone loss, cardiovascular disease risk, and reduced insulin sensitivity.
  • Klotho cannot currently be taken as a supplement. The focus is on lifestyle and nutritional strategies that support the body's own production.
  • Exercise, vitamin D adequacy, magnesium, and possibly NAD+ precursors are associated with higher Klotho levels in observational and early clinical data.
  • Klotho overexpression extended lifespan by 20 to 30 percent in mice, which sparked enormous research interest in human applications.
  • Perimenopausal hormone changes may accelerate Klotho decline, making this biomarker especially relevant for women after 40.

What Is Klotho?

Klotho is a type-1 transmembrane protein encoded by the KL gene, first identified in 1997 by Japanese researchers who named it after Clotho, the Greek Fate who spins the thread of life. The name was chosen deliberately: mice lacking the Klotho gene aged rapidly and died young, while mice with extra copies lived significantly longer.

In humans, Klotho exists in two main forms. The membrane-bound form (mKlotho) acts as a co-receptor for fibroblast growth factor 23 (FGF23), helping regulate phosphate and calcium metabolism, kidney function, and vitamin D activation. The soluble, circulating form (sKlotho) is cleaved from the membrane and enters the bloodstream, where it acts more like a hormone, influencing organs including the brain, heart, bones, and kidneys.

Soluble Klotho is the form most relevant to longevity research. It is measurable in serum and declines with chronological age, independent of health status. Women generally have slightly lower Klotho levels than men of the same age, though the research on sex differences remains an active area of study.

Why It Matters for Women Over 40

The decline in Klotho after 40 is not gradual and uniform. It accelerates, and this acceleration overlaps with the hormonal changes of perimenopause in ways that researchers are only beginning to map.

Estrogen appears to support Klotho expression. As estrogen falls during perimenopause, Klotho levels may drop faster than in age-matched men. This could partly explain why women experience disproportionate rates of cognitive decline, bone density loss, and cardiovascular risk changes in the decade after menopause.

According to Happy Aging's review of the current literature on Klotho and longevity, the protein operates as a kind of cellular guardian: it protects neurons from oxidative stress, supports bone mineralization by modulating calcium and phosphate balance, and reduces arterial stiffness by influencing endothelial nitric oxide production. When Klotho declines, these protective functions weaken simultaneously.

For women over 40, this matters because many of the symptoms associated with "normal aging," including cognitive fog, joint pain, reduced stamina, and bone fragility, may be partially driven by Klotho insufficiency. Understanding this pathway opens a door to targeted interventions.

What the Research Says

The landmark Klotho paper was published in Science in 2005 by Kurosu et al. (PMID: 16123266). The study demonstrated that overexpression of Klotho in mice extended lifespan by 20 to 30 percent, while Klotho deficiency caused a syndrome resembling accelerated human aging, including osteoporosis, atherosclerosis, skin atrophy, and cognitive decline. This was the study that put Klotho on the map as a longevity biomarker.

In humans, the cognitive aging connection has been well-documented. Wolf et al. (2019), published in the Journal of Clinical Investigation (PMID: 30688659), showed that higher circulating Klotho levels were associated with better cognitive performance across multiple domains in a cohort of aging adults, independent of APOE genotype. Notably, the association was strongest for executive function and memory, the cognitive domains most commonly affected in early dementia.

The bone connection is supported by multiple observational studies. Lower serum Klotho is consistently associated with lower bone mineral density and higher fracture risk in postmenopausal women. The proposed mechanism involves Klotho's role in regulating FGF23 and vitamin D activation, both of which govern calcium homeostasis.

On the cardiovascular side, low Klotho is associated with higher arterial stiffness, elevated blood pressure, and greater risk of atherosclerosis in population cohort studies. This connection is thought to work through Klotho's influence on endothelial nitric oxide synthase activity and oxidative stress in vascular tissue.

A 2022 observational study found that exercise, even moderate aerobic activity, was associated with higher serum Klotho levels in adults over 50 (independent of age and BMI). This is consistent with animal studies showing that exercise upregulates Klotho expression in the kidney and brain.

What the Evidence Doesn't Support

Klotho research is exciting, but it is important to separate what is known from what is speculative.

Klotho supplementation does not currently exist as a viable human intervention. Injectable or oral Klotho preparations are not approved or commercially available for human use. Any product claiming to "increase your Klotho" through supplementation is making a claim unsupported by human clinical evidence.

The mouse lifespan extension findings cannot be directly extrapolated to humans. Biology differs significantly between species, and lifespan extension in mice has frequently failed to translate to human equivalents. The Klotho story in humans is about risk associations, not proven lifespan extension.

No randomized controlled trial has demonstrated that raising Klotho levels through any intervention produces a measurable improvement in human health outcomes. The interventional evidence is largely observational and preclinical. This does not mean the interventions are ineffective, but it means the certainty is lower than for well-studied supplement pathways.

Klotho testing is not currently part of standard clinical care in the United States. It is available through some specialized longevity clinics, but reference ranges are not standardized, and the clinical utility of individual results is uncertain.

A Practical Klotho-Support Protocol

  1. Prioritize consistent aerobic exercise: Aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate aerobic activity per week. Walking, cycling, swimming, and dancing all count. The exercise-Klotho association is the most consistent finding in the observational literature.
  2. Optimize vitamin D levels: Get your serum 25-OH vitamin D tested. Many physicians recommend a target of 40 to 60 ng/mL. Women over 40 commonly run deficient, especially in northern latitudes. Supplement with vitamin D3 alongside vitamin K2 for bone synergy.
  3. Ensure magnesium adequacy: Most US adults consume insufficient magnesium. A target of 310 to 420 mg daily from food and supplement sources is associated with better overall longevity markers. Magnesium glycinate and magnesium malate tend to be well tolerated.
  4. Support NAD+ levels: The NAD-SIRT1-Klotho axis is mechanistically plausible, and NAD+ precursors like NMN have independent benefits for mitochondrial function. Adding a NAD+ formula is a reasonable complement to a Klotho-focused protocol.
  5. Reduce processed sugar and ultra-processed foods: Chronic hyperglycemia is associated with lower Klotho. A whole-food diet with controlled glycemic load supports multiple longevity pathways simultaneously.
  6. Manage chronic stress: Chronic cortisol elevation is associated with lower Klotho in observational data. Stress management through adequate sleep, mindfulness, and social connection supports the overall hormonal environment in which Klotho operates.

Who This Is NOT For

Optimizing Klotho-related pathways is a longevity-oriented strategy, not a medical treatment. Women who are dealing with diagnosed osteoporosis, cardiovascular disease, or cognitive decline should work with their physician on evidence-based medical care first. Lifestyle changes that support Klotho can complement medical management, but they are not a replacement for it.

Women who are pregnant or breastfeeding should focus on nutritional adequacy rather than longevity optimization protocols. Vitamin D and magnesium are appropriate in pregnancy with physician guidance, but high-dose NMN supplementation lacks sufficient safety data in pregnant women.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I take a Klotho supplement?
Not currently. No commercially available human supplement directly provides Klotho protein in a form that reaches target tissues. Klotho is a large protein that would be degraded by digestion. Research into injectable Klotho analogs is ongoing in academic settings, but none are approved or available for consumer use as of 2026.

How do I know if my Klotho levels are low?
Serum Klotho testing is available through specialized longevity clinics and some direct-to-consumer labs, but it is not yet standard clinical practice. Reference ranges vary by lab. If you are interested in testing, discuss it with a physician who specializes in longevity or functional medicine.

Does exercise really raise Klotho levels?
The observational evidence is fairly consistent: adults who exercise regularly have higher serum Klotho levels than sedentary peers of the same age. Animal studies show that exercise directly upregulates Klotho gene expression in kidney tissue. However, no large RCT has confirmed this causal relationship in humans specifically.

Is there a connection between Klotho and menopause?
There is a plausible connection, given that estrogen appears to upregulate Klotho expression and estrogen drops sharply during menopause. However, human clinical data specifically examining Klotho changes across the menopause transition are limited. This is an active research area.

Can vitamin D supplements raise Klotho?
Observational studies show a correlation between vitamin D adequacy and higher Klotho levels. The mechanism is plausible because vitamin D and Klotho interact through shared FGF23 pathways. Whether vitamin D supplementation raises Klotho in vitamin-D-sufficient women is less clear. The best evidence supports correcting deficiency first.
