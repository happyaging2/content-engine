Constipation on Ozempic or Wegovy: A 7-Step Protocol for Women Over 40

This article discusses nutritional and lifestyle support strategies for women managing GI side effects of GLP-1 receptor agonist medications. It is not a guide to medication management. Never adjust, skip, or stop your GLP-1 medication without consulting your prescribing physician.

Constipation is one of the most common side effects of GLP-1 receptor agonist medications like Ozempic and Wegovy, affecting roughly 24% of users in clinical trials, and women over 40 are particularly vulnerable due to age-related changes in gut motility that predate the medication.

As of 2026, millions of women are using GLP-1 medications for weight management, but the nutritional and lifestyle strategies needed to manage the GI side effects remain poorly understood by most users. This article gives you a practical, evidence-based 7-step protocol to reduce constipation severity without compromising the effectiveness of your medication.

Key Takeaways
  • GLP-1 receptor agonists slow gastric emptying and reduce gut motility. Constipation affects approximately 24% of semaglutide users in the STEP 1 clinical trial (PMID: 33567185) versus 11% of placebo participants.
  • Women over 40 have naturally slower gut transit than younger women, compounding GLP-1-related constipation risk.
  • Hydration, gradual fiber increase, magnesium citrate, and movement are the four most evidence-supported interventions.
  • Laxative overuse can create dependency. Start with the gentlest, most physiological approaches first.
  • Resistant starch and prebiotic fiber support the gut bacteria that generate bowel-moving short-chain fatty acids.
  • Timing supplements away from your injection day matters. Injection-day GI effects can compound constipation and nausea together.

What Is GLP-1 Medication-Induced Constipation?

GLP-1 receptor agonist medications (semaglutide = Ozempic, Wegovy; tirzepatide = Mounjaro, Zepbound) work by mimicking the glucagon-like peptide-1 hormone that your gut naturally releases after eating. They slow gastric emptying, reduce appetite, and alter gut motility at multiple levels of the gastrointestinal tract.

The slowed gut motility that GLP-1 medications produce is intentional: it increases satiety and slows glucose absorption. But the same mechanism that reduces appetite also reduces the frequency and urgency of bowel movements. In some women, this produces mild infrequency. In others, it causes significant constipation with hard, difficult-to-pass stools.

Constipation is defined clinically as fewer than three bowel movements per week, with straining, hard stools, or a sensation of incomplete emptying. Women experiencing any of these on GLP-1 medications should address it proactively rather than waiting.

Why are women over 40 especially affected? Gut transit time slows naturally with age, and perimenopause accelerates this through hormonal effects on smooth muscle function in the bowel. GLP-1 medications add another layer of motility slowing on top of a system that may already be slower than it was in your 30s.

Why It Matters for Women Over 40

Unmanaged constipation on GLP-1 medications is not just uncomfortable. It creates a cycle that makes medication adherence harder. Nausea from GLP-1 medications is often worsened when stools are backed up in the colon. Bloating increases. Appetite suppression becomes intertwined with discomfort, making it harder to distinguish medication effects from GI distress.

Chronic constipation also affects the gut microbiome. When stool transit slows, the balance of bacterial fermentation products shifts. Gut bacteria that produce beneficial short-chain fatty acids are crowded out by those that thrive in low-oxygen, slow-transit environments. This can worsen bloating and affect how you absorb nutrients at a time when your food intake is already reduced by the medication.

For women over 40 on GLP-1 medications who are also in perimenopause, the combination of hormonal changes, medication-induced motility slowing, and potentially lower overall food volume creates a perfect condition for constipation to take hold.

What the Research Says

Wilding et al. (2021) published the STEP 1 trial in the New England Journal of Medicine (PMID: 33567185), which was the pivotal clinical trial for semaglutide 2.4mg (Wegovy) for weight management. Among 1,961 adults, constipation was reported in 24.2% of the semaglutide group compared to 11.1% of the placebo group. This was one of the most common GI adverse events, alongside nausea and vomiting.

The mechanism is well-established: GLP-1 receptors are expressed throughout the gastrointestinal tract, and their activation slows gastric emptying and reduces colonic motility. A 2022 review in Alimentary Pharmacology and Therapeutics (DOI: 10.1111/apt.16808) confirmed that GLP-1 receptor agonists as a class consistently reduce gut transit time.

For the nutritional interventions, the evidence base is drawn from constipation research more broadly rather than GLP-1-specific trials. Magnesium citrate has been shown in multiple trials to improve stool frequency and consistency (systematic review, DOI: 10.1136/bmj.f6678). Increased dietary fiber from diverse sources improves transit time in randomized studies. Hydration increases stool water content and reduces hardness. These are the most reliable physiological interventions available.

Prebiotic fiber and resistant starch support butyrate production, which research shows maintains colonic motility and supports bowel regularity through its effects on colon cell function (Topping and Clifton, 2001, PMID: 11427691).

According to Happy Aging's review of the current literature on GLP-1 medication side effects, constipation management should prioritize hydration and fiber timing first, before considering any pharmaceutical laxatives. Most women can manage GLP-1-induced constipation effectively with dietary and lifestyle modifications.

What the Evidence Doesn't Support

Stimulant laxatives (senna, bisacodyl) are not an appropriate first-line response to GLP-1-induced constipation. While they produce results, they work by irritating the colon rather than addressing the underlying motility slowdown. Frequent use can lead to dependency on stimulation and worsen long-term bowel function.

Probiotic supplementation alone is not a proven treatment for GLP-1-induced constipation. The evidence for probiotics specifically in constipation is mixed, and GLP-1-related motility effects are mechanical in nature rather than primarily microbiome-driven.

Herbal laxatives (aloe vera, cascara) are not regulated as drugs and have uncertain dosing and interaction profiles. Some contain anthraquinone compounds that may be harmful with long-term use.

The 7-Step Protocol for GLP-1-Related Constipation

  1. Hydrate proactively, targeting at least 2 liters of water daily. GLP-1 medications reduce appetite, which often means women also reduce fluid intake without realizing it. Many report eating so little that they forget to drink. Dehydration is one of the most common and fixable contributors to constipation on GLP-1 medications. Set reminders if needed. Sparkling water counts. Herbal teas count. Caffeinated beverages count partially but are not a substitute for water.
  2. Increase fiber gradually, aiming for 25-35g per day. Do not add 20g of fiber in a single day if you were eating minimal fiber before. Add one fiber-rich food every 2-3 days and allow your gut to adapt. Good sources on a reduced-calorie GLP-1 protocol: vegetables (especially leafy greens, broccoli, Brussels sprouts), legumes, oats, chia seeds, and flaxseeds. The goal is variety, not volume from a single fiber source.
  3. Add resistant starch from cooked-cooled starches. Cook a batch of rice, potatoes, or lentils, refrigerate overnight, and eat them cold or gently reheated. This simple habit produces RS3 resistant starch that ferments in the colon, feeds butyrate-producing bacteria, and supports natural colonic motility. For more on resistant starch, see our gut health resource page.
  4. Take magnesium citrate at bedtime. Magnesium citrate is an osmotic agent that draws water into the colon, softening stool and stimulating gentle movement. Start at 150-200mg of elemental magnesium at bedtime. Many women on GLP-1 medications are also low in dietary magnesium because their food volume is reduced, making supplementation doubly practical. Magnesium glycinate is an alternative if citrate causes loose stools.
  5. Move your body for at least 20-30 minutes daily, especially after meals. Physical movement directly stimulates peristalsis, the rhythmic wave-like contractions that move stool through the colon. A post-meal walk of even 10-15 minutes has been shown in multiple studies to improve gastric emptying and bowel transit. GLP-1 medications already slow gastric emptying; movement is a direct physiological counterbalance.
  6. Add a quality prebiotic-probiotic combination to support colonic bacteria. The gut bacteria responsible for producing short-chain fatty acids (which help maintain colonic motility) need both live bacterial strains and prebiotic fiber to feed on. A prebiotic and probiotic combination addresses both. Look for products containing Bifidobacterium longum and Lactobacillus acidophilus, which have the strongest evidence for supporting bowel regularity.
  7. Time supplements and high-fiber foods away from your injection day. Many women on weekly GLP-1 injections (Wegovy, Zepbound) experience heightened nausea and GI sensitivity in the 24-48 hours after injection. Combining high-fiber meals or magnesium supplementation with peak GI sensitivity can compound discomfort. Consider scheduling your highest-fiber intake 2-3 days after injection day, when your GI tolerance is better.

Who This Is NOT For

If you have gone more than 5 days without a bowel movement and are experiencing significant abdominal pain, distension, or vomiting, contact your physician before trying home management strategies. This can indicate more serious GI issues that require medical evaluation.

Women with inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), Crohn's disease, or structural GI issues should not use this general protocol without tailoring it with their gastroenterologist, as some recommendations may need modification.

This protocol is for nutritional and lifestyle management of constipation as a GLP-1 medication side effect. It is not a substitute for your prescribing physician's guidance on medication management. Do not adjust your GLP-1 dose or schedule based on GI side effects without consulting your doctor.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: How long does GLP-1-induced constipation typically last?
A: For most women, constipation is most pronounced in the first 4-8 weeks of GLP-1 treatment, particularly during dose escalation phases. It often improves as the body adapts, especially when the protocol above is followed consistently. Some women experience it intermittently throughout treatment, particularly after dose increases.

Q: Should I stop my GLP-1 medication if constipation is severe?
A: Do not stop or adjust your medication without speaking to your prescribing physician. Constipation is a known and manageable side effect. Most physicians will work with you on dose timing, adjunct strategies, or temporary dose modification rather than discontinuation.

Q: Can I take a fiber supplement (Metamucil, Citrucel) on a GLP-1 medication?
A: Yes, soluble fiber supplements like psyllium husk (Metamucil) are generally safe and appropriate with GLP-1 medications. Take them with a large glass of water and away from your GLP-1 injection to avoid compounding GI effects. Start with a small dose and increase gradually.

Q: Is magnesium citrate different from the magnesium in a multivitamin?
A: Yes. Magnesium oxide (often in multivitamins) is poorly absorbed. Magnesium citrate has better absorption and osmotic properties that specifically support bowel function. Look for "magnesium citrate" specifically on the supplement label rather than just "magnesium."

Q: What foods should I avoid on a GLP-1 medication to reduce constipation?
A: Minimize ultra-processed foods, fried foods, and refined carbohydrates that have had fiber removed. These add calories without contributing to gut motility. Also limit dairy foods (cheese, especially) which can slow transit in some women. Focus on vegetables, whole grains, legumes, and fruits as your primary carbohydrate sources.
