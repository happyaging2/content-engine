As of 2026, passionflower has become one of the more popular herbal additions to sleep supplement blends. It has a long history in traditional medicine, a plausible mechanism, and a reasonable safety profile. What it does not have, yet, is the kind of large-scale, women-specific human clinical data that would put it in the same category as better-evidenced sleep support ingredients like magnesium glycinate or glycine.

What Is Passionflower?

Passionflower is a climbing vine native to the southeastern United States and Central and South America. The part of the plant used in supplements is typically the aerial parts (leaves, stems, and flowers), dried and extracted for standardized compounds including flavonoids, particularly chrysin and vitexin.

For women over 40, sleep is one of the most common and impactful health concerns. Perimenopause brings hormonal shifts that disrupt the architecture of sleep directly: progesterone, which has natural GABA-like calming effects, declines, making it harder to fall and stay asleep. Estrogen fluctuations drive nighttime hot flashes and wake-ups. This is not simple stress-induced insomnia; it is a hormonally driven change in brain chemistry that requires a thoughtful approach to support.

Why Sleep Disruption Affects Women Over 40 Differently

The sleep architecture changes that come with perimenopause are distinct from ordinary insomnia. Progesterone acts on GABA receptors in the brain, promoting the calm, drowsy state that helps you fall asleep. As progesterone declines in the 40s, this natural sedation effect weakens, meaning the brain has to work harder to initiate and maintain sleep.

Hot flashes and night sweats, driven by estrogen fluctuations, are another direct disruptor. Research suggests that nocturnal hot flashes often wake women during the transition between sleep stages, which explains the common experience of sleeping for seven or eight hours but waking feeling unrested. This is reduced slow-wave and REM sleep, not just reduced total hours.

Understanding this context is important when evaluating passionflower. A supplement that mildly modulates GABA activity could plausibly help with sleep onset, but it is unlikely to address the hormonal drivers of sleep disruption in perimenopause on its own.

What the Research Says

The most cited study on passionflower and sleep is a 2011 randomized crossover trial published in Phytotherapy Research that found passionflower herbal tea significantly improved sleep quality scores compared to a placebo tea, as measured by the Sleep Diary questionnaire. The sample size was small (41 participants), the duration was short (one week per condition), and the population was healthy adults, not perimenopausal women specifically. Still, the effect was statistically significant and the researchers attributed it to GABA-modulating compounds in the plant extract.

Additional studies have examined passionflower in combination with other botanicals, including valerian and hops, with generally positive effects on anxiety and sleep onset. The challenge with combination studies is that it is difficult to isolate the specific contribution of passionflower versus the other ingredients.

According to Happy Aging's review of the passionflower literature through 2026, the most consistent finding is a modest improvement in subjective sleep quality, particularly sleep onset, in low-stress, otherwise healthy adults. The evidence for passionflower addressing the deeper hormonal sleep disruption of perimenopause specifically is not yet available.

For comparison, magnesium supplementation has stronger evidence. A randomized placebo-controlled trial by Abbasi et al. published in the Journal of Research in Medical Sciences (PMID 23853635) found that magnesium supplementation significantly improved insomnia severity, sleep efficiency, and early morning awakening scores in elderly subjects. This population-level data gives magnesium a stronger evidence position than passionflower currently holds.

What the Evidence Does Not Support

The evidence does not support passionflower as a treatment for insomnia, sleep apnea, or hormone-driven sleep disruption. It does not have the kind of large-scale, long-duration, women-specific trial data that would justify calling it a first-line intervention. The small sample sizes in existing studies make it difficult to generalize findings broadly.

The idea that passionflower tea is equivalent to a standardized extract supplement is also not supported. The GABA-active flavonoids in passionflower vary significantly depending on the plant part used, extraction method, and standardization. A tea made from dried herb and a standardized capsule supplement are not interchangeable in terms of expected effect.

Passionflower also does not address the root hormonal drivers of sleep changes after 40. Women whose sleep disruption is primarily driven by hot flashes, night sweats, or progesterone decline will likely find passionflower alone insufficient. A comprehensive approach to sleep support needs to address multiple mechanisms.

Happy Aging's position: passionflower is a genuinely interesting botanical with a plausible mechanism and modest evidence for sleep quality improvement. It earns a supporting role in a comprehensive sleep formula, but it should not be the sole active ingredient relied upon by women navigating hormonal sleep disruption after 40.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can passionflower help with perimenopause sleep problems?

Passionflower may help modestly with sleep onset by supporting GABA activity, which is one component of the sleep disruption that comes with perimenopause (since declining progesterone reduces natural GABA-like calming effects). However, it does not address hot flashes, night sweats, or the circadian disruption that are also common drivers of perimenopausal sleep changes. A comprehensive sleep formula is a better strategy.

Is passionflower tea as effective as a capsule supplement?

Not necessarily. The concentration of active compounds in passionflower tea varies considerably depending on preparation, plant material, and steep time. Standardized capsule extracts provide a more consistent dose of the flavonoids believed to drive the sleep-supporting effects. If you prefer the ritual of herbal tea, it may still have some benefit, but do not expect it to match a standardized supplement for predictable effects.

How quickly does passionflower work for sleep?

Most small studies observed effects within one to two weeks of consistent use. Passionflower is not designed to work like a pharmaceutical sedative taken acutely; its effects build with regular use. Taking it 30 to 60 minutes before bed consistently is the most common approach.

Is passionflower safe to take every night?

Research suggests passionflower is safe for short-term daily use in otherwise healthy adults. Long-term safety data (beyond a few months) is limited. If you are taking any prescription medications, particularly sedatives, anti-anxiety medications, or blood thinners, consult your healthcare provider before using passionflower regularly.

What does passionflower taste like as a supplement?

As a capsule or tincture, passionflower has a mild earthy taste that most people find neutral or slightly herbal. In tea form, it is mild and slightly sweet. It mixes well with other calming herbal ingredients like lemon balm or chamomile if you prefer a blended herbal tea approach.
