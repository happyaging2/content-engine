Both NR (nicotinamide riboside) and NMN (nicotinamide mononucleotide) effectively raise NAD+ levels in humans, and the honest answer about which is "better" is that the head-to-head evidence is limited: dose, delivery form, and individual metabolism likely matter more than the NR vs NMN distinction itself.

What Are NR and NMN?

NAD+ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide) is a coenzyme present in every cell in your body, essential for energy metabolism, DNA repair, and the activity of sirtuins (longevity-associated proteins). NAD+ levels decline with age: estimates suggest a 40 to 50 percent decline in tissue NAD+ concentrations between ages 20 and 60. This decline is associated with many of the cellular changes of aging, including reduced mitochondrial efficiency, accumulated DNA damage, increased cellular senescence, and impaired stress response.

NR (nicotinamide riboside) and NMN (nicotinamide mononucleotide) are both precursors to NAD+: compounds that the body converts into NAD+ through slightly different biochemical pathways. Think of them as two different roads leading to the same destination.

NR enters cells through specific nucleoside transporters, then is phosphorylated to NMN inside the cell, and then converted to NAD+. NMN can be transported into cells directly through the Slc12a8 transporter (identified in mouse intestine), or it can be dephosphorylated to NR extracellularly and then follow the same NR pathway. This overlap in pathways is one reason why the NR versus NMN debate is less clear-cut than it appears in marketing materials.

Both compounds have been developed as supplements because orally supplemented NAD+ itself is broken down before it can enter cells. The precursor approach works around this limitation: smaller molecules that can be absorbed and converted to NAD+ intracellularly.

Why It Matters for Women Over 40

For women over 40, the relevance of NAD+ support goes beyond general aging. The perimenopausal transition involves significant cellular stress: fluctuating hormones, increased oxidative stress, mitochondrial changes in ovarian cells, and the biological demands of the hormonal shift all increase NAD+ consumption. This means that the baseline NAD+ decline of aging is compounded by the specific metabolic demands of the perimenopausal transition.

The practical symptoms associated with declining NAD+ in this population overlap significantly with common perimenopause complaints: fatigue that sleep does not fully resolve, reduced cognitive clarity or "brain fog," impaired exercise recovery, and a sense of reduced metabolic efficiency. Whether NAD+ restoration fully explains these symptoms is debated, but the mechanistic case for supplementation in this life stage is stronger than the general aging argument alone.

The question of which precursor form to choose is therefore practically relevant to women making supplement decisions in this space, and it deserves an honest analysis rather than brand-driven advocacy.

What the Research Says

The NR evidence base begins with Conze et al. 2016 (PMID 28532835), which demonstrated that oral NR supplementation raises NAD+ concentrations in blood in healthy adults. This was a critical proof-of-concept study establishing that NR is bioavailable and effective as an oral supplement. Multiple subsequent trials have confirmed NR's ability to raise NAD+ in diverse populations.

The NMN evidence base includes the Yoshino et al. 2021 RCT (PMID 34590197), which specifically examined 250mg per day of NMN in premenopausal and postmenopausal women. This trial showed that NMN raised blood NAD+ concentrations and improved muscle insulin sensitivity in the postmenopausal group. This women-specific data is notable because much of the earlier NMN research was conducted in male subjects or mixed populations.

The direct head-to-head human comparison (Uddin et al. 2020, a small comparative study) showed comparable NAD+ elevation for both NR and NMN, with neither compound demonstrating clear superiority. The study was small, and the finding of equivalence should be interpreted as "not enough evidence to differentiate" rather than "proven equal."

According to Happy Aging's review of the NR versus NMN literature, the clinical data does not support a definitive claim that either compound is superior. Both effectively raise NAD+ in humans. The evidence base for NR is larger and older; the evidence base for NMN is growing and includes more recent women-specific data. Dose, bioavailability form, and individual metabolic context likely produce more meaningful variation in outcomes than the choice between NR and NMN at comparable doses.

What the Evidence Doesn't Support

The NR versus NMN debate has been significantly shaped by commercial interests. TruNiagen is the dominant commercial NR brand and has invested substantially in NR clinical research. Their comparative publications naturally frame the NR evidence favorably. Happy Aging uses NMN and NMNH forms. Being transparent about these commercial interests is important for evaluating any head-to-head claim from either side.

What the evidence does not support is the claim that either NR or NMN is clearly the superior NAD+ precursor for all women in all contexts. The head-to-head human data is insufficient to make this claim. Studies conducted or funded by manufacturers of one form should be weighted accordingly, not dismissed, but contextualized.

NMNH (reduced NMN) is the newest development in this space. Theoretically, the reduced form is more directly incorporated into NAD+ biosynthesis and may be more bioavailable than standard NMN. The mechanistic logic is sound. Direct human clinical head-to-head data comparing NMNH to NMN and NR is limited. Theoretical advantages are not the same as demonstrated clinical advantages.

Happy Aging's position: The NR vs NMN question does not have a definitive evidence-based winner. Both are legitimate. The meaningful differentiators are dose (higher doses from either compound produce more NAD+ elevation), delivery form (liposomal formulations may improve bioavailability), and consistency of use. Women should prioritize a reliable, quality-tested product at an adequate dose over endless comparison between precursor types.

Who This Is NOT For

Women with active cancer should discuss NAD+ precursor supplementation with their oncologist. NAD+ plays roles in both normal cellular repair and, theoretically, in supporting rapidly dividing cells. The clinical evidence in cancer patients is mixed and context-dependent; this is a conversation that requires physician guidance.

Women who are pregnant or breastfeeding should not supplement with NR, NMN, or NMNH without physician guidance, as safety data in these populations is limited.

Women who expect dramatic short-term energy changes similar to caffeine will likely be disappointed. NAD+ precursors work through gradual cellular restoration over weeks to months, not acute energy stimulation. If acute energy is the goal, the mechanism does not match the expectation.

Women looking for a single supplement to address all perimenopause symptoms will find that NAD+ precursors address the cellular energy and resilience aspects but do not directly target hot flashes, vaginal changes, or bone density. A comprehensive perimenopause protocol requires additional targeted supplements for those specific symptom clusters.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is NR or NMN better for energy?

Both compounds raise NAD+, and raised NAD+ supports mitochondrial function which underlies cellular energy production. The available evidence does not show that one form produces more energy benefit than the other at comparable doses. Individual variation in response is likely large. Most women who notice energy improvements report them after four to eight weeks of consistent use, not in the first days.

Does TruNiagen really have better research than NMN brands?

TruNiagen (NR) has a larger published clinical trial base accumulated since 2016, much of it funded by ChromaDex (TruNiagen's parent company). The research is genuine and NR's safety and efficacy are well-established. NMN's research base has grown substantially since 2019 and is catching up. Neither brand's research base should be interpreted as proving that their compound is superior; the actual head-to-head human comparison data does not support a superiority claim for either.

Should I take NMN in the morning or evening?

Most clinical trials that show positive effects administered NMN in the morning, which aligns with the natural NAD+ synthesis cycle that follows circadian rhythms. Morning supplementation is the convention, though the evidence specifically comparing morning versus evening timing in women over 40 is limited. Consistency of timing matters more than perfect timing.

How long until I feel a difference from NMN or NR?

Blood NAD+ levels typically rise within two to four weeks of consistent supplementation, based on pharmacokinetic studies. Functional effects (energy, cognitive clarity, exercise recovery) may take four to twelve weeks to become noticeable, and some women notice primarily a maintenance of function rather than dramatic improvement, which is mechanistically consistent with gradual cellular restoration. Give any NAD+ precursor protocol at least 90 days of consistent use before evaluating results.

Can I take NMN and NR together?

There is no known safety concern with combining NR and NMN. Since they partially overlap in their metabolic pathways, there may also be limited additive benefit compared to simply taking a higher dose of one form. The more established approach in the research literature is to use one form at an adequate dose rather than stacking two forms at lower doses each. If you are considering a combination, discuss the specific formulation and rationale with a healthcare provider.
