Most women who pay close attention to their nutrition have heard about vitamins, omega-3s, and magnesium. Far fewer know that choline is one of the most important nutrients for brain health and liver function, and that the vast majority of women over 40 consume less than the recommended amount. Choline was officially recognized as an essential nutrient by the Institute of Medicine in 1998, yet it still does not appear on standard nutrition labels in many countries and receives almost no attention in mainstream health messaging. This gap has real consequences, particularly for cognitive function as women navigate the brain changes of midlife.

What to Know

  • Choline is an essential nutrient that the body cannot produce in sufficient quantities, requiring regular dietary intake
  • It is the direct dietary precursor to acetylcholine, the brain's primary memory and attention neurotransmitter
  • Studies show that more than 90% of Americans do not meet the recommended adequate intake (AI) for choline
  • Women's choline needs increase after 40 as estrogen decline reduces the body's ability to synthesize choline internally
  • The richest food sources are egg yolks, beef liver, fish, and cruciferous vegetables

What Choline Is and What It Does

Choline is a water-soluble nutrient that belongs to the B-vitamin family group, though it is technically not a vitamin. It was classified as an essential nutrient after researchers established that humans develop fatty liver, muscle damage, and cognitive impairment when deprived of dietary choline for extended periods.

Choline serves several distinct and critical functions in the body. First and most relevant for brain health: it is the direct precursor to acetylcholine. Neurons in the basal forebrain, hippocampus, and cortex take up choline from the bloodstream and convert it to acetylcholine using the enzyme choline acetyltransferase. Without adequate dietary choline, the raw material for this synthesis is limited, and acetylcholine production falls.

Second, choline is a component of phosphatidylcholine, the dominant phospholipid in cell membranes. Every cell in the body requires phosphatidylcholine for membrane structure and signaling. The brain is disproportionately dependent on adequate phosphatidylcholine for maintaining the integrity of neuronal membranes and the efficiency of synaptic communication.

Third, choline is essential for liver fat metabolism. Phosphatidylcholine is required to package fat for export from the liver as VLDL particles. Without adequate choline, fat accumulates in liver cells, which is the mechanism behind non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) in the absence of alcohol consumption. This connection between choline deficiency and liver health becomes increasingly relevant after 40, when metabolic changes increase fatty liver risk in women.

Fourth, choline donates methyl groups in the methylation cycle, contributing to gene expression regulation, DNA repair, and the conversion of homocysteine (a cardiovascular risk marker) to methionine.

Why Women Over 40 Need More Choline

The official adequate intake (AI) for choline is 425mg per day for women, but this figure was established with the assumption that the body can synthesize a portion of its choline needs endogenously via the PEMT enzyme pathway. The PEMT pathway, however, is directly induced by estrogen. As estrogen declines in perimenopause and postmenopause, endogenous choline synthesis falls substantially.

A 2007 study by Fischer and colleagues published in the American Journal of Clinical Nutrition demonstrated that postmenopausal women developed signs of organ dysfunction (elevated liver enzymes and muscle damage markers) at choline intakes that were adequate for premenopausal women. Postmenopausal women in the study required significantly higher dietary choline to avoid deficiency signs, suggesting that the AI recommendation may be insufficient for women in the second half of life.

Beyond the hormonal change, many dietary patterns common among health-conscious women after 40 inadvertently reduce choline intake. Vegan and plant-based diets eliminate the richest choline sources (egg yolks, liver, meat). Egg-white-only eating habits eliminate the yolk where 90% of the choline resides. Low-fat diets reduce intake of fat-soluble choline sources. The result is a population of women who are simultaneously at higher choline need and lower choline intake.

Choline and Cognitive Function: What the Evidence Shows

The connection between dietary choline and cognitive health is supported by both observational population studies and intervention trials.

A large analysis of the Framingham Heart Study offspring cohort found that higher dietary choline intake was associated with better performance on memory and verbal learning tests, as well as reduced white matter hyperintensity volume on brain MRI, a marker of vascular brain aging. The association held after controlling for overall diet quality, suggesting choline specifically contributed to the cognitive benefit.

A 2018 epidemiological analysis of over 2,500 adults found that those in the highest quartile of choline intake had significantly better verbal and visual memory test scores compared to those in the lowest quartile. The cognitive advantage was most pronounced in adults over 50.

Intervention trials with choline supplementation (in the form of alpha-GPC or citicoline) consistently show improvements in memory, attention, and processing speed in older adults and in people with mild cognitive impairment, confirming that increasing choline availability has functional cognitive effects.

Best Food Sources of Choline After 40

Reaching the 425mg per day adequate intake, let alone the potentially higher needs of postmenopausal women, requires intentional food choices.

Egg yolks: 147mg per large egg yolk. Two whole eggs daily provides approximately 295mg.

Beef liver: 356mg per 100g, the most concentrated food source. Monthly inclusion can meaningfully contribute to choline status.

Chicken liver: Approximately 220mg per 100g, more approachable than beef liver for most people.

Salmon: Approximately 90mg per 100g, with the added benefit of EPA and DHA.

Shrimp: Approximately 80mg per 100g.

Broccoli: 40mg per cup cooked.

Brussels sprouts: 32mg per cup cooked.

Soybeans (edamame): Approximately 70mg per cup.

A diet that includes two whole eggs daily, two servings of fish per week, and regular cruciferous vegetables can approach 400mg per day without supplementation. Women on plant-based diets will have more difficulty reaching adequate levels from food alone.

Choline Supplements: When Food Is Not Enough

For women who do not regularly eat eggs, liver, or fatty fish, supplemental choline may be the most practical way to address a dietary gap that is affecting brain health.

Choline bitartrate is the most common and least expensive form. It raises blood choline levels but has modest brain-penetrating ability compared to other forms.

Alpha-GPC is the form with the strongest evidence for brain-specific effects. It crosses the blood-brain barrier efficiently and delivers choline directly to cholinergic neurons. Clinical trials showing cognitive benefits used 300-600mg/day of alpha-GPC.

Citicoline (CDP-choline) provides choline plus cytidine and has robust clinical evidence for memory and attention improvement in older adults at 250-500mg/day.

Taking any form of choline with food containing fat improves absorption. Women who take choline supplements and experience headaches (a known side effect of choline excess) should reduce the dose, as headaches indicate excessive cholinergic activity.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is choline the same as vitamin B?

Choline is often grouped with the B vitamins because it is water-soluble and functions in related metabolic pathways (including the methylation cycle), but it is not technically classified as a vitamin. It was recognized as an essential nutrient by the Institute of Medicine in 1998, separate from the established B-vitamin classifications. Some researchers and practitioners use the term "vitamin B4" informally for choline, but this is not official nomenclature.

Can you get enough choline from a vegan diet?

It is possible but requires careful planning. The richest choline sources are animal products (egg yolks, liver, fish). Vegan sources include soybeans, sunflower seeds, wheat germ, and cruciferous vegetables, but reaching 425mg per day from plant sources alone is challenging without deliberate meal planning. Postmenopausal women following vegan diets are at particularly high risk of choline insufficiency and may benefit most from choline supplementation.

What happens if you are choline deficient?

Clinical choline deficiency produces fatty liver (hepatic steatosis), muscle damage, and cognitive impairment. Subclinical insufficiency, which is far more common than frank deficiency, is associated with poorer memory performance, higher cardiovascular risk (through elevated homocysteine), and greater susceptibility to NAFLD. Most women in Western countries are in the insufficiency range rather than clinical deficiency range.

How does choline interact with other B vitamins?

Choline works closely with folate, vitamin B12, and betaine in the methylation cycle. They share the role of donating methyl groups for DNA methylation, gene regulation, and homocysteine conversion. Low folate or B12 increases the demand on choline for methylation, making adequate intake of all these nutrients together more important than optimizing any single one.

Does cooking destroy choline in foods?

Choline is relatively heat-stable in most cooking methods. Boiling eggs reduces choline somewhat due to leaching into water. Scrambling, poaching, or frying eggs preserves more choline. Cooking liver or fish at moderate temperatures (not excessive charring) preserves most of the choline content. In general, normal home cooking does not dramatically reduce choline content compared to raw foods.

How does choline affect homocysteine levels?

Choline donates methyl groups in the methylation cycle, where it contributes alongside folate, B12, and betaine to the conversion of homocysteine to methionine. Elevated homocysteine is an independent cardiovascular risk marker and is also associated with cognitive decline. Adequate choline intake ensures sufficient methyl group donation to keep homocysteine in the normal range. Women with MTHFR gene variants that reduce folate-mediated methylation efficiency may be particularly dependent on choline as an alternative methyl donor, making choline status more critical for this subgroup.

Is there a test for choline deficiency?

There is no widely standardized clinical blood test for choline status specifically, though some functional medicine labs measure plasma choline or phosphatidylcholine as part of a broader nutritional panel. More indirectly, elevated liver enzymes (ALT, AST) in the absence of other causes can reflect choline insufficiency affecting liver fat metabolism. Elevated homocysteine with adequate B12 and folate may also suggest methyl group insufficiency that could involve choline. The most practical approach for most women is to assess dietary intake against the adequate intake target (425mg/day) and correct any gap through diet or supplementation before deficiency signs develop. Tracking dietary choline with a food logging app for 3-7 days reveals most women's typical intake and makes the gap visible and actionable.
