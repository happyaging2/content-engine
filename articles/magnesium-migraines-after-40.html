Migraines are one of the most debilitating health conditions affecting women, and they are three times more common in women than men, with hormonal fluctuations playing a central role in why. After 40, as estrogen levels become erratic in perimenopause, many women notice that their migraine pattern changes: attacks may become more frequent, more unpredictable, and harder to manage. Magnesium has emerged as one of the most thoroughly studied and clinically recommended prevention strategies for migraines, with evidence strong enough that the American Academy of Neurology and the American Headache Society both include magnesium in their official migraine prevention guidelines. Here is what the science shows and how to use it effectively.

What to Know

  • Magnesium deficiency is found in up to 50 percent of migraine sufferers and is significantly more common in migraine patients than in headache-free controls
  • Magnesium prevents migraines through multiple mechanisms: reducing cortical spreading depression (the wave of neural activity underlying aura), regulating serotonin receptors, and reducing NMDA receptor-mediated pain sensitization
  • The American Academy of Neurology rates magnesium as a Level B evidence migraine preventive, meaning it is "probably effective" based on multiple randomized trials
  • Hormonal migraines tied to menstrual cycles and perimenopause respond particularly well to magnesium, likely because estrogen regulates magnesium retention in cells
  • The dose used in clinical trials (400 to 600mg of magnesium oxide or glycinate daily) is higher than standard dietary intake; supplementation is typically needed to achieve the preventive dose

The Magnesium-Migraine Connection: Why It Works

Magnesium is involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions in the body, but its relevance to migraines is most concentrated in three specific neurological mechanisms.

Cortical spreading depression (CSD): migraines with aura are caused by a wave of electrical activity and suppression that moves across the cortex, the brain's outer layer. This wave, called cortical spreading depression, requires activation of NMDA glutamate receptors. Magnesium is a natural NMDA receptor blocker: when magnesium is present in adequate amounts in the extracellular space, it physically occupies the receptor's channel and prevents glutamate from activating it fully. Low brain magnesium allows NMDA receptors to activate more easily, lowering the threshold for CSD initiation. Studies using brain spectroscopy have found that magnesium levels in the occipital cortex (the visual processing center) are lower in migraine patients, particularly during and between attacks.

Serotonin receptor modulation: serotonin (5-HT) dysregulation is a core feature of migraine pathophysiology. Serotonin levels drop sharply at the onset of a migraine, and the triptan medications (sumatriptan and others) that abort migraines work by activating 5-HT1B and 5-HT1D receptors. Magnesium modulates serotonin receptor function and influences the synthesis and release of serotonin; low magnesium may contribute to the serotonin instability that triggers migraine initiation.

Platelet aggregation and vasoconstriction: magnesium reduces platelet aggregation and inhibits vasoconstriction in cerebral arteries. Both platelet aggregation (releasing vasoactive substances including serotonin and thromboxane A2) and cerebral vasoconstriction are implicated in migraine initiation, and magnesium's inhibitory effects on both reduce the vascular component of migraine pathophysiology.

Clinical Evidence for Magnesium in Migraine Prevention

Multiple randomized controlled trials have tested oral magnesium supplementation for migraine prevention, and the evidence is consistently positive for prophylactic (preventive) effects.

A landmark 1996 randomized trial by Peikert and colleagues published in Cephalalgia enrolled 81 migraine patients and found that 600mg of magnesium citrate daily over 12 weeks reduced migraine frequency by 41.6 percent compared to 15.8 percent reduction in the placebo group. The difference was statistically significant, with the greatest effects observed in the second and third months of supplementation, consistent with the time needed to restore brain magnesium stores.

A 1995 trial by Pfaffenrath and colleagues, also using 600mg/day of magnesium, found similar prevention rates but noted that the benefits were concentrated in the subgroup of patients who had low baseline magnesium levels in blood mononuclear cells, suggesting that testing magnesium status before supplementing may identify who benefits most.

A 2004 systematic review for the American Academy of Neurology reviewed three controlled trials of magnesium and found consistent evidence for prophylactic efficacy, concluding that magnesium is "probably effective" for migraine prevention. This Level B evidence designation puts magnesium on par with several prescription preventive medications in terms of evidence strength.

Hormonal Migraines and Perimenopause: Why This Matters After 40

The hormonal connection between migraines and the perimenopausal transition is significant and specific. Estrogen promotes cellular magnesium retention; as estrogen falls and fluctuates in perimenopause, magnesium tends to be excreted more readily, reducing intracellular magnesium stores. This creates a mechanistic link between estrogen decline and increased migraine susceptibility that goes beyond the direct effects of hormone fluctuation on neurovascular tone.

Menstrual migraines, which occur in the days before and during menstruation when estrogen drops sharply, are one of the most treatment-resistant forms of migraine. A controlled trial specifically in menstrual migraine found that magnesium pyrrolidone carboxylate significantly reduced the duration and intensity of menstrual migraines compared to placebo, with the benefit particularly pronounced in the perimenstrual window.

The pattern of increasingly frequent migraines in the 40s, often preceding the more dramatic hormonal shifts of actual menopause, is a recognized clinical phenomenon called "perimenopause-associated migraine." Magnesium supplementation's ability to stabilize the neurological sensitivity (through NMDA blocking and serotonin modulation) that hormone fluctuations destabilize makes it a particularly rational prevention strategy during this transition.

Choosing the Right Form and Dose of Magnesium

Not all magnesium supplements are created equal, and the form matters for both effectiveness and gastrointestinal tolerance.

The trials showing migraine prevention used primarily magnesium citrate or magnesium oxide at 400 to 600mg per day. Magnesium oxide has the highest elemental magnesium content per gram but the lowest bioavailability and the most pronounced laxative effect at migraine-prevention doses. Magnesium citrate has intermediate bioavailability and is the most commonly recommended general-purpose form.

Magnesium glycinate (magnesium bound to glycine) has the highest bioavailability and the gentlest gastrointestinal profile: it is the best-tolerated form at higher doses and is therefore often preferred for migraine prevention where doses in the 400 to 600mg range are needed without causing diarrhea. Magnesium threonate is a newer form with demonstrated ability to raise brain magnesium specifically (unlike other forms that primarily raise peripheral magnesium) and may offer additional cognitive benefits alongside migraine prevention, though it has not been tested in migraine-specific trials.

Consistency of supplementation matters more than the exact timing. Taking magnesium daily, with food to improve tolerance, is more important than taking it at a specific time. The migraine prevention trials ran for 12 weeks before showing full benefit, suggesting that building and maintaining brain and tissue magnesium stores over time is the mechanism, not any acute effect of a single dose.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does magnesium take to reduce migraines?

The clinical trials showing migraine prevention ran for 12 weeks, with the greatest benefit accumulating in the second and third months. This timeline reflects the time needed to replenish tissue magnesium stores that have been depleted. Some women report improvements within 4 to 6 weeks, but the full preventive benefit typically requires consistent supplementation for 8 to 12 weeks. This is one of the reasons magnesium is often started as a 3-month trial in clinical practice before evaluating its effectiveness.

Can you take magnesium with migraine medications?

Yes, magnesium is generally safe to combine with both acute migraine treatments (triptans, NSAIDs, anti-nausea medications) and other preventive medications (topiramate, beta-blockers, amitriptyline). IV magnesium sulfate is actually used in emergency settings to treat status migrainosus (a prolonged, refractory migraine attack), confirming that magnesium is considered safe even in therapeutic clinical contexts. Inform your neurologist or physician about all supplements you take; they may be able to adjust your overall migraine prevention protocol based on your magnesium status.

Does magnesium help with migraines that already started?

Primarily, magnesium's evidence is for prevention rather than acute treatment. However, some people find that taking additional magnesium at the very early onset of a migraine (the prodrome phase, before the headache peak) may reduce severity. IV magnesium is used acutely in some clinical settings with evidence of benefit. Oral magnesium at acute onset is not well-studied as an abortive therapy, but given its excellent safety profile and plausible mechanism (NMDA blockade reducing pain sensitization), it is worth trying alongside your regular acute treatment during the first signs of an attack.

Should women over 40 get their magnesium levels tested?

Standard serum magnesium testing is not very informative: serum magnesium represents less than 1 percent of total body magnesium and is tightly regulated even when total body and tissue stores are significantly depleted. Red blood cell (RBC) magnesium or magnesium retention testing after a loading dose are more accurate measures of tissue magnesium status. For practical purposes, most integrative physicians recommend a therapeutic trial of magnesium supplementation for migraine prevention without requiring testing first, given its safety profile and the high prevalence of suboptimal magnesium status in women over 40.

What foods are highest in magnesium?

Dark leafy greens (spinach, Swiss chard), pumpkin seeds, almonds, cashews, dark chocolate (70 percent or higher), legumes (black beans, edamame), whole grains, and avocado are the most magnesium-dense commonly available foods. A half cup of cooked spinach provides about 78mg; one ounce of pumpkin seeds provides 150mg; one ounce of almonds provides 77mg. The daily adequate intake for women over 30 is 320mg; the doses used in migraine prevention trials (400 to 600mg) exceed this baseline, explaining why supplementation is typically needed to achieve preventive therapeutic levels through diet alone.

Can magnesium help with other perimenopausal symptoms beyond migraines?

Yes. Magnesium's mechanisms overlap with multiple perimenopausal symptoms. Its GABA-modulating and HPA axis-calming effects reduce anxiety and improve sleep quality, both of which are commonly disrupted in perimenopause. Magnesium deficiency is associated with increased muscle cramps and restless legs, common complaints in midlife women. Magnesium also plays a role in insulin sensitivity and glucose metabolism, relevant for the metabolic changes of perimenopause. Additionally, some research suggests that adequate magnesium reduces inflammatory markers and supports bone density maintenance, addressing two other priorities for women over 40. For women with migraines who are also experiencing perimenopausal anxiety, sleep disruption, or muscle symptoms, magnesium supplementation provides multi-target support through a single intervention with an excellent safety profile.
