Butyrate is a short-chain fatty acid produced by bacteria in the colon when they ferment dietary fiber, and it is one of the most important molecules in the body that most people have never heard of. It serves as the primary fuel for colonocytes (the cells lining the colon), supports gut barrier integrity, reduces intestinal inflammation, and, increasingly relevant to midlife women, communicates with the brain through multiple pathways that affect mood, cognition, and neuroinflammation. After 40, butyrate production often declines as the microbiome shifts and fiber intake decreases, making it one of the key mechanisms behind the gut-brain connection that affects how women feel mentally and emotionally during perimenopause and beyond.

What Butyrate Is and How It Is Made

Butyrate (butyric acid) is a four-carbon short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) produced through bacterial fermentation of dietary fiber in the colon. The primary butyrate-producing bacteria include Faecalibacterium prausnitzii, Roseburia intestinalis, Eubacterium rectale, and Butyrivibrio fibrisolvens, all members of the Firmicutes phylum. These bacteria ferment resistant starch, soluble fiber, pectin, and inulin through a metabolic pathway that produces butyrate as a major end product alongside acetate and propionate (the other two primary SCFAs).

The colon depends on butyrate as its preferred fuel source: colonocytes (the epithelial cells lining the colon) obtain approximately 70 percent of their energy from butyrate oxidation. This makes butyrate availability directly tied to the structural health of the gut lining. When butyrate production falls, colonocyte energy supply is compromised, tight junction proteins that maintain gut barrier integrity weaken, and the gut becomes more permeable to bacterial products including lipopolysaccharide (LPS), an inflammatory molecule that should stay inside the gut but increasingly leaks through a compromised barrier.

After 40, butyrate production tends to decline for several converging reasons: reduced dietary fiber intake (highly common in Western diets), changes in microbiome composition that reduce the relative abundance of Firmicutes butyrate producers, increased intestinal transit time (which shifts fermentation dynamics), and gut microbiome disruptions from antibiotics, stress hormones, or medications accumulated over decades.

How Butyrate Communicates with the Brain

The gut-brain axis has multiple channels through which gut conditions influence brain function, and butyrate plays a role in several of them simultaneously.

The vagus nerve channel: butyrate stimulates enteroendocrine cells (specialized gut cells) to release signaling peptides including peptide YY, glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1), and other gut hormones that signal the brain via the vagus nerve. These signals influence appetite, mood, anxiety, and the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, which regulates the stress response. In this way, gut butyrate production directly influences how the brain experiences and regulates stress.

The serotonin channel: approximately 90 to 95 percent of the body's total serotonin is produced in the gut by enterochromaffin cells. Butyrate stimulates serotonin biosynthesis by upregulating tryptophan hydroxylase 1 (TPH1), the rate-limiting enzyme in serotonin synthesis from dietary tryptophan. Because gut serotonin also signals to the brain (through vagal afferents and through serotonin spillover to the bloodstream), butyrate production has a measurable effect on the serotonergic tone that influences mood, anxiety, and cognitive function.

The direct brain channel: butyrate crosses the blood-brain barrier (particularly when circulating butyrate levels are elevated) and functions in the brain as a histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibitor. HDAC inhibition is an epigenetic mechanism that opens chromatin and allows increased gene expression at specific loci. In the brain, butyrate's HDAC inhibition increases the expression of BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor), the neuroplasticity protein most critical for memory and learning. This connection between gut fiber fermentation and brain BDNF production through butyrate is one of the most compelling mechanisms linking diet to cognition.

Butyrate and Neuroinflammation After 40

Neuroinflammation, chronic low-grade inflammation within the brain, is increasingly recognized as a driver of both mood disorders and cognitive decline in aging adults. Butyrate production is one of the key factors that determines whether neuroinflammation is kept in check.

When gut permeability increases from low butyrate production, LPS and other bacterial products enter the bloodstream in higher concentrations. These microbial products activate systemic inflammation through Toll-like receptor 4 signaling, and this systemic inflammation crosses the blood-brain barrier and activates microglia (the brain's immune cells). Chronically activated microglia produce pro-inflammatory cytokines including IL-1beta and TNF-alpha that directly impair neuronal function, reduce BDNF, and contribute to depressive symptoms.

Conversely, butyrate directly reduces microglial activation through its HDAC inhibition and anti-inflammatory properties. Animal studies have shown that butyrate supplementation reduces hippocampal neuroinflammation and improves memory performance in aged rodents. While direct human neuroinflammation data is limited, the mechanistic chain from butyrate to gut permeability to systemic inflammation to neuroinflammation is well-supported across these levels of evidence.

For women over 40, who often experience increasing systemic inflammation as estrogen declines (estrogen has significant anti-inflammatory effects), protecting butyrate production is one of the key dietary strategies for keeping neuroinflammation in check.

How to Increase Butyrate Naturally

The most direct way to increase butyrate production is to consistently feed the butyrate-producing bacteria the substrates they need. Resistant starch is the most potent butyrate precursor: it is found in cooked and cooled potatoes and rice (cooling converts some digestible starch to resistant starch), green bananas, oats, and legumes. Soluble fiber from sources like oats, barley, psyllium husk, and fruits supports butyrate production through a different fermentation pathway.

Diversity of plant foods drives microbiome diversity, which in turn supports the resilience of the butyrate-producing community. Eating 30 or more different plant foods per week (a target from gut microbiome research showing this threshold produces significantly more diverse microbiomes) provides a range of fiber structures that nourish different fermentation communities.

Tributyrin is a directly supplementable form of butyrate that is more stable than butyrate sodium (which has a strong odor) and releases butyrate in the colon where it is needed. Research on tributyrin supplementation shows increases in colon butyrate availability and improvements in gut barrier markers. Probiotics containing butyrate-producing strains (Faecalibacterium prausnitzii is difficult to deliver as a probiotic due to its oxygen sensitivity, but other Roseburia and Eubacterium strains are becoming available) may support butyrate production when combined with prebiotic fiber.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does low butyrate feel like?

There is no single symptom that definitively signals low butyrate, but patterns associated with reduced butyrate production include bloating and digestive discomfort without a clear food trigger, loose stools or irregular bowel movements, recurring gut inflammation, persistent low mood or anxiety without obvious cause, brain fog, and elevated inflammatory markers on blood tests. These symptoms overlap with many conditions, but if multiple are present alongside a low-fiber diet, addressing gut fiber intake and microbiome support is a reasonable first step.

Can you test your butyrate levels?

Stool butyrate measurement is available through some functional medicine laboratories and is more relevant than blood butyrate (which is metabolized quickly by colonocytes and rarely reflects gut butyrate production status). However, stool butyrate testing is not standardized across labs and the clinical interpretation is limited by lack of consensus reference ranges. Measuring symptoms alongside dietary fiber intake is a practical functional approach when formal testing is not available or accessible.

Does butyrate supplementation work as well as producing it naturally?

Direct butyrate supplementation (as tributyrin or butyrate salts) can bypass the need for bacterial fermentation and deliver butyrate directly to the colon. However, natural fermentation-derived butyrate also produces acetate and propionate alongside butyrate, which have their own beneficial effects on gut and brain health. Additionally, the bacteria that produce butyrate also produce other beneficial metabolites through the fermentation process. Supplementation is a useful adjunct when fiber intake is insufficient, but it does not fully replicate the ecosystem-level benefits of thriving butyrate-producing communities fueled by diverse dietary fiber.

How does stress affect butyrate production?

Stress reduces butyrate production through multiple mechanisms. Cortisol reduces gut motility and changes the pH of the colon in ways that favor pathogenic bacteria over the Firmicutes butyrate producers. Stress also reduces appetite and changes food choices in ways that reduce fiber intake. Additionally, stress increases gut permeability directly through mast cell activation and cortisol effects on tight junctions, creating a vicious cycle: stress reduces butyrate, which reduces gut barrier integrity, which increases LPS-driven systemic inflammation, which activates the HPA stress axis and raises cortisol further.

Is butyrate the same as ghee?

Not exactly, though there is a connection. Ghee is clarified butter that contains tributyrin, a triglyceride form of butyrate that is found naturally in dairy fat. When consumed, tributyrin is hydrolyzed in the small intestine, releasing butyrate that can then be absorbed. However, the butyrate from dietary fat is absorbed in the small intestine before reaching the colon, so it does not contribute to colon butyrate levels the same way that fermentation-derived butyrate does. Tributyrin in supplement form is formulated to deliver butyrate specifically to the colon. The butyrate from ghee is not the same as the fermentation-derived butyrate that fuels colonocytes and supports gut-brain signaling.

How do I know if my butyrate production is low?

There is no mainstream clinical test for gut butyrate levels, but the pattern of symptoms provides useful indirect evidence. Low butyrate production is associated with an inflamed, permeable gut lining, so symptoms like bloating after high-fiber meals (Firmicutes bacteria should be fermenting that fiber productively), irregular bowel habits that do not clearly improve with dietary changes, increased food sensitivities, and persistent brain fog or mood changes alongside digestive symptoms all suggest a microbiome that may not be producing adequate butyrate. Comprehensive stool analysis from functional medicine laboratories can measure short-chain fatty acid profiles including butyrate directly, and can characterize the relative abundance of butyrate-producing bacteria like Faecalibacterium prausnitzii and Roseburia. For most women, the practical starting point is increasing diverse fiber intake and monitoring symptoms over 4 to 8 weeks rather than waiting for testing, since the intervention for low butyrate and the dietary foundation for gut health are the same regardless of specific butyrate measurement.
