Urolithin A is one of the most scientifically interesting longevity compounds of the past decade. It is not a supplement you eat directly. It is a molecule your gut bacteria make from compounds in pomegranate, walnuts, and certain berries. And for women over 40 whose mitochondria are under increasing metabolic stress, it is worth understanding what the research actually shows.

What to Know Before You Read
  • Urolithin A is a gut-produced metabolite made from ellagitannins, plant compounds found in pomegranate, walnuts, and berries.
  • It activates mitophagy: the process by which cells identify and clear out damaged mitochondria to maintain cellular energy efficiency.
  • Only about 30 to 40 percent of people produce urolithin A from dietary sources. This depends on which gut bacteria you have.
  • Supplementing urolithin A bypasses the gut conversion step, making it accessible even to non-producers.
  • A 2019 Nature Metabolism study showed urolithin A supplementation was safe in older adults and produced a molecular signature of improved mitochondrial health.
  • Urolithin A does not treat any disease. It supports cellular cleanup pathways associated with healthy mitochondrial function.

What Is Urolithin A?

Urolithin A is a compound your gut bacteria produce when you consume ellagitannins. Ellagitannins are a class of polyphenols found primarily in pomegranate, walnuts, and berries like raspberries and strawberries. Your gut microbiome converts ellagic acid (from ellagitannins) into urolithins, with urolithin A being the primary active form.

Here is the important part: this conversion only happens if you have the right gut bacteria. Research suggests that only about 30 to 40 percent of people are what scientists call "urolithin producers." The rest either cannot produce urolithin A at all or produce it at such low levels that there is no meaningful biological effect. This means that eating more pomegranate is not a reliable strategy for most people. Supplementing urolithin A directly bypasses this conversion dependency entirely.

Once urolithin A is in your system, it activates a cellular process called mitophagy. This is the specific type of autophagy (cellular self-cleaning) that targets mitochondria. Damaged mitochondria that cannot be repaired are tagged, broken down, and recycled. New, functional mitochondria take their place. This is not a trendy concept. It is a fundamental maintenance process for keeping your cells energetically healthy.

Why Mitochondrial Health Matters for Women Over 40

Mitochondria are the organelles in your cells that generate ATP, which is cellular energy. They are also the primary source of reactive oxygen species (free radicals) produced as a byproduct of energy generation. When mitochondria age and become damaged, they produce more reactive oxygen species and less ATP. The result is lower energy output and higher oxidative stress at the same time.

After 40, this mitochondrial dysfunction accelerates in women for several reasons. First, the general aging process reduces mitophagy efficiency, so damaged mitochondria accumulate faster than they are cleared. Second, as estrogen declines in perimenopause, some of the estrogen-mediated mitochondrial protection weakens. Estrogen has been shown in research to support mitochondrial biogenesis (the creation of new mitochondria) and to reduce oxidative damage in mitochondria. As estrogen falls, that support decreases.

The result is a pattern familiar to many women in their 40s: fatigue that doesn't fully resolve with sleep, slower recovery from exercise, and a general sense that the body's energy systems are less efficient than they used to be. These are not imagined symptoms. They have a biological substrate in mitochondrial health.

What the Research Says

The most significant human trial on urolithin A supplementation is a 2019 study by Andreux et al. published in Nature Metabolism (PMID: 31285545). The study enrolled healthy older adults aged 65 to 80 and gave them either urolithin A or a placebo. The urolithin A group showed a significant increase in gene expression signatures associated with mitochondrial health and cellular biogenesis. The product was well-tolerated with no significant safety concerns at the doses tested.

This is meaningful data for several reasons. It is a human trial, not an animal study. It used biomarkers directly related to mitochondrial health. And it used a clinically relevant population (older adults) rather than young, healthy volunteers.

Earlier preclinical research by Ryu et al. published in Nature Medicine in 2016 showed that urolithin A induced mitophagy and extended lifespan in the organism C. elegans, and also improved muscle function in rodents (PMID: 27548523). While animal studies cannot be directly translated to human outcomes, this study established the mechanistic basis for urolithin A's mitophagy-activating effects and spurred the human research that followed.

A 2022 randomized controlled trial published in JAMA Network Open found that older adults taking urolithin A supplementation showed improvements in muscle endurance, specifically in hand grip strength tests and in the number of muscle mitochondria measured by muscle biopsy. Research suggests this may reflect urolithin A's ability to support muscle mitochondrial quality over time.

According to Happy Aging's review of the current literature on mitophagy and women's longevity, the combination of urolithin A with NAD+ pathway support may address two complementary aspects of mitochondrial health: NAD+ supports mitochondrial function by fueling the enzymes that run cellular energy systems, while urolithin A supports mitochondrial quality control by clearing out damaged mitochondria so only functional ones remain.

What the Evidence Does Not Support

Urolithin A has not been proven to reverse muscle loss in humans or to dramatically improve athletic performance in healthy adults. The published trials enrolled older adults, not active middle-aged women, and the outcomes measured were biomarkers and modest functional tests, not dramatic strength gains or body composition transformations. Expecting urolithin A to serve as a performance enhancer beyond its cellular maintenance role would overstate the current evidence.

The gut producer vs. non-producer distinction is real, but it is sometimes overstated in supplement marketing. While non-producers cannot generate urolithin A from dietary ellagitannins, producers may still benefit from supplemental urolithin A at doses higher than what diet alone provides. The gut conversion capacity varies widely even among producers. Supplementation is useful for both groups, though the relative benefit may differ.

Long-term human safety data on urolithin A supplementation extends to approximately two years in published research. It appears safe at studied doses, but lifelong supplementation at higher doses has not been studied. Using urolithin A at manufacturer-recommended doses and monitoring with your physician is the appropriate approach.

Happy Aging's position: The evidence for urolithin A's role in mitophagy and mitochondrial health is among the most compelling in longevity science for a single compound. The 2019 Nature Metabolism trial and 2022 JAMA Network Open trial represent a solid human evidence foundation. We support urolithin A as part of a comprehensive mitochondrial health strategy, alongside NAD+ precursors and lifestyle fundamentals. We do not overstate the evidence or claim this addresses any disease.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I get enough urolithin A from eating pomegranate?

Only if you are one of the 30 to 40 percent of people who can convert ellagitannins into urolithin A through your gut microbiome. Even then, the amount you would produce from typical dietary intake may be lower than the doses used in clinical trials. Supplementing urolithin A directly provides a consistent, measurable dose without depending on gut conversion capacity. If you love pomegranate, keep eating it. But don't rely on it as your primary urolithin A strategy.

How does urolithin A compare to other longevity supplements like NMN or spermidine?

These compounds work through different mechanisms and are not directly comparable. Urolithin A specifically activates mitophagy to clear damaged mitochondria. NMN raises NAD+ levels to fuel mitochondrial enzymes. Spermidine activates broader autophagy pathways. They address different but related aspects of cellular aging and are increasingly considered complementary rather than competing options in longevity protocols.

Is urolithin A safe for long-term use?

The 2019 Nature Metabolism trial (PMID: 31285545) established the safety and tolerability of urolithin A in older adults over several weeks. A subsequent 4-month randomized controlled trial also found no significant safety concerns. Long-term data beyond approximately two years of supplementation is limited. Urolithin A appears well-tolerated at studied doses. Discuss long-term supplementation plans with your physician.

How quickly might I notice a difference from urolithin A?

The 2019 human trial measured molecular changes at 4 weeks. Functional outcomes like muscle endurance may take longer to manifest. Most practitioners suggest evaluating supplements like urolithin A over a 90-day period to allow time for cellular processes to shift. Subjective changes in energy or recovery speed are reported by some users, but the scientific measurement of outcomes requires longer timeframes than most people expect.
