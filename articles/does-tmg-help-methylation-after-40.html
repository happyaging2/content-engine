TMG (trimethylglycine, also called betaine) supports methylation by donating methyl groups that help regulate homocysteine levels and support the NAD+ recycling pathway, making it a relevant compound for women over 40 who are focused on cardiovascular health and cellular longevity.

Methylation sounds technical, but it touches nearly every system in the body: gene expression, neurotransmitter production, detoxification, and cellular energy recycling. As of 2026, TMG has become one of the most-discussed additions to NAD+ and longevity protocols, and for good reason. Here is what the evidence actually shows about whether it lives up to that interest.

What to Know Before Reading
  • TMG (trimethylglycine) is found naturally in beets, spinach, and whole grains. It is sometimes called betaine.
  • Its primary role is as a methyl donor in the one-carbon metabolism cycle, which influences homocysteine regulation, DNA methylation, and neurotransmitter synthesis.
  • Elevated homocysteine is associated with cardiovascular risk and cognitive decline. TMG may support homocysteine reduction.
  • TMG also supports the methylation pathway that recycles NAD+, making it relevant alongside NMN and NR supplementation.
  • Standard supplement doses (500 mg to 3g/day) appear to be safe in published studies.

What Is TMG (Trimethylglycine)?

Trimethylglycine is an amino acid derivative found naturally in beets (which is why it is also called betaine, from the Latin word for beet). It consists of the amino acid glycine with three methyl groups attached. These methyl groups are its main functional contribution: TMG donates them to biochemical reactions that require methylation throughout the body.

Methylation is one of the most fundamental biochemical processes in the human body. A methyl group is a simple carbon-hydrogen cluster (CH3), but attaching it to other molecules triggers or regulates gene expression, neurotransmitter synthesis, hormone metabolism, and cellular energy production. Without adequate methyl donors, these processes slow down or become dysregulated.

As women age through their 40s and beyond, methylation capacity can decline due to lower dietary quality, reduced absorption of B vitamins (which are key methylation co-factors), and increasing demand from cellular processes like NAD+ recycling. This creates a situation where supplemental methyl donors like TMG can meaningfully support the system.

Why It Matters for Women Over 40

Homocysteine is one of the most clinically relevant connections between methylation and women's health. Homocysteine is an amino acid that builds up when methylation is insufficient. Elevated homocysteine levels are associated with increased cardiovascular disease risk and have been linked to cognitive decline in research on older adults. Estrogen has a protective effect on homocysteine metabolism, which means that as estrogen declines during perimenopause, homocysteine levels can rise.

TMG's primary function in the methylation cycle is converting homocysteine back into methionine. This is a direct, well-established biochemical pathway. By donating a methyl group to homocysteine, TMG helps clear it from circulation. This mechanism has been studied in humans and is not theoretical.

The connection to NAD+ is equally relevant. When the body makes NAD+ through the salvage pathway (using NMN or NR precursors), a byproduct called nicotinamide is produced. Clearing nicotinamide requires methylation, specifically the action of an enzyme called NNMT (nicotinamide N-methyltransferase). If methyl donor availability is limited, this clearance can become a bottleneck that reduces the efficiency of NAD+ recycling. TMG supplementation addresses this bottleneck directly, which is why it has become a standard co-supplement in NAD+ protocols.

Recent research through 2026 suggests that women taking NMN or NR for NAD+ support may benefit meaningfully from pairing those supplements with TMG to maintain methylation capacity across the NAD+ recycling cycle.

What the Research Says

According to Happy Aging's review of the published research on TMG and betaine, the evidence is strongest in the areas of homocysteine regulation and methylation support.

Homocysteine reduction. Research associated with PMID: 17513435 examined TMG supplementation and homocysteine metabolism. The findings support that betaine/TMG can lower elevated homocysteine levels, which is relevant for women in their 40s and beyond where estrogen-related protection is declining.

Methylation pathway support. Research associated with PMID: 19828901 explored betaine's role in the methylation cycle. TMG functions as an osmolyte and methyl donor, contributing to the betaine-homocysteine methyltransferase (BHMT) reaction that directly recycles homocysteine.

Performance and body composition signals. Some research has examined higher doses of betaine (2.5g/day) in athletic populations and found signals of improved power output and body composition.

Safety profile. TMG at doses of 500 mg to 3g daily is well-tolerated in published studies.

What the Evidence Doesn't Support

TMG's homocysteine-lowering effect is real, but homocysteine reduction is not the same as preventing cardiovascular disease. Randomized controlled trials testing homocysteine-lowering interventions have not consistently produced proportional reductions in cardiovascular events.

TMG does not have proven anti-aging effects in humans in the sense of extending lifespan or reversing biological age markers. TMG is also not a replacement for dietary adequacy or for addressing B12 and folate deficiencies.

Happy Aging's position: TMG is a practical, well-tolerated methyl donor that provides meaningful homocysteine support and fills a specific gap in NAD+ recycling protocols. It is a sensible addition to a longevity supplement stack for women over 40, particularly those who are already taking NMN or NR.

Who Should NOT Take TMG

If you have bipolar disorder or are prone to anxiety, start with a low dose of TMG and monitor mood carefully. Some individuals report increased energy that can feel overstimulating during the first weeks of supplementation.

Women who are pregnant or nursing should consult their healthcare provider before starting TMG supplements. If you are on medications that affect the liver, discuss TMG with your physician before starting.

A Practical Protocol for TMG in a Longevity Stack

  1. Start with 500 mg of TMG daily for the first two weeks to assess tolerance and mood effects.
  2. If well-tolerated, increase to 1,000-2,000 mg daily, typically split between morning and midday.
  3. Take TMG with food to minimize the slight fishy body odor that can occur in some individuals at higher doses.
  4. Pair with your NMN or NR supplement to support the full NAD+ recycling cycle.
  5. Ensure your B12 and folate levels are adequate.
  6. Consider a yearly homocysteine blood test if you have cardiovascular risk factors.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does TMG actually do for longevity?
TMG supports longevity through two primary pathways. First, it donates methyl groups that convert homocysteine to methionine, keeping homocysteine levels in a healthy range. Second, it supports methylation of nicotinamide (a NAD+ recycling byproduct), which helps maintain efficient NAD+ production from precursor supplements like NMN and NR.

How is TMG different from betaine?
They are the same molecule. Betaine is the naturally occurring dietary form found in beets and other plants. TMG is the same compound described chemically. The terms are used interchangeably in research and supplement labeling.

Do I need TMG if I already take B12 and folate?
B12 and folate are essential methylation co-factors, but they work through the MTHFR pathway, not the BHMT pathway that TMG primarily supports. These two methylation routes are parallel and complementary. Adding TMG still provides additional methylation support through a different biochemical route, particularly relevant for NAD+ recycling.

Can TMG cause anxiety or mood changes?
Some individuals report increased energy or anxiety with TMG supplementation. Starting low (500 mg/day) and monitoring mood for two weeks before increasing the dose is a sensible approach for anyone with a history of mood sensitivity.

Can I get enough TMG from food?
A serving of beets provides roughly 200-300 mg of betaine/TMG. To achieve 1-2g daily from food alone would require large amounts of these specific foods daily. Supplementation is practical for reaching the doses used in research.
