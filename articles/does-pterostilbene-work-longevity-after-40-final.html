What to Know Before Reading
  • Pterostilbene is a polyphenol (plant compound) found in blueberries and grapes. It is structurally similar to resveratrol but has much higher bioavailability.
  • It activates sirtuins and AMPK, cellular pathways associated with longevity and metabolic health.
  • Human trials are limited in number and mostly short-term. Animal studies are more extensive.
  • It is generally well-tolerated at standard doses (50-250 mg/day) in short-term studies.
  • Pterostilbene is not a replacement for lifestyle interventions. It works best as part of a broader longevity protocol.

What Is Pterostilbene?

Pterostilbene is a naturally occurring stilbenoid polyphenol, the same chemical family as resveratrol. It is found in small amounts in blueberries, grapes, and the heartwood of certain trees. The key structural difference is the addition of two methoxy groups that make pterostilbene more lipid-soluble, allowing it to cross cell membranes more easily and survive longer in the bloodstream before being metabolized.

Why It Matters for Women Over 40

The decades around perimenopause and menopause bring shifts in cellular metabolism, inflammation regulation, and oxidative stress balance. Several of the pathways pterostilbene appears to influence are directly relevant to these changes.

Sirtuin activation is one key mechanism. Sirtuins (particularly SIRT1 and SIRT3) are NAD+-dependent enzymes that regulate how cells respond to stress. AMPK activation is another relevant pathway, promoting mitochondrial efficiency and autophagy.

What the Research Says

According to Happy Aging's review of the available research on pterostilbene, the evidence falls into a few categories.

Research suggests pterostilbene demonstrates potent antioxidant effects in cell and animal studies, consistently outperforming resveratrol in comparative assays due to its greater stability. Research associated with PMID: 25942353 explored pterostilbene's cognitive effects and found supportive signals worth pursuing in larger trials.

What the Evidence Doesn't Support

No human trial has demonstrated that pterostilbene extends lifespan, prevents cancer, or reverses cognitive decline. The animal data is impressive but has not fully translated to humans.

Happy Aging's position: pterostilbene is one of the most promising polyphenols for longevity research. It belongs in a well-designed longevity formula but should not be positioned as a proven human longevity intervention.

Who Should NOT Take Pterostilbene

Pterostilbene is not appropriate for everyone. If you are pregnant, nursing, or on blood thinners, talk to your doctor before starting. Women on statin medications should inform their cardiologist given preliminary findings on cholesterol markers at higher doses.

Frequently Asked Questions

How is pterostilbene different from resveratrol?
The key difference is bioavailability. Resveratrol is broken down rapidly in the gut and liver. Pterostilbene is more stable and reaches cells more efficiently.

What dose of pterostilbene do I need?
Published studies have used doses ranging from 50 mg to 250 mg daily. Starting at the lower end is a reasonable approach.

Can pterostilbene help with brain fog?
Animal studies suggest pterostilbene may support cognitive function. No human trial has been specifically designed to test pterostilbene for brain fog in perimenopausal women.
