Glycine, an amino acid found in collagen-rich foods, may improve sleep quality in women over 40 by lowering core body temperature at bedtime, a mechanism that has been confirmed in published human trials at a dose of 3 grams taken before sleep.

Sleep problems are one of the most common concerns women bring up starting in their 40s. Whether it is trouble falling asleep, waking at night, or simply not feeling rested, the impact on daily life is real. As of 2026, glycine has moved from a fringe sleep supplement to one with a plausible mechanism and human trial data worth taking seriously. Here is what the evidence actually shows.

What to Know Before Reading
  • Glycine is a non-essential amino acid, meaning the body can make it, but dietary and supplement intake can add to endogenous production.
  • Its proposed sleep mechanism is distinct from sedatives or melatonin: it may lower core body temperature, a key signal for sleep onset.
  • Human trials using 3g of glycine before bed have shown improvements in subjective sleep quality and daytime alertness.
  • It is not a sedative and does not impair alertness or reaction time the following day.
  • Glycine appears safe in short-term studies at standard sleep doses (3-5g/night).

What Is Glycine?

Glycine is the simplest amino acid, with a single hydrogen atom as its side chain. It is found abundantly in collagen, gelatin, and connective tissue. Foods like bone broth, skin-on chicken, pork rinds, and gelatin are naturally rich in glycine. It is classified as non-essential because the liver can synthesize it from serine and other precursors, but research suggests many people, particularly as they age, may not produce enough to meet optimal physiological demands.

In the nervous system, glycine acts as an inhibitory neurotransmitter, particularly in the spinal cord and brainstem. It also works as a co-agonist at NMDA receptors in the brain, meaning it has complex, context-dependent effects on neurological signaling rather than simple sedation.

The sleep-specific mechanism of glycine is believed to operate through peripheral blood vessel dilation. When glycine is taken before bed, research suggests it promotes heat dissipation through the skin of the extremities, which lowers core body temperature. This core temperature drop is one of the primary physiological signals that triggers sleep onset and promotes deeper sleep stages.

Why It Matters for Women Over 40

Sleep architecture changes meaningfully for women in their 40s and beyond. The combination of declining estrogen and progesterone (both of which have sleep-supportive effects), higher nighttime cortisol patterns, and changes in thermoregulation makes restorative sleep harder to achieve. Hot flashes and night sweats further disrupt the temperature regulation that glycine appears to support.

Recent research through 2026 suggests that thermoregulation dysfunction is one of the most underappreciated contributors to poor sleep quality in perimenopausal women. While glycine's temperature-lowering effect is modest in absolute terms, it may be particularly relevant for women whose core body temperature is dysregulated during the sleep-onset window.

Deep sleep (slow-wave sleep) is where most cellular repair, memory consolidation, and growth hormone release occur. Women in midlife frequently report spending less time in restorative deep sleep even when total sleep time appears adequate. Interventions that support sleep architecture, not just sleep onset, are particularly valuable in this age group.

What the Research Says

According to Happy Aging's review of the human trial literature on glycine for sleep, the evidence is more developed than most people realize.

Subjective sleep quality improvement. A study by Bannai and Kawai (PMID: 22529837) tested 3g of glycine before bed in adults with sleep complaints. Participants reported significant improvements in sleep quality, feeling of falling asleep, sleep efficiency, and daytime sleepiness compared to placebo. Notably, they performed better on a cognitive task the morning after supplementation, suggesting carryover benefits to waking function.

Sleep architecture signals. Research associated with PMID: 23290082 explored glycine's effects on sleep staging. Findings suggested glycine supplementation was associated with reduced time to sleep onset and improvements in sleep stage patterns, though this evidence is preliminary and requires replication with larger samples.

Core temperature mechanism. The proposed thermoregulatory mechanism for glycine's sleep effect has been studied in animal models and is consistent with the human subjective findings. Glycine appears to promote cutaneous vasodilation (blood vessel widening in the skin), which facilitates heat loss and the resulting body temperature drop that supports sleep initiation.

Safety. Glycine at 3-5g nightly is well-tolerated in published studies with no serious adverse events reported. It does not produce dependence, tolerance, or next-morning sedation at these doses.

What the Evidence Doesn't Support

Glycine has not been proven to treat insomnia as a clinical disorder. If you have diagnosed insomnia or sleep apnea, these require medical evaluation and potentially cognitive behavioral therapy or other evidence-based treatments. Glycine should not be used as a replacement for those interventions.

The human evidence for glycine's sleep effects is real but limited in scale. The published trials are small and mostly conducted in Japanese populations. Whether the findings generalize across ethnicities, ages, and health statuses is not yet confirmed.

Glycine is not a sedative and does not work the same way as melatonin, magnesium, or pharmaceutical sleep aids. It does not make you feel drowsy. Its proposed mechanism is more subtle: creating physiological conditions (lower core body temperature) that are favorable to natural sleep onset. This is actually a feature, not a limitation, but it means some people expecting a sedative effect may be disappointed.

Happy Aging's position: glycine at 3g before bed is among the best-supported non-sedating sleep quality interventions available in supplement form. It deserves a place in a sleep protocol for women over 40, particularly those struggling with sleep onset or sleep depth rather than acute anxiety-driven wakefulness.

Who Should NOT Take Glycine

If you have kidney disease, consult your doctor before supplementing with amino acids including glycine. The kidneys play a key role in amino acid metabolism, and supplemental amino acid loads may need medical oversight in those with compromised renal function.

Women who are pregnant or nursing should consult their healthcare provider before adding any supplement including glycine, as safety data in these populations is limited for supplemental doses above dietary levels.

If you are taking any medication that affects the nervous system, including benzodiazepines, sleep medications, or antidepressants, discuss glycine supplementation with your prescribing physician before starting. While glycine's inhibitory nervous system effects are gentle, interactions in the context of neurologically active medications should be considered.

A Practical Sleep Protocol with Glycine

If you and your healthcare provider decide glycine is appropriate for your sleep support routine, here is a structured approach:

  1. Take 3g of glycine powder or capsules 30-60 minutes before your intended sleep time.
  2. Pair with sleep hygiene fundamentals: keep your bedroom cool (65-68 degrees Fahrenheit), dark, and quiet. Glycine's temperature mechanism works best when your environment is already supporting heat dissipation.
  3. Consider adding magnesium glycinate (200-300 mg) if stress or muscle tension is contributing to sleep difficulty. Glycine in magnesium glycinate delivers some additional glycine alongside the magnesium.
  4. Avoid bright screens and stimulating content in the 60 minutes before bed. This supports the melatonin rise that glycine's temperature effect works alongside.
  5. Track sleep subjectively for two weeks before starting glycine to establish a baseline, then compare for two weeks after starting. Simple daily logs (1-10 scales for sleep quality and morning energy) are sufficient.
  6. If perimenopausal night sweats are a primary concern, discuss this specifically with your OB-GYN or menopause specialist. Glycine may complement medical management but is not a standalone treatment.
  7. Re-evaluate at 30 days. Glycine's effects may accumulate gradually rather than appearing immediately on the first night.

Frequently Asked Questions

How does glycine help you sleep differently from melatonin?
Melatonin signals the circadian rhythm that it is nighttime. Glycine works through a different mechanism: it promotes heat dissipation through the skin, which lowers core body temperature. Core temperature dropping is one of the primary physiological triggers for sleep onset and deep sleep maintenance. They can complement each other rather than competing.

What is the right dose of glycine for sleep?
The dose used in the most-cited human study (Bannai and Kawai, 2012) was 3 grams taken 30-60 minutes before bed. This is the standard evidence-based starting dose. Higher doses have not been shown to produce proportionally better effects and are not typically recommended for sleep applications.

Can glycine help with hot flashes at night?
Glycine's proposed mechanism of promoting heat dissipation may have some theoretical benefit for women experiencing nighttime temperature dysregulation. However, no clinical trial has specifically studied glycine for hot flash management. Night sweats that are severe or frequent warrant evaluation by a healthcare provider for appropriate management options.

Is glycine the same as collagen?
No, but glycine is the primary amino acid in collagen, making up roughly one-third of collagen's amino acid content. Taking collagen supplements does increase blood glycine levels, which is one reason some women report improved sleep as a side benefit of collagen supplementation. Supplementing with pure glycine is a more direct and dose-controlled way to achieve a specific glycine intake for sleep purposes.

Can I take glycine every night long-term?
Published studies do not report adverse effects from regular glycine use at sleep doses (3-5g nightly). No tolerance or dependence effects have been documented, unlike sedative sleep aids. Annual review with your physician is a reasonable practice for any ongoing supplement regimen.
