Vitamin K2 is one of the most underappreciated nutrients in women's bone health, and understanding what it does changes how you think about both calcium supplementation and long-term skeletal protection. Most women know calcium builds bones and vitamin D helps absorb it. What fewer women know is that without adequate vitamin K2, much of that calcium may end up in the wrong place. Vitamin K2 is the nutrient responsible for directing absorbed calcium specifically into bone tissue and keeping it out of soft tissues like arteries, where it contributes to calcification and cardiovascular risk.

What to Know

  • Vitamin K2 activates osteocalcin, the protein that binds calcium to the bone mineral matrix. Without K2, osteocalcin remains inactive and calcium is not properly deposited into bone.
  • K2 also activates Matrix Gla Protein (MGP), which removes excess calcium from artery walls. Low K2 is associated with arterial stiffness and cardiovascular calcification.
  • The MK-7 form of vitamin K2 (menaquinone-7) is the most bioavailable and longest-lasting form for supplementation, with a half-life of 72 hours versus 1 to 2 hours for MK-4.
  • Clinical trials show that daily MK-7 supplementation significantly reduces bone loss in postmenopausal women and may improve arterial flexibility.
  • Vitamin K2 works synergistically with vitamin D3 and calcium. Each nutrient has a distinct role, and the combination produces better bone outcomes than any one alone.

What Is Vitamin K2 and How Does It Differ From K1?

Vitamin K is not a single nutrient but a family of related fat-soluble compounds that share a common role in activating certain proteins through a chemical modification called carboxylation. The two main dietary forms are vitamin K1 (phylloquinone) and vitamin K2 (menaquinone).

Vitamin K1 is abundant in green leafy vegetables, particularly kale, spinach, and broccoli. It is primarily taken up by the liver and used to activate clotting factors, the proteins that allow blood to coagulate when you are injured. K1 is essential for coagulation health and is efficiently recycled by the liver for this purpose.

Vitamin K2 is found primarily in fermented foods (particularly natto, a Japanese fermented soybean food that contains extraordinarily high amounts), hard cheeses, egg yolks, and certain meats. K2 is distributed more broadly throughout the body, reaching bone, arteries, kidneys, and other peripheral tissues where it activates a different set of proteins: specifically osteocalcin in bone and Matrix Gla Protein (MGP) in the vascular system.

The key distinction is that K1 predominantly serves liver-based coagulation functions, while K2 primarily serves extra-hepatic functions in bone and cardiovascular tissue. This means adequate K1 intake does not compensate for K2 insufficiency in bone and vascular health, and the two vitamins are not interchangeable for these purposes.

How Vitamin K2 Works in Bone: The Osteocalcin Connection

The mechanism by which vitamin K2 protects bone is specific and well understood. Osteocalcin is a protein produced by osteoblasts (bone-building cells) that acts as a binding anchor for calcium within the hydroxyapatite mineral matrix of bone. For osteocalcin to function, it must be carboxylated, a chemical modification that adds carboxyl groups to specific glutamic acid residues in the protein's structure. This carboxylation step requires vitamin K2 as a cofactor.

When vitamin K2 is insufficient, osteocalcin remains undercarboxylated (ucOC). Undercarboxylated osteocalcin cannot bind calcium effectively, which means calcium absorbed through the intestine (facilitated by vitamin D) cannot be properly anchored into bone mineral. This creates a disconnect where adequate calcium intake and vitamin D levels still fail to produce optimal bone density because the final incorporation step is missing.

Studies measuring ucOC levels in postmenopausal women consistently find higher proportions of undercarboxylated osteocalcin compared to premenopausal women, and elevated ucOC is associated with lower bone mineral density and higher fracture rates. A randomized controlled trial by Knapen and colleagues published in Osteoporosis International found that daily MK-7 supplementation at 180 mcg significantly reduced ucOC levels and improved bone strength indices in postmenopausal women over three years, with the most pronounced effects in women with the lowest initial K2 status.

This research establishes a causal pathway: K2 deficiency leads to undercarboxylated osteocalcin, which leads to impaired calcium incorporation into bone, which contributes to lower bone density. Correcting K2 status activates osteocalcin, improves calcium deposition, and supports bone density maintenance.

Vitamin K2 and Cardiovascular Health After Menopause

The cardiovascular angle of vitamin K2 is equally compelling and particularly relevant for women after menopause, when cardiovascular risk rises significantly.

Matrix Gla Protein (MGP) is the most potent inhibitor of arterial calcification known. It works by binding calcium in arterial walls and preventing it from forming crystalline deposits (vascular calcification) that stiffen arteries and narrow blood vessels. Like osteocalcin, MGP must be carboxylated by vitamin K2 to become active. Inactive MGP cannot bind calcium in arteries, allowing calcification to proceed.

A landmark epidemiological study published in the Journal of Nutrition by Geleijnse and colleagues analyzed dietary vitamin K2 intake in nearly 5,000 participants from the Rotterdam Heart Study. Higher dietary K2 intake was associated with a 41 percent lower risk of coronary heart disease mortality and significantly lower rates of aortic calcification. These associations were specific to K2 and did not appear for K1 intake, reinforcing the view that these are functionally distinct nutrients.

For postmenopausal women, who experience accelerated arterial stiffening as estrogen-mediated vascular protection is withdrawn, maintaining adequate vitamin K2 for MGP activation represents a meaningful, food-based strategy for supporting vascular health alongside bone protection. The same nutrient serves both systems simultaneously.

MK-4 vs MK-7: Which Form of K2 Is Best?

Vitamin K2 supplements come in two main forms: MK-4 (menatetrenone) and MK-7 (menaquinone-7). They differ primarily in their source, pharmacokinetics, and the dose required to produce clinical effects.

MK-4 is synthesized in the body from K1 via conversion in peripheral tissues, and it has a short half-life in the bloodstream of only 1 to 2 hours. The Japanese clinical trials using MK-4 for bone health typically used pharmacological doses of 45 mg per day (45,000 mcg), far above dietary levels. At this dose, MK-4 has shown significant benefits for bone density in postmenopausal women. However, 45 mg is considered a drug dose in Japan and is not comparable to typical supplement doses in the West.

MK-7 is derived from bacterial fermentation (as in natto) and has a half-life of approximately 72 hours. This means a single daily dose maintains active K2 levels in the blood throughout the day, which is ideal for activating proteins in bone and arteries. MK-7 is effective at much lower doses than MK-4, with studies showing benefits at 90 to 200 mcg per day, making it the more practical choice for routine supplementation.

The research group led by Leon Schurgers compared MK-7 and MK-4 pharmacokinetics directly and found that MK-7 produced superior sustained blood levels at comparable doses. For women over 40 looking to supplement vitamin K2 for bone and cardiovascular support, MK-7 at 100 to 200 mcg daily is the current best-practice recommendation from bone health researchers.

Vitamin K2 and the D3 Combination: Why They Work Better Together

The relationship between vitamin D3 and vitamin K2 is one of the most practically important nutrient synergies in bone health. Understanding it helps explain why taking both produces outcomes that neither achieves alone.

Vitamin D3 dramatically increases calcium absorption in the intestine. This is its primary contribution to bone health, and it is well established. However, the increased calcium flowing into the bloodstream after vitamin D optimization needs to be directed somewhere. The body's default when calcium is abundant is to deposit it broadly, including in soft tissues. Vitamin K2 activates the protein systems (osteocalcin and MGP) that ensure calcium specifically reaches bone rather than accumulating in arteries, kidneys, or other tissues where it is harmful.

Some researchers have raised the concern that optimizing vitamin D without optimizing K2 might increase vascular calcification risk by raising circulating calcium without the K2-dependent guidance systems to direct it appropriately. While this hypothesis is not definitively proven, it provides mechanistic rationale for supplementing both nutrients together rather than vitamin D in isolation.

Practical guidance from bone health specialists increasingly recommends combining vitamin D3 (1,000 to 2,000 IU) with vitamin K2 MK-7 (100 to 200 mcg) daily, alongside adequate dietary or supplemental calcium (to close gaps to 1,000 to 1,200 mg total) and magnesium (for vitamin D activation and bone crystal formation). This four-nutrient approach addresses bone health comprehensively in a way that single-nutrient supplementation does not.

Food Sources of Vitamin K2 and Practical Intake Strategies

Getting adequate vitamin K2 from food alone is challenging for most women who do not regularly eat fermented foods, particularly natto.

Natto (fermented soybeans) is by far the richest food source of vitamin K2, containing approximately 850 to 1,000 mcg of MK-7 per 100-gram serving. A single small serving provides far more K2 than a daily supplement dose. However, natto has a strong, distinctive flavor and a sticky texture that most Western palates find difficult to enjoy. For women who eat it regularly, it provides exceptional K2 nutrition without supplementation.

Hard cheeses, particularly aged cheeses like Gouda, Edam, and Brie, contain meaningful amounts of MK-7 and MK-8, typically 10 to 40 mcg per 100-gram serving. Egg yolks contribute some K2 (approximately 5 mcg per yolk). Chicken liver and dark chicken meat contain MK-4.

For most women over 40 who do not regularly eat natto and who want bone-protective levels of K2, supplementation with MK-7 at 100 to 200 mcg daily is more reliable than attempting to meet the therapeutic range through diet alone. K2 supplements are fat-soluble and absorb best when taken with a fat-containing meal, similar to vitamin D. There are no known safety concerns at typical supplement doses, and K2 does not interfere with blood clotting medications at dietary supplement levels (though women on warfarin should discuss K2 supplementation with their physician before starting).

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between vitamin K1 and vitamin K2?

Vitamin K1 (phylloquinone) is found in green vegetables and is used primarily by the liver to activate blood clotting proteins. Vitamin K2 (menaquinone) is found in fermented foods and animal products and activates proteins outside the liver, specifically osteocalcin in bone and Matrix Gla Protein in arteries. They are not interchangeable for bone and cardiovascular functions.

How much vitamin K2 should women over 40 take?

Research on bone health and arterial calcification supports MK-7 supplementation at 100 to 200 mcg daily. The three-year Knapen trial showing improved bone strength used 180 mcg per day of MK-7. Doses in this range are safe and well-tolerated. Women on anticoagulant medications should consult their physician before supplementing with K2, as it is in the same vitamin family as K1, which affects coagulation.

Can vitamin K2 reverse existing arterial calcification?

Research suggests vitamin K2 may slow or partially reduce arterial calcification that is already present, by activating MGP to bind and remove excess calcium from artery walls. A clinical trial in dialysis patients found that supplemental MK-7 reduced arterial stiffness over 270 days. However, reversing calcification requires time and consistent K2 supplementation. The evidence for reversal is more limited than the evidence for prevention.

Is it safe to take vitamin K2 with vitamin D supplements?

Yes, and they are typically recommended together for bone health. Vitamin D3 increases calcium absorption, and vitamin K2 directs that calcium into bone via osteocalcin activation and away from arteries via MGP activation. The two nutrients work in a complementary sequence. Most bone health supplement protocols now include both, alongside calcium and magnesium.

Does vitamin K2 interfere with blood thinners like warfarin?

Warfarin works by blocking vitamin K-dependent clotting factor activation, and high doses of vitamin K (including K2) can reduce warfarin's effectiveness. Women on warfarin should discuss K2 supplementation with their physician before starting. Dietary levels of K2 from food typically do not cause significant interactions, but supplemental doses should be monitored with INR testing in women on anticoagulation therapy.
