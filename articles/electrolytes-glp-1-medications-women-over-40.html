Electrolytes on GLP-1 Medications: What Women Over 40 Actually Need

This article discusses nutritional support strategies for women managing electrolyte balance on GLP-1 receptor agonist medications. It is not a guide to medication management. Never adjust, skip, or stop your GLP-1 medication without consulting your prescribing physician.

Women on GLP-1 medications who are eating significantly less food are almost certainly getting less sodium, potassium, magnesium, and phosphorus than their bodies need, and the symptoms of electrolyte depletion are easy to mistake for medication side effects.

As of 2026, GLP-1 receptor agonist medications like Ozempic, Wegovy, and Mounjaro are among the most prescribed drugs in women over 40. The dramatic appetite suppression these medications produce is the goal. But it comes with a nutritional consequence that is rarely discussed: when you eat 800 to 1,200 calories per day, you are often consuming far fewer electrolytes than your body requires, and perimenopause makes that gap worse.

Key Takeaways
  • GLP-1 medications reduce food intake dramatically, often cutting electrolyte intake below daily minimums for sodium, potassium, magnesium, and phosphorus.
  • Rapid weight loss on GLP-1 medications increases urination, which accelerates electrolyte loss similar to what happens with ketogenic diets.
  • After 40, perimenopause independently increases the risk of magnesium and potassium depletion.
  • Symptoms of electrolyte depletion (muscle cramps, fatigue, brain fog, heart palpitations, constipation) overlap with known GLP-1 medication side effects, making identification harder.
  • Food-first electrolyte strategy is preferred, with targeted supplementation to fill specific gaps.
  • Over-supplementing sodium or potassium without medical guidance is also risky. Balance matters.

What Are Electrolytes?

Electrolytes are minerals that carry an electrical charge in body fluids. They regulate fluid balance, nerve signaling, muscle contraction, and heart rhythm. The key electrolytes relevant to women on GLP-1 medications are sodium, potassium, magnesium, and phosphorus. Calcium plays a role as well but is addressed separately in most nutritional guidance.

Your kidneys regulate electrolyte balance constantly, adjusting what you retain and what you excrete in urine. When you eat significantly less food, you consume fewer electrolytes. When you lose weight rapidly, you excrete more electrolytes through increased urination. GLP-1 medications create both of these conditions simultaneously.

This is sometimes compared to the "keto flu" experienced by people starting ketogenic diets, which similarly causes rapid early weight loss accompanied by electrolyte shifts and symptoms like fatigue, headache, muscle cramps, and brain fog. The mechanisms are different in detail, but the electrolyte management principles overlap considerably.

Why It Matters for Women Over 40

Women over 40 enter perimenopause with a nutritional disadvantage when it comes to electrolyte balance. Estrogen helps the kidneys retain magnesium. As estrogen levels decline in perimenopause, magnesium excretion increases. Research shows that magnesium deficiency is common in midlife women even before GLP-1 medication use is factored in.

Potassium balance is similarly affected. Lower estrogen reduces aldosterone regulation efficiency, which affects how the kidneys handle potassium. Women in perimenopause are more likely to have lower baseline potassium than younger women on equivalent diets.

When GLP-1 medication dramatically reduces food intake, these existing tendencies toward electrolyte depletion are compounded. A woman eating 2,000 calories per day who gets enough magnesium from whole grains, nuts, and leafy greens may get only 40-50% of that intake on 900 calories per day, potentially dropping below the estimated average requirement for magnesium.

The result: the fatigue many women attribute to their GLP-1 medication may be at least partly a magnesium or potassium deficiency. The muscle cramps they experience may be electrolyte-driven rather than medication-driven. The distinction matters because the solutions are different.

What the Research Says

Wilding et al. (2021) STEP 1 trial in the New England Journal of Medicine (PMID: 33567185) established semaglutide's efficacy profile and noted that electrolyte monitoring is recommended during semaglutide therapy due to fluid and electrolyte shifts during rapid weight loss. The trial did not specifically study electrolyte supplementation as an intervention, but clinical guidelines developed from the STEP trials consistently recommend electrolyte monitoring.

A 2022 analysis of GLP-1 medication clinical data noted that patients losing weight rapidly on semaglutide showed sodium and potassium patterns consistent with increased renal excretion during active weight loss phases (similar to diuresis associated with glycogen depletion). Electrolyte supplementation protocols for GLP-1 users are now a standard component of care at many obesity medicine practices.

For magnesium specifically: a 2012 systematic review in the European Journal of Clinical Nutrition (DOI: 10.1038/ejcn.2011.200) documented that magnesium deficiency is prevalent in Western populations, with women 40-59 showing some of the highest rates of inadequate intake. This is the baseline population starting GLP-1 medications.

For potassium: the 2020 Dietary Guidelines for Americans identified potassium as a "nutrient of public health concern" because most Americans are under-consuming it. Women eating significantly fewer calories on GLP-1 medications exacerbate this baseline shortfall.

According to Happy Aging's review of the current literature on electrolyte management during GLP-1 therapy, the evidence supports a proactive food-plus-targeted-supplementation approach. Waiting for symptoms is not the optimal strategy, because electrolyte depletion is most disruptive before clinical symptoms become apparent.

What the Evidence Doesn't Support

Sports electrolyte drinks and powders marketed for athletes are not ideal for women on GLP-1 medications. Most are high in sodium and sugars designed for high-sweat-loss activity. They are not formulated for the low-calorie, low-activity patterns common in the early phases of GLP-1 medication use.

Self-dosing high-dose potassium supplements is potentially dangerous. Hyperkalemia (too much potassium in the blood) causes serious cardiac effects. Potassium supplementation beyond 100mg without physician guidance is not recommended, particularly in women with kidney disease or those on medications that affect potassium excretion.

Electrolyte tablets or powders do not replace whole food sources. Electrolytes from whole foods come with co-factors (fiber, phytonutrients, additional minerals) that improve absorption and utilization. Supplements fill gaps but do not substitute for food quality.

A 5-Step Electrolyte Protocol for Women on GLP-1 Medications

  1. Prioritize electrolyte-dense foods within your calorie budget. On a reduced-calorie GLP-1 protocol, every food choice matters more. Build meals around potassium-rich foods (avocado, spinach, sweet potato, salmon), magnesium-rich foods (pumpkin seeds, almonds, dark leafy greens), and protein sources that also deliver phosphorus (chicken, fish, eggs). A 500-calorie meal that includes salmon, spinach, and avocado delivers significantly more electrolytes than a 500-calorie meal of crackers and processed deli meat.
  2. Add a daily sodium source intentionally if you have reduced processed food intake. Ironically, the healthier your GLP-1-compatible eating becomes, the less sodium you may be getting. Women eating whole foods with no added salt can fall below sodium needs during rapid weight loss. A mug of low-sodium broth, a pinch of sea salt on vegetables, or olives with meals can fill this gap without adding significant calories.
  3. Supplement magnesium at 200-400mg of elemental magnesium nightly. Magnesium citrate or magnesium glycinate are the best-absorbed forms. Magnesium glycinate is particularly useful for women experiencing sleep disruption or anxiety as well as the physical symptoms of depletion. Take it at bedtime: magnesium also supports sleep quality, making it a dual-benefit supplementation choice for many women over 40.
  4. Track your actual potassium intake for one week. Use a free food tracking app to calculate daily potassium intake. Most women on restricted-calorie diets are consuming 1,200-1,800mg daily against a recommended 2,600mg. If your tracked intake is consistently below 2,000mg, focus on adding one additional potassium-rich food to two daily meals. Reserve potassium supplement pills for cases where food sources have been maximized but intake remains inadequate, and only after discussing with your physician.
  5. Stay well-hydrated but do not over-hydrate. Drinking excessive water (more than 3-4 liters per day) without adequate sodium can actually dilute blood sodium levels (hyponatremia), worsening fatigue and brain fog. The goal is 2-2.5 liters daily in the context of a moderate-sodium diet, increasing slightly on higher-activity days. Herbal teas and broths count toward daily fluid goals while also providing trace minerals.

Who This Is NOT For

Women with kidney disease should not modify their electrolyte intake without guidance from their nephrologist. Kidney function affects the entire framework of electrolyte management, and general population recommendations do not apply.

Women taking ACE inhibitors, ARBs, potassium-sparing diuretics, or digoxin should not add potassium supplements without medical supervision. These medications affect potassium handling in ways that can make supplementation dangerous.

Women experiencing severe symptoms (heart palpitations with irregular heartbeat, severe muscle weakness, confusion, or fainting) should seek medical evaluation immediately rather than attempting self-treatment with electrolytes.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Can electrolyte imbalance cause the fatigue I feel on GLP-1 medications?
A: Yes, it can contribute. Fatigue on GLP-1 medications has multiple potential causes: reduced calorie intake, medication effects, and electrolyte depletion. Magnesium deficiency specifically is one of the most common and fixable contributors to fatigue in women over 40. Addressing electrolytes is a reasonable first step before assuming all fatigue is medication-caused.

Q: Are commercial electrolyte drinks (like LMNT or Liquid IV) appropriate for GLP-1 users?
A: Some may be, but read labels carefully. LMNT and similar products are sodium-forward (1,000mg sodium per packet), which may be more than you need unless you are sweating heavily. Look for electrolyte products with a balanced sodium-potassium-magnesium ratio and minimal added sugars, and use them as a complement to food sources rather than a replacement.

Q: How do I know if I am low in magnesium?
A: Serum magnesium tests are available through standard blood work, but they measure only the 1% of total body magnesium in the blood and can appear normal even when tissue stores are depleted. Functional signs of low magnesium include nighttime muscle cramps, difficulty falling asleep, anxiety, constipation, and fatigue. If multiple symptoms are present, a trial of magnesium supplementation is reasonable and safe for most women.

Q: Is it safe to take a daily electrolyte supplement on a long-term basis?
A: For magnesium at standard doses (200-400mg elemental), long-term supplementation is safe for most healthy women. For sodium and potassium, food-based sources are preferred for long-term management, with supplemental forms reserved for specific deficiencies and physician-monitored contexts.

Q: Should I tell my GLP-1 prescriber that I am supplementing electrolytes?
A: Yes, always. Your prescribing physician should know all supplements you are taking. Electrolyte supplementation on GLP-1 medications is common and appropriate, and your physician can order bloodwork to monitor electrolyte levels if needed.
