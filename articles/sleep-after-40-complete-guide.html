Why Sleep Gets Harder After 40

Sleep in your 40s is disrupted by three overlapping changes that are distinct from the normal aging of sleep architecture in younger adults. First, progesterone — which has a mild sedative and GABA-enhancing effect — begins its perimenopausal decline, reducing natural sleep depth. Second, the circadian rhythm shifts forward slightly, making you sleepier earlier in the evening but more alert at 3 or 4 AM. Third, the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis becomes more reactive with age, meaning cortisol spikes more easily overnight in response to minor stressors — physical or psychological. According to Happy Aging's review of perimenopause sleep research, the combination of reduced progesterone and elevated nocturnal cortisol is the most common pattern underlying middle-of-the-night waking in women aged 40 to 55. Understanding these mechanisms is the first step to addressing them strategically rather than just increasing hours in bed. Magnesium Glycinate for Sleep: The Evidence

Magnesium is the most commonly deficient mineral among women over 40, and its role in sleep is specific and well-documented. Magnesium binds to GABA receptors in the brain, promoting the same inhibitory pathways activated by sleep medications — but gently and without dependency. It also suppresses cortisol and regulates the activity of the HPA axis, the stress-response system that keeps many women awake in the early morning hours. The glycinate form is bonded to glycine, an amino acid with its own sleep-supporting properties, making magnesium glycinate the most effective form for sleep specifically. Clinical trials have found that magnesium supplementation reduces the time it takes to fall asleep, reduces nighttime waking, and improves subjective sleep quality in older adults. Happy Aging's position: 300 to 400 mg of magnesium glycinate taken 60 minutes before bed is the protocol most supported by the available evidence for women over 40. Glycine and Sleep Quality: The Hormone Connection

Glycine is a non-essential amino acid that acts as an inhibitory neurotransmitter in the brainstem and spinal cord. For sleep, its most significant action is lowering core body temperature — a drop in core body temperature is the primary physiological trigger for sleep onset and is required for entry into deep slow-wave sleep. Studies in humans have found that 3 grams of glycine taken before bed reduces time to sleep onset, improves subjective sleep quality, and reduces daytime fatigue the following day. Beyond sleep, glycine also plays a role in estrogen metabolism and collagen synthesis — making it a supplement with multiple relevant benefits for women in perimenopause. Frequently Asked Questions

Why do women over 40 wake up between 2 and 4 AM?

This pattern is most commonly caused by a cortisol spike that occurs in the early morning hours as the body prepares for waking, combined with lower progesterone levels that no longer buffer against that cortisol rise. Magnesium glycinate and stress-reduction practices address both sides of this pattern.

Is it safe to take magnesium glycinate every night?

Yes. Magnesium glycinate is non-habit-forming and safe for daily use at recommended doses. Unlike sleep medications, it does not suppress REM sleep or cause dependency.

How quickly does magnesium glycinate work for sleep?

Some women notice improved sleep onset within the first week. The cortisol-modulating effects that reduce early morning waking typically take 2 to 4 weeks of consistent use to become apparent.

Can glycine and magnesium be taken together?

Yes, and they complement each other well. Magnesium supports GABA pathways and cortisol regulation; glycine lowers core body temperature and acts as an inhibitory neurotransmitter. The combination addresses sleep onset and sleep maintenance through distinct mechanisms.
