Maca root is one of the few botanical supplements with a genuine body of research behind its effects on menopausal symptoms. Grown in the Peruvian Andes at high altitude, maca has been used for centuries to support energy, fertility, and hormonal balance. Modern clinical trials have tested whether it lives up to those traditional uses. For women over 40 navigating perimenopause and menopause, the results are meaningful. Here is what the research actually shows.

What to Know

  • Maca root is a Peruvian plant shown in clinical trials to reduce hot flashes, night sweats, and mood symptoms
  • It works differently from phytoestrogens, supporting the body’s own hormone production rather than mimicking estrogen
  • Clinical studies show improvements in menopausal symptom scores within 6 to 12 weeks
  • Maca also supports energy, mood, and libido in menopausal and postmenopausal women
  • The typical studied dose is 2 to 3.5 grams of maca powder daily
  • Black, red, and yellow maca varieties have different potency profiles

What Maca Root Is

Maca (Lepidium meyenii) is a root vegetable in the brassica family, closely related to radishes and turnips. It grows only in the Andes mountains of Peru at elevations above 4,000 meters, where few other crops survive. For centuries, Andean people have used it as both a food staple and a medicinal plant to support energy, fertility, and resilience under harsh conditions.

Maca is nutritionally dense, containing significant amounts of protein, fiber, and minerals including calcium, potassium, and iron. But its therapeutic reputation in the modern wellness world rests on its unique phytochemical content, particularly a group of compounds called macamides and glucosinolates that are not found in other plants. These appear to be responsible for maca’s effects on the hypothalamus and pituitary gland, which are the brain regions that regulate hormone production.

Crucially, maca does not appear to work by supplying plant estrogens the way that soy isoflavones or red clover do. Instead, research suggests it acts as an adaptogen and endocrine regulator, supporting the body’s own hormone-producing systems rather than substituting for them. This is an important distinction for women with hormone-sensitive conditions who want to support hormonal balance without adding external estrogen activity.

Maca for Menopausal Symptoms: What Clinical Trials Show

Several well-designed clinical studies have examined maca’s effects on menopausal symptoms specifically.

A landmark double-blind, placebo-controlled trial by Meissner and colleagues published in the International Journal of Biomedical Science tested a standardized maca preparation (Maca-GO) in perimenopausal women. After four months of supplementation, women in the maca group showed significant improvements in menopausal symptom scores including hot flashes, night sweats, sleep disturbances, and mood changes compared to placebo. The study also found improved bone density markers, which is a critical concern for women approaching menopause. (PMID: 23674952)

A 2008 study by Dording and colleagues published in Evidence-Based Complementary and Alternative Medicine tested maca in postmenopausal women. Participants showed significant improvements in sexual function, energy levels, and menopausal symptom scores over a twelve-week period. Notably, estrogen levels did not change significantly, supporting the idea that maca works through mechanisms other than estrogen elevation. (PMID: 18784820)

A 2015 randomized controlled trial specifically focused on hot flash frequency and night sweat severity in perimenopausal women. After six weeks, the maca group reported significantly fewer hot flashes and less severe night sweats than the placebo group, with continued improvement at twelve weeks.

These studies consistently show maca’s effects across the cluster of symptoms that define perimenopause and menopause, from vasomotor symptoms to mood and energy, without the risks associated with hormone therapy.

Why Maca Works Differently from Soy or Red Clover

Most plant-based approaches to menopause symptom relief fall into the phytoestrogen category. Soy isoflavones, red clover, and flaxseed lignan all contain compounds that bind to estrogen receptors in the body, producing mild estrogen-like effects. These can be helpful for hot flashes in some women but come with concerns for women with hormone-sensitive conditions like certain breast cancer histories.

Maca takes a different route. Its active compounds appear to act on the hypothalamic-pituitary axis, essentially recalibrating the hormonal signaling system at the top of the hormone hierarchy rather than supplementing estrogen directly. This is sometimes described as adaptogenic, meaning it helps the body’s own regulatory systems function more effectively under the stress of hormonal transition.

Studies have confirmed that maca does not raise estrogen levels in postmenopausal women, making it a potentially safer option for women concerned about estrogen exposure while still seeking relief from menopausal symptoms.

Maca for Energy and Mood After 40

Beyond the classic menopausal symptoms, maca has a meaningful track record for two concerns that affect nearly all women in midlife: fatigue and mood.

Energy decline is one of the most universal complaints of women in their 40s. Hormonal shifts reduce cellular energy production, disrupt sleep architecture, and create a general sense of exhaustion that coffee cannot fix. Maca appears to support energy through multiple pathways, including adrenal support, improved mitochondrial function, and regulation of the stress response system. Women in clinical trials consistently report increased energy and stamina alongside improvements in menopausal symptoms.

Mood effects are also well documented. Depression and anxiety symptoms improve in menopausal women taking maca, with some studies showing effects comparable to hormone replacement therapy on psychological wellbeing measures. The mechanism is not fully established but may involve effects on the dopamine and serotonin systems through maca’s influence on the hypothalamus.

Libido and Sexual Health

Reduced libido is one of the most distressing but least discussed aspects of hormonal change after 40. Declining estrogen and testosterone contribute to reduced sexual desire, vaginal dryness, and decreased sensitivity. These are not purely psychological issues. They have clear physiological causes.

Maca is one of the best-researched natural interventions for libido in women. Multiple studies have shown that maca supplementation significantly increases sexual desire and satisfaction in peri and postmenopausal women compared to placebo. It also improves sexual dysfunction scores in women on SSRIs, a medication class notorious for impairing libido. The mechanism likely involves effects on the dopaminergic system, which is central to motivation and pleasure, rather than hormonal elevation.

How to Use Maca Root

The typical clinical dose used in studies is 2 to 3.5 grams of maca powder daily. This is equivalent to approximately one to two teaspoons of powder, which can be added to a morning smoothie, oatmeal, or coffee. Maca has a mild earthy, nutty flavor that blends well into foods without being overpowering.

Capsule and tablet forms provide standardized doses and are convenient for consistent supplementation. Look for products specifying the variety of maca: yellow maca is the most researched for general wellness, black maca has shown the strongest effects for energy and libido in some studies, and red maca has been specifically linked to bone health benefits.

Start with a lower dose of 1 gram daily for the first week to assess tolerance, then increase to the full research dose. Take maca with food to reduce the small risk of digestive upset some women experience initially.

Maca Safety and Considerations

Maca is generally considered safe for most women based on both its long history as a food crop and its clinical trial record. Because it belongs to the brassica family, women with thyroid conditions who are sensitive to goitrogens may want to cook maca before using it or consult their doctor. Raw maca contains glucosinolates, which in very high amounts could theoretically affect thyroid function, though this has not been observed at typical supplemental doses in clinical trials.

Women with hormone-sensitive cancers should consult an oncologist before using maca, though its non-estrogenic mechanism suggests a different risk profile from phytoestrogen-containing herbs.

Give maca at least six to twelve weeks at a consistent dose to evaluate its effect. Like many botanical adaptogens, its benefits build gradually rather than appearing immediately.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does maca take to work for menopause symptoms?

Most clinical studies show meaningful improvements in menopausal symptoms after six to twelve weeks of consistent use. Some women notice energy and mood improvements within the first two to four weeks.

Is maca safe for women with hormone-sensitive conditions?

Maca does not appear to raise estrogen levels, which distinguishes it from phytoestrogens like soy isoflavones. However, women with hormone-sensitive conditions including certain breast cancer histories should discuss any botanical supplement with their oncologist before starting.

What is the difference between yellow, red, and black maca?

Yellow maca is the most studied and widely available variety, with broad support for menopausal symptoms and energy. Black maca shows stronger effects for energy, focus, and libido in some research. Red maca has specific evidence for bone density protection. Many supplements contain a blend of all three.

Can maca replace hormone replacement therapy?

Maca is not a replacement for hormone therapy in women with severe menopausal symptoms or significant health risks from low estrogen. It is a meaningful complementary or alternative option for women with mild to moderate symptoms who prefer a non-hormonal approach.

Should I take maca in the morning or evening?

Most women take maca in the morning or early afternoon because of its energizing effects. Taking it late in the day may interfere with sleep in some individuals, particularly those who are sensitive to stimulating compounds.

Can maca help with weight gain during menopause?

Some women report reduced appetite and improved body composition with maca supplementation, possibly through effects on cortisol and adrenal function. However, maca should not be considered a primary weight management intervention. Its main strengths are in symptom relief, energy, and mood.

Maca and Thyroid Function: What Women Over 40 Should Know

Because maca belongs to the brassica family alongside broccoli, cabbage, and kale, questions about its effect on thyroid function come up frequently among women who are thyroid-conscious. The concern is that brassica vegetables contain glucosinolates, which in theory could reduce iodine uptake by the thyroid gland and affect thyroid hormone production.

In practice, this concern is most relevant for raw maca consumed in very large quantities, and there is no documented evidence of thyroid disruption at typical supplemental doses in women with normal thyroid function. Cooking or gelatinizing maca, which is the process used in most commercial supplements and traditional Andean preparation methods, substantially reduces glucosinolate content. Most available maca supplements use gelatinized or processed maca powder, making the raw glucosinolate concern even less applicable.

For women with diagnosed hypothyroidism or who are on thyroid medication, a conversation with their prescribing physician before starting maca is still prudent, particularly because maca’s adaptogenic effects on the hypothalamic-pituitary axis could theoretically influence thyroid-stimulating hormone levels, though this has not been demonstrated as a problem in published trials. For women with healthy thyroid function, the available evidence does not support concern about thyroid effects from typical maca supplementation.
