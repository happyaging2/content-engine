What to Know

  • Multiple large meta-analyses, including Bolland et al. (BMJ, 2010, PMID: 20671013), found that calcium supplements (without co-administered vitamin D) are associated with a modestly increased risk of myocardial infarction.
  • Dietary calcium from food sources does not carry the same cardiovascular concern. The mechanism may relate to the acute spike in serum calcium from supplements versus the gradual absorption from food.
  • Most women over 40 consume 500-800 mg of calcium per day through diet. Total daily intake from food plus supplements should stay under 2,000 mg (the tolerable upper intake level), with supplemental intake ideally limited to filling the gap between diet and the 1,000-1,200 mg daily recommendation.
  • Calcium works synergistically with vitamin D (absorption cofactor) and vitamin K2 (directs calcium to bones rather than arteries). These are not optional add-ons.
  • The form of calcium matters: calcium citrate is better absorbed on an empty stomach; calcium carbonate requires stomach acid and is best taken with food.
  • Bone health after 40 requires more than calcium: weight-bearing exercise, protein intake, vitamin D, vitamin K2, and magnesium all play critical roles.

Calcium supplements are one of the most commonly recommended nutrients for women over 40, and also one of the most misunderstood. The advice to "take calcium for your bones" is not wrong, but it is incomplete in ways that matter for your cardiovascular health. Here is what the current evidence actually shows, and how to use calcium supplementation in a way that makes sense.

What Is Calcium's Role in the Body?

Calcium is the most abundant mineral in the human body, with approximately 99% stored in bones and teeth. The remaining 1% circulates in blood and tissues, where it is essential for muscle contraction (including heart muscle), nerve signaling, blood clotting, and cell membrane function. The body maintains serum calcium within a narrow range through a hormonal system involving parathyroid hormone (PTH), calcitonin, and vitamin D. When dietary calcium is insufficient, PTH signals the bones to release stored calcium into the bloodstream to maintain those critical functions, which is one mechanism through which chronically low calcium intake contributes to bone loss over time.

After 40, and especially after menopause, several changes increase the importance of calcium intake: estrogen helps the intestines absorb calcium efficiently, so as estrogen declines, absorption efficiency drops. Bone turnover (the cycle of bone breakdown and rebuilding) shifts toward net loss without the estrogen signal that favored formation. This is the biological basis for the increased risk of osteoporosis in postmenopausal women.

Why It Matters for Women Over 40

The bone loss acceleration that begins in perimenopause is real and significant. Women can lose 1-2% of bone density per year in the years surrounding menopause, a rate that slows after the transition but does not reverse. The cumulative effect is why women have roughly four times the osteoporosis rate of men, and why fractures (particularly hip fractures, which carry serious health consequences) are a major women's health concern.

Against this backdrop, the advice to supplement calcium makes intuitive sense. But the way calcium supplementation became standardized (often 1,000-1,500 mg per day in supplement form) may not reflect the nuance the research now supports. The question is not just whether calcium supplements help bones, but whether the cardiovascular concerns raised by large meta-analyses change the calculus for women who are already at increased cardiovascular risk after menopause.

What the Research Says

The cardiovascular concern around calcium supplements emerged prominently from work by Mark Bolland and colleagues at the University of Auckland. Their 2010 BMJ meta-analysis (PMID: 20671013) analyzed patient-level and trial-level data and found that calcium supplements without co-administered vitamin D were associated with a 27-31% increased relative risk of myocardial infarction. A 2011 follow-up analysis (reanalysis of the Women's Health Initiative dataset, PMC: PMC3079822) added nuance: calcium plus vitamin D did not show a statistically significant cardiovascular increase in the primary analysis, though subgroup analyses were more complex.

The proposed mechanism involves the difference between how supplements and dietary calcium raise serum calcium. Food-based calcium is absorbed slowly, producing a gradual and modest rise in blood calcium. Supplement calcium, especially in larger single doses, can produce a more acute spike in serum calcium, which may promote arterial calcification and platelet aggregation. This does not mean supplements are uniformly dangerous; it means the dose, timing, and context matter.

More recent research has refined this picture. A 2021 meta-analysis of clinical trials (PMID: 33530332) found that the cardiovascular risk appears most associated with high-dose supplemental calcium (above 1,000 mg per day from supplements) and in women who already had relatively high dietary calcium intake, making supplementation additive rather than gap-filling.

What the Evidence Doesn't Support

The idea that more calcium is always better for bones is not supported by the evidence. Bone density is influenced by at least a dozen variables: vitamin D status (which governs calcium absorption), vitamin K2 (which activates proteins that direct calcium into bone matrix rather than arteries), magnesium, protein intake, weight-bearing exercise, hormonal status, and genetic factors. Calcium is one input into a complex system, not a standalone bone-building solution.

The "1,200 mg calcium supplement" recommendation that became standard for postmenopausal women originated partly from older trials that did not account for baseline dietary calcium intake. A woman getting 800 mg per day from diet does not need 1,200 mg from a supplement; she needs 200-400 mg as a gap-filler. Taking the higher dose fills an imaginary gap while potentially adding cardiovascular risk.

It is also worth noting that calcium supplementation has not been definitively shown to reduce fracture risk in large, well-designed trials when controlling for dietary calcium, vitamin D status, and physical activity. The USPSTF (U.S. Preventive Services Task Force) updated its guidance to note that evidence is insufficient to recommend calcium plus vitamin D supplementation for fracture prevention in postmenopausal women who are not vitamin D deficient, a more cautious position than the decades-long "take your calcium" refrain.

Happy Aging's position: Calcium supplementation for women over 40 makes sense when it fills a genuine dietary gap, is dosed at 200-400 mg per day rather than 1,000-1,200 mg, is taken with vitamin D and vitamin K2, and is part of a comprehensive bone health strategy that includes exercise and adequate protein. The cardiovascular concerns are not a reason to avoid calcium entirely; they are a reason to be more precise about how you use it.

The Happy Aging Recommendation

Before adding a calcium supplement, estimate your dietary calcium intake. One serving of dairy (milk, yogurt, or cheese) contains roughly 200-400 mg. A woman eating two servings of dairy and some leafy greens per day may already be getting 700-900 mg from diet. In that case, a 200-300 mg supplement with meals, combined with vitamin D3 (1,000-2,000 IU daily) and vitamin K2 (90-180 mcg daily), fills the gap without overshooting. If you are eating little to no dairy or fortified foods, a slightly higher supplemental dose makes more sense but should still be combined with D3 and K2 and taken in divided doses with food (no more than 500 mg at a time for best absorption and lowest acute serum calcium spike).

Weight-bearing exercise and resistance training are the most evidence-backed interventions for maintaining bone density after 40 because they create the mechanical load signals that tell bone to stay strong. No supplement substitutes for this.

Who This Is NOT For

Women with a history of kidney stones (particularly calcium oxalate stones) should discuss calcium supplementation carefully with their doctor, as supplemental calcium (especially outside of meals) may increase oxalate absorption and stone risk; dietary calcium, interestingly, can be protective. Women with hypercalcemia (elevated blood calcium) should not supplement calcium without medical supervision. If you have a history of cardiovascular disease or are at high cardiovascular risk, the Bolland et al. data is especially relevant to your conversation with your physician about whether supplemental calcium makes sense for you specifically.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I stop taking calcium supplements because of the heart risk?

Not necessarily, but you should reconsider the dose and context. The cardiovascular concern appears most associated with high-dose supplemental calcium (over 1,000 mg per day from supplements) and in women who are not actually calcium-deficient from diet. If you are taking 500-1,000 mg in supplement form on top of a reasonable dietary calcium intake, talking with your doctor about reducing to a smaller gap-filling dose makes sense. Taking it with food and splitting doses (no more than 500 mg at a time) reduces the acute serum calcium spike associated with risk.

What is the difference between calcium carbonate and calcium citrate?

Calcium carbonate is the most common and least expensive form. It requires stomach acid for absorption, so it should be taken with food, and absorption can be reduced in women with low stomach acid (more common after 40). Calcium citrate does not require stomach acid, can be taken with or without food, and tends to be better tolerated. For women over 50 or those on acid-reducing medications, calcium citrate is generally the better choice despite being slightly more expensive.

Is vitamin K2 really necessary with calcium?

The evidence for K2's role in bone and vascular health is growing. Vitamin K2 activates osteocalcin (which binds calcium into bone matrix) and Matrix Gla Protein (which inhibits calcium deposition in arteries). In populations with higher K2 intake (notably Japan, where fermented soybean natto is common), rates of osteoporosis and certain cardiovascular conditions differ from populations with low K2 intake. Clinical trial evidence specifically proving fracture reduction is still building, but the mechanistic support for K2 as a calcium cofactor is well established. Most nutrition-informed bone health protocols include both D3 and K2 alongside calcium.

How much calcium do I actually need after 40?

The National Institutes of Health recommends 1,000 mg per day for women aged 51-70 and 1,200 mg per day for women over 70. These are total calcium targets from all sources, not supplement targets. The gap between what most women get from diet and this recommendation is much smaller than the standard supplement dose of 1,000-1,200 mg. Most women need 200-400 mg in supplemental form to reach the recommended total, not a full 1,200 mg supplement on top of their diet.

Does exercise really matter as much as calcium for bone health?

Yes. Weight-bearing exercise (walking, running, hiking, dancing) and resistance training create mechanical load on bones, which is the primary signal for osteoblasts (bone-building cells) to maintain and increase bone density. Studies consistently show that physically active women have meaningfully better bone density outcomes than sedentary women regardless of calcium intake. Calcium is a necessary building material; exercise provides the signal to use it. Both matter.
