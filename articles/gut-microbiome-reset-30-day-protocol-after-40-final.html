What Is the Gut Microbiome and How Does It Change After 40?

The gut microbiome refers to the vast community of bacteria, fungi, viruses, and other microorganisms living in the digestive tract, primarily in the large intestine. A healthy microbiome contains thousands of species working together to digest food, produce short-chain fatty acids, regulate immune function, synthesize some vitamins, and communicate with the brain via the gut-brain axis.

After 40, several interconnected changes occur:

Diversity declines: Research consistently shows that microbial species diversity drops with age. Fewer species means a less resilient ecosystem. A microbiome with 300-400 species is more stable and functional than one with 150-200.

Beneficial bacteria decrease: Populations of Bifidobacterium (associated with immune health, anti-inflammation, and estrogen metabolism) and certain Lactobacillus species naturally decline with age. Simultaneously, species associated with inflammation may increase.

The estrobolome is affected: The estrobolome is the collection of gut bacteria responsible for metabolizing estrogen. These bacteria produce an enzyme called beta-glucuronidase that deconjugates estrogens in the gut, making them available for reabsorption. When the estrobolome is disrupted, estrogen metabolism becomes dysregulated, potentially contributing to symptoms of hormonal imbalance that overlap with perimenopause.

Gut transit slows: Reduced estrogen and progesterone affect gut motility. Slower transit means more time for fermentation of food particles, contributing to bloating, gas, and constipation, the most common gut complaints of women in their 40s and 50s.

Recent research through 2026 highlights that gut health is not just about digestion. In women over 40, a disrupted microbiome is associated with increased inflammatory markers, worsened menopausal symptoms, greater risk of metabolic syndrome, and poorer mental health outcomes (the gut produces 90-95% of the body's serotonin).

What the Research Says

Diet is the most powerful driver of microbiome change: Research from Stanford and other leading institutions shows that a high-fiber, fermented-food-rich diet consistently increases microbial diversity within weeks. The change is measurable with microbiome testing within 2-4 weeks of dietary modification.

Fermented foods outperform fiber alone for diversity: A landmark clinical trial comparing high-fiber versus high-fermented-food diets showed that the fermented food group achieved significantly greater microbiome diversity and lower inflammatory markers. Both diets were beneficial, but fermented foods (yogurt, kefir, kimchi, sauerkraut, kombucha) had a stronger and faster effect on diversity.

Bifidobacterium and Lactobacillus supplementation is evidence-backed: Multiple randomized trials support specific probiotic strains for reducing bloating, improving bowel regularity, and enhancing immune markers in adults. Bifidobacterium longum, B. bifidum, Lactobacillus acidophilus, and L. rhamnosus have among the strongest evidence profiles.

Prebiotic fiber feeds beneficial bacteria: Prebiotics are dietary fibers (like inulin, fructooligosaccharides, and resistant starch) that selectively feed beneficial gut bacteria. Research shows they increase Bifidobacterium populations, improve short-chain fatty acid production, and reduce constipation. Dietary sources include Jerusalem artichokes, chicory root, garlic, onions, asparagus, bananas, and oats.

Polyphenols act as postbiotics: Colorful plant compounds (polyphenols) from berries, dark chocolate, green tea, olive oil, and other sources are metabolized by gut bacteria into compounds that support gut lining integrity and reduce inflammation. Research suggests that polyphenol intake is one of the most consistent predictors of microbiome diversity.

What the Evidence Doesn't Support

Probiotic supplements replacing dietary change: A probiotic capsule with 10-50 billion CFU cannot meaningfully alter the microbiome without accompanying dietary support. Supplemented bacteria are transient; they do not permanently colonize the gut. They help most when the dietary environment (plenty of fiber, fermented foods, reduced ultra-processed foods) supports their activity.

Detox cleanses or juice fasts for gut reset: There is no evidence that juice fasts or commercial cleanses improve microbiome diversity. They typically eliminate the fiber and diverse plant foods that actually feed beneficial bacteria. Many cleanse products also contain laxatives that disrupt the very microbial balance they claim to restore.

High-dose single-strain probiotics for all gut issues: Gut issues are caused by many different imbalances, and a single-strain probiotic cannot address all of them. A multi-strain, multi-species product combined with dietary diversity is better supported than a single-strain megadose for general microbiome restoration.

The 30-Day Gut Microbiome Reset Protocol

This protocol is organized in four phases that build on each other:

  1. Week 1: Eliminate (Days 1-7). Remove the main disruptors of gut microbiome health. This means eliminating ultra-processed foods, added sugars, artificial sweeteners (particularly sucralose and saccharin, which research suggests negatively affect microbiome composition), and alcohol for the full 30 days if possible. Reduce red and processed meat to no more than 2-3 times per week. You do not need to be perfect, but these are the foods most consistently linked to reduced microbiome diversity in research. Also eliminate unnecessary antibiotic use (obviously only with medical guidance) and reduce NSAIDs if you take them regularly, as both disrupt the microbiome.
  2. Week 2: Replenish (Days 8-14). Begin adding fermented foods daily. Start with 1-2 servings per day and work toward 2-3 servings by end of week. Options include plain full-fat yogurt or kefir (dairy or coconut-based), kimchi, sauerkraut, or miso. If you are not accustomed to fermented foods, start slowly to allow the microbiome to adjust. Begin a high-quality multi-strain probiotic supplement containing at least Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium strains. Take it with meals and store according to product instructions (many require refrigeration).
  3. Weeks 3-4: Rebuild (Days 15-28). Increase dietary fiber significantly. Aim for 30 or more different plant foods per week. This does not mean 30 servings; it means 30 different types (spices, herbs, grains, legumes, fruits, vegetables all count). Research from the American Gut Project shows that people eating 30-plus plant types per week have significantly greater microbiome diversity than those eating fewer than 10. Add a dedicated prebiotic fiber supplement (inulin or FOS) if dietary fiber intake is insufficient. Increase colorful vegetables and polyphenol-rich foods: berries, extra-virgin olive oil, dark leafy greens, green tea, and dark chocolate (70%+ cacao).
  4. Day 29-30 and Beyond: Maintain. Assess your baseline symptoms from Day 1 against how you feel at Day 30. Common improvements include reduced bloating, more regular bowel movements, reduced gas, improved energy, and better mood. The maintenance phase is about sustaining the dietary changes that drove improvement: 30-plus plant types per week, 1-3 daily servings of fermented foods, continued probiotic supplementation if helpful, and ongoing reduction of ultra-processed food intake.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if my microbiome actually needs a reset?
Common signs of microbiome disruption in women over 40 include chronic bloating (especially after meals), irregular bowel habits (constipation, diarrhea, or alternating patterns), frequent gas, food sensitivities that developed in adulthood, persistent fatigue, brain fog, and mood instability. These symptoms can also have other causes, so ruling out other conditions with your doctor is worthwhile before attributing everything to the microbiome.

Can I do a gut reset while eating a mostly plant-based diet?
Yes, and plant-based diets are generally associated with higher microbiome diversity than animal-protein-heavy diets. Focus on diverse plant variety (legumes, grains, vegetables, fruits, nuts, seeds, herbs, and spices), include fermented plant-based foods (kimchi, miso, coconut yogurt, kombucha), and consider a multi-strain probiotic with Bifidobacterium strains, which are especially important for plant-based eaters since Bifidobacterium populations are lower in some plant-based individuals.

Should I do microbiome testing before and after the 30-day reset?
It can be informative, though it is not required. Consumer microbiome tests (like Viome or Thrive) measure relative species abundance and can show changes after dietary intervention. However, the science of interpreting microbiome tests is still developing, and clinical guidelines do not yet support microbiome testing for routine clinical decision-making. Use it as an educational tool, not a diagnostic one.

Will probiotics help if I have just finished a course of antibiotics?
Yes. Post-antibiotic probiotic use is one of the best-supported applications for probiotic supplementation. Antibiotics disrupt the microbiome significantly, and research supports multi-strain probiotics (including Saccharomyces boulardii) during and after antibiotic treatment to reduce antibiotic-associated diarrhea and speed microbiome recovery. Take probiotics at least 2 hours away from antibiotic doses to prevent the antibiotic from killing the probiotic bacteria.

What is the connection between the gut microbiome and perimenopause symptoms?
The estrobolome (gut bacteria involved in estrogen metabolism) plays a direct role in estrogen recirculation. A diverse, healthy microbiome with adequate Bifidobacterium maintains appropriate beta-glucuronidase activity, helping the body reabsorb estrogen that might otherwise be excreted. When the estrobolome is disrupted, estrogen metabolism can become dysregulated, potentially worsening hot flashes, mood changes, and other hormonal symptoms. This gut-hormone axis is an active area of research with significant clinical implications for women in perimenopause.
