Zone 2 cardio is a low-to-moderate intensity exercise pace that research suggests activates the same mitochondrial and NAD+ pathways that are targeted by longevity supplements, making it one of the most powerful free tools available to women over 40. If you have heard the phrase "zone 2 training" and wondered whether it is worth adding to your routine alongside your supplement stack, the answer has strong biological backing. As of 2026, the combination of zone 2 exercise and NAD+ support represents one of the most evidence-informed approaches to slowing the cellular aging process that accelerates in midlife.

What to Know

  • Zone 2 cardio means exercising at 60-70% of your maximum heart rate, a conversational pace where you could speak in full sentences but feel gently challenged.
  • This training zone primarily burns fat through mitochondria, stimulates the formation of new mitochondria (biogenesis), and activates AMPK, an enzyme that supports NAD+ biosynthesis.
  • Research suggests 150-180 minutes of zone 2 per week is the optimal target for meaningful mitochondrial and metabolic adaptation.
  • Combining zone 2 training with NAD+ supplementation may have additive effects on mitochondrial health, though direct human trials combining both interventions in women over 40 are limited as of 2026.

What Is Zone 2 Cardio?

Zone 2 cardio is a heart rate training zone defined as approximately 60 to 70% of your maximum heart rate. To understand how NAD+ supports the cellular benefits of this exercise, see our full NAD+ and longevity guide. At this intensity, your body relies primarily on fat oxidation (burning fat for fuel) rather than glucose, and this fat-burning process happens almost entirely inside mitochondria. The practical test: you should be able to speak in full sentences during zone 2, but you would not want to sing. It is harder than a stroll but easier than a jog where you are breathing hard. Common zone 2 activities include brisk walking, cycling at a moderate pace, light jogging, swimming at an easy rhythm, and elliptical at low resistance. For a 45-year-old woman, maximum heart rate is approximately 175 beats per minute (using the formula 220 minus age), making her zone 2 target range approximately 105 to 122 beats per minute. For a 55-year-old, maximum heart rate is approximately 165, and zone 2 is roughly 99 to 116 beats per minute. A fitness watch with heart rate monitoring makes it easy to stay in this range.

Why This Matters After 40

After age 40, mitochondrial function declines in parallel with NAD+ levels. Mitochondria are the organelles inside your cells that convert nutrients into ATP (usable energy). When they become fewer in number and less efficient, you experience the fatigue, slower recovery, reduced exercise tolerance, and metabolic sluggishness that many women attribute simply to "getting older." Zone 2 training directly addresses this by stimulating mitochondrial biogenesis, the process by which cells create new, healthy mitochondria. Research suggests zone 2 exercise activates AMPK (AMP-activated protein kinase), an enzyme that acts as a cellular energy sensor and promotes the biosynthesis of NAD+ through the salvage pathway. More NAD+ means more fuel for sirtuins, the longevity-associated proteins that regulate cellular stress responses, inflammation, and mitochondrial maintenance. This creates a biologically elegant loop: zone 2 raises NAD+ demand and also stimulates its production, while NAD+ supplementation ensures that supply keeps pace with demand. According to Happy Aging's review of the longevity exercise literature, zone 2 training is the single exercise modality most consistently associated with the mitochondrial adaptations that matter most after menopause.

What the Research Says

A comprehensive review by Rajman, Chwalek, and Sinclair (2018, Cell Metabolism; PMID 29502867) established that NAD-boosting molecules and their associated biological pathways (including sirtuins and PARPs) are deeply integrated with mitochondrial health and exercise adaptation. This review provides the mechanistic foundation for combining exercise-based NAD+ stimulation with supplemental precursors.

Research by Yoshino et al. (2021, Science; PMID 34811282), the landmark randomized controlled trial in 25 prediabetic postmenopausal women, found that 300 mg per day of NMN for 10 weeks improved skeletal muscle insulin sensitivity. The researchers noted that this improvement was particularly evident in the context of physical activity, suggesting that exercise and NAD+ precursors may work synergistically on muscle metabolism. While this trial did not specifically study zone 2 exercise, the muscle insulin sensitivity outcome is directly relevant to the mitochondrial goals of zone 2 training.

Mills et al. (2016, Cell Metabolism; PMID 28273063) found that long-term NMN administration mitigated age-associated physiological decline in mice, with particular benefits to energy metabolism and physical activity levels, further supporting the biological plausibility of combining NAD+ support with structured aerobic exercise.

Research suggests that 150 to 180 minutes of zone 2 training per week produces meaningful improvements in mitochondrial density, fat oxidation capacity, and metabolic flexibility in women. Below 90 minutes per week, adaptations are modest. The sweet spot appears to be four to five sessions of 35 to 45 minutes each.

Happy Aging's position: Zone 2 cardio is arguably the most powerful non-pharmaceutical tool for mitochondrial health that women over 40 can implement. NAD+ supplementation is a valuable complement to this practice, not a replacement for it.

How to Start Zone 2 Cardio: A Step-by-Step Protocol

  1. Calculate your zone 2 heart rate range. Use the formula: 220 minus your age equals estimated maximum heart rate. Multiply by 0.60 for the low end and 0.70 for the high end of zone 2. Example: a 48-year-old woman has an estimated max HR of 172 bpm; her zone 2 range is approximately 103 to 120 bpm.
  2. Choose a zone 2 activity you enjoy. Walking briskly, cycling (outdoors or stationary), swimming, rowing, or using an elliptical all work well. The best activity is the one you will actually do consistently. Avoid inclines or paces that push you out of zone 2 without realizing it.
  3. Start with 3 sessions of 30 minutes per week. If you are new to structured cardio, begin at three sessions. The goal is to accumulate 150 minutes per week over 4 to 6 weeks. Many women find that adding a fourth session once they are comfortable is easy and enjoyable.
  4. Use a heart rate monitor to stay honest. Zone 2 is often slower than people expect. Many women who think they are jogging in zone 2 are actually in zone 3 or 4 when they check. A fitness watch keeps you accountable and prevents drifting into higher intensities.
  5. Add your NAD+ support in the morning. Taking NAD+ precursors in the morning, when cellular energy demand is rising, aligns with your body's circadian patterns. If you exercise in the morning, take your supplement with breakfast within an hour before or after your session.
  6. Progress gradually to 150-180 minutes per week. Once you reach 150 minutes per week consistently, you are in the range where research suggests meaningful mitochondrial adaptation occurs. You can extend individual sessions or add a fifth day.
  7. Complement with 1-2 weekly resistance training sessions. Zone 2 builds mitochondrial quality; resistance training builds muscle quantity. Both are necessary for comprehensive longevity fitness after 40. Do not sacrifice one for the other.

What the Evidence Doesn't Support

No published randomized controlled trial has specifically tested the combination of zone 2 exercise plus NAD+ supplementation versus either intervention alone in women over 40, as of 2026. The synergy hypothesis is biologically sound and supported by mechanistic research, but it has not been proven in a head-to-head human clinical trial. Additionally, zone 2 training is not a shortcut: the mitochondrial adaptations that matter require consistent, sustained effort over months, not weeks. Research does not support the idea that zone 2 is sufficient on its own without also addressing nutrition, sleep, stress, and resistance training as foundational pillars of healthy aging.

Frequently Asked Questions

What heart rate is zone 2 for women over 40?

Zone 2 is approximately 60 to 70% of your maximum heart rate. To estimate your maximum heart rate, subtract your age from 220. For a 45-year-old woman: 220 minus 45 equals 175 bpm maximum; zone 2 is approximately 105 to 122 bpm. For a 50-year-old: 220 minus 50 equals 170; zone 2 is approximately 102 to 119 bpm. A heart rate monitor will give you real-time feedback so you can stay in the zone.

How long does it take to see results from zone 2 training?

Measurable mitochondrial adaptations, including increased mitochondrial density and improved fat oxidation, generally require 6 to 12 weeks of consistent training at the recommended volume (150 minutes per week). Many women report subjective improvements in energy and recovery within 4 to 6 weeks of starting a regular zone 2 practice, but the deeper cellular changes that the research documents take longer to fully develop.

Can I do zone 2 if I have never exercised much?

Yes. Zone 2 is intentionally low intensity, which makes it one of the most accessible starting points for women who are returning to exercise or beginning for the first time. A brisk walk where you feel slightly warm but can hold a conversation is zone 2 for most untrained women. Start with 20 to 30 minutes three times per week and build from there.

Does zone 2 cardio help with weight management?

Zone 2 cardio relies primarily on fat oxidation for fuel, which means your body is burning fat during the exercise itself. Over time, improved metabolic flexibility (the ability to efficiently burn fat versus glucose) supports healthier body composition. However, research suggests that exercise alone is rarely sufficient for significant weight loss without also addressing nutrition. Zone 2 is best viewed as a metabolic health and longevity tool that has secondary body composition benefits.

Should I take NAD+ before or after zone 2 exercise?

There is no definitive human trial comparing morning versus post-exercise NAD+ timing, so the evidence does not support a strong recommendation either way. Taking NAD+ precursors in the morning with breakfast is convenient and aligns with circadian patterns for cellular energy demand. If you exercise in the morning, taking your supplement at breakfast (before or after the session) is a practical approach.

Is zone 2 better than HIIT for women over 40?

Zone 2 and HIIT serve different purposes and are not direct substitutes. Zone 2 specifically targets mitochondrial biogenesis and fat oxidation capacity, which research suggests are particularly important for metabolic and longevity outcomes after 40. HIIT builds VO2max and cardiovascular fitness efficiently but in shorter sessions. Research suggests the optimal protocol for women over 40 includes 3 to 4 zone 2 sessions per week and 1 to 2 HIIT sessions per week, rather than choosing one exclusively.
